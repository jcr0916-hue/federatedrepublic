The Second Renewal

Year 8. The convoy escort operation in the Halvern Strait — authorized in Year 6, renewed in Year 7 — approaches its second renewal. §14.3 now controls: authorization may not be renewed again unless the Senate has approved a Transition and Reconstruction Plan specifying how the Republic's involvement ends. The Legat Consul argues an open-ended maritime commitment cannot honestly carry a withdrawal timeline. The Senate's answer: then it cannot honestly carry a third authorization.

§14.3 Military Accountability §14.1 Authorized Purposes for Military Force §14.2 Defense Authorization and Allocation §2.2 Military Authorization Framework

Constitutions are good at starting wars and bad at ending them. Authorization is a single dramatic vote; termination is a hundred small decisions no one wants to own, and the default — in nearly every constitutional order that has faced the problem — is drift. Operations outlive their purposes because ending them requires someone to move, and the constitutional machinery only ever required someone to move at the beginning. §14.3 attacks the drift at its load-bearing joint: the renewal. An authorization may be renewed once on its original terms. The second renewal — the moment a commitment reveals itself as open-ended — may not occur unless the Senate has approved a Transition and Reconstruction Plan: alliance obligations, civilian reconstruction support, transitional justice support, and a withdrawal timeline, published to the NRS and binding on both executives.

The Halvern Strait operation began in Year 6 under the cleanest possible §14.1 authority. Armed interdiction of commercial shipping in the strait — flag-of-convenience raiders operating from the collapsed littoral state to the south — had struck vessels of Caldenmere registry and of a treaty ally. The Legislature authorized convoy escort and interdiction, stating the authorized purpose as protection of the Republic's citizens and treaty allies, with force categories and an expenditure ceiling under §14.2. The Legat Consul executed it well. Attacks fell by four-fifths in the first year. The Year 7 renewal passed the Senate 44-9.

And there the operation settled into the shape §14.3 was written to interrupt: successful, popular, affordable — and structurally permanent. The raiders had not been defeated; they had been suppressed, and suppression is a subscription. The littoral state showed no near prospect of reconstituting a coast guard. Every strategic assessment reached the same honest conclusion: the escorts were working, and would be needed indefinitely.

The Objection

The Legat Consul, Maren Josk, put the objection to the Senate directly when the second renewal entered committee in Year 8, Month 3. A withdrawal timeline, she argued, is a promise to the enemy as much as to the chamber: publish the date the escorts leave and the raiders need only wait. An open-ended maritime security commitment was not a war drifting past its purpose — it was the purpose, functioning as designed, and forcing it into a departure schedule would trade real security for constitutional tidiness. The argument was serious, and the Senate's Military Affairs committee treated it as such. What the committee could not do was treat it as an exemption. §14.3's text carries no open-ended-commitment exception; legislative authorization may not be renewed beyond the second renewal unless the Senate has approved a Plan, and where authorization lapses, §14.2 shuts off expenditure with a 90-day wind-down and no new commitments. The constitutional pressure ran entirely one direction.

§14.3 — What a Plan Must Be
The provision does not demand a fixed exit date. It demands a specified end state and the path to it.

The committee's answer to Josk reframed the requirement. A withdrawal timeline under §14.3 is a timeline of conditions and stages, not necessarily a calendar date — but it must be real: named obligations to be transferred, named capacities to be built, and defined triggers under which each escort element stands down. What the provision prohibits is not a long involvement. It is an involvement with no articulated end at all, renewed indefinitely on the strength of its own momentum.

Josk's staff and the committee negotiated for eleven weeks — the Senate may amend the Plan in consultation and coordination with the Legat Consul, and this one was built jointly from the first draft.

National Record System — Senate Approval — Year 8, Month 7
TRANSITION AND RECONSTRUCTION PLAN — Halvern Strait Escort Operation
Approved under §14.3 — Military Accountability

Alliance obligations: escort of treaty-ally shipping transfers to the joint maritime framework under the existing defense treaty, on that instrument's burden-sharing schedule. Civilian reconstruction: Republic support for littoral coast guard reconstitution — vessels, training mission, harbor infrastructure — funded and scheduled in Annex B. Transitional justice: interdiction detainee cases referred under the framework in Annex C. Withdrawal timeline: escort elements stand down in three stages, each triggered by certified littoral patrol capability thresholds; final stage no later than Year 12 absent renewed authorization on new grounds.

This Plan is binding on both executives within their respective domains.

— The Senate of the Republic

The Renewal, and the Plan at Work

With the Plan approved and published, the second renewal passed in Month 8. What changed was not the escorts — they sailed the next morning as they had the previous morning — but the operation's constitutional character. It now ran toward something. The coast guard mission stood up in Year 9; the first littoral patrol squadron certified in Year 10 and the first escort element stood down on the trigger, not on a vote; the EM's annual military compliance assessment under §9.1.b began measuring the operation against the Plan's stages, published to the NRS beside the expenditure audit. The binding ran both ways, and on both executives: the Civic Consul's domain carried the reconstruction funding through two budget cycles because a published constitutional plan, not an executive preference, said it would.

The strait was not made safe by Year 12; straits rarely are. But the third-stage assessment in Year 12 was a real decision on a real record — patrol capability certified but uneven, one escort element retained under a fresh, narrower authorization stating its own §14.1 purpose. The Republic chose to stay in a smaller way, on new grounds, with open eyes. That is the entire ambition of §14.3: not that every operation ends on schedule, but that no operation continues by default.

What This Scenario Tests

§14.3 places the burden of continuation where drift is otherwise unopposed. First authorization is a decision; first renewal is a decision; by the second renewal, the constitution presumes the commitment is becoming structural and demands the one document institutions never volunteer — the plan for how it ends. The price of continuing is articulating the ending. A Legislature unwilling to approve a Plan is, by that unwillingness, deciding the operation should stop, and §14.2's expenditure cutoff makes the decision self-executing.

The Josk objection is the provision's honest stress test, and the answer matters: §14.3 does not force false dates onto genuinely open-ended security needs. It forces the need to be named, staged, and owned — and where an obligation is truly permanent, it forces that conclusion into a treaty or a standing framework the Senate ratifies as such, rather than letting permanence accrete through serial renewals of an authorization drafted for an emergency.

And the Plan's bindingness on both executives closes the dual-executive seam. Post-conflict obligations are mostly civilian — reconstruction money, justice frameworks, refugee consequences — sitting in the Civic Consul's domain while the operation sits in the Legat Consul's. A plan binding only the executive who fights would end wars without ending them. §14.3 binds the Republic.
