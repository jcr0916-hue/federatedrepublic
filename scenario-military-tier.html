Military Authorization · §2.1.a–d · Three-Tier War Powers

The Ninety-Six Hours

Armed forces cross the northern border at 0340 on a Tuesday. The President invokes Tier 1. The 96-hour Emergency Defense Allocation is self-executing. At hour ninety-two, the PM notifies the President she will not certify continued expenditure. The Senate Armed Services Committee votes to authorize Tier 2. Four clocks are running simultaneously, and only one of them is controlled by the President.

§2.1.a Tier 1 — Immediate Defense §2.1.b Tier 2 — Senate Armed Services §2.1.c Tier 3 — Legislative Declaration §2.1.d Funding Lock §2.1.h Dual Executive Conflict §12.1 Authorized Purposes

The incursion begins before dawn: irregular forces, estimated at battalion strength, cross the Republic’s northern border at three points simultaneously. They are not uniformed. Their national affiliation is unknown. Within six minutes of the first confirmed crossing, the President invokes §2.1.a and authorizes Tier 1 defensive operations. The notification to both chambers is transmitted at 0346. The 96-hour Emergency Defense Allocation activates automatically.

What Tier 1 Authorizes

Tier 1 is the constitution’s emergency carve-out from the principle that war requires democratic consent. It is narrow by design. The President may deploy forces already positioned, authorize defensive fire, and direct the immediate neutralization of the identified threat. Tier 1 does not authorize offensive operations into foreign territory. It does not authorize sustained deployment. It does not authorize action against a declared adversary state. It expires when the immediate threat is neutralized — or when the President seeks to extend operations, whichever comes first.

The Emergency Defense Allocation provides 96 hours of automatic funding without PM certification. The design is explicit: the Republic cannot leave its borders undefended while the executives negotiate. But the 96-hour limit is also explicit: it is not a blank check. At hour 96, if the PM has not certified continued expenditure, uncertified spending is void and a pay freeze on military personnel directly supporting the operations activates automatically under §2.1.d.

§2.1.a — Tier 1 · Hour 0
President invokes Tier 1; Emergency Defense Allocation begins

Republic defensive forces engage the incursion at three points. By hour six the northernmost crossing is contained. The central and southern crossings are still active. The President’s military advisors estimate that full containment requires 72 to 120 additional hours of sustained deployment — well past the 96-hour Tier 1 window.

At hour eighteen the President requests a classified briefing session with the Senate Armed Services Committee. She is already building the Tier 2 case.

The PM’s Objection

The Prime Minister has been briefed continuously since hour one. She does not dispute the immediate defensive response. What she disputes is the strategic framing the President’s office is developing: that the incursion represents a pattern of aggression from a neighboring state requiring sustained deterrent positioning along the northern border — a deployment that would last months, not days.

At hour seventy-two, the PM transmits a formal written notification to the President: she will not certify military expenditure beyond the 96-hour Emergency Defense Allocation for operations exceeding immediate defensive containment. Her stated grounds are fiscal: a sustained northern deployment at the scale the President is contemplating would require emergency reallocation from infrastructure and BNF programs, triggering a Fiscal Objection under §2.4.b. The notification is published to the NRS at hour seventy-four per §2.7’s executive transparency requirements.

The President does not accept the PM’s framing. She files a written NRS objection under §2.1.h at hour seventy-eight, asserting that the PM’s refusal to certify constitutes interference with an active military operation falling squarely within her constitutional domain. The 12-hour negotiation window begins.

The Tier 2 Vote

While the executives are in their 12-hour negotiation window, the Senate Armed Services Committee convenes. The President has already provided the classified briefing required by §2.1.b. At hour eighty-eight, the Committee votes 7-4 to authorize Tier 2 operations for 30 days. The authorization is published to the NRS.

This changes the constitutional geometry. Tier 2 is not dependent on PM certification for its initial grant of authority — it is a legislative authorization running directly from the Committee to the President within the President’s security domain. But it does not override the funding lock. The PM’s refusal to certify continued expenditure remains in effect. Tier 2 authorization without PM funding certification means the President has the legal authority to conduct operations she cannot constitutionally pay for.

§2.1.d — Funding Lock · Hour 90
Negotiation window closes; SC petition filed

The negotiation produces no resolution. The President petitions the Supreme Court at hour 90 for an emergency domain determination under §2.1.h: does the PM’s refusal to certify military expenditure beyond the Emergency Defense Allocation, during an active Tier 2-authorized operation, constitute interference with the President’s constitutional security domain?

The SC has 24 hours to rule. The Emergency Defense Allocation expires at hour 96 — six hours before the SC’s ruling deadline. Pending the ruling, §2.1.h provides that the Presidential order governs operations directly connected to the active military situation. But military personnel cannot be paid with a void certification, and §2.1.d is explicit that the pay freeze activates automatically.

What the Constitution Does at Hour 96

At 0340 on Saturday — exactly 96 hours after the incursion began — the Emergency Defense Allocation expires. The PM has not certified continued expenditure. Military pay for personnel directly supporting the northern deployment freezes automatically. Operations do not automatically cease — the Tier 2 authorization from the Senate Armed Services Committee remains valid — but the funding mechanism that pays for them is constitutionally void until the PM certifies or a court orders otherwise.

At hour 112, the Supreme Court rules. The 3-2 majority holds that the PM’s refusal to certify constitutes a fiscal objection within her constitutional authority under §2.4.b and does not constitute interference with the President’s security domain under §2.1.h. The President has authority to conduct the operations. She does not have authority to compel PM certification of their funding. The two things are constitutionally separable.

SC Ruling — Hour 112 — Published to NRS

The President’s constitutional domain encompasses the decision to deploy forces in response to an armed incursion and to seek legislative authorization through the Tier structure. The PM’s constitutional domain encompasses the certification of all military expenditure beyond the self-executing Emergency Defense Allocation. Neither domain is superior. Neither executive may compel the other to act within their respective domain.

The funding lock of §2.1.d is not a veto on military operations — it does not authorize the PM to halt an operation the President has Tier 2 authority to conduct. But it does mean that operations not certified by the PM must be funded from the Emergency Defense Allocation during its active window, and cannot proceed on void appropriations thereafter. The pay freeze operates automatically by constitutional design. Its resolution requires either PM certification or emergency legislative appropriation.

Resolution

The House passes an emergency military appropriation by simple majority at hour 118, providing 30 days of funding for the Tier 2-authorized operations without requiring PM certification. The PM does not invoke a Fiscal Objection to the appropriation — her objection was to unilateral executive expenditure, not to legislatively-authorized funding. The pay freeze lifts at hour 119. Northern operations continue under Tier 2 authorization for the full 30-day window.

The scenario ends without a Tier 3 Declaration. The full legislature is never asked to authorize the operation, and the 30-day Tier 2 window expires without renewal. All operations in excess of immediate territorial defense cease automatically at day 30 by operation of §2.1.b — no presidential override, no PM objection required. The constitution’s timer runs out and the authorization dissolves.

What the scenario demonstrates is that the three-tier system is not a simple escalation ladder where each tier supersedes the last. The tiers govern authorization. The funding lock governs expenditure. They operate on parallel tracks, and the PM’s power to withhold certification is not neutralized by a Tier 2 vote — it is simply routed around by a legislative appropriation. The constitution anticipated exactly this resolution: separated the decision to fight from the decision to fund, and provided a democratic path to fund what the executives could not agree on.
