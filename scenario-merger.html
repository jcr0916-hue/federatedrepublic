Territorial Merger · §15.7 · Statehood Pathway

The Smaller Half

The eastern districts of Territory Valdric petition to merge with the adjacent State of Arveld rather than pursue standalone statehood. A 60% referendum, a State approval process, a joint audit of the combined entity, and a viability question about the 340,000 people left behind. The first time §15.7 has ever been used.

Valdric is a Territory of 890,000 people, geographically divided by the Keln Ridge — a mountain range that has defined the territory's politics longer than the Republic has existed. The western districts, centered on the capital Valdric City, contain most of the population, most of the economy, and all of the territory's federal administrative infrastructure. The eastern districts — four of them, strung along the Arveld border — contain 340,000 people in the west and 550,000 in the east. The east has always looked toward Arveld. The west has always looked toward Statehood.

In Year 14, Valdric passes its first Statehood Audit as a unified territory. The path to Statehood is open. The eastern districts' governance council publishes a statement the same week: they are not going to Statehood with the west. They want to go to Arveld.

The Petition — §15.7

The eastern districts' petition is the first ever filed under §15.7. The LI receives it and begins the eligibility assessment. The provision has three requirements: geographic contiguity with the target State, a petition resolution from the Territory's governance body, and LI certification that the proposed merger boundaries are clearly defined and on the NRS.

The contiguity question is straightforward — the four eastern districts share a 240-kilometre land border with Arveld. The petition resolution requires more care. The eastern districts have a regional governance council, but Valdric's territorial governance structure gives that council advisory authority only. The petition resolution must come from the full Valdric Territorial Council — which includes representatives from the western districts who are strongly opposed to the split.

§15.7 — Governance Council Requirement
The petition must come from the Territory's governance body. Not from the petitioning region.

The LI's eligibility ruling is careful: §15.7 requires "a petition resolution adopted by the Territory's governance council or equivalent body." The eastern districts' regional council is not the territorial governance council. The LI publishes an advisory to the NRS: for the petition to be procedurally valid, the full Valdric Territorial Council must adopt the resolution — or the eastern districts must demonstrate that they constitute a "defined geographic subdivision" operating under a distinct governance authority recognized by the Territory's own legal structure.

The Valdric Territorial Council votes 34–18 to adopt the petition resolution. Fourteen western-district representatives vote in favour — enough to carry it. Their reasoning, published in the debate record: the eastern districts will leave one way or another, and a constitutional process is better than a constitutional crisis.

The LI certifies the petition as eligible. Boundaries are defined as the four named eastern districts as constituted on the date of petition, with their existing registered voter rolls. The certification is published to the NRS. The 18-month dual approval clock begins.

Two Referendums — §15.7

§15.7 requires affirmative approval from both the merging territory and the target State. For the eastern districts, that means a referendum of eligible voters achieving 60% affirmative support with at least 50% participation. For Arveld, the constitution leaves the approval mechanism to the State — Arveld's own constitution and statute govern how and whether the State approves.

The eastern districts' referendum runs over a three-day national voting window administered by the Elections Panel under Article XI. The campaigns are sharp. The west does not have a formal vote in this referendum — §15.7 explicitly confines the Territory referendum to the population of the merging portion. But western-district voices are present in the eastern campaign, arguing that merger with Arveld means permanently ceding any voice in the Republic's Senate to an existing State's representation rather than building Valdric's own. The eastern districts' pro-merger campaign responds simply: Arveld has two Senate seats now. So does an independent Valdric. Merger costs nothing in Senate terms. And Arveld's economy is twice Valdric's per-capita GDP.

Arveld's approval process runs through its state legislature, which under Arveld's constitution requires a simple majority of the full State Assembly for territorial incorporation. The debate in Arveld is less about whether to approve and more about what eastern Valdric's incorporation means for Arveld's existing House apportionment. Arveld currently holds six House seats. Adding 550,000 people at the next census will likely add one, possibly two more. The economic argument is also strong — the eastern districts' agricultural capacity and water rights are resources Arveld has wanted access to for decades.

The Joint Audit — §15.7

Both approvals are now on the NRS within the 18-month window. The LI and JI commence the Joint Statehood Audit of the combined entity: Arveld as it is, plus the four eastern districts. The audit applies the §15.2 fiscal integrity criteria and §15.2 Article I compliance criteria to the combined entity. The existing State retains its constitutional status throughout — audit failure blocks the merger but does not affect Arveld's Statehood.

The fiscal audit of the combined entity is uncomplicated. Arveld is one of the Republic's fiscally strongest States. Adding the eastern districts — which have agricultural revenues, federal social state pass-through funds, and no significant debt — improves Arveld's per-capita fiscal metrics marginally. The §15.2 threshold is passed with comfortable headroom.

The Article I compliance audit is more complex. The JI examines Arveld's existing compliance record plus the eastern districts' compliance record as a portion of Valdric. The eastern districts have a clean record on equality enforcement. Arveld has a single outstanding matter: a housing exclusion case from three years ago that the Arveld state government has appealed to the Appellate Court. The JI's audit finding is that this matter is pending judicial resolution and does not constitute a pattern of Article I non-enforcement. The combined entity passes the §15.2 audit.

Joint Statehood Audit Finding — §15.7

The combined entity of Arveld and the four eastern districts of Valdric meets the fiscal integrity standard under §15.2 and the Article I compliance standard under §15.2. Existing Statehood Audit certification of Arveld is unaffected. The merger may proceed to Senate ratification under §15.7.

One matter is noted: the Arveld housing exclusion case (Appellate Docket 14-2281) is pending. This does not constitute audit failure. The JI will monitor the outcome as part of Arveld's standard annual compliance review following any merger.

The Viability Question — §15.7

Before the Senate ratification vote, the LI must assess whether the remaining Territory — western Valdric, 340,000 people — is viable for eventual standalone Statehood. The assessment is not whether the remaining territory currently meets the Statehood Audit criteria. It is whether a credible path to meeting them exists.

This is the provision that pauses the merger. Western Valdric has 340,000 people. The Republic's Statehood Audit has no explicit population floor — but the LI's published methodology has consistently found that fiscal sustainability at the constitutional standard requires either a sufficiently large population or exceptional natural resource endowment. Western Valdric has neither. Valdric City is a regional administrative hub, not an economic engine. The eastern districts' agricultural revenues and water rights — the economic base that made unified Valdric's first Statehood Audit barely passable — are leaving with the merger.

§15.7 — Viability Assessment
Is there a credible path to Statehood for what remains?

The LI's assessment is published 81 days after the merger petition. It is not a finding of non-viability. It is a conditional finding: western Valdric's path to Statehood exists only if one of three conditions is met within fifteen years — federal investment in economic diversification under a statute specifically committed to the NRS before Senate ratification, negotiated resource-sharing arrangements with Arveld covering the eastern districts' agricultural revenues, or population growth through immigration incentives that brings western Valdric above 500,000 people.

The LI's assessment stops short of finding that the partial merger "may not proceed." The provision permits the merger to proceed if the Republic commits by statute to a specific development and support program published to the NRS before the Senate ratification vote. The ball is in the Senate's court.

The Senate debates the development commitment for six weeks. Three competing statutes are introduced. The version that eventually passes provides for a fifteen-year federal infrastructure and economic diversification programme for western Valdric, funded at a defined level, with LI annual compliance reporting. The statute is narrow — it commits to the support but does not guarantee Statehood, which the constitution does not permit Parliament to guarantee. It is published to the NRS before the ratification vote.

Senate Ratification — §15.7

The ratification vote requires a simple majority of full seated Senate membership. The merger has now been through an 18-month dual approval process, a clean joint audit, and a conditional viability assessment with a statutory development commitment. The procedural case for ratification is complete.

The political case is contested. Eight Senators vote against on principle: the merger mechanism should not exist, because it rewards separatist movements within territories and creates an incentive for economically stronger regions to detach from weaker ones before the combined entity seeks Statehood. The response from the floor: §15.7 exists precisely because forced unity is not a constitutional value. A Territory that cannot achieve viable Statehood as a unified entity may have subparts that can. The question is not whether separation is good but whether the constitutional conditions for it have been met. They have.

After Ratification

The merger takes effect on the date of ratification. The four eastern districts are incorporated into Arveld by operation of law. Their 550,000 residents become Arveld citizens for all constitutional purposes. Arveld's House seat allocation will be recalculated at the next census — the early projections suggest one additional seat. Arveld's two Senate seats remain unchanged.

The LI publishes updated State boundaries to the NRS within the 30-day statutory period. The eastern districts' existing statutes, local governance structures, and court jurisdictions transition under a two-year harmonization statute passed in the Arveld Assembly before ratification.

Western Valdric continues as a Territory under §15.1. Its Statehood Audit baseline carries forward from where unified Valdric stood — it does not restart. The federal development statute published before ratification activates. The LI begins its first annual compliance report on the programme within twelve months.

The Appellate Court rules on the Arveld housing exclusion case fourteen months after ratification. The ruling goes against the Arveld state government on narrow grounds — the exclusion policy violated §1.6's non-exclusion principle. Arveld amends the policy within 90 days. The JI's next Article I compliance audit notes the correction and closes the matter.

What §15.7 Does and Does Not Require

§15.7 does not ask whether merger is good policy. It asks whether the constitutional conditions have been met: geographic contiguity, a governance council petition, a 60%/50% territorial referendum, a State approval, a joint audit of the combined entity, and a viability assessment of what remains. The Senate's role is ratification of a process that has run correctly — not a fresh policy judgment on the merits.

The Senators who voted against were not wrong to raise the incentive question. A Territory with one strong region and one weak region now has a constitutional mechanism for the strong region to leave. That mechanism exists, and this scenario is the first time it has been used. Whether the fifteen-year development programme for western Valdric produces a viable State remains to be seen. If it does not, western Valdric has §15.3 and §15.4 available to it — the constitutional framework for Territories that cannot sustain Statehood on their own.
