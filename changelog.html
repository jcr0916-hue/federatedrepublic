Changelog

Every revision to the Federated Republic — what changed, why it changed, and what gap it closes. The constitution is a living document; this is its institutional memory.

About versioning. Version numbers track the constitutional document, not the website. A version increment means at least one provision was added, revised, or removed. Minor fixes — terminology corrections, cross-reference repairs, drafting errors — are included where they affect meaning. The document is available for download at each current version. v126 May 2026 Current
Federal-State Hierarchy · Organizational Standing · Ripeness and Mootness

Two remaining constitutional gaps closed following the gap analysis. §15.6.b defines the Federal-State statutory hierarchy — federal statutes prevail over conflicting State statutes, concurrent operation preserved where no conflict exists, field preemption by implication requires clear legislative intent. §4.3.b and §4.3.c expand judicial standing to organizational petitioners and codify the ripeness and mootness doctrines, protecting the no-advisory-opinion principle across all proceedings.

Article XV — Constitutional Supremacy
  • §15.6.b newFederal-State Statutory Hierarchy: federal statutes within the Legislature's constitutional competence prevail over conflicting State statutes. Where no conflict exists, both operate concurrently. Courts apply conflict test then obstacle test before finding preemption; field preemption by implication requires clear evidence of legislative intent. Legislature may expressly designate whether a federal enactment occupies a field exclusively, permits concurrent regulation, or sets a floor.
Article IV — Judicial Architecture
  • §4.3.b newOrganizational Standing: an organization may bring constitutional claims on behalf of members where at least one member has individual standing, the claim is germane to the organization's constitutional purpose, and individual participation is not required. Organization must publish identification of an injured member to the NRS concurrent with filing.
  • §4.3.c newRipeness and Mootness: courts may not rule on unripe cases (injury not yet concrete) or moot cases (controversy no longer live). Two exceptions to mootness: recurring conduct capable of evading review, and voluntary cessation without court assurance against resumption. Supreme Court determines standards by published decision; Legislature may not alter them by statute.

v125 May 2026 Minor
Treaty Implementation · Treaty Withdrawal · Non-Derogable List Completed

Two constitutional gaps closed following gap analysis: the domestic force of ratified treaties (previously unaddressed) and the mechanism for presidential treaty withdrawal (previously absent). The §1.25.b non-derogable rights list completed — §1.1 (right to life) and §1.27 (non-refoulement) were non-derogable by their own terms but omitted from the authoritative list. A duplicated sentence in §8.1.b removed.

Article III — Senate
  • §3.2.e.i newTreaty Domestic Application: ratified treaties are directly applicable in domestic courts as federal law from the date of ratification, unless the treaty requires implementing legislation or its provisions are insufficiently definite for judicial application. Legislature must pass a treaty implementation framework within two years of Phase 4 activation. In the absence of a legislature designation requiring implementing legislation, courts apply treaties directly. A legislature determination requiring implementing legislation is not subject to presidential veto.
  • §3.2.e.ii newTreaty Withdrawal: President may initiate withdrawal by publishing notice to NRS with minimum six-month effective date. Three tracks: (1) General treaties — Senate may block by simple majority within six months, otherwise withdrawal proceeds automatically; (2) Mutual defense treaties — Senate must affirmatively confirm by simple majority within six months or treaty remains in force; withdrawal may not be initiated during active Tier 2/3 operations under the treaty; (3) Indigenous treaties — no withdrawal or modification by any Republic authority; Article XIV governs.
Article I — Non-Derogable List
  • §1.25.bNon-derogable rights list completed: §1.1 (right to life) and §1.27 (non-refoulement) added. Both were already non-derogable by their own provisions' express terms but were omitted from the authoritative §1.25.b list. The list is now complete and explicitly stated to be exhaustive — all other Article I rights remain subject to the §1.25.a proportionality standard during a valid Declaration of National Emergency.
Drafting
  • §8.1.bDuplicate sentence removed. The EI candidate assessment description appeared twice in §8.1.b.

v124 May 2026 Minor
Final Constitutional Build — Pending Notes Applied · IOC Eliminated

Application of all pending scenario-testing fixes and institutional design changes accumulated since v121. The Inspectorate Oversight Committee is eliminated and replaced by the State Designee Panel — a rotating body of State-appointed representatives with the single function of verifying For-Cause IG removal grounds. Eight structural and accountability gaps closed. Nine fixes across Articles II, IV, V, VIII, and X.

Article II — Executive Accountability
  • §2.1.h newDual executive conflict resolution during active military operations: President has 6 hours to file written objection; Emergency Panel rules within 24 hours; PM domestic orders remain operative pending resolution; failure to object within 6 hours waives the conflict claim.
  • §2.2.g newMinisterial appointments defined: PM appoints and removes ministers within the Domestic Affairs Council; all appointments published to NRS within 24 hours; ministers exercise only PM-domain authority; unrecorded ministerial orders are void; same civil servant duty of refusal applies.
  • §2.10.aStatement of Grounds required with House removal vote: specifies the basis for removal in plain language, published simultaneously with the vote; serves as Articles of Impeachment for §3.5.c trial mechanics; Senate may not convict on grounds not included in the Statement.
Article IV — Judicial Architecture
  • §4.1.bLegislature-defined confirmation period ceiling constraint added: confirmation period cannot be set at a duration that, combined with elapsed time since vacancy, would push Deemed Confirmation past the 180-day constitutional ceiling. Late nominations automatically shorten the remaining confirmation window.
  • §4.2.eEmergency Panel rotation rules simplified: "at least two members of the sitting Panel must have served on the prior cycle's Panel" replaces the 18-month minimum service requirement and two-member maximum replacement rule. Resolves the mid-cycle retirement edge case left unaddressed by the old language.
Article V — Citizenship
  • §5.4.bCLT Administrator explicit eligibility criteria defined: no government position in prior 5 years; no financial interest in educational testing or credentialing entities; demonstrated expertise in psychometrics, constitutional law, educational policy, or civic literacy research. Constitutional floor; Legislature may add requirements by statute.
Article VIII — Inspectorates
  • §8.4.dSimultaneous IG vacancy provision: Senate under §3.2.g survivor quorum replaces the IOC for temporary IG appointments during catastrophic events. Faster activation; uses a body already constitutionally mobilized during catastrophes.
  • §8.5.aFor-Cause removal verification: State Designee Panel replaces IOC. Panel finding is a prerequisite for the legislative vote regardless of which way it goes — a finding of unsubstantiated grounds does not block the vote, it becomes the permanent public record alongside it. Added: the removal vote itself is not a Rule Violation solely by reason of political motivation where the process is constitutionally complete.
  • §8.9IOC eliminated entirely. Section 8.9 is now the State Designee Panel: composition of pool, Electoral Commission lot draw at each two-year cycle, single mandate (For-Cause verification), term transition rules, active-service-only compensation, and SC appeals mechanism.
Article X — Public Finance
  • §10.4.dMA Director veto on methodology grounds must be accompanied by a Supreme Court methodology challenge filed within 48 hours, or the veto lapses automatically. Board override proceeds regardless of any pending challenge. Director's removal protection runs from the date the challenge is filed, not the date of the veto.

v120 May 2026 Major
Inspectorate Model Revision · Legislature Terminology

Two significant changes. First: the Inspectorate candidate assessment model was redesigned — nominating actors now submit candidate disclosure records; Inspectorates review for accuracy and publish recommendations rather than generating independent assessments. Second: "Parliament" and "Parliamentary" replaced throughout with "Legislature" and "Legislative", reflecting the document's universal applicability rather than a British parliamentary association.

Inspectorate Assessment Model
  • §8.1.bEI mandate description updated: reviews submitted candidate disclosure records, publishes recommendations. Does not generate independent assessments.
  • §8.3.bExecutive IG appointment: House submits candidate disclosure record to JI (not EI); JI reviews and publishes recommendation before Senate confirmation.
  • §8.4.aQualified Jurist Registry: candidates submit registration records; JI reviews for accuracy and eligibility; publishes recommendation. SC appeals under §8.9.d unchanged.
  • §8.6.g newCitizen Discrepancy Petition added. Any citizen who identifies a discrepancy in a published candidate disclosure record may petition their elected representative. No fee, threshold, or filing requirement.
  • §4.2.cTriad nominee vetting: Registry record serves as the disclosure record. JI confirms Registry status and no disqualifying change since admission — does not generate a new assessment.
  • §5.4.b / §9.3 / §10.4.a / §17.4.gAll PM-nominated positions updated: PM submits candidate disclosure record to EI; EI reviews and publishes recommendation. Old proactive assessment model removed.

Government-system descriptors ("parliamentary vs presidential") preserved unchanged. 186 run-level changes in the constitutional document; 189 changes across the website.
v119 May 2026 Major
Presidential Removal · Triad Revision · Judicial Overhaul · MA Board Model

The most structurally significant revision since the document's founding. Fourteen distinct amendments across Articles II, III, IV, V, IX, X, XVI, and XVII. Four areas of focus: presidential accountability (two new removal tracks), Supreme Court nomination (Triad redesigned), judicial removal and vacancy (three-track architecture for all courts), and Monetary Authority governance (elected Director plus board with veto/override).

Article II — Presidential Accountability
  • §2.7.e newRule Violation modified consequences for legislators: suspension from voting and committee participation; mandatory §3.6 expulsion referral within 30 days. Chambers retain the expulsion decision but cannot avoid commencing proceedings.
  • §2.10 newPresidential removal — two political tracks independent of §2.7. Path 1: 2/3 House to initiate + 2/3 Senate to convict (Statement of Grounds required). Path 2: Senate simple majority refers to national recall referendum; removal requires majority of eligible voters; one referral per term.
  • §2.2.g newMinisterial appointments defined: PM appoints and removes ministers within the Domestic Affairs Council; all appointments published to NRS; ministers exercise only authority delegated within the PM's domain.
Article III — Legislative Accountability
  • §3.2.h newSenate Speaker vacancy: Deputy Speaker assumes succession role immediately; Senate must elect new Speaker within 72 hours. Protects the presidential succession line.
  • §3.5Scope clarified: §3.5 impeachment applies exclusively to executive and judicial officers. Legislative members governed exclusively by §3.6.
  • §3.6.eExpanded: States and Territories may establish recall mechanisms for both Senators and House members.
  • §3.6.f newVacancy filling: States and Territories determine replacement process by their own statute. Legislature sets maximum vacancy period — no seat may remain indefinitely unfilled.
Article IV — Judicial Architecture
  • §4.1.bInferior court vacancy: 180-day constitutional ceiling. PM has 90 days to nominate; if fails, Supreme Court nominates from Qualified Jurist Registry. Deemed Confirmed prevents House inaction veto. Legislature-defined confirmation period cannot push past ceiling.
  • §4.1.cInferior court judge removal: three independent tracks — Rule Violation (§2.7), judicial initiation (2/3 SC petition + legislative supermajority), political removal (mirroring presidential tracks). §3.5 explicitly excluded.
  • §4.2 TriadSenate replaces House as Triad nominator. Senate/PM/President each nominate one candidate from Qualified Jurist Registry. Senate eligibility confirmation round eliminated — JI Registry vetting is the sole eligibility filter. All certified nominees proceed directly to national RCV election.
  • §4.2.b newMid-term vacancy process: President and PM nominate; Senate has 90 days for third nominee. If Senate fails, two-nominee public election. Replacement serves remainder of original term.
  • §4.2.c / §4.2.dJI vetting and public election provisions revised to reflect new Triad and Registry model.
  • §4.2.d Emergency PanelRotation rules simplified: "at least two members of the sitting Panel must have served on the prior cycle" replaces the 18-month minimum and two-retained-justices rules, resolving a mid-cycle retirement edge case.
  • §4.2 political removalSupreme Court justices: political removal mirrors presidential tracks. 2/3 both chambers or Senate referral to popular recall. For-Cause only; JI verification required before Legislature votes.
Article X — Monetary Authority
  • §10.4.aMA Director is now elected by national RCV on National Election Day to a single non-renewable 7-year term — the only administrative officer directly elected by citizens. Five board members nominated by PM from Candidate Register, Senate-confirmed on staggered terms.
  • §10.4 veto/overrideDirector may veto any board determination. Board may override Director's veto by 4-of-5 vote. Both veto and override reasoning published to NRS immediately. Applies to all board determinations including BNF certifications.
  • §10.4.bIndependence provision updated: any Director or board member who exercises veto or override under political direction commits a Rule Violation.
Other Articles
  • §9.3 / §9.4RSA Director: 7-year term defined; removal threshold established; §9.3 Emergency Mode renamed §9.4 to resolve duplicate numbering.
  • §5.4.b / §17.4.gCLT Administrator and National Trust Administrator: confirmed officer removal thresholds defined; mid-term vacancy provisions added; explicit eligibility criteria added for CLT Administrator.
  • §16.5.d newElectoral Commission mid-term vacancy: special election within 90 days for popular seats; joint legislative session within 90 days for legislative seats. Quorum defined; odd-number requirement preserved.
Gap this revision closes: Prior to v119, the document had no political removal mechanism for the President beyond the §2.7 Rule Violation track, no defined process for mid-term Supreme Court vacancies, and a Triad that effectively gave the PM two nominations (House + PM). It also lacked explicit removal thresholds for several constitutional officers and had no vacancy provisions for inferior court seats. v119 addresses all of these simultaneously.

Document at Baseline
