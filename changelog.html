Document Consistency and Clarity Review — Ten Fixes

Full constitutional document review for internal consistency and ease of understanding. Ten issues corrected across three categories: structural errors (misplaced §11.1.c, §4.4 renumbering, duplicate paragraph), inconsistent processes for similar situations (missing eligibility criteria for RSA Director and National Trust Administrator, missing EI review for inferior court judges, MA Director No-Cause removal bar, absolute majority definition scope), and clarity improvements (§9.5.c publication timing, §2.5.a cross-reference).

Structural Errors Corrected
  • §11.1.cReferendum Thresholds and Finality was physically located inside Section 11.2 (Subsidiarity and Local Government). Moved to its correct position immediately after §11.1.b in the Article XI Optional Referendum provisions.
  • §4.4Section 4.4 (Interstate Recognition) jumped from subsection (d) to (f) with no (e). The prior subsection (e) was removed at some point without renumbering. §4.4.f renamed to §4.4.e (Limits — No Obligation Beyond Republic Borders); §4.4.g renamed to §4.4.f (Legislative Implementation Mandates). No cross-references to these specific subsections existed elsewhere.
  • §8.1.bDuplicate paragraph removed. The sentence describing the EI's candidate assessment function appeared twice verbatim in §8.1.b.
Inconsistent Processes — Aligned
  • §9.3 RSA DirectorConstitutional eligibility criteria added. Previously the section said the EI "applies the constitutional eligibility criteria" but those criteria were nowhere defined. Now defined: no current government position; no financial interest in NRS node infrastructure or technical standards suppliers; demonstrated expertise in distributed systems, cryptographic infrastructure, public records management, government IT governance, or records system architecture; no prior Rule Violation conviction.
  • §17.4.g National Trust AdministratorSame problem as RSA Director — EI told to apply criteria that didn't exist. Now defined: no current government position; no financial interest in entities holding, developing, or benefiting commercially from National Trust lands; demonstrated expertise in ecological stewardship, public land management, environmental law, or natural resource governance; no prior Rule Violation conviction.
  • §4.1.b Inferior Court JudgesEI disclosure review added. Every other PM-nominated position in the constitution requires the PM to submit a candidate disclosure record to the EI before confirmation. Inferior court judges were the only exception. PM must now submit disclosure record to EI before House confirmation vote; EI reviews for factual accuracy and publishes. SC-originated nominations (when PM fails the 90-day window) are covered by the JI's existing QJR vetting.
  • §10.4.a MA DirectorExplicit No-Cause removal bar added. EC members and the Judicial IG both have explicit constitutional prohibitions on No-Cause removal. The MA Director and board members had only the For-Cause dereliction standard but no explicit bar on No-Cause removal, leaving a theoretical gap. Now: "No-Cause removal is not available for the MA Director or for confirmed MA board members."
  • §2.3.a Absolute Majority"This definition applies throughout this Article" expanded to "this definition of absolute majority applies throughout this Constitution wherever the term appears." The definition was functionally used document-wide but the scope qualifier limited it to Article II.
Clarity Improvements
  • §9.5.c Classification Registry"Must be registered on the National Record System promptly upon classification" → "within 24 hours of classification." Consistent with all other constitutional NRS publication requirements throughout the document.
  • §2.5.a Emergency Legislative Continuity"The 72-hour presidential review window" corrected to "the presidential legislative review period defined by statute under §2.5." The presidential veto period is defined by statute (up to 20 days) — there is no constitutional 72-hour period. This was a remnant of an earlier draft where the review window was shorter.

v131 May 2026 Minor
§10.4 MA Director — Qualification for an Elected Office

The MA Director is the only elected administrative officer in the constitution and the only elected position with no pre-election qualification filter. Three additions to §10.4.a establish a minimal gatekeeping framework that maximizes voter information while minimizing institutional power over the ballot: four narrow objective disqualifiers, an EI factual disclosure review, and an advisory Board assessment. Board member eligibility criteria are also made constitutionally explicit.

§10.4 — Monetary Authority
  • §10.4.aMA Director Ballot Access — Narrow Disqualifiers: four objective criteria are the sole grounds for removal from the ballot: (1) currently holds any elected or appointed government position at any level; (2) holds a current financial interest materially and directly affected by MA monetary policy decisions; (3) not a Republic citizen; (4) convicted of a Rule Violation during any prior federal service. These are the only disqualifiers — the electorate determines candidate qualification through the information environment, not institutional gatekeeping.
  • §10.4.aMA Director Candidate Register and Disclosure: EI maintains a standing, publicly accessible Candidate Register on the NRS. Candidates submit full disclosure records (professional history, financial interests, prior government positions, prior party activity, Rule Violation history). EI reviews for factual accuracy only — not expertise quality. Accurate disclosure proceeds to ballot regardless of content, including where it reveals limited monetary expertise. Factual inaccuracy or a disqualifying circumstance → removal from ballot with specific grounds published to NRS. SC appeal within 14 days on whether disqualifier criterion is met.
  • §10.4.aMA Director Advisory Assessment: The MA Board publishes an advisory expert assessment of each candidate's disclosed expertise relevant to price stability, fiscal solvency, and GNR certification — the MA's constitutional mandate. Advisory only, no gatekeeping authority, does not affect ballot access. Published to NRS no later than 90 days before National Election Day. Candidates may respond publicly on the NRS within 30 days.
  • §10.4.aMA Board Member Eligibility: Constitutional floor made explicit — at least 5 years of professional experience in monetary policy, macroeconomics, public finance, fiscal policy, or related field; no current government position; no direct financial conflict; no prior Rule Violation conviction. PM may not nominate below this floor. Board Candidate Register publicly accessible on NRS with same disclosure and EI review requirements as Director Register.

v130 May 2026 Minor
§14.6 Tightened — Initiation vs. Achievement, Return Path After Independence

The "at any time" language in §14.6 has been clarified: the right to initiate a status change process is continuous, but the change itself takes effect only when the relevant constitutional process is complete. Status changes are not instantaneous governance decisions — each transition follows the constitutional requirements of its destination framework. A nation that completes independence under §14.6(d) cannot subsequently re-elect Article XIV compact status; having formally exited as a sovereign nation, re-entry follows the general sovereign pathways available to any sovereign (§20 Associated Community or §13.6 territorial incorporation).

Article XIV — §14.6
  • §14.6Opening paragraph revised: "right to initiate a status change process" replaces "may elect their constitutional status." The right to initiate is continuous; the change takes effect when the constitutional process for the relevant transition is complete. The Republic may not use procedural delay to block or discourage an election.
  • §14.6Process requirements section added: each transition is effective when the relevant process is complete. Compact → Associated Community requires Article XX compact and JI certification. Compact → Territory requires the two-step §14.5.d → §13.6 pathway and Legislature acceptance. Compact → Independence requires treaty negotiation and Senate ratification; the 24-month period is the Republic's obligation to negotiate, not a waiting period.
  • §14.6Return path after independence: a nation that has completed independence under §14.6(d) may not re-elect Article XIV compact status. The Article XIV framework is a founding-moment acknowledgment of prior sovereignty within the Republic's jurisdiction. Having formally departed, re-entry follows the general sovereign pathways — Associated Community (§20) or territorial incorporation (§13.6) — not Article XIV.

v129 May 2026 Minor
Indigenous Nation Status Elections · Pathway Clarifications

Indigenous nations may now formally elect their constitutional status relationship from a full menu at any time: Article XIV compact (default), Associated Community, territorial integration, or full sovereign independence. Geographic position within Republic territory does not affect the right to any status. Two clarifying cross-references close the gray area around §13.6 and §14.5.d. Essay Paper No. 9 (in development) frames the underlying philosophical questions about indigenous sovereignty in constitutional design.

Article XIV — Indigenous Nations
  • §14.6 newIndigenous Nation Status Elections: four options available at ratification and at any subsequent time through the nation's own governance process — (a) Article XIV compact (default, no action required), (b) Associated Community status via §14.5.d, (c) territorial integration via the two-step §14.5.d → §13.6 pathway, (d) full sovereign independence with Republic treaty obligation within 24 months covering transit, trade, citizenship, and infrastructure. Geographic position does not affect the right to independence — only the treaty arrangements that follow. Republic may not prefer, discourage, or offer inducements toward any option. Phase 1 notification requirement.
  • §14.5.dClarifying sentence: election of Associated Community status does not foreclose subsequent territorial incorporation under §13.6, and does not foreclose any other subsequent status election available under §14.6.
Article XIII — Territories
  • §13.6Cross-reference added: indigenous nations within Article XIV compacts use the §14.6 status election pathway first; the sovereign entity requirement of §13.6 is satisfied by that prior election.
Essays
  • Paper No. 9The Sovereignty Question — In Development. Frames the core philosophical questions: what does it mean for a constitution to acknowledge a sovereignty it did not create? The nature of prior sovereignty, the paradox of constitutionalizing indigenous governance, the landlocked sovereignty problem, UNDRIP and self-determination, and whether Article XIV's distinctiveness represents a corrective for historical racism or a new form of categorical differentiation.

v128 May 2026 Minor
Presidential Succession Redesigned — Temporary vs. Permanent

The presidential succession framework now distinguishes between temporary incapacity (medical procedure, planned absence, short recovery) and permanent succession (death, removal, resignation). A Senate Speaker covering a medical procedure no longer has to vacate their Senate seat. §2.6.b replaced entirely with an Acting Presidential Authority provision. §2.10.b recall referendum period updated to use acting authority rather than permanent succession.

Article II — Executive Succession
  • §2.6.aClarified that Senate seat vacation applies only to permanent succession events (death, removal, resignation, permanent incapacitation) — not to Acting Presidential Authority under §2.6.b. Deputy Speaker automatically assumes the Speaker role upon permanent succession.
  • §2.6.b — full replacementNew Acting Presidential Authority provision. Two activation tracks: (1) Voluntary — President publishes declaration to NRS; (2) Involuntary — PM and senior SC justice jointly certify incapacity within 6 hours. During acting period: Speaker exercises all presidential powers, Speaker retains Senate seat, Deputy Speaker handles Senate presiding duties, no special election triggered, acting period does not constitute a succession event. Ends automatically when President publishes restoration or PM/SC justice certify incapacity ended. 90-day permanence assessment: if acting extends beyond 90 days, mandatory joint assessment; permanent certification converts to §2.6.a full succession.
  • §2.10.bDuring a Senate-referred recall referendum, the Speaker now assumes Acting Presidential Authority under §2.6.b — not §2.6.a succession. Speaker retains Senate seat during the recall period since the referendum may fail. If referendum fails, acting period ends and President returns. If referendum succeeds, §2.6.a permanent succession activates at that moment and the Speaker's seat vacates.

Constitutional Office Review — Five Structural Gaps Closed

Full section-by-section review of every constitutional office identified five structural gaps where the document's operative text did not fully support its stated design. All five addressed in this version: House term limits moved from non-operative Schedule A to operative text; Caretaker PM fund certification authority clarified for military operations; Electoral Commission joint session vacancy procedure during House dissolution; inferior court judge minimum tenure protection against short statutory terms; Schedule A IOC reference corrected to State Designee Panel.

New Provisions
  • §3.1.f newHouse Term Limits added to operative text. Maximum 6 consecutive 2-year terms (12 years); mandatory 1-term cooling-off; eligible again with no cumulative lifetime restriction. Previously existed only in non-operative Schedule A — constitutionally unenforceable. Senate term limits at §3.2.a were already in operative text; the House now matches.
  • §2.3.cCaretaker PM Military Fund Certification: Caretaker PM retains full fund certification authority for military operations active at the date of PM vacancy. New Tier 2 authorizations during vacancy require additional Senate Armed Services Committee written certification before Caretaker PM may certify. Closes the gap where a new military commitment during a PM vacancy had no clearly authorized certifying authority.
  • §4.1.d newJudicial Tenure Protection: Inferior court judges serve during good behavior once commissioned. Legislature may establish maximum service age or period by statute but the maximum total service period may not be less than 15 years. Fixed terms shorter than 15 years prohibited. Ensures the three-track §4.1.c removal system is the operative accountability mechanism — the appointment cycle cannot substitute for it.
  • §16.5.dEC Vacancy during House Dissolution: Where the House is dissolved when an EC joint-session seat falls vacant, the Senate acts alone by absolute majority. Replacement serves remainder of original term. Prevents the Electoral Commission from going without its full complement during House dissolution periods.
Corrections
  • Schedule AIG For-Cause removal entry corrected: "Oversight Committee (2/3)" → "State Designee Panel verification." The IOC was eliminated in v122; Schedule A retained the old reference.

v126 May 2026 Minor
Federal-State Hierarchy · Organizational Standing · Ripeness and Mootness

Two remaining constitutional gaps closed following the gap analysis. §15.6.b defines the Federal-State statutory hierarchy — federal statutes prevail over conflicting State statutes, concurrent operation preserved where no conflict exists, field preemption by implication requires clear legislative intent. §4.3.b and §4.3.c expand judicial standing to organizational petitioners and codify the ripeness and mootness doctrines, protecting the no-advisory-opinion principle across all proceedings.

Article XV — Constitutional Supremacy
  • §15.6.b newFederal-State Statutory Hierarchy: federal statutes within the Legislature's constitutional competence prevail over conflicting State statutes. Where no conflict exists, both operate concurrently. Courts apply conflict test then obstacle test before finding preemption; field preemption by implication requires clear evidence of legislative intent. Legislature may expressly designate whether a federal enactment occupies a field exclusively, permits concurrent regulation, or sets a floor.
Article IV — Judicial Architecture
  • §4.3.b newOrganizational Standing: an organization may bring constitutional claims on behalf of members where at least one member has individual standing, the claim is germane to the organization's constitutional purpose, and individual participation is not required. Organization must publish identification of an injured member to the NRS concurrent with filing.
  • §4.3.c newRipeness and Mootness: courts may not rule on unripe cases (injury not yet concrete) or moot cases (controversy no longer live). Two exceptions to mootness: recurring conduct capable of evading review, and voluntary cessation without court assurance against resumption. Supreme Court determines standards by published decision; Legislature may not alter them by statute.

Final Constitutional Build — Pending Notes Applied · IOC Eliminated

Application of all pending scenario-testing fixes and institutional design changes accumulated since v121. The Inspectorate Oversight Committee is eliminated and replaced by the State Designee Panel — a rotating body of State-appointed representatives with the single function of verifying For-Cause IG removal grounds. Eight structural and accountability gaps closed. Nine fixes across Articles II, IV, V, VIII, and X.

Article II — Executive Accountability
  • §2.1.h newDual executive conflict resolution during active military operations: President has 6 hours to file written objection; Emergency Panel rules within 24 hours; PM domestic orders remain operative pending resolution; failure to object within 6 hours waives the conflict claim.
  • §2.2.g newMinisterial appointments defined: PM appoints and removes ministers within the Domestic Affairs Council; all appointments published to NRS within 24 hours; ministers exercise only PM-domain authority; unrecorded ministerial orders are void; same civil servant duty of refusal applies.
  • §2.10.aStatement of Grounds required with House removal vote: specifies the basis for removal in plain language, published simultaneously with the vote; serves as Articles of Impeachment for §3.5.c trial mechanics; Senate may not convict on grounds not included in the Statement.
Article IV — Judicial Architecture
  • §4.1.bLegislature-defined confirmation period ceiling constraint added: confirmation period cannot be set at a duration that, combined with elapsed time since vacancy, would push Deemed Confirmation past the 180-day constitutional ceiling. Late nominations automatically shorten the remaining confirmation window.
  • §4.2.eEmergency Panel rotation rules simplified: "at least two members of the sitting Panel must have served on the prior cycle's Panel" replaces the 18-month minimum service requirement and two-member maximum replacement rule. Resolves the mid-cycle retirement edge case left unaddressed by the old language.
Article V — Citizenship
  • §5.4.bCLT Administrator explicit eligibility criteria defined: no government position in prior 5 years; no financial interest in educational testing or credentialing entities; demonstrated expertise in psychometrics, constitutional law, educational policy, or civic literacy research. Constitutional floor; Legislature may add requirements by statute.
Article VIII — Inspectorates
  • §8.4.dSimultaneous IG vacancy provision: Senate under §3.2.g survivor quorum replaces the IOC for temporary IG appointments during catastrophic events. Faster activation; uses a body already constitutionally mobilized during catastrophes.
  • §8.5.aFor-Cause removal verification: State Designee Panel replaces IOC. Panel finding is a prerequisite for the legislative vote regardless of which way it goes — a finding of unsubstantiated grounds does not block the vote, it becomes the permanent public record alongside it. Added: the removal vote itself is not a Rule Violation solely by reason of political motivation where the process is constitutionally complete.
  • §8.9IOC eliminated entirely. Section 8.9 is now the State Designee Panel: composition of pool, Electoral Commission lot draw at each two-year cycle, single mandate (For-Cause verification), term transition rules, active-service-only compensation, and SC appeals mechanism.
Article X — Public Finance
  • §10.4.dMA Director veto on methodology grounds must be accompanied by a Supreme Court methodology challenge filed within 48 hours, or the veto lapses automatically. Board override proceeds regardless of any pending challenge. Director's removal protection runs from the date the challenge is filed, not the date of the veto.

v119 May 2026 Major
Presidential Removal · Triad Revision · Judicial Overhaul · MA Board Model

The most structurally significant revision since the document's founding. Fourteen distinct amendments across Articles II, III, IV, V, IX, X, XVI, and XVII. Four areas of focus: presidential accountability (two new removal tracks), Supreme Court nomination (Triad redesigned), judicial removal and vacancy (three-track architecture for all courts), and Monetary Authority governance (elected Director plus board with veto/override).

Article II — Presidential Accountability
  • §2.7.e newRule Violation modified consequences for legislators: suspension from voting and committee participation; mandatory §3.6 expulsion referral within 30 days. Chambers retain the expulsion decision but cannot avoid commencing proceedings.
  • §2.10 newPresidential removal — two political tracks independent of §2.7. Path 1: 2/3 House to initiate + 2/3 Senate to convict (Statement of Grounds required). Path 2: Senate simple majority refers to national recall referendum; removal requires majority of eligible voters; one referral per term.
  • §2.2.g newMinisterial appointments defined: PM appoints and removes ministers within the Domestic Affairs Council; all appointments published to NRS; ministers exercise only authority delegated within the PM's domain.
Article III — Legislative Accountability
  • §3.2.h newSenate Speaker vacancy: Deputy Speaker assumes succession role immediately; Senate must elect new Speaker within 72 hours. Protects the presidential succession line.
  • §3.5Scope clarified: §3.5 impeachment applies exclusively to executive and judicial officers. Legislative members governed exclusively by §3.6.
  • §3.6.eExpanded: States and Territories may establish recall mechanisms for both Senators and House members.
  • §3.6.f newVacancy filling: States and Territories determine replacement process by their own statute. Legislature sets maximum vacancy period — no seat may remain indefinitely unfilled.
Article IV — Judicial Architecture
  • §4.1.bInferior court vacancy: 180-day constitutional ceiling. PM has 90 days to nominate; if fails, Supreme Court nominates from Qualified Jurist Registry. Deemed Confirmed prevents House inaction veto. Legislature-defined confirmation period cannot push past ceiling.
  • §4.1.cInferior court judge removal: three independent tracks — Rule Violation (§2.7), judicial initiation (2/3 SC petition + legislative supermajority), political removal (mirroring presidential tracks). §3.5 explicitly excluded.
  • §4.2 TriadSenate replaces House as Triad nominator. Senate/PM/President each nominate one candidate from Qualified Jurist Registry. Senate eligibility confirmation round eliminated — JI Registry vetting is the sole eligibility filter. All certified nominees proceed directly to national RCV election.
  • §4.2.b newMid-term vacancy process: President and PM nominate; Senate has 90 days for third nominee. If Senate fails, two-nominee public election. Replacement serves remainder of original term.
  • §4.2.c / §4.2.dJI vetting and public election provisions revised to reflect new Triad and Registry model.
  • §4.2.d Emergency PanelRotation rules simplified: "at least two members of the sitting Panel must have served on the prior cycle" replaces the 18-month minimum and two-retained-justices rules, resolving a mid-cycle retirement edge case.
  • §4.2 political removalSupreme Court justices: political removal mirrors presidential tracks. 2/3 both chambers or Senate referral to popular recall. For-Cause only; JI verification required before Legislature votes.
Article X — Monetary Authority
  • §10.4.aMA Director is now elected by national RCV on National Election Day to a single non-renewable 7-year term — the only administrative officer directly elected by citizens. Five board members nominated by PM from Candidate Register, Senate-confirmed on staggered terms.
  • §10.4 veto/overrideDirector may veto any board determination. Board may override Director's veto by 4-of-5 vote. Both veto and override reasoning published to NRS immediately. Applies to all board determinations including BNF certifications.
  • §10.4.bIndependence provision updated: any Director or board member who exercises veto or override under political direction commits a Rule Violation.
Other Articles
  • §9.3 / §9.4RSA Director: 7-year term defined; removal threshold established; §9.3 Emergency Mode renamed §9.4 to resolve duplicate numbering.
  • §5.4.b / §17.4.gCLT Administrator and National Trust Administrator: confirmed officer removal thresholds defined; mid-term vacancy provisions added; explicit eligibility criteria added for CLT Administrator.
  • §16.5.d newElectoral Commission mid-term vacancy: special election within 90 days for popular seats; joint legislative session within 90 days for legislative seats. Quorum defined; odd-number requirement preserved.
Gap this revision closes: Prior to v119, the document had no political removal mechanism for the President beyond the §2.7 Rule Violation track, no defined process for mid-term Supreme Court vacancies, and a Triad that effectively gave the PM two nominations (House + PM). It also lacked explicit removal thresholds for several constitutional officers and had no vacancy provisions for inferior court seats. v119 addresses all of these simultaneously.
