The First Nomination

Year 2. The first scheduled SC vacancy. The Civic Consul nominates from the Elections Panel-maintained Judicial Pool. The Senate votes 61-39 — short of the 2/3 required. What happens next.

§4.4 Supreme Court Selection §9.4.a Judicial Pool §9.8 Constitutional Pool Framework §3.5 The Senate

The first scheduled Supreme Court vacancy arose in Year 2, when Justice Hiroshi Tanaka-Osei reached the end of her twelve-year non-renewable term. Under §4.4, the Civic Consul had ninety days from the scheduled vacancy to nominate. In the Elections Panel's published Judicial Pool, 34 citizens were eligible at the time the vacancy arose.

Civic Consul Diallo-Mensah nominated Deputy Chief Justice Amara Mensah-Park on Day 47. The Elections Panel confirmed Pool status the same day. The Elections Panel published the nominee's full record to the NRS — including the Elections Panel's eligibility confirmation and the EM's financial disclosure review — within 72 hours.

The Senate Vote — Day 167

The confirmation hearing occupied twelve days of Senate committee sessions. The Senate voted on Day 120 of the Senate confirmation window — Day 167 from the original vacancy date (Day 47 nomination plus 120 days), exactly at the constitutional ceiling. The final count was 61-39 in favor. Confirmation requires 2/3 of the full seated Senate membership. With 100 senators, that threshold was 67. The vote fell six short.

§4.4 — No Deemed Confirmation. Civic Consul Nominates Again.
The Senate must vote within 120 days of JM publication. No deemed confirmation applies — failure to confirm within that period is a constitutional compliance breach certified by the LM. A declined nomination triggers a new nomination within sixty days.

The LM certified the 61-39 vote as a valid decline — not a compliance breach, because the vote had occurred within the statutory window. The Civic Consul had sixty days to submit a new nomination from the Pool. Note on timing: the most senior Appellate Court judge had already been designated as Temporary Associate Justice within 5 days of Justice Tanaka-Osei's term expiry — §4.4's auto-designation triggers on any vacancy, immediately, not on a failed confirmation vote. The TAJ was already serving at full voting rights throughout the first confirmation process.

Thirty-one days later, Civic Consul Diallo-Mensah nominated Chief Justice Yuki Osei-Kuffour from the same Pool. The nominee had been in the Pool since founding — a twenty-two year career in appellate law, no financial disclosure concerns, strong cross-coalition confirmation signals during informal Senate consultations. The second nomination was confirmed 74-26 on Day 52 of the new window.

The Judicial Pool as Constitutional Architecture — §9.4.a

The Elections Panel's Judicial Pool was publicly accessible throughout both nomination processes. Citizens, senators, and the press could see the full field of 34 certified candidates at the time of the first nomination and 36 at the time of the second. The Civic Consul's choice — and the field she chose not to nominate — was visible in a way that had no equivalent in any prior system.

§9.4.a — The Pool Is Always Public
The Elections Panel maintains the standing Judicial Pool. The Pool is publicly accessible on the NRS at all times.

A senator who voted against the first nominee was asked in committee whether she would have preferred a different nominee from the Pool. She named three. All three were in the public Pool record. The question — and the answer — would have been impossible in any prior appointment system. The Pool does not constrain the Civic Consul's choice. It makes the Civic Consul's choice visible, and visible choices are accountable choices.

What This Scenario Tests

§4.4 tests the interplay between the Civic Consul's nomination discretion, the Senate's 2/3 threshold, the Temporary Associate Justice mechanism, and the Elections Panel's Judicial Pool. The 2/3 threshold is deliberately high — it requires broad Senate consensus and prevents a Civic Consul from installing a preferred nominee on coalition votes alone. The Pool is the constitutional transparency mechanism that makes that requirement meaningful: the Senate confirms or declines against a publicly known field, not against the executive's private assessment of who was available.

The Temporary Associate Justice provision closes the gap that any nomination process creates: the Court sits at full capacity while the vacancy is being filled, ensuring that no single vacancy can disrupt the Court's functioning for months at a time. Where the Senate fails to vote within the 120-day window, the LM certifies a constitutional compliance breach under §4.4. Only after such an LM breach finding does §4.4.a become available: the Civic Consul may — with 2/3 Assembly consent — place the Temporary Associate Justice on the next federal electoral period ballot for public confirmation at 60% of votes cast. This is a critical distinction: a Senate that repeatedly votes no is exercising its constitutional authority, not breaching it. The bypass is not available for rejection; it is only available for failure to vote. The design deliberately leaves the Senate free to say no as many times as it wishes — only non-action triggers the escape valve.
