The First Nomination

Year 2. The first scheduled SC vacancy. The PM nominates from the Elections Panel-maintained Registry. The Senate votes 61-39 — short of the 2/3 required. What happens next.

§4.4 Supreme Court Selection §8.4.e SC Candidate Registry §8.13 Constitutional Pool Framework §3.5 The Senate

The first scheduled Supreme Court vacancy arose in Year 2, when Justice Hiroshi Tanaka-Osei reached the end of her twelve-year non-renewable term. Under §4.4, the PM had ninety days from the scheduled vacancy to nominate. Under the Elections Panel's published Registry, 34 citizens had been JI-certified as eligible at the time the vacancy arose.

PM Diallo-Mensah nominated Deputy Chief Justice Amara Mensah-Park on Day 47. The JI confirmed Registry status the same day. The Elections Panel published the nominee's full record to the NRS — including the JI eligibility certification and the EI's financial disclosure review — within 24 hours.

The Senate Vote — Day 167

The confirmation hearing occupied twelve days of Senate committee sessions. The Senate voted on Day 167 of the ninety-day window's extension period under the statute. The final count was 61-39 in favour. Confirmation requires 2/3 of the full seated Senate membership. With 100 senators, that threshold was 67. The vote fell six short.

§4.4 — No Deemed Confirmation. PM Nominates Again.
The Senate must vote within 120 days of JI publication. No deemed confirmation applies — failure to confirm within that period is a constitutional compliance breach certified by the LI. A declined nomination triggers a new nomination within sixty days.

The LI certified the 61-39 vote as a valid decline — not a compliance breach, because the vote had occurred within the statutory window. The PM had sixty days to submit a new nomination from the Registry. The most senior Appellate Court judge was designated as Temporary Associate Justice under §4.4, serving with full voting rights until the vacancy was filled.

Thirty-one days later, PM Diallo-Mensah nominated Chief Justice Yuki Osei-Kuffour from the same Registry. The nominee had been in the Registry since founding — a twenty-two year career in appellate law, no financial disclosure concerns, strong cross-party confirmation signals during informal Senate consultations. The second nomination was confirmed 74-26 on Day 52 of the new window.

The Registry as Constitutional Architecture — §8.4.e

The Elections Panel's Registry was publicly accessible throughout both nomination processes. Citizens, senators, and the press could see the full field of 34 certified candidates at the time of the first nomination and 36 at the time of the second. The PM's choice — and the field she chose not to nominate — was visible in a way that had no equivalent in any prior system.

§8.4.e — The Registry Is Always Public
The Elections Panel maintains the standing SC Candidate Registry. The Registry is publicly accessible on the NRS at all times.

A senator who voted against the first nominee was asked in committee whether she would have preferred a different nominee from the Registry. She named three. All three were in the public Registry record. The question — and the answer — would have been impossible in any prior appointment system. The Registry does not constrain the PM's choice. It makes the PM's choice visible, and visible choices are accountable choices.

What This Scenario Tests

§4.4 tests the interplay between the PM's nomination discretion, the Senate's 2/3 threshold, the Temporary Associate Justice mechanism, and the Elections Panel's Registry. The 2/3 threshold is deliberately high — it requires cross-party Senate support and prevents a PM from installing a preferred nominee on coalition votes alone. The Registry is the constitutional transparency mechanism that makes that requirement meaningful: the Senate confirms or declines against a publicly known field, not against the executive's private assessment of who was available.

The Temporary Associate Justice provision closes the gap that any nomination process creates: the Court sits at full capacity while the vacancy is being filled, ensuring that no single vacancy can disrupt the Court's functioning for months at a time.
