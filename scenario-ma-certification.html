Fiscal Independence · §10.4 · Monetary Authority Under Pressure

The Certification

The Monetary Authority certifies a GNR figure 8.3% below what the government projected. The PM's budget collapses. The deficit threshold is triggered. The government tries every available mechanism — methodology challenge, statutory amendment, public pressure, for-cause removal inquiry. None of them work. The number is what it is.

§10.4.a Director Election §10.4.b Independence §10.4.c Transparency §10.4.d Methodology Integrity §10.3.a Deficit Threshold §8.5 For-Cause Removal

MA Director Ranjit Mehta is in the fourth year of his seven-year non-renewable term. He was elected with 54.3% in the national RCV election of Year 11, on a platform of methodological rigor and transparent publication of the MA's full calculation workings. He has delivered on both. Every MA decision since his election has been published to the NRS in real time, with full methodology documentation. The EI's annual audit has found no compliance issues. He is not popular with governments that find his numbers inconvenient.

In Year 15, he is very unpopular with this government. The PM's proposed budget — the centrepiece of her coalition's second term — was built on a projected GNR of 847 billion. Director Mehta certifies the annual GNR at 776 billion. The gap is 71 billion, or 8.3%. The certified figure triggers the deficit threshold under §10.3.a. The budget, as drafted, is unconstitutional.

What the Government Tries

Route 1 — Methodology Challenge

§10.4.d requires the MA to use a unified, internationally recognised national income accounting standard applied consistently. The PM's chief economic adviser publishes a 47-page analysis arguing that Director Mehta's methodology underweights forward-looking revenue estimates that other recognised accounting systems include. The analysis is published to the NRS within hours. It is technically substantive. Director Mehta's response, published the following morning, is also technically substantive: it identifies the specific international standard the MA applies, the specific peer review it has undergone, and the specific reason the alternative approach identified in the adviser's paper is not internationally recognised in the relevant sense. Both documents are public. Economists disagree publicly for several weeks.

§10.4.d — The Methodology Integrity Provision
The Legislature cannot require a methodology that produces a predetermined outcome

The PM asks the Legislature to pass a statute specifying which international accounting standard the MA must use — one that incorporates the forward-looking revenue estimates the government prefers. The Legislature's legal counsel publishes an opinion: §10.4.d prohibits the Legislature from requiring the MA to use a methodology that produces a predetermined outcome. Any statute designed to produce a specific GNR figure — rather than to specify a recognised standard on neutral grounds — violates this provision. The Legislature does not pass the statute.

Even if the Legislature had passed such a statute, it would affect the next year's certification, not the current year's. Director Mehta certified this year's GNR under the methodology the MA has consistently applied. Nothing in the constitution permits retroactive recertification.

Route 2 — Removal Inquiry

A faction within the coalition government opens a for-cause removal inquiry into Director Mehta. §8.5 permits For-Cause removal on grounds of documented dereliction of duty, incapacity, or criminal conviction. The inquiry lasts three weeks. It finds none of these grounds. Director Mehta has published every decision, responded to every methodological challenge publicly, followed every applicable procedure, and produced a certification that the EI's independent audit confirms was calculated correctly under the stated methodology. There are no For-Cause grounds. The inquiry closes.

§10.4.b is explicit: the MA "may not be directed, overruled, or pressured by either executive or by the Legislature." The EI publishes a finding noting that the removal inquiry was opened without documented grounds and characterising the opening as pressure on an independent constitutional institution. The finding is published to the NRS. It is not a binding determination — the EI publishes facts — but it is permanent.

Route 3 — Public Campaign

The PM gives a press conference calling Director Mehta's methodology "technically deficient and politically motivated." She does not produce new evidence. She does not identify a specific methodological error. She produces a political characterisation of a technical disagreement. Director Mehta publishes a four-paragraph response to the NRS: the MA's methodology has been applied consistently for seven years; every calculation has been published in full; political characterisations of technical work are not technical arguments; the GNR is 776 billion.

The Budget

Six weeks after certification, the government submits a revised budget built on the certified figure. The revision requires: reducing three major capital infrastructure programmes to planning stages; suspending a planned expansion of the Basic Needs Floor above its constitutional floor; and increasing the projected timeline for retiring the inherited structural deficit. It is not the budget the PM campaigned on. It is a budget that complies with the constitution.

What This Scenario Tests

§10.4's independence guarantee is tested most severely not in the abstract but in the specific moment when the certified figure matters — when a government's political programme is built on a different number and has no constitutional path to the number it wants. The scenario shows what "independence" means in practice: not a theoretical protection, but an actual limit on what the Legislature and executives can do when they disagree with a certified outcome.

The three routes the government tries — methodology challenge, removal inquiry, public pressure — are all the tools available. None of them change the certified figure. The methodology challenge fails because the standard is recognised and consistently applied. The removal inquiry fails because there are no for-cause grounds. The public campaign fails because it is not a technical argument. The MA's transparency requirement (§10.4.c) works against the government here: every decision is published, every methodology is documented, every claim can be checked. A government trying to discredit a certification it dislikes has to fight on the MA's ground, with the MA's full workings publicly available.

The EI's finding about the removal inquiry is the scenario's most significant secondary output. The MA was not actually threatened with removal — the inquiry found nothing. But the finding that opening the inquiry without documented grounds constitutes pressure on an independent constitutional institution is permanently on the NRS. Any future government that opens a groundless removal inquiry against an MA Director will find that precedent waiting for it.
