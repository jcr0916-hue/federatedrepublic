Digital Infrastructure Commission · §16.1.b · Commission Governance

The Director

Year 2. The six DIC administrators elect their first Director from among themselves. The vote is 3-3. The constitution's resolution mechanism and what it reveals about institutional design.

§16.1.b The Director §16.1 Digital Infrastructure Commission §16.1.c Commission Operations and Independence

The Digital Infrastructure Commission's six administrators had been confirmed over a fourteen-month period following the first federal electoral period. Three served on the NRS Panel; three on the Elections Panel. All six had been confirmed to staggered terms — no two terms would expire in the same two-year window. The terms ranged from eight years (the three longest-serving) to four years remaining (the three most recently confirmed).

Under §16.1.b, the Commission elects its Director from among the six administrators by majority vote. The Director serves not more than four years in that role. The commission's first Director election was called for Day 210 following the confirmation of the sixth administrator.

The 3-3 Vote

Two candidates emerged from informal consultations: NRS Panel administrator Yuki Mensah-Larbi, whose background was in distributed systems architecture, and Elections Panel administrator Adaeze Okafor-Park, whose background was in elections administration and constitutional electoral design. The Commission voted. The result was 3-3.

§16.1.b — Director Vacancy Resolution
Where the Director role is vacant, the Commission elects an Acting Director from among its members within 7 days.

The 3-3 vote did not produce a Director. It produced a vacancy — the Director role had not been filled. Under §16.1.b, the Commission had seven days to elect an Acting Director. The Acting Director holds the role until the Commission elects a full Director.

The Commission held a second vote on Day 213 — specifically for the Acting Director position. This vote produced a different result: 4-2 for Mensah-Larbi. The difference was not a change of view on the full Director question but a recognition that operational continuity required someone in the role while deliberations on the full Director position continued. Mensah-Larbi accepted the Acting Director role without prejudice to the full Director election.

The Full Director Election — Day 51 of Acting Status

Over seven weeks, the Commission held three working sessions specifically on the Director question. The substantive debate was about the Commission's institutional posture — whether the Director should come from the NRS side (emphasising records infrastructure continuity) or the Elections side (emphasising democratic process independence). On Day 261, the Commission voted again: 4-2 for Mensah-Larbi as full Director.

Administrator Okafor-Park, who had been one of the two dissenting votes, made a brief statement published to the NRS: "The constitution requires us to elect a Director from among ourselves and gives us no formula beyond majority vote. We reached majority. I have full confidence in Administrator Mensah-Larbi's independence and her commitment to the Commission's mission on both panels."

§16.1.b — Director Cannot Direct Operational Functions
The Director may not direct either panel in operational matters within that panel's independent domain.

Mensah-Larbi came from the NRS Panel. Her Director role gave her the tie-breaking vote for joint Commission decisions and the chair of joint sessions. It gave her no authority over Elections Panel operational functions — the Elections Panel retained sole operational authority over election conduct and result certification, regardless of the Director's origin. The administrative origin of the Director is constitutionally irrelevant to panel independence.

What This Scenario Tests

§16.1.b's Director election mechanism is deliberately sparse: majority vote, four-year maximum, no consecutive terms. The constitution does not provide a tie-breaking mechanism for the Director election itself. What it provides is a vacancy mechanism: if the election produces no majority, the Commission elects an Acting Director within seven days and continues. The Acting Director is fully operational; the full election can proceed without time pressure.

The scenario also tests the independence architecture. A Director who comes from the NRS Panel has no authority to direct the Elections Panel in operational matters. This separation is not dependent on the Director's personal commitment to it — it is structural. The Commission chair function and the panel operational functions are constitutionally distinct regardless of who holds the Director role.
