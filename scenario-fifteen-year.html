The Fifteen-Year Bench

Year 15. The first inferior court judge to reach the constitutional term limit steps down. What non-renewable judicial terms mean for institutional knowledge, judicial independence, and the succession process.

§4.1.c Judicial Tenure Protection §4.1.b Appointment of Inferior Courts §4.1.d Judicial Removal §3.2.d Senate Confirmation

Judge Mira Okonkwo was appointed to the Third District Court in Year 0, one of the forty-seven judges commissioned in the Phase 3 institutional construction period. Her term began on Day 310 of the transition. Fifteen years later, on the morning of Day 310 of Year 15, her constitutional term ended.

She could not be reappointed. §4.1.c is unambiguous on this point: inferior court judges serve a single non-renewable term of 15 years. At the conclusion of the term, the judge may not be reappointed to any inferior court seat. Okonkwo had spent fifteen years on the district bench, developing expertise in constitutional property law, NRS compliance disputes, and the complex intersection of indigenous compact rights and Republic statute that the Third District's geographic jurisdiction produced more than most. None of that experience would follow her to a new appointment — because there was no new appointment to be had.

The Design Intention — §4.1.c

§4.1.c — Non-Renewable Term Design
Term limits for inferior courts align with the SC model. Non-renewable terms serve judicial independence; they sacrifice institutional continuity.

The 15-year non-renewable term was chosen to parallel the SC's 12-year model while recognising that inferior court judges handle volume and career-track judicial work. The additional three years reflect the developmental time the role requires. The non-renewable design was chosen for the same reason it was chosen for the SC: a judge who cannot be reappointed cannot be influenced by reappointment pressure. Independence is structural, not dependent on individual character.

The cost of non-renewable terms is institutional memory. Okonkwo's fifteen years on the Third District bench produced a body of knowledge that the district's litigants, practitioners, and co-judges had come to rely on. The loss of that knowledge on a fixed schedule is not incidental — it is a deliberate constitutional trade. The independence gains from non-renewable terms are judged to outweigh the institutional memory losses. That judgment can be contested. It cannot be reversed without a constitutional amendment.

The Succession — §4.1.b

The PM's obligation under §4.1.b is triggered by the vacancy: nominate within 90 days. The Senate must confirm by supermajority as defined by statute. Third District judicial nominations had historically been uncontroversial — district court judges are less politically salient than appellate judges. But the first scheduled term limit vacancy was different. Every element of the process was new and observed.

The PM nominated Judge-designate Tariq Osei-Bonsu, a practising barrister with 19 years of constitutional property law experience — the same specialisation that Okonkwo had developed. The nomination was deliberate: the PM's advisors had flagged, in their internal briefings, that the Third District's caseload in NRS compliance and property disputes argued for a nominee with adjacent expertise. The registry search, conducted through the JI's publicly accessible qualification database, identified Osei-Bonsu's practice profile specifically.

§4.1.b — The Senate Supermajority
Inferior court confirmation requires a supermajority defined by statute — not less than 55% of full Senate membership.

The statute had set the confirmation threshold at 60% of full seated Senate membership — 26 of 42 senators. Osei-Bonsu's confirmation vote was 29-13. The vote proceeded without the sustained debate that had characterised the SC nomination process. The district court bench, while important, did not carry the same political weight as the SC appointment. The supermajority requirement was met with five votes to spare.

The process took 63 days from vacancy to confirmation — within the 90-day PM nomination window, well within the Senate's statutory confirmation period, and with no Deemed Confirmation triggered. The Third District bench filled without interruption.

The Question Okonkwo Left Behind

In her final week, Okonkwo wrote three opinions that constitutional scholars would later discuss at length — not for their conclusions, which were unremarkable, but for their explicit references to fifteen years of Third District precedent that only she had been present for the entirety of. The opinions read, in part, as documentation: a deliberate effort to put into writing the interpretive context that would otherwise leave the bench with her.

What This Scenario Tests

§4.1.c's non-renewable term design reaches its first empirical test. The scenario confirms the design's independence benefits — Okonkwo served fifteen years free from reappointment pressure, and the record showed it — while documenting the institutional memory cost that comes from losing a judge on a fixed schedule regardless of their performance.

The succession process under §4.1.b runs cleanly: PM nomination within 90 days, Senate confirmation by supermajority, new judge commissioned. The continuity risk at the district court level is different from the SC — district courts handle volume, not constitutional precedent at the highest level, and the institutional knowledge loss is real but recoverable through the jurisdiction's practice community.

Okonkwo's final opinions are a constitutional artefact: a judge using her last days on the bench to do what the term limit design does not provide for — preserving institutional knowledge through documented interpretation rather than through continued presence. Whether future term-limited judges do the same will depend on individual temperament. The constitution does not require it, and does not prevent it.
