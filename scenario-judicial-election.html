Focused Scenario · §4.2 Supreme Court Election

The Judicial Election Scenario

A Supreme Court seat opens mid-term. The Triad nominates three candidates from the Qualified Jurist Pool. The Senate confirms all three eligible. Citizens choose. What does a judicial election actually look like?

§4.2.a Triad Nomination §4.2.b Senate Eligibility §4.2.c Direct Public Election §4.3.b Qualified Jurist Pool §4.2.f Recusal Framework

Justice Amara Chen announces her retirement fourteen months before the end of her 12-year term — the first voluntary early retirement in the Republic's history. The seat has handled three of the Republic's most consequential constitutional rulings. Her departure triggers the Triad nomination process: the House, the Prime Minister, and the President each nominate one candidate from the Qualified Jurist Pool. The Pool currently contains 847 names. Each nominator has six weeks.

The three nominations arrive within days of each other. The EI publishes factual assessments of all three to the NRS within 72 hours of each nomination — professional background, published decisions, academic work, no editorial commentary. Citizens can read all of it before the Senate eligibility vote begins.

Dr. Yaw Asante-Mensah
Nominated by the House

Former Chief Justice of the Northern State Supreme Court. 22 years on the bench. Known for methodical, narrow rulings. Reversed on constitutional grounds three times in eleven years. No published academic work outside judicial opinions. Uncontroversial.

Prof. Leila Nazari
Nominated by the PM

Constitutional law scholar. 14 years on the bench, 9 years academia. Author of the leading treatise on the Republic's dual executive jurisprudence. Has written publicly that the current Emergency Panel jurisdiction is too narrow. Strong views, clearly documented. Polarising.

Justice Kofi Darko-Mensah
Nominated by the President

Currently serving on the Second Circuit Court of Appeal. Former public defender — the first in the Qualified Jurist Pool with that background. No published academic work. Known for proportionality reasoning in Article I cases. Largely unknown outside the legal profession.

Senate Eligibility Vote — Round 1
All three confirmed eligible

The Senate's role is narrow: confirm that each candidate meets the constitutional qualifications for Pool membership, not evaluate their jurisprudential philosophy. All three are Pool members — the eligibility question is largely procedural. The Senate confirms all three eligible by the required supermajority within the statutory period. The Senate Judiciary Committee holds hearings. Three Senators vote against Nazari's eligibility on the grounds that her published views on Emergency Panel jurisdiction constitute pre-commitment to a ruling. The Supreme Court, in an expedited ruling, confirms that published academic views do not constitute disqualifying pre-commitment. The confirmation stands. The election proceeds with all three candidates.

✦

The campaign runs eight weeks. Public financing only — each candidate receives the same campaign allocation from the Electoral Commission. No private funds. The EI's factual assessments are the baseline material for every voter. The candidates hold four nationally broadcast debates. The format is constitutional law questions put by a panel of legal academics — not gotcha moments, but genuine questions about how the candidates would approach hard cases. The debates are, by general consensus, the most substantive political broadcast in the Republic's history to that point.

Prof. Nazari is brilliant in the debates and makes no effort to hide her views. She argues that the Emergency Panel's jurisdiction should extend to the proportionality of emergency measures, not just their domain boundary — a position that would significantly expand judicial oversight of executive emergency powers. Justice Darko-Mensah argues methodically for proportionality as the central constitutional principle and discusses his public defender experience in terms of how constitutional rights function for citizens without resources. Dr. Asante-Mensah argues for judicial restraint and the importance of narrow rulings that don't resolve questions the facts don't require.

The national RCV election is held on the same day as the Republic's municipal elections — a deliberate choice to drive participation. Turnout: 71%.

Citizens who preferred the most restrained candidate, when asked to choose between the polarising scholar and the methodical circuit judge, chose the judge. Justice Darko-Mensah is elected. The Electoral Commission certifies the result. He is sworn in twenty-two days later.

§4.2.f — The Recusal Question That Arises Immediately

Three months into his term, Justice Darko-Mensah is assigned to a case involving the public defenders' office of one of the States — an Article I challenge to mandatory representation caseload limits. His former employer. The recusal framework Parliament enacted under §4.2.f requires recusal where a justice received material campaign support from a party to the case, or has a direct financial interest. The public defenders' office is a State government body, not a campaign donor. There is no financial interest. The framework does not require recusal. The JI publishes a note confirming this. Darko-Mensah does not recuse. He writes the majority opinion, 5-4, finding the caseload limits violate the right to effective representation under §1.17. His opinion does not reference his professional background. It references the constitutional text and three prior rulings. It is subsequently cited fourteen times in the first two years after publication.

What This Scenario Tests

Three things the appointment model cannot test because they don't happen under appointment. First, that the Qualified Jurist Pool separates the competence question from the electoral question. No unqualified candidate appeared on the ballot. The election chose among three demonstrably qualified jurists — and chose the one whose electoral coalition was broader than his first-preference count suggested. RCV produced a different result than plurality would have: Nazari would have won on first preferences alone. The majority chose differently when asked to rank rather than select.

Second, that published views are not disqualifying. Nazari's well-documented position on Emergency Panel jurisdiction — the most controversial element of her candidacy — survived a Senate eligibility challenge and a Supreme Court ruling. The electorate then weighed those views against Darko-Mensah's record and chose him. Democratic accountability produced a result, not constitutional suppression of dissenting judicial opinion.

Third, that the recusal framework works as a contained question separate from the appointment question. Darko-Mensah's background was visible to every voter. His first assignment testing that background was handled by the framework — which found no recusal required — and by the judge himself, who ruled on constitutional text rather than personal history. The NRS records both the JI's recusal note and the opinion. Both are there for any citizen who wants to evaluate whether the elected court is working as designed.
