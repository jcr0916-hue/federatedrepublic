Institutional Process · Two Tests · §4.2 Open-Candidacy SC Selection

The Supreme Court Election

Two tests of the open-candidacy SC selection model introduced in v141. Part A: a mid-term vacancy with a JI certification dispute routed to the Appellate Courts — not the sitting Court. Part B: a regular election seat with eleven candidates and the Judicial Selection Board's advisory assessment under political pressure.

§4.2.a Candidate Registration §4.2.b Judicial Selection Board §4.2.c JI Certification §4.2.d Public Election §4.2.e Mid-Term Vacancies §8.4.e SC Candidate Registry §8.9.d Appellate Challenge
Part A
Mid-Term Vacancy — Certification Dispute

Justice Kwame Asante dies in office in Month 8 of Year 5, seven years into his 12-year term. The vacancy activates the compressed 90-day mid-term election process under §4.2.e. The Electoral Commission sets the schedule: candidate registration closes Day 14, EI review completes by Day 28, Judicial Selection Board assessment published by Day 45, JI certification by Day 55, election by Day 90. The replacement will serve only the five remaining years of Asante's term.

Fourteen candidates register from the SC Candidate Registry within the first two weeks. The EI reviews all fourteen disclosure records for factual accuracy within the 28-day window. Thirteen pass without issue. The fourteenth — Professor Dara Nwosu, a constitutional law scholar with 19 years of legal practice — receives an EI finding that a financial interest she disclosed is potentially disqualifying. The EI finds the disclosure factually accurate but flags that Nwosu holds a 4% equity stake in a legal technology firm whose contracts with the federal court system are active. Under §8.4.e, this could constitute a financial interest that affects SC impartiality.

§4.2.c — Day 34
JI certification refused — Nwosu disputes

The Judicial Inspectorate reviews Nwosu's Registry status and concludes that the active court contracts create a financial interest that disqualifies her under §8.4.e criterion (4). The JI publishes its certification refusal and grounds to the NRS. Nwosu is not placed on the ballot pending appeal.

Nwosu files a challenge with the Appellate Court within 14 days of the JI's decision, as §4.2.c expressly provides. She argues that a 4% passive equity stake — held since before her legal career — in a firm that contracts with the courts does not constitute a financial interest that would "systematically affect impartiality on the Supreme Court." The court contracts are ministerial, not litigated. She has no operational role in the firm.

The Appellate Court Review

This is the provision's first exercise. In the prior Triad model, a nomination dispute would have gone to the sitting Supreme Court — the watched branch ruling on its own membership. Under §4.2.c, it goes to the Appellate Courts. The sitting SC has no role. Three Appellate judges hear the case under emergency jurisdiction given the compressed 90-day timeline.

The court receives arguments within 48 hours. The JI's position: the test is systematic potential for impartiality to be affected, not probability. A passive equity stake in a firm with active federal contracts is exactly the kind of interest the provision was designed to catch, regardless of the stake's size. Nwosu's position: the provision requires the financial interest to affect SC impartiality — a 4% passive stake in a ministerial contractor cannot do so.

Appellate Court — Day 47
Court rules 2-1 for Nwosu — JI's refusal overturned

The majority holds that the §8.4.e criterion requires a financial interest that would "systematically affect impartiality on the Supreme Court" — not any conceivable financial connection to any entity that touches the court system. A 4% passive equity stake in a ministerial technology contractor, with no operational role, no litigation exposure, and no interest in any matter likely to come before the SC, does not meet the systematic-effect threshold. The JI's reading would disqualify candidates for financial connections so attenuated they could not reasonably affect judicial judgment.

The dissenting judge argues the majority's reasoning creates an interpretive gap: if the equity stake were 40%, not 4%, at what point does the threshold trigger? The §8.4.e language should be read broadly to give the JI meaningful gatekeeping authority. The majority notes that the JI has no gatekeeping authority over the ballot — §4.2.b expressly gives that to no one.

Nwosu is certified and placed on the ballot. The JI publishes the Appellate ruling to the NRS. The election proceeds with thirteen certified candidates.

The Election

Day 90: national special election under RCV among thirteen certified candidates. Nwosu wins the RCV majority in the fifth round of counting after eleven candidates are progressively eliminated. She receives 34% of first-preference votes — the largest single share — and builds a majority through transfers from candidates with overlapping judicial philosophy profiles. She takes the Official Oath within 24 hours. She serves the five remaining years of Asante's term, not a new 12-year term.

Part B
Regular Election — Eleven Candidates, Board Under Pressure

Three years later, Justice Miriam Oyeleke's 12-year term expires on National Election Day. Unlike a mid-term vacancy, this is a scheduled seat — the full open-candidacy process runs on the standard timeline. Twenty-two citizens from the SC Candidate Registry register their candidacy. The EI reviews all twenty-two. Nineteen pass; three are removed for material inaccuracies in their disclosure records. Eleven of the nineteen reach the ballot after JI certification confirms continued Registry eligibility.

The Board's Advisory Assessment

The Judicial Selection Board — five inferior court judges selected by lottery — publishes its advisory assessment 60 days before election day. The assessment covers all eleven candidates. For each, the Board evaluates judicial record or legal practice depth, demonstrated constitutional reasoning, and professional independence relative to the SC's mandate.

The Board's assessment generates controversy. Three candidates with strong public profiles and name recognition receive assessments describing their judicial reasoning as "outcome-oriented" and their independence as "not fully demonstrated." One candidate, a former appellate judge, receives an assessment noting a pattern of procedural inconsistency in her rulings that the Board finds relevant to SC function. Two candidates receive assessments the Board characterizes as "strongest demonstrated constitutional reasoning in the field."

Constitutional Question
Can the Board's assessment determine the outcome?

The three candidates who received critical assessments publicly challenge the Board's authority. They argue the Board's characterizations constitute de facto gatekeeping in violation of §4.2.b's explicit prohibition on endorsement. If voters follow the Board's assessments, candidates with poor assessments are effectively excluded from realistic contention — the same outcome as removal from the ballot.

§4.2.b is clear: the Board "has no authority to remove candidates from the ballot and makes no endorsement." The candidates are on the ballot. The Board's assessment is advisory, published, and publicly accessible — but it is information, not authority. The constitutional design accepts that information affects electoral outcomes. A voter who reads the Board's assessment and concludes a candidate's reasoning is "outcome-oriented" and votes accordingly is exercising exactly the judgment the election is designed to elicit. An assessment is not an endorsement. Removing the Board's ability to make substantive comparative observations would make the assessment meaningless — the provision would give voters an expert voice that wasn't allowed to say anything useful.

The challenges are rejected. The Board's assessment stands. All eleven candidates remain on the ballot. The electoral outcome is for the voters to determine on the merits.

Election Day

National Election Day: the SC seat is one of seven items on the federal ballot. Voter engagement with the SC race is higher than any prior election — the Board's published assessment has made eleven candidates legible to voters who would otherwise have had no framework for evaluating a large field of jurists. The two candidates the Board described as demonstrating "strongest constitutional reasoning" finish first and second in first-preference votes. The winner — the former appellate judge who received the procedural inconsistency note — wins the RCV majority in the seventh round. The Board's assessment noted her strength in constitutional interpretation alongside the procedural concern. Voters weighed both.

What These Scenarios Test

Part A tests the provision that most visibly separates the new model from the Triad: vetting disputes go to Appellate Courts, not the sitting Supreme Court. The self-selection dynamic is closed. The Appellate Court's ruling — that the §8.4.e financial interest threshold requires systematic potential effect, not attenuated connection — is the first judicial interpretation of the Registry criteria. It sets a standard: passive, de minimis financial connections do not disqualify. The JI's gatekeeping authority is real but bounded. Nwosu wins the election. The process worked.

Part B tests the Board's advisory function under exactly the pressure it was designed to handle. The Board's assessment contains substantive, comparative observations that affect electoral reality without being legally determinative. The candidates who received critical assessments are correct that the assessment influences the outcome — but that is what the provision intended. A board that could only say every candidate is equally qualified would provide no useful information. The constitutional design accepts that expert advisory assessment shapes democratic choice without replacing it. The Board advises. The electorate decides. The sitting Court has no role.
