The Combined Score

On the presidential election — why one person one vote is necessary but not sufficient for a federal executive.

~1,960 words
The simplest demand in democratic reform is also the most intuitive: count the votes. Whoever gets the most votes wins. One person, one vote — equal weight, transparent arithmetic, no distortion. The pure national popular vote is the obvious mechanism and the obvious improvement on every system that departs from it.

This document departs from it. The presidential election uses a combined score that weights national popular support at 65% and geographic breadth at 35%. The case for why requires engaging the intuition honestly rather than explaining it away.

The Democratic Equality Objection

✦

The combined score devalues some votes. A vote cast in a contested region contributes to the 35% regional component in a way that a vote in a non-contested region does not. That is a departure from democratic equality.

The objection identifies something real. The 35% regional component does weight geographic distribution of support as a distinct dimension of presidential legitimacy. That weighting is intentional. The question is whether it is justified.

Begin with what the combined score actually does. The 65% national popular vote component is absolutely equal — every ballot cast in every State and Territory counts identically toward that calculation. There is no distortion, no threshold, no winner-takes-more. A vote in the most remote Territory and a vote in the most densely populated urban centre carry precisely the same weight in that component. On the dimension that measures national democratic will, the system is pure.

The 35% regional component measures something different: the geographic distribution of that support. It asks not just how many citizens support a candidate but whether that support is distributed across the Republic's State structure or concentrated in its most populous corners. A candidate who wins 70% of the national vote by running up 95% margins in four densely populated State clusters while receiving almost nothing elsewhere has a national mandate in the numerical sense. Whether that candidate has the kind of distributed federal mandate appropriate to the presidency of a federation is a different question.

The Republic is a federation of States, not a single unitary polity. The presidency is a federal office — responsible not just for the aggregate of citizens but for the governance of a structure in which States have constitutional standing, distinct interests, and geographic identities. The combined score reflects both dimensions of that federal mandate: the democratic will of the national population and the geographic distribution of that will across the federal structure.

The 65/35 weighting reflects a deliberate judgment about how much the geographic dimension should count. Sixty-five percent means the popular vote component dominates: a candidate who wins the popular vote substantially will almost always win the presidency. The regional component can influence close elections and can distinguish between candidates with similar popular vote shares but different distribution profiles. It cannot override a decisive popular mandate.

The Electoral College Objection

✦

This looks like the Electoral College. The Electoral College was designed to give geographic weight to presidential elections. It has produced presidents who lost the popular vote. The Republic has replaced one geographic distortion with another.

The comparison is superficially plausible. Both systems weight geography in presidential elections. In every other meaningful dimension they are different systems solving different problems.

The Electoral College allocates electoral votes by State on a winner-takes-all basis. A candidate who wins a State by one vote receives all of that State's electoral votes. A candidate who loses a State by one vote receives none of them. This produces enormous distortions: a candidate who runs up 95% margins in losing States wastes those votes entirely, while a candidate who wins closely contested States efficiently accumulates electoral votes far beyond their popular vote share. Small States are explicitly over-weighted by design — they receive a minimum of three electoral votes regardless of population. The system is not designed to measure geographic breadth of support. It is designed to measure State-by-State electoral outcomes, with structural biases built in from the original political compromises of 1787.

The combined score uses proportional regional fractions, not winner-takes-all State allocation. A candidate who wins a region by one vote and a candidate who wins a region by a million votes each receive the same regional credit — one region won. The regional component is not an amplifier of margins. It is a binary count of regions carried. More importantly, the regions are not States. They are drawn by the Legislative Inspectorate under neutral criteria with population comparability constraints, without regard to State boundaries, using the same redistricting standards that govern House districts. No region is systematically over-weighted. No region is under-populated by design.

A worked example shows the difference clearly. Suppose four candidates run in a Republic with five presidential election regions. Candidate A wins 48% of the national first-preference vote and carries four of five regions. No candidate achieves an outright RCV majority. The combined score for Candidate A: (0.65 × 0.48) + (0.35 × 4/5) = 0.312 + 0.28 = 0.592. Candidate B wins 42% of the national vote and carries the one remaining region: (0.65 × 0.42) + (0.35 × 1/5) = 0.273 + 0.07 = 0.343. Candidate A wins decisively. Now change the scenario: Candidate A wins 46% of the national vote concentrated in two urban regions, carrying those two regions convincingly but registering almost nothing elsewhere. Candidate B wins 44% distributed across four regions. Candidate A: (0.65 × 0.46) + (0.35 × 2/5) = 0.299 + 0.14 = 0.439. Candidate B: (0.65 × 0.44) + (0.35 × 4/5) = 0.286 + 0.28 = 0.566. Candidate B wins. The system has done exactly what it was designed to do: identified that the candidate with slightly lower national support has broader federal legitimacy, and weighted that legitimacy in the result. The difference between the candidates' national vote shares is two percentage points. The difference in their geographic distribution is meaningful. The combined score reflects both.

The Manipulation Objection

✦

Parliament defines the regions. Whoever controls Parliament can draw regions to advantage their candidate — a geographic version of the gerrymandering problem.

The manipulation objection identifies the right vulnerability and misidentifies where the protection sits.

Parliament sets the number of regions — at least three, no more than one per ten States, fixed for the duration of a drawing cycle. Parliament does not draw the lines. The Legislative Inspectorate draws the lines, under the same neutral criteria hierarchy and prohibited inputs that govern House redistricting. The LI may not approve a regional map where statistical evidence shows it could not have been drawn by a neutral actor applying the criteria correctly. That standard is the same one that governs House districts. The separation between setting the number and drawing the lines is the protection: Parliament can choose to have five regions or seven, but it cannot choose which geographic areas those regions contain.

The number itself is constrained. Parliament cannot increase or decrease the regional count outside of a drawing cycle — any change in count that does not coincide with a regional redrawing is void. A Parliament that wants to manipulate the regional component cannot redraw mid-cycle to help a candidate in the next election. It must wait for the next drawing cycle, at which point the new lines are drawn by the LI under neutral criteria.

The regional drawing also has a different political incentive structure than House redistricting. House gerrymandering is attractive because it directly translates district lines into seat outcomes for the party drawing them. Regional boundaries for presidential elections affect the geographic distribution calculation for a national race — they don't reliably advantage a specific party the way district-level manipulation does, because the candidate who benefits from any given regional configuration is not known in advance. The incentive to manipulate is weaker because the benefit is less predictable.

What the Score Is For

✦

The combined score is not designed to override popular will. It is designed to add a dimension to it.

The democratic tradition has long recognised that national popular vote pluralities, while necessary, are not always sufficient for the legitimacy of a federal executive. France's two-round presidential system requires the eventual winner to build a broader coalition than their initial plurality — it explicitly values demonstrated breadth of support over raw first-choice concentration. Australia's preferential voting requires candidates to achieve majority support through the full elimination process rather than winning on plurality. Neither system is satisfied by a candidate who leads the first count. Both ask whether the candidate's support is broad enough to govern a complex, diverse polity.

The combined score asks the same question with a geographic dimension appropriate to a federation. It does not ask every candidate to win everywhere — a candidate with 65% of national first-preference votes wins the presidency regardless of regional distribution. It asks that candidates who win the presidency by narrow national margins have demonstrated that their support is not entirely concentrated in the Republic's most populous areas. That is a reasonable condition for a federal office. It is what the 35% regional component provides.

The system has a final safeguard that no geographic weighting should be without. Where two candidates finish within 0.5% of each other on the combined score, a national ranked-choice runoff between those two candidates settles the result. No president takes office on a margin of 0.3 combined score points. The runoff ensures that the closest results are resolved by the broadest possible democratic mandate — a head-to-head national RCV contest that requires the eventual winner to achieve actual majority support. The combined score narrows the field. The runoff settles the result.

A presidency that reflects both the numerical will of the national population and the geographic distribution of that will across the federal structure is more legitimate than one that reflects only the first. The combined score provides both. The weighting ensures the popular mandate dominates. The regional component ensures the federation is not incidental to the outcome. The runoff ensures the closest results are decided cleanly.
