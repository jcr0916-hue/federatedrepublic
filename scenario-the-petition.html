Voluntary Devolution · §15.4 · State Referendum · Senate Ratification

The Petition

Year 7. A State that has been a net federal redistribution contributor initiates voluntary devolution — conversion from State to Territory status. The constitutional requirements, the negotiation, and what devolution actually means.

§15.4 Voluntary Devolution and Merger§15.5 Re-Qualification Pathway§3.5 The Senate§12.1 The Social State and Monetary Authority

Cassren had been a net contributor to federal redistribution for eleven consecutive years. Its legislature had calculated that its residents paid substantially more in federal taxes than the value of federal benefits and social state services they received. The majority coalition ran on a platform of fiscal autonomy. After winning, they moved to initiate voluntary devolution.

Voluntary devolution under §15.4 is not secession. It is a constitutional restructuring: Cassren would convert from State to Territory status, giving up its two Senate seats. As a Territory, its fiscal relationship with the Republic — including federal tax obligations — would be governed by the Legislature and could be addressed in the restructuring agreement under §15.4. The pathway is constitutional, not political — meaning it cannot be blocked by federal officials who oppose it, provided the mechanism is followed correctly.

§15.4 — The Requirements
Three steps, in sequence: a State referendum on the principle of devolving, negotiation of a restructuring agreement, and Senate ratification of that agreement.

The first step is the principle referendum. Cassren's legislature placed the question — should the State pursue conversion to Territory status — before the State's eligible voters. The ballot did not yet specify restructuring terms; under §15.4, negotiation only happens once the principle vote succeeds, so the State and the federal government aren't spending months negotiating fiscal arrangements and transition terms that voters might reject outright. The referendum achieved 64% approval on 71% turnout. The threshold was met.

Only then did negotiation begin. This produced the substantive document: how existing federal contracts are handled; what happens to the federal use rights on land within Cassren's territory; the transition timeline; continuing obligations on both sides; and the fiscal arrangements — including federal tax obligations — that would govern Cassren's relationship with the Republic going forward. Cassren and the federal government negotiated for eighteen months. The agreement was published to the NRS. The final step was Senate ratification, requiring 2/3 of full seated membership — a high threshold that ensured the restructuring had genuine federal consensus, applied now to the actual negotiated terms rather than to a principle the voters had already decided.

§15.5 — What Comes Next
Cassren became a Territory on the effective date. Re-qualification began immediately.

On the effective date specified in the ratified agreement, Cassren's two Senate seats were vacated. Its sitting senators completed their current elected terms — the seats were not filled again until Statehood was restored. Assembly seats were retained proportionally. The restructuring agreement specified the fiscal arrangements with the Republic, including federal tax obligations, which the Legislature approved as part of the ratified agreement. Social state protections continued through the transition without interruption.

§15.5 is automatic: a Territory resulting from voluntary devolution enters the Statehood re-qualification pathway immediately — no waiting period, no penalty, no political vote required to begin. If Cassren's legislature in ten years determined that the fiscal calculus had changed, it could begin the Statehood Audit immediately. The devolution was reversible. The mechanism was designed that way.

The federal government could not have blocked Cassren's petition once the referendum and Senate ratification thresholds were met. No executive veto applied. The constitution provides a path for a State that wants a different relationship with the Republic. Using it is the State's constitutional right.
