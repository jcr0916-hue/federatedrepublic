The Classification

Year 9. An internal audit of the coastal radar procurement finds a 40 percent cost overrun and an undisclosed contractor relationship. The Legat Consul's office classifies the audit, citing the operational-security ground. A staff member publishes a minority report. The EM investigates. §10.2 does not say improper classification is punished. It says it is void.

§10.2 Classification Criteria §10.1 National Record System §9.5 Monitor Operations §9.1.b The Executive Monitor §2.10 Executive Transparency

The constitution's information architecture rests on a presumption most governments run in reverse: publication is the default, and secrecy is the exception that must justify itself. §10.1 makes every constitutional act a permanent NRS record. §10.2 permits classification only on grounds the Legislature has defined by statute, requires the classifying authority to state the statutory ground at the moment of classification, caps everything at 25 years with one 5-year extension, and prohibits three motives outright: concealing a constitutional violation, concealing a policy disagreement, and concealing political embarrassment. Classification for a prohibited purpose is not an offense to be prosecuted after the fact. It is void — a legal act that never occurred.

Year 9, Month 2. The coastal radar modernization program — a Legat Consul domain procurement, four years running, replacing the maritime early-warning network along the Halvern and Neldrath coasts — completed its scheduled internal audit. The audit found what internal audits sometimes find: a 40 percent cost overrun against the authorized baseline, attributable partly to specification changes and partly to a prime contractor that had subcontracted critical components to a firm in which the program's former deputy director had acquired an interest eleven months after leaving the program. Nothing in the audit touched radar coverage, capability gaps, system vulnerabilities, or deployment locations. It was a document about money and conduct.

The Legat Consul's office classified it in full. The stated statutory ground, entered on the classification record as §10.2 requires: operational security of active defense infrastructure.

The Minority Report

§9.5 contains a provision that changes the arithmetic of institutional secrecy: any staff member may publish a minority report directly to the NRS if they believe a finding has been improperly omitted or suppressed, and cannot be disciplined for doing so. Nineteen days after the classification, an analyst in the program's audit division did exactly that. Her report was two pages. It did not reproduce the classified audit. It stated that an audit existed, that it contained no operational content, that its findings concerned cost and contractor conduct, and that in her assessment the classification's stated ground did not describe the document.

National Record System — Staff Minority Report — Year 9, Month 3
MINORITY REPORT — Filed under §9.5, Monitor Operations

The undersigned served on the audit team for the Coastal Radar Modernization Program internal audit, completed Year 9, Month 1, and classified Year 9, Month 2, on the stated ground of operational security.

The audit contains no information concerning system capabilities, coverage, vulnerabilities, or deployment. Its findings concern (1) program cost performance against the authorized baseline and (2) a contractor relationship disclosed in Annex C. In the undersigned's assessment, the stated classification ground does not describe the classified material.

This report is published as of right and is permanently exempt from classification.

— Program Audit Division

The minority report was itself unclassifiable — §9.5 exempts Monitor-channel reports from classification permanently. The Legat Consul's office could not suppress the two pages saying a document was hidden, which is the position the architecture is designed to produce: secrecy about content may sometimes be legitimate; secrecy about the existence of secrecy is structurally unavailable.

The EM Assessment

The Executive Monitor audits both executives under §9.1.b, with full access rights the executive cannot refuse. The EM obtained the classified audit — classification does not run against Monitors — and assessed the classification against its stated ground. Its finding, published to the NRS in Month 5, was written with the flatness the Monitors cultivate: the audit contained no material falling within the operational-security statute's definition; the classification's practical effect was to withhold from public view a cost overrun and a contractor-conduct finding; withholding findings of that character serves the interest §10.2 names as political embarrassment.

§10.2 — The Void Rule
Classification to conceal a constitutional violation, policy disagreement, or political embarrassment is prohibited and void.

Void is the operative word, and it does the work a sanction cannot. If improper classification were merely punishable, the record would stay sealed while the punishment process ran — and a government could buy years of secrecy at the price of eventual censure. Void means the classification never took legal effect. The audit was, as a constitutional matter, an unclassified record from the moment of its completion, subject to §10.1's ordinary publication rule the entire time.

On the EM's finding, the NRS Panel published the audit. No declassification order was needed, because there was nothing to declassify.

What Followed

The published audit produced what the classification had tried to prevent: coverage, an Assembly committee inquiry into the procurement, a referral of the contractor-conduct finding to the Federal Prosecution Service, and a §12.4 disclosure review of the former deputy director. It also produced something the Legat Consul's office had perhaps not weighed. The classification record itself — the stated ground, the date, the authorizing signature — was a permanent NRS document under §2.10, sitting beside the EM's finding that the ground was false. The overrun was a program failure. The classification was a constitutional one, and unlike the overrun, it had a signature on it.

No removal proceeding followed; the responsible domain officer resigned before the committee inquiry concluded. The radar program continued under a restructured contract. The audit division analyst was, as §9.5 guarantees, not disciplined. Her two-page report remains on the NRS beside the audit it surfaced.

What This Scenario Tests

§10.2 is the constitution's Sunlight Test written as law: no permanent secrecy, temporary secrecy only on stated statutory grounds, hard ceilings of 25 and 30 years, and automatic publication on expiry requiring no further government act. The three prohibited motives target the three ways classification actually gets abused — and the void rule means abuse fails retroactively, not just prospectively. A government cannot purchase time with an improper classification, because an improper classification purchases nothing.

The mechanism that surfaces abuse is layered by design. The classifying authority must state its ground in writing at the moment of classification, creating the record against which the claim is later tested. Monitors hold access that classification cannot block. Staff hold an unclassifiable publication channel that no supervisor can close. Each layer assumes the others might fail; together they make the concealment of concealment the hardest act in the Republic's information system.

Note what the scenario does not require: bad faith at the top. The classification may have been reflexive rather than corrupt — a domain office treating everything inside a defense program as presumptively secret. §10.2 is indifferent to the distinction. The test is the stated ground against the actual content, assessed by an institution the classifier does not control, on a record the classifier cannot edit.
