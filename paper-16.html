Paper No. 16

Independent From Whom

On the Monetary Authority. Central bank independence from short-term political pressure has been demonstrated to produce better monetary outcomes. The Republic accepts that conclusion and builds on it — by asking a harder question: independent from whom, exactly, and why?

~2,600 words

The case for central bank independence does not need to be made here. It has been made, and the evidence is extensive enough that this document takes it as given: monetary authorities that operate at arm’s length from short-term political cycles produce more stable prices and more credible commitments than those that do not. The question this document asks is different. Independent from whom?

The conventional model of central bank independence is independence from the executive. The central bank’s leadership is appointed by political actors but protected from interference after appointment. Fixed terms, removal only for cause, operational independence within a statutory mandate — these are the structural guarantees that convert political appointment into institutional independence. The model works because it insulates monetary decisions from the executive’s short-term electoral incentives: the central banker does not need to be re-elected and does not depend on the executive for continued tenure.

But independence from the executive leaves two problems that this model does not address. The first is selection: the same executive whose short-term incentives the model is designed to neutralize chooses who runs the institution. A central bank whose leadership is selected by the executive is independent of executive interference in day-to-day operations, but its leadership reflects the executive’s preferences at the moment of appointment. Over multiple appointments, a determined executive shapes the institution. The independence is real within each term; across terms, it is contingent on the political continuity of whoever is appointing.

The second problem is democratic legitimacy. A central bank that controls interest rates, certifies fiscal positions, and shapes the conditions under which every citizen borrows, saves, and plans operates with enormous consequence and no direct democratic accountability. Its leadership is accountable to whoever appointed them — the executive — and to the institution’s own professional reputation. Neither of these is accountability to the citizens who live under the monetary policy.

The Monetary Authority’s structure is designed to address both problems simultaneously. The result is a hybrid that does not look like the conventional model — and the design choices require explanation.

The Elected Director

⁕

The MA is governed by an elected Director and a board of five appointed members. The Director is elected by national ranked-choice vote on National Election Day to a single non-renewable eight-year term, staggered from the appointment cycle of board members. No political body nominates the Director. Any citizen who meets the eligibility requirements may stand. The election is administered by the Electoral Commission and subject to the same disclosure requirements as any other federal election.

The immediate objection is obvious: doesn’t an elected Director face exactly the electoral pressure that central bank independence was designed to prevent? An official who must win votes to reach office will say what voters want to hear about monetary policy. An official who cannot be removed during their term but still depends on public standing for future opportunities will manage the optics of rate decisions. The electoral mechanism imports exactly the short-term incentive problem that conventional independence was built to exclude.

This objection is real and deserves a direct answer. The Republic’s response is threefold.

First, the eight-year single non-renewable term changes the incentive structure of an elected official compared to a re-electable one. A Director who cannot serve again has no electoral cycle to accommodate after the first year of their term. They ran on a monetary philosophy; they govern on it; they leave. The first-year accountability to campaign commitments is real. The subsequent six years are insulated from re-election in the same way that any non-renewable-term official is insulated from it. The term length is longer than most electoral cycles specifically so that most of the Director’s tenure falls outside any electoral horizon.

Second, the primary mandate is constitutionally fixed and cannot be altered by ordinary legislation. Whatever a Director promises during an election campaign, they govern under a mandate hierarchy the constitution specifies and the Legislature cannot change. Price stability and fiscal solvency come first. Employment conditions and sustainable growth come second, and only where consistent with the primary mandate. A Director cannot deliver loose monetary policy as a political reward because their constitutional mandate prohibits prioritizing secondary objectives over primary ones. The campaign may shape the Director’s approach within the mandate; it cannot override the mandate itself.

Third — and most importantly — an elected Director is independent from the executive in a fundamentally different way than an appointed one. Their democratic mandate comes from citizens, not from the executive who might want accommodating monetary policy before an election. The structural relationship that conventional appointment creates — central banker owes their position to the executive — does not exist. An elected Director with a popular mandate is actually better positioned to resist executive pressure than an appointed one whose tenure could theoretically be threatened by a determined government willing to accept the reputational cost of interference.

The Appointed Board

⁕

The five appointed board members provide what the elected Director alone cannot: technical expertise, institutional continuity, and structural drag against any remaining electoral-cycle pressure.

Board member appointments are staggered from the Director’s election cycle. No single appointing government can replace the entire board simultaneously. Expertise requirements are real — board members are constitutional officers whose qualifications the JI certifies, not patronage appointments available to any executive who wants compliant monetary policy. Fixed terms and for-cause removal only mean that board members who develop expertise and institutional memory retain both for the duration of their terms regardless of which government is in office.

The combination of elected Director and appointed board produces a specific governance structure: the Director provides direction and democratic mandate; the board provides expertise and continuity. A Director who runs on a specific monetary philosophy has the democratic standing to pursue it. Five expert board members who have watched monetary policy across multiple administrations provide the institutional knowledge to implement it carefully and the independent standing to push back when implementation conflicts with the mandate hierarchy.

All board votes are published to the NRS within 48 hours of each meeting, including dissents. A Director who is overriding the board’s expert judgment has their decisions recorded alongside the board members’ objections. A board that is systematically blocking a Director’s legitimate mandate has that pattern on the permanent record. The transparency requirement is not just accountability for the outside world — it is a structural incentive for internal deliberation to be genuine rather than procedural.

The Constitutional Mandate

⁕

The MA’s mandate is constitutionally specified and hierarchically ordered. This is the provision that makes every other structural choice workable.

MA Mandate Hierarchy (§10.4.b) — cannot be altered by ordinary legislation
Primary Price stability and fiscal solvency. Takes precedence in all cases of conflict with the secondary mandate.
Secondary Regard for employment conditions and sustainable growth, where consistent with the primary mandate.

The constitutional specification of the mandate hierarchy addresses one of the most persistent vulnerabilities of central bank independence: mandate drift. An institution whose mandate is defined by ordinary legislation can have that mandate revised — quietly, incrementally, by a Legislature that wants the central bank to accommodate different priorities without the public visibility of direct interference. Mandate drift is operationally equivalent to political capture, but it happens through the legitimate legislative process rather than through visible pressure. It is harder to identify and harder to reverse.

Placing the mandate hierarchy in the constitution removes this vulnerability. The Legislature cannot instruct the MA to prioritize employment conditions above price stability by passing a revised mandate statute. Any such instruction would be constitutionally invalid. The MA Director who wanted to accommodate that instruction would be acting outside their constitutional mandate, subject to the same for-cause removal provisions that apply to any constitutional officer.

The secondary mandate — regard for employment conditions and sustainable growth — is real and is not a formality. Where monetary decisions do not conflict with price stability and fiscal solvency, the MA is constitutionally required to give weight to employment and growth effects. The hierarchy does not authorize indifference to economic conditions below the primary mandate threshold. It prevents the secondary mandate from being used to justify compromising the primary one.

Transparency as Structure

⁕

The MA’s transparency requirements go substantially further than those of most central banks. All rate decisions, board votes, dissents, and mandate analyses are published to the NRS within 48 hours. Not the summary; the full record. Board member dissents are published alongside majority determinations. The mandate analysis — the MA’s published account of how a decision relates to its constitutional mandate hierarchy — accompanies every significant decision.

This level of transparency is controversial in monetary policy circles. The standard argument against publishing dissents and deliberations is that it reduces the MA’s ability to signal unified commitment to a policy path. Markets, the argument goes, respond to perceived central bank consensus; a published dissent from a rate decision introduces uncertainty that undermines the credibility the independence model was designed to create.

The Republic’s answer is that this argument proves too much. An accountability mechanism that requires opacity to function is not an accountability mechanism — it is a request for public trust without public visibility. The transparency requirement does not prevent the MA from making credible commitments. It requires that the basis for those commitments be visible to the people who live under them. A majority MA determination published alongside a dissent is still a determination; the dissent is evidence that deliberation occurred, not that the outcome is uncertain.

The NRS publication requirement has a further property that distinguishes it from press conferences and published minutes: the record is permanent and unalterable. An MA that later revises its account of why it made a decision — an increasingly common occurrence in institutions that manage their communication carefully — cannot do so without the original record remaining on the NRS alongside the revision. The institutional memory of monetary decisions belongs to the public, not to the MA’s communications staff.

The methodology integrity provision extends this principle to calculation. The MA may not change its published methodology for calculating key indicators without 90 days’ notice and a published explanation of the change. A covert methodology change — revising how inflation or fiscal position is calculated without disclosure — is explicitly a constitutional breach. The provision addresses a specific vulnerability: the institutions that produce economic data can shape the appearance of economic conditions by changing how they measure them. The 90-day notice and published explanation requirement does not prevent methodology improvements. It prevents methodology manipulation.

Accountability Within the Constitutional System

⁕

The MA is not outside the Republic’s accountability infrastructure. It feeds the NRS. Its certification functions — certifying social state solvency, certifying severe revenue contractions, reviewing deficit justifications — are audited annually by the Executive Inspectorate. The EI reviews the MA’s certification work for accuracy, not for policy adequacy — the same distinction that applies to every Inspectorate function.

This matters because it answers a question the conventional independence model leaves open: if the central bank is independent of political interference, what prevents institutional capture in the other direction — capture by professional consensus, by the financial sector, by the narrow community of monetary economists whose views the institution naturally reflects? The answer in the conventional model is mostly reputational. The answer in the Republic’s model is structural: the EI audits the certification functions, the NRS records all decisions and dissents, the Director’s election exposes their monetary philosophy to public scrutiny before they take office, and the constitutional mandate hierarchy cannot be revised to accommodate prevailing institutional preferences.

No institution is immune to capture. The question is whether the capture mechanisms are visible and correctable. An MA whose decisions, dissents, mandate analyses, and methodology are all on the permanent NRS record — and whose Director was chosen in a public election on a platform of explicit monetary commitments — is more visible and more correctable than one whose deliberations are managed, whose mandate can be quietly revised, and whose leadership was selected in a process that citizens had no direct role in.

Central bank independence is proven. The Republic does not contest this. What it questions is whether the conventional model answers the right version of the independence question. Independence from the executive is necessary but not sufficient. The elected Director is independent from the executive’s selection preferences. The constitutional mandate is independent from the Legislature’s ability to revise it. The NRS transparency is independent from the institution’s ability to manage its own account. The combination produces an institution that is independent in more dimensions than the conventional model requires — and accountable in dimensions the conventional model leaves unaddressed.
