Scenario · §2.7 · Dual Executive · Fiscal Notice

The Objection

Year 5. The Universal Care Expansion Act passes both chambers with strong majorities — a fifteen-year social state expansion covering three new medical categories. The Civic Consul doesn't suspensive-veto it. She issues a fiscal notice under §2.7: a written NRS statement that the bill will cause or materially worsen a structural deficit. The Assembly Speaker declines to recall the bill. The law takes effect. The warning is permanent.

§2.7 Civic Consul Legislative Instruments §12.1 The Social State and Monetary Authority §12.2 Fiscal Accountability §9.1.b The Executive Monitor

The Universal Care Expansion Act had been in committee for two years. By the time it reached the floor, it had been revised eleven times, costed three times by the MA, and debated more thoroughly than any legislation in the Republic's five-year history. Its core provision was clear: three medical categories currently covered only by private arrangement — advanced neurological care, long-term rehabilitation, and specialist mental health treatment beyond the basic Article XII minimum — would be added to the social state guarantee, phased in over fifteen years. At full implementation, the annual additional cost was estimated by the MA at 2.3% of GDP.

The Assembly passed it 189-111. The Senate passed it 36-25. Both margins were comfortable. The bill went to the Civic Consul for concurrent executive review.

The Civic Consul — Dara Isen, in her second government, still operating against the structural deficit plan from Year 1 that remained on track but hadn't yet closed — neither signed it nor returned it with a suspensive veto. She used the second instrument §2.7 provides: the fiscal notice.

National Record System — Civic Consul Fiscal Notice — Year 5, Month 7
FISCAL NOTICE — Universal Care Expansion Act
Issued under §2.7 — Civic Consul Legislative Instruments

The Civic Consul issues this fiscal notice: the Universal Care Expansion Act, at full implementation, will cause a structural deficit in excess of the MA threshold as defined under §12.2. The MA's Year 5 independent assessment confirms that the Act's 15-year cost trajectory, absent corresponding revenue increases or expenditure reductions, breaches structural balance parameters at Year 11 and remains in breach through Year 20.

This fiscal notice does not constitute a suspensive veto and does not block or delay enactment. This notice entitles the Assembly Speaker to recall the bill for reconsideration within the remaining review window; it creates no further obligation on the Legislature. This notice creates a permanent public record of the fiscal concern for the purposes of fiscal accountability under §12.2.

The Civic Consul will implement this legislation faithfully if the override proceeds.

— Civic Consul

The Override

A fiscal notice carries no override mechanism — there is nothing to vote against. The Assembly Speaker reviewed the notice, consulted with the budget committee chair, and declined to recall the bill. The bill proceeded to enactment by operation of law on Day 10 of the review window. Three members who had mild fiscal reservations during the original vote asked for the record to note they had reviewed the fiscal notice and were satisfied the Act's revenue trajectory was adequate. The Civic Consul made no public comment.

The Universal Care Expansion Act took effect from the moment the override was certified. The Civic Consul's office published an implementation notice two hours later: the Act was law, implementation would proceed on the statutory timeline, and the Phase One enrollment framework would be ready by Year 6, Month 2.

The Civic Consul did not comment further on the fiscal notice publicly. She had said what she needed to say, in the place where it would stay forever. The NRS timestamp of the objection and the MA's underlying assessment were both permanent records. Anyone who wanted to understand the fiscal history of the Universal Care Act in Year 15 or Year 25 would find the Year 5 warning with its specific projections, and would find the override vote, and would find the compliance statement, and could judge for themselves whether the Assembly had acted prudently.

Year 12: The MA Finding

Seven years later, the MA published its Year 12 annual fiscal certification. The structural balance parameters had been breached. The structural deficit was 1.4% of the required baseline — not catastrophic, but real and growing on the current trajectory.

The MA's certification noted, as it was required to, that a structural deficit above the threshold required a published plan for return to balance. It also noted — it was not required to, but did — that the Year 5 fiscal notice had accurately projected the Year 11 breach, citing the notice's NRS record and comparing its projections to actual outcomes. The MA's language was factual: the fiscal notice had identified this specific risk, at approximately this specific timeline, and it had occurred.

Year 12 — The Legislature's Response
A return-to-balance plan is published within 90 days.

The return-to-balance plan submitted by the Assembly included a graduated revenue increase — a levy on high-value property transactions phased in over six years — and a ten-year review trigger that would adjust the Universal Care expansion timeline if the structural deficit exceeded 2%. The MA certified the plan as credible.

The Civic Consul signed the implementing legislation for the revenue measure without comment. She did not reference the Year 5 fiscal notice in her signing statement. She did not need to. The NRS held the complete sequence: the fiscal notice, the Speaker's decision not to recall, the Act, the Year 12 finding, the MA's notation, and now the corrective legislation.

What This Scenario Tests

The fiscal notice is §2.7's informational instrument — distinct from the suspensive veto (which tables the bill for one month and requires Senate 2/3 to override) and distinct from silence (which enacts the bill without comment). The fiscal notice creates one procedural right: the Assembly Speaker may recall the bill for reconsideration within the remaining review window. If the Speaker declines and the CC does not use the suspensive veto, the bill is enacted by operation of law. The design choice is deliberate: the Civic Consul's fiscal concern does not carry blocking power over the Assembly's spending decisions. The constitution chose information power over veto power.

What the fiscal notice provides is a formal, attributable, permanent public record of the fiscal concern and the MA's supporting analysis. The Speaker's decision not to recall is equally permanent. When the structural deficit materializes — if it does — the public can see who warned, who declined to act on the warning, and what the projections said. The accountability isn't prospective pressure; it's retrospective clarity for anyone who wants to understand how the Republic got where it is.

Enacted by operation of law in §2.7 reflects the same principle from the other direction: the Civic Consul cannot hold legislation hostage by neither signing nor objecting. The review period runs whether or not the Civic Consul acts; if it expires without action, the bill is enacted. The instrument is available if the Civic Consul wants to use it, but the Legislature's timeline doesn't depend on it.

The Universal Care Act is, by Year 12, clearly good policy — participation is high, outcomes data is positive, and the political support for the expansion is stronger than it was at passage. The structural deficit is real but manageable. The fiscal notice was accurate in its fiscal projection and arguably wrong in its implicit policy judgment. The NRS holds both the accuracy and the context. That is, exactly, the design.
