Constitutional Amendment · §15.1.b · Path B — Parliamentary and Popular

The Second Threshold

An amendment campaign builds to 68% legislative support — enough for Path B, not for Path A’s three-quarter supermajority. The coalition pivots. Both chambers pass the amendment at 2/3. The People must now ratify it within 180 days. 55% affirmative. 55% participation. Neither stage alone is sufficient.

§15.1.a Path A — Parliamentary §15.1.b Path B — Parliamentary and Popular §15.2 No Executive Veto §16.1 Elections Panel of the Digital Infrastructure Commission §8.1.a LI Certification

The Housing Access Amendment has been in motion for three years. Its core is a direct constitutional change to §1.22: adding an explicit floor on municipal zoning authority that would prohibit any zoning classification whose effect is to exclude multi-family housing from areas with existing transport infrastructure. The coalition behind it argues the existing §1.22.a language is too easily circumvented by procedural local ordinances. Critics argue it is federal overreach into State land-use decisions.

The Path A Ceiling

The campaign’s first strategy was Path A: a three-quarter supermajority of both chambers in a single concurrent vote. No popular referendum required. Clean, fast, and durable. After two years of organizing, the whip count stabilizes at 68% in the House and 71% in the Senate. Both figures comfortably clear the 2/3 threshold. Neither clears three-quarters.

The coalition faces a constitutional arithmetic problem. The senators and representatives they cannot move are not opposed to the amendment’s substance — most have said publicly they support housing access. They oppose the specific constitutional language, arguing it is drafted too broadly. The campaign has offered three rounds of textual concessions. Each round peels off two or three votes and costs two or three others. The coalition cannot get to three-quarters without losing its floor.

§15.1.a — Path A Ceiling · Year 3
Coalition declares Path B pivot

At a press conference attended by the House and Senate coalition leaders, the campaign announces it will pursue Path B: a 2/3 legislative vote followed by a national popular ratification. The pivot is constitutionally straightforward — §15.1.b requires the same legislative text that failed Path A only by not reaching three-quarters. The coalition already has the votes. What changes is that passage is no longer sufficient. The People must also say yes.

§15.2 is the provision that makes this pivot possible without executive interference: neither the President nor the PM has any role in the constitutional amendment process. No signature required. No veto available. The amendment proceeds entirely between the Legislature and the People.

The Legislative Vote

The joint concurrent vote is scheduled for a Thursday in Year 3. The text is unchanged from the version that cleared 68% — the coalition made a deliberate choice not to amend it further, calculating that any new textual changes would require rebuilding support they already hold. The House votes 279-181 (68.2%, clearing 2/3 by 12 votes). The Senate votes 72-28 (72%, clearing 2/3 by 6 votes). The LI certifies the text and the vote counts. The Elections Panel is formally notified to schedule the popular ratification vote within 180 days.

There is no presidential signature ceremony. There is no PM review period. The amendment is not yet law — it will not be law until the People ratify it — but the legislative stage is complete and cannot be revisited. §15.1.b is explicit: neither stage alone is sufficient. The legislative vote is necessary but not sufficient. The popular vote is necessary but not sufficient. Both must occur and both must pass.

The 180-Day Campaign

The popular ratification campaign runs under different constitutional dynamics than the legislative campaign did. In the Legislature, the coalition was negotiating with identifiable individuals whose specific objections could be addressed. In a national popular vote, the campaign is addressing an electorate of tens of millions, and the 55% participation floor means that winning the voters who show up is not enough — the coalition must also make the vote matter enough that reluctant supporters turn out.

The opposition runs an unusual campaign. They are not campaigning for a “no” vote. They are campaigning for non-participation. Their argument: the amendment passed the Legislature at 68%, not 75%, because genuine constitutional consensus was lacking. A popular vote that passes narrowly on low turnout does not represent a mandate for constitutional change. Stay home. Let it fail on participation.

§15.1.b — Popular Ratification · Day 163 of 180
Turnout projections; EC administers vote on Day 172

In the final week before the popular vote, participation models project 57-61% turnout. The floor is 55%. The margin is real but not comfortable. The coalition conducts a final push focused entirely on turning out low-propensity supporters in high-density urban areas where the amendment’s housing access logic is most tangible.

The vote is administered by the Elections Panel of the Digital Infrastructure Commission on Day 172 of the 180-day window. The Elections Panel is constitutionally prohibited from expressing a view on the amendment’s merits — its role is administration and certification, not advocacy.

The EC and LI certify jointly. The Housing Access Amendment is published to the NRS as a constitutional amendment. §1.22 is updated. The amendment is immediately justiciable — any municipal zoning ordinance whose effect is to exclude multi-family housing from areas with existing transport infrastructure is now facially unconstitutional.

What Path B Demonstrates

The scenario illustrates the constitutional logic of having three amendment paths rather than one or two. Path A’s three-quarter threshold protects against amendments that lack genuine broad institutional consensus — but it can be defeated by a determined minority even when a substantial majority supports the change. Path B exists precisely for this case: a majority real enough to win a popular ratification but not large enough to dominate every legislative bloc.

Path B also has a structural property that Path A lacks: it cannot succeed on institutional momentum alone. The Legislature can clear 2/3 in a low-visibility vote on a quiet Thursday. But 55% participation in a national popular vote requires the amendment to matter to real people outside the Legislature. The participation floor is not just a number — it is a check against amendments that pass because no one was paying attention. The opposition’s non-participation strategy was a genuine constitutional play. It failed here. It will not always fail.
