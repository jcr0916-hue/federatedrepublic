The Graduation

Maret Ven graduates from Halvern University, takes a job in Delvane, and moves across state lines. Six months later there is a federal election. She wants to vote. The National Voter System, residency rules, and §1.2 under ordinary pressure — no crisis, just the system doing what it is supposed to do.

§1.2 Right to Vote §11.2 NVS Properties and Voting Rights §8.1 Elections Panel §7.2 Federal Elections

The Situation

Maret Ven is 23 years old. She grew up in Neldrath, voted in her first federal election from her parents' address at age 19, then enrolled at Halvern University in a different state. She updated her NVS registration to her Halvern address in Year 11 and voted in the Year 11 federal election from there. She graduated in Month 6 of Year 12, accepted a position at a civil engineering firm in Delvane, and moved to an apartment in Delvane's capital district in Month 8.

The next federal election is Month 6 of Year 13 — ten months after her move. She wants to vote in Delvane. She goes to the NVS portal to update her registration.

The NVS Registration

Month 8, Year 12 · Residency update
The National Voter System is the Republic's single federal voter registry. Under §11.2, any eligible citizen may register or update their registration at any time; no registration window applies to federal elections.

Maret logs into the NVS portal and submits a residency update — her Delvane address, her employment documentation, and her lease agreement. The system flags a question: her Halvern registration remains active. The NVS requires her to confirm she intends Delvane as her primary residence and that she understands this cancels her Halvern registration. She confirms. Her Halvern registration is cancelled effective immediately. Her Delvane registration is provisional pending address verification.

Address verification takes eleven days — standard processing for a first registration in a new state. On Day 12, her Delvane registration is confirmed and published in the NVS. She is now a registered voter in Delvane's capital district.

The Question She Didn't Expect

In Month 3 of Year 13 — three months before the election — Maret receives a letter from the Delvane Elections Compliance Office. The letter notes that Delvane has a statutory residency requirement: a citizen must have established residency in the state at least six months before the election to vote in that state's Assembly district allocation. Maret moved in Month 8 of Year 12. The election is Month 6 of Year 13. She is ten months resident. She clears the requirement by four months.

But the letter raises a second point: Delvane's six-month rule is a state rule for state elections. For federal elections — Assembly, Senate, LC, and EM confirmation — the NVS and §11.2 govern. The state may not impose a residency requirement that effectively restricts access to federal elections beyond what §1.2 and §11.2 permit. The letter is informational, not a restriction. She is eligible to vote in federal elections from Day 1 of NVS registration in Delvane.

What §1.2 Actually Provides

The constitutional floor
§1.2 guarantees the right to vote to every eligible citizen; the NVS is the mechanism; no state may restrict access to federal elections beyond what §11.2 permits.

Maret could have voted in federal elections from the day her Delvane NVS registration was confirmed — Month 9 of Year 12. She could not have voted in Halvern's state elections after cancelling her Halvern registration. She could not vote in Delvane's state elections until meeting Delvane's six-month residency requirement, which she cleared in Month 2 of Year 13.

The federal and state electoral systems run in parallel. The NVS governs federal eligibility. State registration rules govern state eligibility. A citizen moving between states may be temporarily eligible for federal elections but not state elections in their new home. This is not a constitutional gap — it is a deliberate separation of federal and state electoral administration.

Election Day

Month 6, Year 13: Maret votes in Delvane's capital district. She votes in four simultaneous federal elections: Assembly member for Delvane Capital District, her Senate seat in Delvane's staggered cycle (if applicable), the LC election, and the EM confirmation vote. She also votes in Delvane's own state elections — she cleared the residency requirement in Month 2.

Her ballot is processed through the NVS. There is no provisional ballot, no challenge, no exception to file. Her registration has been confirmed for ten months. The system processed her move, cancelled her old registration, verified her new address, and enrolled her in the correct district — all without human intervention beyond her own submissions.

What This Scenario Tests

The NVS and §1.2 together create a right that is portable and self-administered. No election official discretion is required for ordinary residency changes. No registration window closes before an election. The right to vote in federal elections attaches to current verified NVS registration, not to residence duration.

The scenario surfaces the federal-state distinction cleanly: state residency requirements are valid for state elections, but they cannot restrict access to federal elections. The NVS is the federal floor. States may require more of their own elections; they may not require more of the Republic's.

What makes this scenario worth examining is precisely that nothing went wrong. The system processed a common life event — graduation, relocation, new job — without friction, exception, or bureaucratic failure. The constitution does not only need to be tested under crisis conditions. Ordinary lives moving through a well-designed system is its own kind of proof.
