The Second Declaration

Year 3. A 60-day regional emergency expires. Seventy-two hours later, a new emergency issues — same geography, same rights restrictions, different label. The EM publishes its relabeling assessment on Day 11. The SC rules on two violations at once.

§1.19.b Emergency Declaration Lifecycle §1.19.a Non-Derogable Rights §1.19 Declaration Requirements §9.1.b Executive Monitor §4.5 Judicial Review

The Civic Consul's first emergency declaration covered the Holta Region, where sustained civil unrest following disputed local elections had disrupted infrastructure and public safety. The declaration was narrowly scoped — geographic limits specified, rights restrictions enumerated (freedom of movement and assembly), duration set at 60 days, NRS publication on Day 1. It ran its course and lapsed automatically on Day 60. No extension was sought. No re-enactment followed through ordinary legislation.

Seventy-two hours later, a second declaration issued. This one was titled "Regional Infrastructure Security Emergency." It covered the same geographic boundaries as the first. It restricted the same rights — freedom of movement, assembly, and access to certain public facilities. The stated basis was different: not the unrest itself, but the degraded infrastructure that the unrest had left behind. The Civic Consul's office published it to the NRS immediately as required. The EM's clock started the same day.

The Relabeling Bar

§1.19.b — EM Assessment Within 14 Days
A new label does not reset the constitutional clock. The EM must assess within 14 days whether a new declaration is a relabeled continuation.

§1.19.b requires the EM to publish an assessment of any new declaration within 14 days of issuance, determining whether it constitutes a relabeled continuation of a prior emergency that expired within 60 days. The standard is geographic and rights-based: substantially the same scope, substantially the same restrictions. The stated rationale is explicitly not the test — the constitution does not allow the relabeling bar to be evaded by changing the description of why the emergency exists.

On Day 11, the EM published its finding: the second declaration covered substantially the same geographic scope and affected substantially the same rights as the first. The constitutional bar applied. The EM's assessment did not void the declaration — it is not within the EM's constitutional authority to void executive acts. The assessment was information, published permanently to the NRS, creating the factual record for challenge.

EM Assessment · Year 3 · Day 11 · Holta Region Second Declaration

The Executive Monitor has assessed the Regional Infrastructure Security Emergency declared on Day 3 post-expiry of the prior Holta Region Civil Security Emergency. Finding: the second declaration covers substantially the same geographic scope as the first (identical regional boundaries). The second declaration restricts substantially the same rights as the first (freedom of movement, assembly, and public facility access). The stated rationale differs; the constitutional test under §1.19.b is geographic scope and rights affected, not stated rationale. The §1.19.b bar against relabeled continuation applies to this declaration. This assessment is published permanently to the NRS. No further executive act is required to give effect to this assessment; it is the factual record from which challenge proceedings may proceed.

The Discrimination Finding

§1.19.a — Emergency Measures Applied Unequally
The SC hears two violations simultaneously. The second was not in the EM's primary finding but emerged from the evidence.

Three civil liberties organizations and a coalition of Holta Region residents petitioned the SC the day the EM published. The petition raised the relabeling bar violation. But the evidentiary submissions included something the EM's assessment had noted only peripherally: enforcement of the movement restrictions had been significantly more intensive in the two predominantly Hareni-heritage districts within the declaration zone than in comparable adjacent districts.

§1.19.a is absolute. No emergency measure may be applied in a discriminatory manner on the basis of race, ethnicity, religion, or national origin. Any such application is constitutionally void from the first instance of unequal application — not from the date of a court ruling, not from the date of formal challenge. The court ruling confirms and remedies what is already void.

The SC's Ruling

The SC ruled on Day 19. Two violations; one ruling.

On the relabeling violation: the second declaration was void as a relabeled continuation of the first under §1.19.b. The 60-day bar had not expired when the second declaration issued. All measures taken under the second declaration were void from issuance. The CC was notified immediately. If a genuine infrastructure emergency required emergency response, the path forward was a new declaration after the 60-day bar expired — and that declaration would require a 2/3 concurrent vote of both chambers certifying continued necessity, given that it would follow two declarations within the prior period.

On the discrimination violation: movement restrictions had been applied unequally in the Hareni-heritage districts under both the first and second declarations. Both applications were separately void. The CC's office was required to publish a remediation plan within 30 days specifying how the enforcement disparity would be addressed, what redress was available to those whose movement had been unlawfully restricted, and what monitoring framework would prevent recurrence.

The scenario tests what the relabeling bar is actually for. It is not a technicality — it is the constitution's answer to the risk that emergency powers, once used, create institutional appetite for continued use. The 60-day cooling period forces genuine reconsideration. The EM's mandatory assessment forces transparency. The SC's jurisdiction over emergency rights challenges during an emergency — explicitly preserved under §4.5 — ensures that the cooling period cannot be evaded by relabeling what is substantively the same emergency. A new label does not create a new emergency.
