The Second Declaration

Year 3. A 60-day regional emergency expires. Seventy-two hours later, a new emergency issues — same geography, same rights restrictions, different label. The EM publishes its assessment within 14 days. The SC is petitioned.

§1.19.e No Permanence§1.19.b Non-Derogable Rights§1.19 Declaration Requirements§9.1.b Executive Monitor

The Civic Consul's first emergency declaration covered the Holta Region, where sustained civil unrest following disputed local elections had disrupted infrastructure and public safety. The declaration was narrowly scoped — geographic limits specified, rights restrictions enumerated, NRS publication on Day 1. It ran its constitutional 60 days and lapsed automatically.

Seventy-two hours later, a second declaration issued. This one was titled "Regional Infrastructure Security Emergency." It covered the same geographic boundaries as the first. It restricted the same rights — freedom of movement, assembly, and access to certain public facilities. The stated basis was different: degraded infrastructure from the unrest, rather than the unrest itself.

§1.19.e — The Relabeling Bar
A new label does not reset the constitutional clock.

§1.19.e requires the EM to publish an assessment of any new declaration within 14 days, determining whether it constitutes a relabeled continuation of a prior emergency that expired within 60 days. On Day 11, the EM published its finding: the second declaration covered substantially the same geographic scope and affected substantially the same rights as the first. The constitutional bar applied, regardless of how the new emergency was labeled.

The finding was information only. The second declaration remained in effect while the EM's assessment stood on the NRS. But the assessment created the factual record for challenge. Three civil liberties organizations and a coalition of residents petitioned the SC the same day the EM published.

§1.19.b — Emergency Non-Discrimination
The emergency measures were not being applied uniformly.

The SC heard the challenge under its expedited emergency rights jurisdiction. In addition to the relabeling question, petitioners presented evidence that movement restrictions had been enforced with significantly greater intensity in the two predominantly Hareni-heritage districts within the declaration zone. The EM's published finding noted this enforcement pattern explicitly.

§1.19.b is absolute: no emergency measure may be applied in a discriminatory manner on the basis of race, ethnicity, religion, or national origin. Any such application is constitutionally void. The discriminatory enforcement was void from the first instance of unequal application — it did not require a court ruling to be void, only a court ruling to be confirmed void and remedied.

The SC ruled on Day 19. The second declaration was void as a relabeled continuation. The discriminatory enforcement of both declarations' measures in the Hareni-heritage districts was separately void and gave rise to remediation obligations. Two constitutional violations; one emergency; one ruling. The Republic's emergency power framework had absorbed a bad-faith declaration attempt and produced a clear constitutional result.
