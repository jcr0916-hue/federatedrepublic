The Appointed Supreme Court

On why the Republic appoints rather than elects its Supreme Court — why the open registry, PM accountability, and 2/3 Senate confirmation produces better judicial independence than a national election campaign, and why this is the right trade-off even knowing its limits.

The question that haunts every constitutional court design is legitimacy. Courts issue binding rulings that override democratic majorities. Where does the authority to do that come from? The conventional answer is expertise and process: judges are trusted because they are qualified, because they reason carefully, and because they apply written law rather than personal preference. The democratic answer is accountability: officials who make consequential decisions about citizens' lives should face some form of electoral accountability for those decisions. The Republic's Supreme Court design tries to thread this needle — not by picking one answer but by finding the mechanism that captures the most of both without fully sacrificing either.

Earlier drafts of this constitution elected Supreme Court justices directly by national ranked-choice vote. The argument for direct election was compelling: democratic legitimacy flowing directly from citizens rather than from the political branches that the Court is meant to constrain. A justice who won a national election owes their mandate to no Senator, no Prime Minister, no President. The independence that non-renewable terms provide after election is genuine.

The argument against proved stronger. A national SC election campaign produces political figures. Voters evaluating judicial candidates must assess qualifications most voters cannot evaluate — cryptographic evidence law, constitutional precedent, federal jurisdiction doctrine. What voters can evaluate is whether the candidate seems to share their values and political sympathies. The result, reliably, is that SC elections select for ideological alignment rather than judicial quality. State supreme court elections in jurisdictions that have tried them demonstrate this consistently: they attract campaign money, produce more ideologically extreme justices, and over time corrode the institutional legitimacy that the election was supposed to provide.

What the Registry Does

The open SC Candidate Registry§8.4.e is the mechanism that makes appointment work in this constitution. Any citizen with fifteen or more years of legal practice, or ten or more years of judicial service, may apply. The JI reviews applications for factual accuracy — not to select candidates, not to rank them, but to verify the credentials and publish the complete record. The registry is public, permanent, and freely accessible on the NRS. Every person qualified to be nominated is on it.

This is not appointment from a short list assembled by the Prime Minister's office. The PM must nominate from the registry, but the registry belongs to no one — it is open entry, publicly verified, and contains every qualifying citizen who has chosen to apply. A PM who nominates a personal ally with weak credentials is nominating someone whose credential gap is published on the NRS before the Senate sees the nomination. The registry makes patronage nominations visible rather than concealing them.

The Judicial Selection Board§4.2.b exists alongside the registry as a quality signal. Five senior judges chosen by lot publish advisory assessments of registered candidates — covering judicial record, legal reasoning, and professional independence. The Board cannot restrict nominations; the PM may nominate any JI-certified candidate regardless of the Board's assessment. But the Board's assessment is published to the NRS and transmitted to every Senator before any confirmation vote. A PM who nominates against an adverse Board assessment has made that choice publicly, and Senate confirmation requires 2/3 of full seated membership.

The 2/3 Threshold

The Senate confirmation threshold is where the Republic's SC appointment model does its most important work. At 2/3 of full seated membership§4.2.d — not a simple majority, not 60%, but 2/3 — the PM cannot pack the Court with ideological allies unless their coalition commands an extraordinary Senate supermajority. This is the equivalent function that elections were supposed to serve: preventing any single political faction from controlling the highest court through concentrated appointment power.

Germany's Federal Constitutional Court has operated on an equivalent model since 1951 — 12-year non-renewable terms, parliamentary confirmation requiring 2/3 of the relevant chamber. It is widely regarded as one of the most legitimate and independent constitutional courts in the world. The 2/3 threshold produces nominees who command cross-party support, which in practice means nominees closer to the qualified center than the partisan edge. Not every nominee will be above politics — no mechanism achieves that. But the structural incentive of the 2/3 requirement points in the right direction.

The Accountability Mechanism

The PM is accountable to the House through the confidence mechanism. A demonstrably bad SC nomination — someone with a thin record, a documented conflict of interest, a JI report flagging credential gaps — becomes a political liability. The House confidence mechanism means that sustained poor judgment in nominations is a problem for the PM's continued tenure. This is different from electoral accountability but not weaker than it: it operates through continuous House confidence rather than periodic ballot, which in some ways is more demanding.

Time also provides a structural check. A justice serves 12 years — non-renewable. Any PM serving the maximum 8 consecutive years will, at most, nominate six of the nine justices (assuming normal turnover). Those six will be confirmed by a Senate requiring 2/3 majorities — which means opposition senators participated in every confirmation. The justices then serve for decades regardless of what happens to the PM who nominated them. The institution's long-term composition is never entirely under one government's control.

What This Model Does Not Solve

It would be dishonest to claim this design eliminates judicial politics. PM nominations are political acts. Confirmation hearings are political events. The NRS record of every nomination and every Senate vote is a permanent political document. There will be controversial nominees. There will be confirmation battles. There will be justices who turn out to hold the views their nominating PM hoped they would hold.

The question is not whether politics enters — it always does — but whether the structural incentives reduce its worst effects. The 2/3 threshold creates an incentive for nominees acceptable to the opposition. The open registry prevents purely political nominations from being invisible. The 12-year non-renewable term ensures that once confirmed, a justice faces no re-election incentive. The Judicial Selection Board provides expert assessment that is harder to dismiss than a political attack advertisement.

This is, on balance, a better mechanism than election for the specific institutional function the Supreme Court serves. The Court's legitimacy rests on the quality of its reasoning and the integrity of its process. A confirmation system that selects for legal quality — imperfectly, with friction, through political negotiation — does less damage to that legitimacy than a campaign system that selects for electoral appeal. The Republic makes that bet. The 2/3 threshold is the mechanism it trusts most to make the bet pay off.
