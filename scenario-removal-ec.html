Electoral Integrity · §16.2.a · §16.5.c · EC Member Removal

The Commissioner

An Electoral Commission member's undisclosed conflict of interest is discovered 210 days before National Election Day. The JI finds grounds. The State Designee Panel substantiates them. On day thirty-six the suspension kicks in — outside the 180-day window — and Commissioner Vargas is relieved of EC duties before the election. The removal vote, blocked by the 180-day proximity rule, follows after. Two provisions serving two distinct purposes, operating in sequence.

§16.2.a EC Removal and Suspension §16.5.c 180-Day Proximity Rule §8.1.c JI Oversight of EC §8.9 State Designee Panel §8.9.i SC Review §9.1 NRS Immutability

Commissioner Thalia Vargas was elected to the Electoral Commission in Year 6 of the Republic, on a platform of technical modernization for the National Voting System. She received 61.3% of the national vote — a strong mandate for a body that rarely generates electoral enthusiasm. She serves a six-year non-renewable term. In Year 9, the Judicial Inspectorate's annual audit discovers a problem in her financial disclosures.

The Conflict

Commissioner Vargas disclosed her investment holdings as required under §10.5.b. She disclosed her spouse's general investment portfolio as required. What she did not disclose — and what the JI's forensic audit discovers by cross-referencing her spouse's holdings against EC vendor contracts — is that her spouse holds 340,000 shares of Meridian Electoral Systems, a voting technology company that received a sole-source contract from the EC in Year 8 for supplemental National Voting System infrastructure.

The contract was worth 47 million. Her spouse's holding was worth approximately 4.1 million at the time of the contract award. Commissioner Vargas participated in the EC vote approving the Meridian contract. The vote was 4-1. She voted in favor.

She did not receive any direct financial benefit. Her spouse's shares were already owned before she joined the Commission. Commissioner Vargas maintains she was unaware of the specific Meridian holding within her spouse's managed portfolio. The JI's finding makes no determination about her intent. It makes one determination: the undisclosed holding constitutes an undisclosed conflict of interest under §16.2.a regardless of intent. The conflict existed. It was not disclosed. The EC vote occurred with the conflict undisclosed. That is the factual record.

§8.1.c — JI Annual Audit Finding · Day 0
Published to the NRS — 210 days before National Election Day

The JI's 34-page finding is published on a Thursday morning, 210 days before National Election Day — the biennial election at which roughly 40% of all House seats, the full Electoral Commission election cycle, and two Senate seats are on the ballot. Commissioner Vargas issues a public statement acknowledging the holding. She says she was unaware of its specific composition. She does not resign. She says she will continue to perform her duties unless and until a valid removal process concludes.

The Process

The Legislature files a For-Cause removal motion the following week. The State Designee Panel convenes within fourteen days of the motion. It reviews the JI's documented finding and publishes its binary determination on Day 22: grounds substantiated. One finding, one outcome.

Commissioner Vargas does not petition the Supreme Court to review the Panel's finding under §8.9.b. The undisclosed holding is a documented fact. The Panel applied the correct legal standard to that fact. An appeal on these grounds would not succeed, and she knows it. The 14-day SC appeal window runs from Day 22 to Day 36 without a filed challenge.

The Suspension Trigger — §16.2.a
JI finding published: Day 0 (210 days before NED)
Removal motion filed: Day 7 (203 days before NED)
State Designee Panel substantiates: Day 22 (188 days before NED)
14-day SC appeal window expires without challenge: Day 36
Days before NED at Day 36: 210 − 36 = 174 days
174 days > 180-day threshold — suspension is not proximity-restricted
Day 36: Commissioner Vargas suspended from all EC duties

On the afternoon of Day 36, Commissioner Vargas is relieved of all Electoral Commission duties. She is not removed — removal requires a 2/3 legislative vote that has not yet happened, and she retains the right to return to duties if the Legislature ultimately votes against removal. But she no longer participates in Commission votes, operational decisions, or vendor oversight. The suspension is published to the NRS. The Commission operates with four active members.

The Proximity Rule

While the suspension process was running, the Legislature had been scheduling a removal vote. The vote was set for Day 35 — one day before the suspension would take effect. On Day 33, a legal challenge notes the timing: on the scheduled vote date, 175 days will remain before National Election Day. Under §16.5.c, any removal vote within 180 days of a qualifying national election is automatically suspended pending Supreme Court review within 48 hours.

§16.5.c — The Removal Vote is Blocked. The Suspension is Not.
Two mechanisms, two purposes, one day apart

The Supreme Court's emergency panel confirms: 175 days remain at the scheduled vote date. The removal vote is suspended under §16.5.c. But §16.5.c applies to the legislative removal vote only — it says so explicitly. Panel-triggered suspension of duties under §16.2.a operates independently of election proximity. The suspension cannot be held hostage to a vote that the constitution has legitimately delayed.

The two mechanisms take effect within 24 hours of each other, doing different work. The proximity rule protects against legislative manipulation of election administration — a temporary majority removing a Commissioner in the weeks before a vote it wants to control. The suspension protects the election itself from being administered by someone with substantiated grounds for removal. The constitution does not make these interests compete. It serves both.

The Election and After

National Election Day proceeds with four active Commissioners. With Vargas suspended, the Commission's decision threshold applies to its four active seated members. Three of four votes are required for Commission decisions during the suspension period. The election is administered without incident. Results are certified thirty-one days after the election.

The Legislature's suspended removal motion reactivates immediately upon certification. The vote is rescheduled for twenty-two days later.

Commissioner Vargas removed — effective immediately

Commissioner Vargas has been suspended from EC duties since Day 36. She is now formally removed. Under §16.2.a, the seat is filled through a special election within 90 days; the replacement serves a full new non-renewable 6-year term. The Senate's additional vote on future office disqualification fails 59-41, short of the simple majority of full seated membership required.

What This Scenario Tests

§16.2.a and §16.5.c address two distinct risks that can arise simultaneously. §16.5.c guards against a legislature removing an EC member close to an election specifically to influence that election's administration — the removal vote is blocked within 180 days of a qualifying ballot. §16.2.a's suspension mechanism guards against an election being administered by a member with substantiated grounds for removal, regardless of whether the removal vote can be held.

The scenario shows why separating these two mechanisms matters. Under the old rule — member continues all duties until the removal vote concludes — Commissioner Vargas would have administered the election with the JI's finding and the Panel's substantiation both on the NRS. The proximity rule, designed to prevent abuse, would have had the incidental effect of protecting a compromised commissioner through an election she should not have been running. The revised design breaks that coupling: the suspension is triggered by an independent judicial process, not a legislative act, so it is not subject to the legislative proximity restriction.

The 14-day SC appeal window is the key safeguard on the suspension side. The suspension is not triggered by an allegation or an investigation — it requires a completed JI finding, a Panel substantiation, and either a judicial challenge that fails or an unchallenged window that closes. By the time the suspension takes effect, the factual basis has been independently verified at three separate stages. What the constitution will not do is then require the election to be administered by the suspended member while the legislature works out its vote schedule.
