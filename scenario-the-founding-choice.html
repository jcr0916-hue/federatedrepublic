The Founding Choice

The Transition Window, before the Republic is fully constituted. Every recognized indigenous nation must decide the status it will hold: default into Associated Community sovereignty, or integrate as a Territory with the road to Statehood open. The Corrin Nation is divided, the clock is running, and a federal transition officer has just made an offer that the constitution forbids him to make.

§16.2 Founding Status Election §16.1 Prior Sovereignty & Recognition §15.6 Voluntary Incorporation §16.5 Transition of Existing Arrangements §20.4 Associated Community Withdrawal

The Corrin Nation appears on the founding register — a nation the predecessor government recognized by treaty two centuries before the Republic. That places it, at Day Zero, in front of the choice §16.2 puts to every recognized nation during the Transition Window: elect a status, by the nation's own governance process, from a defined menu. Associated Community, the default. Or Territorial Integration, entering as a Territory under §15.6 with the standard Statehood pathway available. Make no election, and the default holds automatically when the window closes.

Two options, one deadline, and a nation that could not agree.

The Case for Staying Outside

The elders who favored Associated Community status made the sovereignty argument. Corrin had been sovereign before the Republic; §16.1 said that sovereignty was acknowledged, not granted, and that the Republic would deal with Corrin as a sovereign nation. To take Associated Community status was to keep that — to sit beside the Republic in a compact of equals under Article XX, governing Corrin's internal life by Corrin's own law, with the one guarantee that mattered most to them: §20.4, the unconditional right to leave, on notice, forever. A nation that could always walk out was a nation that had never truly been absorbed.

The cost was equally clear, and the elders did not hide it. Outside the constitutional structure, Corrin's members would not be Inhabitants; Article I would not run inside Corrin's territory as a constraint on Corrin's own government, only the non-derogable floor of §20.3 while the compact held. The Republic's social-state guarantees — education funding, healthcare, infrastructure floors — would reach Corrin only insofar as a compact negotiated them. Self-determination in full, with the Republic's protections held at arm's length.

The Case for Coming Inside

The younger council members argued for Territorial Integration. Corrin's economy was already braided into the surrounding region; half its working members crossed into Republic territory daily. To integrate as a Territory under §15.6 was to make that seamless — Corrin's people would be Inhabitants, Article I would run in full within Corrin, and the Statehood pathway of §15.2 would be open, so that a Corrin that governed itself well could become a State and send its own members to the Assembly and the Senate. Not sovereignty beside the Republic, but a full voice inside it.

The cost, again named honestly: a Territory is an entity of the Republic, not a sovereign. There is no §20.4 for a Territory — no unconditional exit. A Territory that later wished to leave would have to climb the whole ladder, to Statehood and then through the three-stage independence process of §15.9, a road measured in decades and votes. Integration meant trading the open door for the full protections, and the door, once traded, would not easily reopen.

The Offer That Could Not Be Made

Into this genuine, difficult deliberation walked a federal transition officer, tasked with helping recognized nations understand their options. He understood his mandate as something more. Integration, he told the council, would come with a favorable share of the regional infrastructure fund — a bridge, a clinic, a paved road to the provincial market — commitments he was prepared to see written down, if Corrin chose to come in.

Corrin's legal advisor stopped the meeting and read §16.2 aloud. The Republic may not prefer, discourage, or offer inducements toward any status; it must provide factual information about each status upon request, and nothing more. The officer's offer was not information. It was an inducement — precisely the thing the constitution forbids the Republic from placing on the scale, because the founding election is meant to be the nation's free choice about its own sovereignty, not a transaction in which the stronger party buys the outcome it prefers.

The advisor filed the exchange to the NRS. The officer's superior withdrew the offer within the day and published a correction: the infrastructure fund was available to Corrin on identical terms regardless of the status it chose, and the earlier statement had been improper. The inducement rule had done its work — not by punishing the officer, but by making the improper offer visible and void, and restoring the choice to the nation.

The Rights Question in the Meantime

One quieter provision shaped the deliberation throughout. Under §16.2, Article I applies within a recognized nation's territory during the Transition Window unless the nation positively rejects it. Corrin did not reject it. So even as the council debated whether to end up inside or beside the Republic, its members carried Article I rights in the interim, and could bring an Emergency Rights Writ directly to the Supreme Court if those rights were violated before the choice was made. The default ran toward protection: silence meant rights applied, and only an affirmative act of the nation's own government could switch them off. The window was not a rightless limbo; it was a bridge with a floor.

The Choice

Corrin chose Associated Community status — narrowly, and with a formal minority statement from the integration faction entered into the record. The deciding argument was §20.4: whichever way the nation guessed about its future, the Associated Community door could always be reopened by Corrin alone, while the Territory road could not. Faced with an irreversible choice, the council chose the reversible status. Existing arrangements with the predecessor government continued provisionally under §16.5 while the Republic and Corrin opened compact negotiations, and the road-and-clinic that the transition officer had once dangled were built anyway, on the neutral terms the correction had promised.

The window closed. Corrin stood beside the Republic, sovereign, with the door at its back unlocked.

What the Case Established

That the §16.2 founding election is a real choice with real and opposite costs — Associated Community trades the Republic's protections for sovereignty and an unconditional exit; Territorial Integration trades the open door for full Article I coverage and a path to a seat at the table. That the no-inducements rule is not decorative: it makes an improper offer visible and void, keeping the founding choice the nation's own. That Article I runs during the window by default, so no nation deliberates in a rights vacuum. And that when a nation must choose under uncertainty, the reversibility of Associated Community status — the §20.4 door — is itself a decisive feature of the design.
