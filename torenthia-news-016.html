Sandris Is Still Running. People Are Starting to Notice Why.

The fourth declared candidate for Legat Consul has been running for three weeks. He has no national profile, no foreign affairs record, and no moment of his own. What he has is an argument that the last two weeks made stronger without him saying a word.

Petra Vend Political Correspondent · Verentum — Year 12, Month 12

Pell Sandris gave his first real interview on a Thursday afternoon in the Aldenmere State Assembly building, in an office that has two chairs and a window facing a parking structure. He has been Legat Consul candidate for twenty-two days. His campaign staff is three people, one of whom is also his Assembly constituency coordinator. He has not been to Korda Territory. He has not addressed a foreign policy forum. He has not issued a statement about Sunderland's internal military faction structure. He was asked about it anyway.

"I read the summary," he said. "Everyone read the summary. What I noticed is that the failure it describes — the diplomatic channel not reaching the relevant command — is the same kind of failure as the EM's Korda Territory fiscal finding and the same kind of failure as the delay in §6.3 proceedings for the Varenne Station survivors. They're all coordination failures. The institutions weren't talking to each other. That's not a foreign affairs problem. That's an architecture problem." He said this without notes, without pausing, in approximately forty seconds. Then he waited.

The Argument

Sandris has been on the Aldenmere State Assembly's Revenue and Infrastructure Committee for six years. In that capacity he has appeared before the Executive Monitor's office four times, reviewed federal fiscal transfer calculations, and spent considerable time understanding how the Republic's constitutional institutions are designed to coordinate and how they fail to do so in practice. He is not a theorist. He is an administrator who has read the relevant provisions and watched the gap between what they require and what happens.

His campaign's core claim is that the race has been conducted as if the Legat Consul is the only constitutional officer that matters — and that this is precisely the kind of thinking that produces coordination failures. The LC runs foreign affairs and defense. But the LC inherits a Civic Consul, a Legislature, an Executive Monitor, a Monetary Authority, and a fiscal architecture that was designed to function as a system. "You cannot be a good Legat Consul," Sandris said, "if you don't understand what the Civic Consul does and why. You cannot run a foreign crisis if you don't know what the EM's fiscal findings on Korda Territory say and why they matter." He paused. "None of the candidates are talking about the EM's findings."

"The candidates are running for the Legat Consul they imagine. I am running for the Legat Consul the constitution describes."

Why Now

Sandris filed his declaration on the same day Vael held his press availability and Carrow published the intelligence summary. Almost nobody noticed. The transcript of his subsequent eleven-minute press availability — three reporters, no cameras — moved on social networks over the following week in the specific way that transcripts move when people read them, stop, and send them to someone else without comment.

The intelligence summary helped him without intending to. Carrow published a document that proved coordination failure at the diplomatic level. Mak responded by arguing that the domestic infrastructure failure and the diplomatic failure are the same failure. Sandris had been making a version of that argument since the day he filed, but abstractly, constitutionally, without the specific example. Now the specific example exists and it is on the NRS and everyone has read it. His argument did not change. Its evidence got stronger.

He is aware of this dynamic and slightly uncomfortable with it. "I don't want to run a campaign on other people's failures," he said. "I want to run a campaign on what the job actually is." He was asked what the job actually is. He answered for eight minutes. The answer included the fiscal equalization mechanism, the EM confirmation vote that will appear on the Year 13 ballot simultaneously with the LC election, the Assembly elections running in the same cycle, and the question of what kind of LC-CC relationship the next six years will require given the current domestic fiscal picture. It did not include a single sentence about what he would do differently at the Sunderland diplomatic channel.

The Problem

Sandris's problem is not his argument. His argument is coherent and increasingly timely. His problem is that the Legat Consul is elected nationally and national elections run on visibility, on moments, on the ability to occupy a news cycle rather than a transcript. He has occupied transcripts. The gap between those two things is not small and six months may not be enough time to close it.

He was asked directly whether he thought he could win. He said: "I think the Republic needs someone to make this argument and I'm the one making it. Whether that's enough to win depends on what the Republic decides it's looking for." It was not a confident answer. It was an honest one.

Three weeks ago almost nobody had heard of Pell Sandris. Most people still haven't. But the people who have read his transcript are sending it to other people. That is a small thing. It is also, in this particular race, not nothing.

Petra Vend is The Torenthian's political correspondent, based in Verentum.
