Institutional Process · Two Tests · §2.3 PM Formation

The Formation Scenarios

Two tests of the §2.3 PM formation provisions. Part A: a post-election deadlock where the House cannot elect a PM within 30 days and the Senate designates a sitting House member with full constitutional authority. Part B: a mid-term vacancy where the SDACO steps up and the House seats a successor.

§2.3.a PM Selection §2.3.b Constructive No Confidence §2.3.c Caretaker and Senate Designation §8.1.a LI Reporting §9.1 NRS Publication

Part A — Post-Election Deadlock

The general election produces a fractured result. The Republic’s four major parties split the House almost evenly: the Progressive Coalition holds 162 seats, the National Alliance 158, the Democratic Reform Party 94, and the Independents and minor parties 86. No combination of two parties commands an absolute majority. Every viable coalition requires at least three parties — and three-party coalitions have a long history of collapsing within months over ministerial allocation disputes.

The House convenes on Day 1. Under §2.3.c, the 30-day window begins. An absolute majority — a majority of the full seated membership, not merely those present — is required to seat a PM. With 500 House members seated, that threshold is 251.

Days 1–30
Coalition negotiations collapse

The Progressive Coalition and Democratic Reform Party open talks. Combined they hold 256 seats — a majority, but only barely. The DRP’s demands for three cabinet positions including Finance prove non-negotiable. Talks collapse on Day 18. The National Alliance opens separate talks with the DRP on Day 20; combined at 252, talks collapse on Day 26 over economic policy. Three-way negotiations begin Day 27 and stall immediately over demands for binding written commitments no major party will give.

Day 30 passes with no PM confidence vote held. The 30-day window expires at midnight.

Day 31 — §2.3.c
No caretaker exists — Senate designation authority activates

§2.3.c is clear: failure to elect a PM within 30 days triggers the caretaker protocol. The SDACO serves as Caretaker PM pending Senate action or a House election. The immediate problem: no Domestic Affairs Council exists. No PM was appointed this term. No PM has appointed ministers. There is no Senior Domestic Affairs Council Officer to step into the role.

Simultaneously, §2.3.c’s Senate designation authority activates. The Senate may at any time designate a serving House member as PM by 2/3 supermajority of full seated membership. The Senate does not wait. With no institutional caretaker to bridge the gap, the designation authority is the operative constitutional mechanism.

The LI begins publishing weekly accountability reports to the NRS documenting each formation vote attempted, candidates proposed, and every member’s individual participation record. These reports are permanent.

Day 36 — §2.3.c
Senate designates: MP Kofi Acheampong

After four days of deliberation, the Senate votes 71–29 to designate Kofi Acheampong, a four-term Independent member representing a mid-country district, as PM. Acheampong has served on the House Budget and Oversight Committee for twelve years. He holds no formal party affiliation and has worked across coalitions throughout his career. He is the only House member all three major party groupings can accept without conceding a political victory to a rival.

Acheampong takes the Constitutional Oath within 24 hours of the Senate vote. The oath is published to the NRS. He assumes full PM authority at noon on Day 37.

This is not a caretaker arrangement. Under §2.3.c, a Senate-designated PM holds the same constitutional authority, obligations, and term limits as a PM elected by the House. Acheampong may initiate legislation, negotiate policy, and form a ministerial council. He is the Prime Minister. The Senate’s 2/3 threshold ensures he carries genuine cross-party legitimacy into that role.

Days 37–84
The designated PM governs; the House negotiates

Acheampong forms a ministerial council and presents a maintenance budget to the House within 14 days. It passes narrowly — the House can agree that government must continue even when it cannot agree on who should lead it. The LI’s weekly accountability reports continue publishing to the NRS. Members whose names appear in 47 days of published deadlock records face a constituency-visible history of failure.

Day 62: The PC and DRP reopen talks. The DRP reduces its cabinet demands. The PC accepts, with Finance going to a DRP-nominated independent economist rather than a party politician. Day 71: Coalition agreement signed. The PC-DRP coalition holds 256 seats.

Day 84 — §2.3.b
Constructive no-confidence terminates the Senate designation

The PC-DRP coalition tables a constructive vote of no confidence naming Adaeze Okonkwo — the PC’s parliamentary leader — as the named successor. The motion passes 256–244. PM Acheampong leaves office. PM Okonkwo assumes authority.

The House did not need Senate permission to replace the Senate-designated PM. It used the ordinary constructive no-confidence mechanism, which requires simultaneously naming and electing a replacement. The Senate designation ended the moment the House found its majority. This is the correct constitutional sequence: the Senate broke the deadlock; the House resolved it.

Part B — Mid-Term Vacancy

Three years into the term, PM Okonkwo resigns due to a health condition. The SDACO — the Deputy PM, appointed by Okonkwo at the start of her government — assumes the Caretaker PM role immediately under §2.3.c. The institutional infrastructure absent in Part A exists here. The Domestic Affairs Council is fully staffed. The transition is seamless.

The House has 30 days to elect a new PM. The PC-DRP coalition’s internal leadership process selects a candidate within three weeks. On Day 26, the House votes 259–241 to elect the new PM by absolute majority of full seated membership. The Senate’s designation authority was available from Day 1 of the vacancy but was never needed. The House acted within its window.

What These Scenarios Test

Part A tests the constitution’s response to the hardest version of PM formation failure: a fractured House where no institutional caretaker exists. The Senate designation mechanism is the answer — a 2/3 supermajority requirement high enough to ensure genuine cross-party legitimacy, designating a sitting House member who keeps the PM within the institution that will hold them accountable. Full authority from day one, because a government without full authority is not a government.

The House’s supremacy is preserved throughout. The Senate designation is not permanent and carries no veto over the House. The moment the House achieves a majority for someone, the constructive no-confidence mechanism terminates the designation cleanly. Part B shows the simpler case: the SDACO mechanism functions when the institutional infrastructure exists, and a 30-day window is sufficient for a coalition with an internal candidate process.

The LI’s weekly accountability reports are the mechanism that converts the 30-day clock into genuine political pressure. Publication does not force resolution. It makes the individual cost of failure visible at the member level — 47 days of permanent NRS records documenting who blocked formation — which is the pressure the formation model relies on instead of dissolution.
