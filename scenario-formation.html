Dual Executive · Government Formation · §2.6

The Formation

Two tests of §2.6's government formation provisions: a post-election coalition that takes forty days to assemble, and a constructive no-confidence vote that removes a PM by installing a named successor.

§2.6 PM Accountability and Formation §2.5 The Prime Minister §3.1 The Legislature §8.1.a The Legislative Inspectorate

The first test of §2.6's formation provisions came thirty-eight days after the first general election. The House had returned without a majority coalition — the three largest formations held 201, 143, and 81 seats respectively. The remaining 75 seats were distributed across four smaller groups. Under §2.6, the House must elect the PM by absolute majority. No absolute majority existed.

On Day 38, a coalition agreement between the first and fourth-largest formations — combining 237 seats — produced the Republic's first PM: Minister-designate Amara Diallo-Mensah of Korda Province, who had spent the prior three years as the constitutional convention's working group chair on executive structure. She had not been anyone's first choice. She was everyone's second.

The Formation Mechanics — §2.6

§2.6 — How a PM Is Formed
The House elects the PM by absolute majority. If it cannot, the most senior confirmed Cabinet Minister continues as Caretaker PM until it does.

The forty-day formation period was within the period the Legislature had defined by statute. On Day 31, the statute's deadline passed without a majority. The most senior confirmed Cabinet Minister from the outgoing caretaker government — Deputy Minister Seun Okafor, who had served as caretaker since Day Zero — continued in that role. The Senate, noting the impasse, voted 29-11 on Day 35 to designate a candidate from the House's largest formation. That designation was terminated four days later when the House's own absolute majority vote installed Diallo-Mensah under §2.6's provision that House election supersedes Senate designation.

The Constructive Vote — Year Three

PM Diallo-Mensah's coalition frayed in Year Three. The fourth-largest formation — whose support had been essential to her installation — withdrew from the coalition over a domestic infrastructure policy dispute. Without those 81 seats, the working majority was gone. The second-largest formation, which had spent three years in structured opposition, had a candidate ready: Minister-designate Kwame Asante, a former trade negotiator who had spent the interregnum building cross-formation relationships.

The constructive no-confidence motion named Asante explicitly. The vote was 222 to 218 — a margin of four seats, exactly the absolute majority of the full seated House required by §2.6. Diallo-Mensah was removed and Asante was simultaneously installed. She was PM for seven minutes between the final tally and the moment Asante's name appeared on the NRS as the new Prime Minister.

§2.6 — The Constructive Vote's Constitutional Logic
A confidence vote that does not name and simultaneously elect a successor has no constitutional effect.

The construction of the constructive vote is the key design feature. The opposition cannot remove a PM without simultaneously installing a replacement. There is no constitutional space between the two acts — no caretaker period, no uncertainty about who governs next. The moment the House votes, two things happen: the sitting PM is removed and the named replacement takes office. The opposition must be ready to govern before it can challenge the right of others to do so.

The LI published its annual compliance report noting the formation — including the forty-day delay and the Senate designation that was superseded — as a fully constitutional sequence. Nothing in the formation process required the House to succeed quickly. The statute's deadline triggered the caretaker mechanism; the House's eventual absolute majority vote resolved it. The system worked as designed.

What This Scenario Tests

§2.6 separates the PM's accountability mechanism — the constructive vote — from the formation mechanism — the absolute majority. A PM who loses their working majority does not automatically fall; a majority must actively assemble around a named alternative before they can remove the incumbent. This is the constitutional answer to the question of what happens when a government's support erodes gradually rather than collapses suddenly.

The forty-day formation scenario tests the statute's definition of the formation period, the Senate's limited fallback designation power, and the hierarchy between Senate designation and House election. The Senate's designation authority is real but clearly subordinate: it exists to prevent indefinite deadlock, not to substitute for the House's primary role.
