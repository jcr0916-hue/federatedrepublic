Executive Power · Clemency · §2.12

The Clemency Scenario

Two tests of §2.12's cross-domain clemency provisions. A Presidential pardon voided as self-interested. A PM clemency grant challenged as reaching into the presidential domain.

§2.12 Clemency §8.1.b The Executive Inspectorate §9.1 National Record System

The Republic's clemency architecture is simple in principle: each executive holds clemency authority within their own domain only. Presidential clemency covers military law and national security offenses. PM clemency covers domestic offenses. Neither may pardon offenses in the other's domain. Self-clemency is absolutely prohibited. The EI publishes a factual background report before any grant.

Test One — The Presidential Grant — Year 6

President Valdez issued a full pardon to a former Director of Intelligence convicted of unauthorized domestic surveillance — an offense under both domestic law and military law. The EI published its pre-grant background report as required, noting that the President had been a co-signatory on the classified order that the Director had implemented. The report did not characterize this as a conflict. The EI's mandate is factual, not advisory.

The NRS published the pardon within hours. The House's Legal Affairs Committee petitioned the SC on two grounds: first, that the offense involved domestic law violations outside the presidential domain; second, that the President had been a direct beneficiary of the conduct being pardoned — the order that caused the offense was the President's own.

§2.12 — No Clemency for Offenses in Which the Executive Was a Participant
No clemency may be granted for offenses in which the granting executive was a participant or direct beneficiary.

The SC ruled within fourteen days. On the first ground — domain — the Court found that the national security component of the offense was sufficient to place it within the presidential domain. The President had clemency authority over that component. On the second ground — participation — the Court found that co-signing the order whose implementation constituted the offense placed the President directly within §2.12's participation bar. The pardon was void as to the domestic law component already; it was also void as to the national security component.

The Director remained convicted. The President faced an EI investigation into whether issuing a void clemency grant constituted a constitutional compliance breach. The EI found breach, published a finding, and referred the matter to the appropriate authority.

Test Two — The PM's Grant — Year 9

PM Asante issued a clemency order to a former intelligence contractor convicted under a domestic fraud statute. The fraud had occurred in the contractor's private capacity, entirely separate from their intelligence work. The Presidential domain officer — the Director of Intelligence — filed an objection, arguing that the contractor's intelligence background brought the clemency into the presidential domain.

The EI's background report documented the contractor's intelligence history but made no assessment of domain. The PM issued the grant. The Director petitioned the SC.

§2.12 — Domestic Offense, PM Domain
PM clemency covers domestic offenses. The relevant question is the nature of the offense, not the background of the offender.

The SC dismissed the petition. The offense was domestic fraud. The contractor's intelligence history was not relevant to the offense charged, the conviction, or the clemency question. Domain is determined by the nature of the offense, not by the identity or background of the person convicted. A domestic fraud conviction does not become a national security matter because the convicted person once held a security clearance. The PM's grant was valid.

What This Scenario Tests

§2.12 tests two of the clemency architecture's most important design features: the participation bar and the domain question. The participation bar — no executive may pardon offenses in which they were a participant or beneficiary — is the constitutionalization of the most obvious self-dealing risk in any clemency system. The EI's mandatory pre-grant background report is the mechanism that makes the bar operational: the public record shows the relevant facts before the grant is made, not after.

The domain question — which executive holds clemency authority for which offenses — requires case-by-case determination. The SC's guidance from Test One (domain follows the nature of the offense, not secondary considerations) and Test Two (background of the offender is not relevant to domain) gives courts and executives a workable rule.
