The Sovereignty Question

What does it mean for a constitution to acknowledge a sovereignty it did not create?

This paper is in development. The questions it addresses arose from the constitutional office review and the drafting of §14.6. The argument is complex enough to warrant its own extended treatment. What follows is an outline of the core questions and the directions the argument will take.

Article XIV of the Federated Republic's constitution begins with a statement that is unusual in constitutional design: the sovereignty it recognizes, it did not create. "The Republic acknowledges that indigenous nations held sovereign authority over their ancestral lands before the formation of the Republic. This acknowledgment is not a grant of sovereignty — it is a recognition of sovereignty that already exists." Most constitutions create the legal order they describe. This one claims to find something already there.

That claim raises a set of questions that the drafting process forced into the open but did not fully resolve. This paper explores them.

The Core Tension

If indigenous sovereignty is real and predates the Republic, the Republic's constitution cannot be the source of that sovereignty. At most it can describe the Republic's side of the relationship. But constitutional drafters inevitably make choices about how to describe that relationship — and those choices reflect assumptions about what indigenous sovereignty is, what it entitles indigenous nations to, and what obligations it places on the Republic.

The tension: a constitution that acknowledges prior sovereignty and then creates a framework for it is doing something philosophically complicated. It is both recognizing something it didn't create and regulating something that exists independently of it. Whether that regulation protects or subtly diminishes the sovereignty it purports to recognize is not a question with a clean answer.

The Questions This Paper Will Address

On the nature of indigenous sovereignty

  • Is "prior sovereignty" a philosophically coherent concept, or is it a legal fiction that colonial legal systems deployed selectively to justify or deny claims?
  • Does sovereignty require a state, or can non-state governance structures hold it?
  • What happens to prior sovereignty when a colonial state overlays it for centuries — does it persist, weaken, or transform?
  • Is there a meaningful difference between a sovereignty that has been suppressed and a sovereignty that has ceased to exist?

On the limits of constitutional frameworks

  • Can a constitution acknowledge a sovereignty that predates it without subordinating that sovereignty to its own legal order?
  • The Federated Republic's Article XIV attempts this — but the very act of constitutionalizing the relationship places it within the Republic's legal framework. Is that unavoidable?
  • The New Zealand Treaty of Waitangi, Canada's Section 35, the US "domestic dependent nations" doctrine: what does each reveal about the limits of constitutional recognition?
  • Is the government-to-government model (Article XIV's approach) more honest about the sovereignty question than a rights-based model?

On the distinction the constitution makes

  • The Federated Republic treats indigenous nations differently from other sovereign entities. Is this distinction philosophically justified, or is it a form of categorization that ultimately limits indigenous nations to a special status — however protective — rather than treating them as simply sovereign?
  • The §14.6 status election provision gives indigenous nations a menu: compact, associated community, territorial integration, or full sovereign independence. Does providing a menu implicitly suggest the Republic has authority over which option is chosen — even if the provision explicitly denies this?
  • Is Article XIV's distinctiveness ultimately a corrective for historical racism, or does it create a new form of categorical differentiation that carries its own risks?

On the landlocked sovereignty problem

  • An indigenous nation choosing full independence while geographically surrounded by Republic territory raises genuine logistical questions — transit, trade, utilities, citizenship. The constitution's answer (treaty obligation within 24 months) is procedurally correct but doesn't resolve the underlying tension: what does sovereignty mean for a nation that cannot access international borders without crossing another sovereign's territory?
  • San Marino and Vatican City provide working precedents. What do they tell us about the minimum conditions for meaningful landlocked sovereignty?
  • Is geographic position a legitimate constitutional factor in defining the scope of sovereignty, or is the Republic obligated to make independence fully viable regardless of geography?

On self-determination as a constitutional principle

  • UNDRIP Article 3: "Indigenous peoples have the right of self-determination." Article 4: "Indigenous peoples... have the right to autonomy or self-government in matters relating to their internal and local affairs." How do these interact with a constitutional framework that creates a specific structure for that self-determination?
  • Does the §14.6 status election provision fulfill the UNDRIP self-determination right, or does it limit it to a menu that the Republic defined?
  • What would a constitution look like that took UNDRIP Article 3 fully seriously?

The Argument This Paper Will Make

The argument will be: that the Federated Republic's approach represents a genuine advance over prior constitutional models, but that it cannot fully escape the paradox of constitutionalizing a sovereignty that predates the constitution. The §14.6 status election provision is the constitution's most honest response to this paradox — it gives indigenous nations the power to exit the constitutional framework entirely, which is the only way a constitutional framework can genuinely respect a sovereignty it did not create.

The argument will also engage directly with the question raised in drafting: is Article XIV's distinctiveness based in historical racism? The answer will be that the distinctiveness exists because of historical racism but is not itself racist — and that the distinction between those two things matters enormously for how the provision is understood and applied over time.

Finally, the argument will note what the constitution cannot do: it cannot resolve the philosophical question of whether prior sovereignty persists or transforms after centuries of colonial suppression. It can only make choices about how the Republic conducts itself in relation to that question. Article XIV and §14.6 represent the choices this constitution made.
