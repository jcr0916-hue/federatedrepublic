Paper No. 9

The Sovereignty Question

On two forms of sovereignty this constitution acknowledges but did not create — the individual sovereignty that government may limit but never eliminate, and the prior sovereignty of Indigenous Nations that precedes and conditions the compact relationship.

This constitution makes a claim that runs deeper than any specific right it enumerates. Every person possesses a sovereignty over their own life, body, and choices that government authority may limit — but may never eliminate.§1.2 The claim precedes every right that follows it. It is the foundation on which the rest of Article I rests. And it is not the only sovereignty this document acknowledges without having created. Article XVI recognizes that Indigenous Nations within the Republic's territory hold a sovereignty that predates the Republic itself. Two forms of sovereignty — individual and national — that the constitutional order inherits rather than grants.

What Individual Sovereignty Is Not

The most common misreading of individual sovereignty in constitutional documents is the libertarian one: sovereignty means autonomy, the right to be left alone, the individual as a sovereign who owes nothing to the state. This is not what §1.2 means. The provision does not say government may not limit individual sovereignty. It says government may not eliminate it. The distinction is the whole point.

A constitution that prohibited government from limiting individual sovereignty would prohibit taxation, criminal law, conscription, and most of what governments do. That is not a constitutional design. Individual sovereignty in this document is better understood as a form of standing — the irreducible recognition that every person remains a person regardless of what the state does to them. The criminal serving a life sentence has had their liberty dramatically limited. They have not been reduced to a non-person. They retain the standing that makes certain acts against them — torture, degrading treatment, discrimination — constitutionally impermissible regardless of their crime or any emergency.

The constitutional significance of the limit/eliminate distinction becomes clear when you examine what elimination would look like. A government that can limit sovereignty operates normally — it can imprison, tax, conscript, regulate. A government that can eliminate sovereignty can declare that certain persons have ceased to be persons at all. This is the constitutional precondition for the worst abuses democratic governments have historically committed. Stateless persons stripped of citizenship. Designated enemy combatants held without legal standing. Classes of persons whose humanity is formally revoked before being systematically harmed. The individual sovereignty guarantee prevents the state from treating any person as though the constitutional order has nothing to say about what may be done to them.

The Floor That Holds in Emergencies

The individual sovereignty floor is non-derogable — it cannot be suspended by emergency, overridden by statute, or waived by executive action.§1.26 This is the provision's most practically significant feature. Emergency powers are constitutionally necessary. Genuine emergencies require the state to act with speed and scope that ordinary constraints would prevent. Almost every stable democratic constitution recognizes this.

But emergency powers are also the mechanism through which constitutional orders have most frequently collapsed. The suspension of normal rights that emergencies require has, repeatedly in democratic history, been extended beyond the emergency, applied to populations the emergency did not reach, and used to entrench whoever declared it. The constitutional response is to identify, in advance, which protections cannot be suspended even when everything else can be.

The individual sovereignty floor is the deepest of those protections. Where an emergency suspends freedom of movement, the floor means the people whose movement is restricted remain persons with standing before the constitutional order. The emergency limits sovereignty. It does not eliminate it. The prohibition on torture and cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment§1.3 is the floor's most direct constitutional expression — no punishment imposed under law may be cruel, degrading, or inhumane, regardless of what emergency exists or what the person has done.

Prior Sovereignty — What the Republic Did Not Create

Article XVI begins with an acknowledgment that the Republic cannot make without consequence: Indigenous Nations within the Republic's territory hold a distinct constitutional relationship based on prior sovereignty.§16.1 Prior sovereignty is not sovereignty granted by the compact. It is sovereignty the compact relationship acknowledges as having existed before the Republic and as continuing to condition that relationship.

The acknowledgment is bilateral: the Republic acknowledges Indigenous prior sovereignty; Indigenous Nations acknowledge the Republic's constitutional framework. Neither party's recognition of the other creates the other. The Republic does not create Indigenous sovereignty by acknowledging it — it recognizes what was already there. The compact relationship is built on that mutual recognition, not on the Republic's authority to grant standing to those it chooses to recognize.

This matters constitutionally because it shapes what the compact relationship can and cannot be. A nation whose sovereignty flows from acknowledgment by the Republic can have that acknowledgment withdrawn. A nation whose sovereignty predates the Republic holds it on a different footing — the compact defines the relationship between two sovereigns, not the conditions under which one of them is permitted to exist.

The Compact Architecture

Article I rights apply in full within all compact territories — no compact provision may limit or qualify any Article I right, and where compact governance conflicts with an Article I right, Article I prevails.§16.3 This is the individual sovereignty floor operating at scale: the collective governance authority of a compact Nation cannot eliminate the individual sovereignty of the people within its territory.

Within that constraint, Indigenous Nations with compacts hold governance, judicial, and resource sovereignty within their territories. They may establish their own courts. Resource extraction within compact territories requires both Republic and Nation approval. The Republic must coordinate with compact governance bodies. Compacts must include provisions for preservation of Indigenous languages and cultural practices, with federal education funding supporting Indigenous-language instruction.

Compacts are protected from unilateral termination by either party. Only the Nation may initiate termination — the Republic cannot exit a compact it finds inconvenient. Where the Nation initiates, the Republic must negotiate exit terms within two years; if no agreement is reached, the compact continues and the dispute goes to the SC.§16.4 Indigenous treaties entered by predecessor governments are binding obligations of the Republic and cannot be diminished by legislation — they must be honored, renegotiated with consent, or formally superseded through the compact process.§16.6

Status and Choice

An Indigenous Nation with a compact may, through its own democratic or traditional governance process, choose a different status.§16.7 Three options exist: continued compact relationship, territorial integration into the Republic's State structure, or full sovereign independence with a treaty negotiated within 24 months. The Republic may not prefer, discourage, or offer inducements toward any option.

The irrevocability of status elections is the design feature that makes genuine choice possible. A Nation that chooses Territorial Integration cannot return to compact status — the choice is permanent. This prevents strategic oscillation and protects the stability of compacts that other Nations depend on. But it also means the choice must be informed and genuinely voluntary, which is why the Republic must provide factual information about each status on request and may not shape the decision through incentive or pressure.

Full Sovereign Independence cannot be forced into a treaty. The Republic is obligated to negotiate in good faith for 24 months. An independent nation that does not wish to conclude a treaty has no obligation to do so. Sovereignty means the freedom to decline as well as to agree.

Two Sovereignties, One Document

The individual sovereignty floor and the prior sovereignty acknowledgment are the same constitutional insight at different scales. Both recognize a sovereignty the state did not create and cannot eliminate. Both establish that the Republic's authority operates within limits set by something that predates it. Both reflect the document's foundational bet: that a constitutional order willing to acknowledge its own limits is more durable than one that claims unlimited authority.

Paper No. 1 describes this document as a bet on citizens — on the assumption that people who can see what their government is doing will hold it accountable. The sovereignty provisions make a prior bet: that a constitutional order built on honest acknowledgment of what it cannot do or undo is more legitimate than one that papers over those limits. The individual sovereignty floor is the acknowledgment that persons exist prior to states. The prior sovereignty provisions are the acknowledgment that nations do too.
