The Sovereignty Question

What does it mean for a constitution to acknowledge a sovereignty it did not create? On indigenous prior sovereignty, self-determination, and the paradox of constitutionalizing what the constitution cannot generate.

Every constitution makes an implicit claim about where legitimate authority originates. Most root that authority in the people — the act of popular ratification creates the government, which derives its powers from the consent of the governed. This story is clean, recursive, and almost entirely false when applied to the relationship between constitutions and the indigenous peoples who occupied the territory before the constituting act took place.

The problem is not difficult to state. Indigenous sovereignty predates the constitution. The peoples who exercised authority over their ancestral lands before the founding did not participate in a social contract that brought the new government into existence. Their sovereignty was not transferred, negotiated away, or absorbed into the founding moment. In many cases it was simply overridden — by conquest, by displacement, by the legal fictions that allowed governments to treat unceded land as terra nullius. A modern constitution that takes prior sovereignty seriously cannot pretend this history did not occur. But it also cannot undo it. What it can do is acknowledge sovereignty that already exists rather than grant sovereignty that the constitution creates.

That distinction is the subject of this essay. It is not a distinction most constitutional frameworks have found a way to honor. Understanding why requires examining what sovereignty actually means, what acknowledgment actually does, and what the limits of any constitutional framework are when confronted with a claim that is prior to and independent of the framework itself.

What Sovereignty Is

Sovereignty, in the classical sense, is the supreme authority within a defined territory — the power that answers to no higher power within its jurisdiction. For most of Western political theory, sovereignty flows from the state: the state is sovereign, citizens are rights-holders within that sovereignty, and no competing authority can claim jurisdiction over the same territory. This is the Westphalian model, and it is deeply inhospitable to indigenous sovereignty claims because it assumes that sovereignty is singular and territorial in a way that makes overlapping claims literally incoherent.

Indigenous sovereignty does not fit this model — not because it is weaker or less real, but because it is organized differently. Indigenous political authority is typically relational rather than territorial in the Westphalian sense: it is authority over a people, exercised through governance structures and traditions of that people, in relationship with the land those traditions recognize. It does not require the consent of a neighboring sovereign to exist. It does not derive its legitimacy from a founding document. It derives its legitimacy from the continuous exercise of governance — from the fact that these peoples governed themselves, maintained legal systems, resolved disputes, managed resources, and organized collective life for generations before any other government arrived to contest the arrangement.

When this document says, in Article XIV, that indigenous nations held sovereign authority over their ancestral lands before the formation of the Republic, and that this acknowledgment is not a grant of sovereignty but a recognition of sovereignty that already exists, it is making a specific philosophical claim: that sovereignty of this kind does not need the Republic's permission to be real. The Republic did not create it. The Republic cannot extinguish it by failing to recognize it. What the Republic can do is bring its own institutional framework into honest alignment with that reality — or not.

The Paradox of Constitutional Acknowledgment

Here is the paradox this document had to confront: any constitutional acknowledgment of prior sovereignty is performed by the very institution whose authority is being called into question. Article XIV says indigenous sovereignty predates the Republic. But Article XIV is part of the Republic's constitution. The Republic is acknowledging, within its own legal framework, a sovereignty that is prior to and independent of that framework. Is this circular?

The Republic did not create indigenous sovereignty. The Republic acknowledges it. Those are not the same act, and conflating them is the error most constitutional frameworks have made.

The answer depends on what kind of act acknowledgment is. If acknowledgment is a grant — if the indigenous nation's sovereignty exists because the Republic recognized it — then the paradox is real and damaging. A sovereignty that depends on the recognizing power's ongoing consent is not sovereignty at all; it is a permission that can be withdrawn. This is the structure of most historical relationships between settler-colonial states and indigenous peoples: the state recognized tribes through treaties and legislation, which meant the state could also de-recognize them through subsequent action.

But acknowledgment need not be a grant. It can be something more like a finding of fact — a constitutional statement that a certain condition obtains independent of the constitution's own authority. When a court acknowledges that a person was tortured, the acknowledgment did not create the torture; it recognized something that happened regardless of whether the court noticed it. When this constitution acknowledges prior indigenous sovereignty, the intended structure is the same: the sovereignty existed and exists, and the constitution is declaring that this is the case. The declaration does not make it so. It aligns the Republic's legal framework with a reality the framework did not produce.

This distinction matters enormously for what follows. If the sovereignty exists independently, then the Republic's obligations to indigenous nations are not obligations it chose to take on as policy — they are obligations that arise from the prior sovereignty's continued existence. The Free, Prior, and Informed Consent requirement of §14.3.a is not a courtesy the Republic extends to indigenous nations. It is the constitutional expression of a government-to-government relationship between entities whose authority is not hierarchically ordered. Neither government answers to the other. FPIC is the mechanism that operationalizes that equality.

Self-Determination and the Status Election

The most consequential design decision in Article XIV is §14.6 — the indigenous nation status election framework. It gives recognized nations four options: remain in the Article XIV compact relationship; elect Associated Community status under Article XX; pursue territorial integration through the two-step pathway; or elect full sovereign independence outside the Republic's constitutional framework. The right to initiate any of these processes is continuous. No election requires Republic consent to begin.

This framework is an attempt to take self-determination seriously as a constitutional principle rather than as rhetorical aspiration. Self-determination in international law means, at minimum, that peoples have the right to freely determine their political status and pursue their economic, social, and cultural development. Most constitutional systems nominally endorse this principle and then construct frameworks in which the only operationally available option is some form of integration into the existing state structure. The integration pathway is smooth; every other pathway is legally undefined, practically unavailable, or actively discouraged.

§14.6 tries to make the menu genuine. The compact option is the default precisely because it should not require any action — an indigenous nation that chooses the existing relationship should not have to do anything to maintain it. The independence option is real because the right to self-determination means nothing if independence is formally acknowledged but practically foreclosed. The constitution is explicit: geographic position within Republic territory does not affect the right to independence. A landlocked nation whose territory is entirely surrounded by Republic land can elect independence if its governance assembly decides that is what its people want. The Republic's obligations — transit corridors, treaty negotiation within 24 months, diplomatic recognition — are designed to make that independence real rather than merely symbolic.

The status election also resolves a question the document had to answer carefully: what is the relationship between the right to initiate a change and the right to achieve it? The right to initiate is continuous — an indigenous nation can begin the process at any time through its own democratic or traditional governance process. But initiation is not completion. The process follows the constitutional requirements of the destination framework at the pace those requirements demand. A governance council vote is the beginning of the independence process, not its end. The 24-month treaty negotiation is the Republic's obligation; Senate ratification completes the transition. This distinction matters because it prevents both manipulation in either direction: the Republic cannot use procedural delay to block a genuine election, but the election's legal effect is not instantaneous. The process is the protection.

The Double-Track Problem

Running the China Scenario through this constitution surfaced the most constitutionally complex version of the sovereignty question: what happens when two different constitutional frameworks apply simultaneously to the same geographic space? The Uyghur determination — that the Uyghur people constituted an indigenous nation with prior sovereignty, while the administrative territory of Xinjiang was simultaneously undergoing a Statehood Audit — created a situation the document had to handle without explicit instruction.

The resolution that emerged was that the two tracks are genuinely independent. The Statehood Audit assesses the Territory's governance institutions against constitutional criteria. The Article XIV framework governs the Uyghur Nation's relationship with the Republic. These are different objects operating on different legal bases producing different obligations. The Territory has a governance trajectory. The Nation has a constitutional relationship. They coexist in the same geography, and the FPIC requirement is what makes the coexistence workable — any federal infrastructure project within Uyghur ancestral lands requires the Uyghur Nation's consent regardless of what the State of Xinjiang's position might be. The State cannot consent on the Nation's behalf.

This structure requires abandoning the assumption that a single piece of territory can only be in one constitutional relationship at a time. The assumption fails because indigenous sovereignty is not primarily about territory in the Westphalian sense — it is about the relationship between a people and the land they have governed. The Uyghur Nation's compact with the Republic is a government-to-government relationship. The State of Xinjiang's audit is an assessment of territorial governance. These are genuinely different things even when the geography overlaps.

What a Constitution Cannot Do

This essay would be dishonest if it did not acknowledge the limits of what any constitutional framework can accomplish here. Three deserve naming directly.

First, a constitution cannot undo dispossession. Article XIV acknowledges prior sovereignty and establishes the framework for ongoing relationships. It does not restore the land that was taken, compensate the communities that were displaced, or repair the intergenerational harms of colonization. The Transitional Accountability Mechanism (§19.9) provides some institutional space for addressing predecessor-government harms, but that space is bounded by design. Constitutional acknowledgment of prior sovereignty does not by itself produce restitution.

Second, a constitution cannot guarantee that acknowledgment is genuine. The FPIC requirement is constitutionally binding, but a government determined to override indigenous interests will find ways to formally satisfy the consent requirement while actually evading it — through controlled information environments, through economic pressure, through the structural coercions that produce consent from parties who lack genuine alternatives. The publication requirement, the NRS record, and the Supreme Court's injunctive authority create accountability mechanisms. They do not create goodwill.

Third, a constitution cannot resolve the fundamental tension between the framework's universalism and the particularity of indigenous legal traditions. The unamendable prohibitions — torture, slavery, arbitrary detention, denial of public trial — apply within indigenous jurisdictions as they apply everywhere else. The Republic does not seek consent for these. It enforces them through the targeted Emergency Rights Writ mechanism when specifically violated, rather than through general law enforcement operations. This is the correct balance, but it is still a balance the Republic sets. A constitution built on the premise that certain rights are universal cannot make them optional within any jurisdiction — even one whose prior sovereignty the constitution acknowledges. The universalism and the acknowledgment coexist uncomfortably, and the constitution holds that discomfort openly rather than resolving it by collapsing either side.

What the Document Tries to Be

Given these limits, what is Article XIV actually doing? Not restoring what was taken. Not resolving the philosophical tension between universal rights and particular sovereignties. Not guaranteeing that future governments will honor what this document requires.

What it is doing is establishing the terms of honesty. The Republic exists on land that indigenous peoples governed before the Republic existed. The prior sovereignty is real, not contingent on the Republic's recognition, and not subject to withdrawal by unilateral Republic action. The government-to-government relationship is between equals. The consent requirement is genuine. The status options are real. The independence right includes the mechanisms that make independence meaningful. The unamendable rights apply everywhere.

These commitments are not sufficient. They are, however, honest. A constitution that acknowledged prior sovereignty only in a preamble while giving indigenous nations no operational rights would be performing acknowledgment rather than practicing it. A constitution that gave formal rights but made the independence pathway practically unavailable would be creating the appearance of self-determination while structuring its impossibility. Article XIV is an attempt to avoid both failures — to mean what it says rather than to say what sounds good.

Whether it succeeds depends on the institutions that interpret it, the governments that implement it, and the indigenous nations that choose to engage with it on those terms. The constitution can establish the framework. It cannot populate the framework with actors of good faith. That is always the wager — not just in Article XIV, but in every provision of a document that asks people to honor something they could, if they chose, ignore.

The sovereignty question does not have a clean answer. But the attempt to answer it honestly, with mechanisms that make rights real rather than merely declared, is what separates a constitutional framework that takes self-determination seriously from one that merely invokes it. This document tries to make that distinction. The attempt is genuine. The limits are real. Both deserve to be said plainly.
