Paper No. 9

The Sovereignty Question

On §1.2's individual sovereignty floor — what it means for the state to be able to limit a person's sovereignty but never eliminate it, and why that distinction is the bedrock on which every other right in this document rests.

The first provision of the rights article does not enumerate a right. It makes a claim. Every person, it says, holds an individual sovereignty that government authority may limit only as this Constitution permits — and may never eliminate.§1.2 The claim precedes every specific right that follows it. It is the foundation on which the rest of Article I rests.

Understanding what that claim means — and what it does not mean — requires being precise about a word that carries more philosophical baggage than almost any other in constitutional design. Sovereignty, in most constitutional contexts, refers to the authority of states over territory. The Republic uses it differently. It uses it to describe something that belongs to persons, not polities — an irreducible standing that no government act can extinguish.

What Individual Sovereignty Is Not

The most common misreading of individual sovereignty in constitutional documents is the libertarian one. Sovereignty, on this reading, means autonomy: the right to be left alone, to make one's own choices free from interference, to resist government action as an intrusion on a pre-political liberty. The individual as a sovereign is the individual who owes nothing to the state and from whom the state may take nothing without consent.

This is not what §1.2 means. The provision does not say government may not limit individual sovereignty. It says government may not eliminate it. The distinction is the whole point. A constitution that prohibited government from limiting individual sovereignty would prohibit taxation, criminal law, conscription, health and safety regulation, and most of what governments do in any modern society. That is not a constitutional design. It is an anarchist manifesto with a seal.

The individual sovereignty §1.2 establishes is better understood as a form of standing — the irreducible recognition that every person remains a person regardless of what the state does to them. The criminal serving a life sentence has had their liberty limited dramatically and lawfully. They have not been reduced to a non-person. They retain the standing that makes certain acts against them — torture, degrading treatment, discrimination on protected grounds — constitutionally impermissible regardless of their crime, regardless of any emergency, regardless of any legislative majority that might prefer otherwise.

The Limit/Eliminate Distinction

The constitutional significance of the limit/eliminate distinction only becomes clear when you examine what elimination would look like in practice. A government that can limit sovereignty within constitutional constraints operates normally — it can imprison, tax, conscript, regulate. A government that can eliminate sovereignty can do something qualitatively different: it can declare that certain persons no longer have the standing to make any claim on the constitutional order at all.

This is not a hypothetical. The elimination of individual sovereignty — its reduction to zero for a particular class of persons — is the constitutional precondition for the worst abuses democratic governments have historically committed. Stateless persons stripped of citizenship. Designated enemy combatants held without legal standing. Classes of persons whose humanity is formally revoked before being systematically harmed. In each case, the abuse was made possible not by violating individual rights but by first eliminating the standing that made rights claimable.

§1.2's guarantee that sovereignty may never be eliminated is the constitutional answer to this specific mechanism of abuse. It does not prevent the state from treating people badly in the thousand ways that ordinary governance can be cruel, unjust, or mistaken. It prevents the state from treating any person as though they have ceased to be a person — as though the constitutional order has nothing to say about what may be done to them.

The Floor That Holds in Emergencies

The individual sovereignty floor is unamendable and unsuspendable.§1.2 No emergency, no official, no institution can suspend it. This is the provision's most practically significant feature, and it interacts directly with Article I's emergency protections.§1.26

Emergency powers are constitutionally necessary. Genuine emergencies — armed conflict, pandemic, natural disaster — can require the state to act with speed and scope that ordinary constitutional constraints would prevent. Almost every stable democratic constitution recognises this and provides some mechanism for emergency governance. The Republic is no exception.

But emergency powers are also the mechanism through which constitutional orders have most frequently collapsed. The suspension of normal rights that emergencies require has, repeatedly in democratic history, been extended beyond the emergency, applied to populations the emergency did not reach, and used to entrench the political position of whoever declared the emergency. The constitutional response to this pattern is to identify, in advance, which protections cannot be suspended even when everything else can be.

The individual sovereignty floor is the deepest of those protections. Where an emergency suspends the right of free movement, the sovereignty floor means the people whose movement is restricted remain persons with standing before the constitutional order — they can challenge the restriction, demand justification, seek redress. Where an emergency suspends freedom of assembly, the sovereignty floor means the prohibition on cruel treatment still applies to how that suspension is enforced. The emergency limits sovereignty. It does not eliminate it. That is the floor's function: to hold in exactly the conditions where every other protection is under pressure.

What the Floor Requires

The individual sovereignty floor has two constitutional pillars that give it practical content. The first is the absolute prohibition on torture and cruel, degrading, or inhumane treatment.§1.3 This prohibition is non-derogable — it cannot be suspended by emergency, overridden by statute, or waived by executive action. It is the constitutional floor's most direct expression: regardless of what a person has done, regardless of what emergency exists, there are things that may not be done to them because doing those things denies their standing as a person.

The prohibition also governs punishment. No punishment imposed under law may be cruel, degrading, or inhumane. This formulation deliberately leaves open the question of capital punishment — neither permitting nor prohibiting it. That question is left to democratic resolution: the Legislature may address it by statute, and citizens may address it through referendum under Article XI. The constitution does not predetermine the answer. It sets the floor that constrains whatever answer democracy reaches. A democratic majority may determine that capital punishment is just. It may not determine that capital punishment is exempt from the prohibition on cruel, degrading, or inhumane methods.

The second pillar is the equality guarantee.§1.9 Equality before the law is not just a right — it is the constitutional operationalisation of the sovereignty claim. If every person holds individual sovereignty, then the state may not treat the sovereignty of different persons differently on the basis of protected characteristics. A law that applies the full force of criminal sanction to one group for conduct it exempts in another has not merely treated members of those groups differently. It has implicitly treated their sovereign standing differently — as though the constitutional floor sits at different heights for different people. The equality guarantee closes that gap.

The Floor and the Rest

The individual sovereignty floor is not the most detailed provision in Article I. It is the most foundational one. Every specific right that follows it — to free expression, to privacy, to due process, to equal treatment — is an application of the underlying claim that persons have standing before the constitutional order that the state may not extinguish.

This structure has a practical implication that is easy to overlook. Where a specific right has not been enumerated — where Article I is silent about a particular form of state action — the sovereignty floor is still operative. A government that cannot point to constitutional authority for treating a person as a non-person cannot do so, even if no specific provision addresses the precise form of treatment in question. The floor is not a list. It is a baseline from which the entire rights architecture rises.

Paper No. 14 addresses the six provisions that are placed beyond revision by the most demanding amendment threshold this constitution provides. The individual sovereignty floor is among them — not because it is perfect, but because it is the condition under which the rest of the constitutional order can be questioned, revised, and improved. A constitution that allows its own foundation to be amended away has not protected anything. The floor holds so that everything built on it can continue to be contested, corrected, and made better by the people it serves.
