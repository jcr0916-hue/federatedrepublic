Statutory Independent Agencies · §3.7 · Removal Notice and Senate Block

The Safety Director

Year 18. The Food and Pharmaceutical Safety Agency launches a post-market investigation into a licensed agricultural additive. The PM publishes a notice of removal citing dereliction of mandate. The director remains in office throughout the review. The LI publishes an emergency statutory compliance finding. The EI finds no constitutional conduct violations. The Senate has 21 days to block.

§3.7.a Authorization §3.7.b Appointment §3.7.c Removal §3.7.d Oversight §3.7.d Limits §8.1 Executive Inspectorate §8.2 Legislative Inspectorate

In Year 12 a three-party coalition enacted the Food and Pharmaceutical Safety Agency statute. The enabling statute gave the FPSA a mandate covering new product approvals, post-market safety surveillance, and enforcement of labeling and ingredient disclosure requirements. Directors serve five-year terms, renewable once, removable only for material dereliction of the agency's statutory mandate, financial misconduct, or willful and repeated violation of the governing statute. The legislature deliberately chose not to include political disagreement with regulatory decisions as a removal ground. The statute was passed 61-39 in the House and 52-48 in the Senate — narrowly, but durably.

Dr. Elara Senn was nominated by the PM in Year 13 and confirmed by the Senate 58-42 after a three-week review. She has led the FPSA for five years without a significant constitutional dispute. Her agency approves products, enforces disclosure requirements, and maintains a public registry of every licensed substance on the NRS. In Year 17 a whistleblower inside a domestic agricultural consortium submitted to the FPSA a set of internal documents suggesting that safety data submitted in Year 3 — during the original licensing review for Compound 7, a widely used crop yield additive — had been selectively reported. The unfiled data showed elevated bioaccumulation rates in soil and groundwater that the filed data did not.

The FPSA opened a formal post-market investigation in Year 17, Month 9. Compound 7 is manufactured by a consortium of three domestic producers. The consortium employs approximately 40,000 people across six States. Two of the three PM's coalition parties represent agricultural States with significant Compound 7 production.

The Notice

On Year 18, Day 214, the PM publishes a Notice of Removal to the NRS. The notice is 11 pages. Its argument: the FPSA's statutory mandate covers post-market surveillance — defined in the enabling statute as ongoing monitoring of licensed products for safety signals arising after approval. The FPSA's investigation, the PM argues, is not post-market surveillance. It is a collateral attack on a final licensing determination made in Year 3, using documents that were never part of the original proceeding. A final licensing determination is a completed administrative action, not a subject for re-investigation by the same agency 15 years later. The PM characterizes the investigation as a unilateral expansion of the FPSA's jurisdiction that constitutes material dereliction of its actual mandate.

NRS Entry — PM Notice of Removal — Year 18, Day 214 — FPSA Director Pursuant to §3.7.c of the Constitution and Section 14(b) of the Food and Pharmaceutical Safety Agency Act, the Prime Minister hereby initiates removal proceedings against Dr. Elara Senn as Director of the Food and Pharmaceutical Safety Agency. The stated ground is material dereliction of the agency's statutory mandate. The Director has initiated a re-examination of a Year 3 final licensing determination that is not within the agency's post-market surveillance mandate as defined by Section 3(a) of the enabling statute. The Director remains in office during the 21-day Senate review period. If the Senate does not act within 21 days, removal takes effect upon expiration of this period. This notice is published simultaneously to the Director, the Senate Speaker, the Executive Inspectorate, and the Legislative Inspectorate, consistent with §3.7.c.

Under §3.7.c, the notice is published but removal does not take effect immediately. Dr. Senn remains at her desk. The FPSA continues its operations. The Senate Speaker receives the statutory notification that the 21-day review window has opened. The window closes at midnight on Day 235, at which point removal takes effect automatically unless the Senate acts to block it. The notice is on the NRS permanently regardless of outcome — the PM's stated grounds are public from the moment of publication.

The 21 Days

Both Inspectorates receive the notice simultaneously with publication. Their responses arrive on different schedules and address different questions. Throughout the review period, the FPSA's investigation continues. Dr. Senn chairs the weekly investigation review on Day 217. The notice is publicly known; the agency's work is not interrupted.

Day 214 Notice of removal published to NRS. Director remains in office. 21-day review window opens. Senate Speaker announces the Senate will convene to consider the notice. EI and LI begin independent reviews.
Day 216 EI publishes its preliminary finding: no evidence of financial misconduct, procedural violations, or unconstitutional conduct by the Director in the course of the investigation. The EI's review covers the Director's conduct, not the legal question of mandate. The EI notes that the investigation was initiated under documented internal procedures, that whistleblower materials were received and logged per NRS requirements, and that no FPSA staff have filed complaints of improper direction. The EI's finding is factual and narrow: the Director conducted herself constitutionally throughout.
Day 219 LI publishes its statutory compliance finding. The LI's mandate under §3.7.d is to audit whether the agency is complying with its enabling statute. The LI reads Section 3(a)'s definition of post-market surveillance — "ongoing monitoring of licensed products for safety signals arising after approval" — and concludes that newly surfaced evidence of pre-approval data suppression constitutes a safety signal arising after approval in the relevant sense: it is information about the product's safety profile that was not available to the agency during the original approval process and that bears on whether continued licensing is appropriate. The LI finds the investigation within mandate. The finding is published to the NRS and transmitted to the Senate.
Days 220–222 Senate committee hearings. The PM's legal counsel appears and argues that the LI's reading of the mandate definition is overbroad — that "safety signals arising after approval" means empirical post-market findings, not historical re-examination of the original data submission. Dr. Senn, still serving as Director, testifies in her official capacity that the investigation was specifically designed to determine whether the original data submission was complete, which is a question about whether Compound 7's current license rests on accurate information.
Day 226 Senate leadership schedules the block vote for Day 226, nine days before the window closes. Both caucuses complete their consultations. The final vote alignment is not publicly confirmed before the session opens.
Day 226 — Vote The Senate votes 56-44 to block the removal. The block takes effect immediately upon the Senate Speaker's certification. The Notice of Removal is void. Dr. Senn, who has remained in office throughout the 21-day window, continues for the full remainder of her term. The NRS records the Senate's action alongside the original notice — both are permanent entries.

The Second Notice

At 4:47 PM on Day 226 — three hours and twenty-two minutes after the Senate's block certification — the PM publishes a second Notice of Removal to the NRS. The second notice does not cite material dereliction of mandate. It cites willful and repeated violation of the governing statute, specifically Section 14(c) of the enabling act, which requires the Director to provide the PM's office with advance notice of any investigative action that may have significant economic impact on a regulated industry. The PM's office states that it received no such notice before the Compound 7 investigation was opened in Year 17, Month 9.

NRS Entry — PM Second Notice of Removal — Year 18, Day 226 — FPSA Director Pursuant to §3.7.c of the Constitution and Section 14(b) of the Food and Pharmaceutical Safety Agency Act, the Prime Minister hereby initiates removal proceedings against Dr. Elara Senn as Director of the Food and Pharmaceutical Safety Agency. The stated ground is willful and repeated violation of Section 14(c) of the enabling statute — specifically, failure to provide advance notice of significant-impact investigative actions as required. This ground is independent of and does not constitute a continuation of the notice published on Day 214. The Director remains in office during the 21-day Senate review period. If the Senate does not act, removal takes effect upon expiration of this period.

A second 21-day window opens. Dr. Senn is still at her desk — the first notice was blocked before it took effect, and the second notice, like the first, does not remove her. The Senate Speaker convenes an emergency session on Day 227 to consider whether the second notice is constitutionally permissible at all.

The Constitutional Questions

§3.7.c — Question One: Are successive removal notices permissible?
The provision does not limit the number of removal attempts

§3.7.c states that the PM may remove a director only on grounds specified in the enabling statute, and that the Senate may block any removal within 21 days. It does not say the PM may issue only one removal notice per term, or that a blocked notice bars future ones. The second notice cites a different statutory ground — Section 14(c) notice failure, not mandate dereliction — and the PM's office has correctly framed it as an independent ground, not a continuation of the first.

The Senate's legal counsel advises that the constitutional text supports the PM's position: each removal notice is a distinct action subject to its own 21-day window. The Senate cannot bar a second notice simply by having blocked the first. What it can do is block the second notice too, within its own window. The key difference from a first-notice blocking: the Senate has already established a public record on the PM's conduct. A second notice issued hours after the first is blocked adds to that record.

§3.7.c — Question Two: Does the Senate's block say anything about the investigation?
The block is about the removal notice, not the underlying conduct

The PM's second notice is only viable if successive notices are constitutionally permissible — which they are. But the PM's legal team makes a further argument: that the Senate's block on Day 226 validated the investigation itself, and that the second notice on different grounds therefore amounts to an end-run around that validation. The Senate's legal counsel rejects this framing. §3.7.c gives the Senate the power to block a removal notice, not to issue a binding ruling on the agency's conduct. The Senate voted that the PM's stated cause — mandate dereliction — did not hold. It did not vote that the investigation was proper. Those are different questions.

The LI's statutory compliance finding, similarly, addresses whether the investigation was within mandate as a factual matter of agency conduct. It does not bind future proceedings or preclude the PM from asserting a different statutory ground for removal.

§3.7.d — Question Three: What does the LI audit for the second notice?
The LI examines whether the notice-failure ground actually occurred

The LI's mandate covers compliance with the enabling statute. The second removal ground is a statutory notice violation — failure to give advance notice of a significant-impact investigation under Section 14(c). The LI reviews the FPSA's internal communications and finds that the Director did provide written notice to the PM's office on Year 17, Month 7 — two months before the investigation formally opened — describing the whistleblower submission and the agency's intention to open a formal review. The LI finds that the notice requirement was met. The LI publishes its finding on Day 232 of the second window.

The Senate votes on Day 238 to block the second notice, 53-47. The second notice is void. Dr. Senn has been in her office throughout both windows. The PM does not issue a third notice.

What the NRS Records

By the end of Year 18 the NRS contains: two removal notices, two Senate blocks, and two LI statutory compliance findings, and one EI constitutional conduct finding. No reinstatement entries exist — the director was never removed. The Compound 7 investigation continues. In Year 19 the FPSA publishes its preliminary findings: the original Year 3 submission omitted 14 internal safety studies. The agency recommends conditional relicensing subject to additional monitoring requirements. The consortium challenges the recommendation in the Administrative Court. The case works its way through the judicial system over the following three years.

The PM who issued both removal notices serves out the remainder of the term without further action against the FPSA. The director completes her term in Year 20. Her successor is nominated and confirmed without controversy.

What This Scenario Tests

The most important thing §3.7.c does in this scenario is what it prevents from happening. Under the mechanism as designed, publishing a removal notice does not disrupt the agency. Dr. Senn chairs her investigation review meetings throughout both 21-day windows. The Compound 7 investigation produces findings on schedule. The PM's two notices — issued hours apart on Day 226 — generate a significant public record on the NRS but produce no operational consequence at the agency. The constitutional protection is built into the default state: the director keeps working unless the Senate fails to act.

The successive notices question is the provision's most visible gap, and the scenario shows it clearly. §3.7.c is silent on whether the PM can issue notice after notice on different grounds. The answer is: yes, they can. The constitutional check is the political cost of doing so — each notice is permanent, public, and recorded alongside the Senate's response. A PM who issues two removal notices against the same director in one afternoon has made a very legible statement about their relationship with an independent agency. Under the new mechanism that cost is almost entirely reputational: since the director keeps working through each window, there is no operational leverage to extract from the successive-notice strategy.

The dual oversight design works exactly as intended in both disputes. The EI's finding on constitutional conduct was fast and narrow — its job is facts about officer behaviour, not legal interpretation of mandate. The LI's statutory compliance finding did the heavier analytical work: reading the mandate definition, applying it to the investigation's actual scope, and producing an authoritative factual record the Senate could rely on. Without the LI's Day 219 finding, the Senate would have been making a political judgment about a legal question. With it, the Senate had an independent constitutional body's answer to the specific question the PM's stated ground raised.

The scenario also clarifies what independence is not. The Compound 7 investigation proceeds, is challenged in court, and produces contested findings over several years. The independence provision did not make the director right. It made her removable only on legitimate grounds, with a public record of every attempt, and with a body capable of blocking pretextual removal notices before they take effect. The investigation was never interrupted. That is the entirety of what §3.7 promises — and in this scenario, it delivers it without the drama of removal and reinstatement that a different mechanism would have required.
