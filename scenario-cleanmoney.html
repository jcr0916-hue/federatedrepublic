The Clean Money Election

The Republic's public campaign financing framework faces its first significant test — a major donor attempts to circumvent the constitutional contribution limits through a complex intermediary structure.

§8.1 Campaign Finance Framework§8.1.a Contribution Limits§8.2 Public Financing§12.4 Ethics Framework§11.1 NRS Panel and Elections Panel

The Republic's electoral finance framework was designed to reduce the dependence of elected officials on large private donors. Public campaign financing, constitutional contribution limits, disclosure requirements, and the Elections Panel's certification role together constitute the framework. In Year 3, the first national election cycle under the full constitutional framework, the system faced a challenge that was sophisticated in design and straightforward in constitutional outcome.

Industrialist Cassian Roth had been a prominent political donor in the predecessor government. Under the Republic's constitutional framework, direct contributions to candidates were limited by statute; the Elections Panel monitored compliance; and all campaign finance transactions were published to the NRS within 48 hours of receipt. Roth's direct contributions were, by the time of the election, well within the published limits. His indirect contributions were not.

The Structure — §8.1.a

§8.1.a — Contribution Limits and Circumvention
Constitutional contribution limits apply to all financial value directed toward a campaign, not only direct monetary transfers.

The intermediary structure Roth had constructed ran through three advocacy organisations and a policy research entity. Each organisation received contributions within the constitutional limits. Each organisation then funded activities — research publications, media placements, event sponsorships — that had the direct effect of promoting specific candidates. The organisations were formally independent of the campaigns. The financial trail from Roth to campaign benefit required, in the Elections Panel's analysis, fourteen steps to fully document.

The Elections Panel's investigation — triggered by an NRS anomaly flag in the transaction publication system — took six weeks. The Panel published its findings under its §11.x certification authority. The finding was straightforward: the intermediary structure was designed to direct financial value to specific campaigns in excess of the constitutional contribution limits while structuring each step to avoid the direct transaction the limits were designed to capture.

The EM opened an ethics investigation under §12.4.h simultaneously with the Elections Panel's certification process. Roth's structure, in the EM's assessment, constituted an attempt to circumvent constitutionally mandated electoral limits. The investigation findings were published to the NRS. The referral — potential criminal violations — went to prosecutorial authorities.

The Candidates — §8.2

Three candidates had benefited from the Roth intermediary structure. Two had received indirect benefits without knowledge of the structure's design — they had accepted funding from the advocacy organisations as independent entities without investigating their financing chains. One had a closer relationship with one of the organisations. The Elections Panel's certification findings were individual and specific.

§8.2 — Public Financing and the Electoral Record
The NRS publication requirement makes campaign finance transparent not just in the moment but permanently.

All three candidates' NRS finance records were updated with the Elections Panel's investigation findings. The two candidates who had received benefits unknowingly were required to return the equivalent value to their public financing account. Their campaigns continued with the corrected financing records publicly visible. The third candidate — whose relationship with the advocacy organisation was sufficiently close to constitute knowing circumvention in the Elections Panel's finding — faced disqualification proceedings under the statutory framework.

The disqualification proceedings resolved before the election. The candidate withdrew rather than contest the Elections Panel's finding in the available appellate process. The withdrawal was itself published to the NRS as a permanent record alongside the investigation findings. Future election cycles would begin with that record accessible to any candidate, any voter, any journalist researching the history of the Republic's campaign finance framework.

What This Scenario Tests

The electoral finance framework's constitutional architecture depends on three concurrent mechanisms: contribution limits that apply to financial value not merely direct monetary transfers, NRS real-time publication that makes transactions visible before violations become entrenched, and the Elections Panel's certification authority that can flag anomalies and investigate structures the literal contribution text might not capture.

The scenario demonstrates that transparency does enforcement work that investigation cannot. The NRS anomaly flag that triggered the Elections Panel's investigation existed because the 48-hour transaction publication requirement was producing a real-time data set. A contribution structure that took fourteen steps to document was generating fourteen NRS entries, each individually within limits, each contributing to a pattern that the publication system's monitoring function could detect.

The distinction between knowing and unknowing receipt of circumvented contributions is constitutionally important. The two candidates who received benefits unknowingly face a remediation — return the value, continue the campaign with corrected records. The candidate who knowingly participated faces disqualification. The framework's proportionality is deliberate: deterrence works through graduated consequences, not blanket punishment for proximity to a violation.
