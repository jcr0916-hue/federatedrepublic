Digital Infrastructure · §9.3 · Advisory Council · Decennial Review

The Decade

Year 10 of the Republic. Thirty-four States have built the Advisory Pool through years of nominations. The JI has certified 29. Five members are drawn by public lottery — the first Digital Infrastructure Advisory Council in the Republic's history. They review both systems, find nine issues, and publish. The Senate holds its public session. The Commission responds to every recommendation.

§9.3 Digital Infrastructure Advisory Council §9.3.a Mandate and Trigger §9.3.b Advisory Pool — State Nominations §9.3.c Pool Reset §9.3.d Advisory Council Draw §9.3.f Tri-Inspectorate Oversight §9.3.g Findings and Commission Response §9.3.h Senate Public Session

The Advisory Pool had been accumulating for seven years. The constitution required no particular pace of nominations — States could nominate whenever they chose, as many candidates as they wished, and the JI would review each within thirty days. Some States had nominated eagerly: Kailos submitted four nominees in the first year alone, all with strong cryptographic infrastructure backgrounds. Others had taken longer, uncertain what "relevant expertise" looked like in practice or which of their State's technical officials met the qualification standard. Eight States had still not nominated anyone by the time Year 10 arrived.

The JI's published qualification reports — one for every nominee, published to the NRS — had themselves become a minor public institution. Citizens followed them. Technology journalists wrote about the nominees. Three nominees who received unfavourable JI reports — finding gaps in their stated expertise — had contested the reports publicly, producing a small body of NRS commentary that the Republic's first constitutional scholars were already studying.

The Pool at Year 10 — §9.3.b

When the ten-year trigger arrived under §9.3.a, the JI published the current state of the Advisory Pool to the NRS: 34 States had submitted nominations; 29 nominees held current JI certification; 5 had been declined; 3 had withdrawn. No more than three certified members from the same State were eligible at the draw — a constraint that reduced the effective draw pool by two, since Kailos had five certified nominees but only three were eligible simultaneously.

Lottery draw: 5 members from 27 eligible nominees

The draw was conducted in a public joint session of all three Inspectorates. All 27 eligible nominees' names were loaded into the verified draw system. The draw ran in under four minutes. Five names appeared: a distributed systems architect from Varentis; a cryptographer from Arveld; an election systems auditor from a smaller inland State; a public records specialist who had spent twenty years in State archival governance; and a network security researcher who had spent a decade in private sector infrastructure before entering government work. No two from the same State. The draw certification was signed by all three Inspector Generals and published to the NRS before the session closed.

The Review — Weeks 1 through 14

The Council's mandate covered both systems: whether the NRS and NVS architectures continued to meet their constitutional requirements under §9.1 and §16.3. The Digital Infrastructure Commission Director opened the first working session with a statement of cooperation: the Commission had prepared full access documentation for both systems and would answer any question. The Council's mandate was advisory — the Commission's response was its own — but there was no adversarial posture. The Commission wanted an honest external assessment.

The NRS review found eight years of organic growth had introduced some architectural inconsistencies. The original node distribution model assumed 42 States; the Republic now had 44 States and three significant Territories with their own NRS access nodes. The node distribution formula in the original statute had not been updated. The nodes were working — redundancy was adequate — but the formula no longer reflected the actual architecture, which created a potential gap if a future node failure hit an asymmetric part of the distribution.

§9.3.a — Scope of the Advisory Council
Advisory only. The Commission decides what to do with the findings.

The Council's cryptographer raised a second NRS issue: the backup encryption standard for the long-term archive used an algorithm that had not been compromised but was approaching the end of its recommended lifecycle. No active threat. No constitutional failure. But the Council's assessment was that the Commission should begin the migration planning process now rather than wait for the next decennial review.

The NVS review was cleaner but had one substantive finding: the NVS's resilience testing protocol had not been updated since Year 3. The constitutional requirement under §16.3 is that the NVS operate in degraded conditions without compromising ballot secrecy or auditability. The tests existed. They had last been run under Year 3 conditions. Three hardware generations of NVS infrastructure had been deployed since. The Council recommended annual resilience testing using current-generation hardware degradation profiles.

The Findings — Nine Recommendations

The Council published nine recommendations to the NRS jointly certified by all three Inspectorates. Two were classified as significant architectural concerns; six were maintenance and process recommendations; one was a forward-looking planning note with no immediate action required.

Advisory Council Findings Summary — Year 10

NRS Architecture: (1) Node distribution formula requires statutory update to reflect current 44-State, 3-Territory topology — significant; (2) Long-term archive encryption algorithm migration planning should commence — forward-looking; (3) Citizen submission review latency has grown from 12 to 34 days — maintenance; (4) Three minor metadata standard inconsistencies introduced in Years 6–8 updates — maintenance.

NVS Architecture: (5) Annual resilience testing protocol requires update to current hardware generation — significant; (6) Voter participation data purge schedule not implemented in two States — maintenance; (7) Remote voting cryptographic audit trail format diverged from primary NVS format in Year 7 update — maintenance; (8) Ballot secrecy verification documentation requires update — maintenance; (9) NVS degraded-mode documentation no longer reflects current architecture — maintenance.

The Senate Session — §9.3.h

The Senate scheduled the public session on Day 22 after the findings were published — two days within the 30-day scheduling deadline. The session was held on Day 67. Five Council members at the table, 100 Senators in the chamber, the session live-streamed and published in full to the NRS.

The questions were pointed. Three Senators from States that had not submitted nominees asked whether the absence of their States from the pool had affected the Council's assessment of NVS performance in their States' regions. The election systems auditor gave the answer the constitution intended: the Council's assessment was based on the technical architecture, not on which States had nominated them. The draw mechanism existed precisely so that no State's presence or absence in the pool would affect the independence of the review. A Senator from Kailos — which had submitted four nominees, none of whom were drawn — asked whether the Council's findings were comprehensive given that three of its five members had no prior experience with Kailos's specific node architecture. The network security researcher responded that the constitutional standard is whether the architecture meets the requirements, not whether the reviewers are familiar with every State's implementation.

The Commission's Response — Day 84

The Commission published its formal response to all nine recommendations on Day 84 — within the 90-day window. Seven recommendations were accepted. One — the archive encryption migration — was accepted with modification: the Commission accepted the planning recommendation but noted that the migration timeline would be determined by the NRS Panel after a full technical assessment rather than by the Council's suggested three-year horizon. One recommendation was declined: the suggestion that metadata standard inconsistencies be resolved through a constitutional amendment to the NRS technical standards provision. The Commission's position, published with full reasoning: the inconsistencies were a statutory matter, not a constitutional one, and could be addressed through the Legislature's standard process.

What the Advisory Council Mechanism Achieved

Nine findings in ten years is not a crisis. It is a clean bill of health with maintenance notes. The architecture of both systems held. The constitutional requirements were met. The issues found were the kind of organic drift that any critical infrastructure accumulates over a decade of operation.

The Senate session produced something the Commission could not have produced itself: public accountability for the systems' independent assessment. Senators asking whether the pool's composition affected the review, whether findings were comprehensive, whether the Commission would actually act — these questions were not hostile. They were the democratic function the mechanism was designed to serve. The States that nominated the Council members completed the accountability chain. The Senate, as their representatives, interrogated the results.
