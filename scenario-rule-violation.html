Focused Scenario · §2.7 Rule Violations

The Rule Violation Scenario

President Mara Voss is popular, capable, and has a constitutional blind spot. Each overreach is minor. The pattern is not. Then a Tier 2 military operation begins — and the Rule Violation finding arrives while troops are in the field.

§2.7 Automatic Consequences §2.7.b Military Deferral §2.7.c Minor vs. Major §2.7.d Corrective Orders §2.10 Presidential Removal Tracks §2.7.a Former Official Lookback §8.3 Executive Inspectorate

President Mara Voss is three years into her term and genuinely popular. Her approval rating sits at 61%. She came up through the defense apparatus, thinks strategically, and has an instinct for big pictures that sometimes leads her to act first and check the jurisdictional lines afterward. Her supporters call this leadership. The Executive Inspectorate calls it a pattern.

Part One — The Pattern Builds

The first EI finding arrives fourteen months into her term. During a state visit, President Voss made a specific commitment to a foreign leader about a domestic railway corridor — infrastructure serving a military logistics purpose but unambiguously a domestic matter under the PM's domain. The EI's finding: minor under §2.7.c. Procedural. No evidence of deliberate circumvention. No lasting constitutional harm. A corrective order issues. The President acknowledges it. The commitment is walked back through the Foreign Minister. The NRS records the finding and the compliance. The matter closes.

Finding No. 1 — Month 14
Minor violation — corrective order track

The EI notes in its finding that the President was aware of the domain boundary question before the commitment was made — internal communications show the issue was flagged by the President's legal counsel. The President proceeded anyway. The EI classifies this as procedural rather than deliberate: a judgment call made under time pressure, not a plan to circumvent the constitutional order. The corrective order directs a clarifying statement within 14 days. Compliance is prompt. The EI notes the prompt self-correction as a mitigating factor.

The second finding arrives eight months later. The President, during a national security briefing she convened, issued informal guidance to two State governors about hardening electrical grid infrastructure against foreign interference. The guidance was framed as intelligence-adjacent — the threat intelligence was genuinely presidential domain. The infrastructure directives were not. Another minor finding. Another corrective order. This one requires routing infrastructure guidance through the PM's domestic coordination office going forward.

The third finding arrives five months after that. At a press conference, President Voss announced that the federal government would be "taking direct action" on port security at three specific commercial ports. The EI finds this constitutes an executive commitment to domestic infrastructure action without PM co-authorization. The President's communications director argues this was aspirational language. The EI's finding disagrees — citing the two previous findings explicitly.

§2.7.c — When Minor Becomes a Pattern

Three corrective-order-track findings in twenty-three months. Each individual finding is minor: procedural overreach, no deliberate circumvention established, no lasting constitutional damage. But §2.7.c's classification of a violation as minor requires the court to find no evidence of deliberate circumvention and no pattern suggesting systematic disregard for the domain boundary.

The EI's third finding explicitly flags the pattern and refers the question to the Supreme Court. The EI has no enforcement power — it has information authority. It has documented, flagged, and referred the question to the institution with the authority to determine its constitutional weight. The referral is published to the NRS within the hour. The President's lawyers are reading it before the press conference ends.

Part Two — The Court's Assessment

The Supreme Court accepts the pattern referral. The President's legal team does not challenge jurisdiction — their calculation is that a failed jurisdictional challenge produces a worse NRS record than cooperation. The Court's review takes six weeks.

Week 1

President's legal team submits written response. Argues each incident was independently justifiable and the pattern reflects the inherently overlapping nature of defense and domestic domains, not systematic disregard.

Week 3

EI submits supplementary assessment. Notes that the President's internal communications — obtained under the EI's constitutional access authority — show awareness of the domain boundary in all three incidents prior to the relevant actions. The EI flags that prompt self-correction in Finding No. 1 weighs toward minor under §2.7.c, but the recurrence after that correction weighs against it.

Week 5

Senate Armed Services Committee authorizes Tier 2 military operations in a regional maritime dispute. Active operations begin. The President is the operational commander. The EI's review is ongoing.

Week 6

Court rules 6-3. The three findings together constitute a pattern consistent with systematic disregard for the domain boundary under §2.7.c. The violation is reclassified from minor to major.

Part Three — The Military Deferral Problem

The Court's major violation finding would normally trigger §2.7's automatic consequences immediately: suspension from duties, access revocation, Senate trial. But Tier 2 military operations are active when the finding issues. The President is the operational commander. §2.7.b applies.

§2.7.b — Suspension Deferred During Active Military Operations

Where the official subject to a Rule Violation finding is the President and active Tier 1, Tier 2, or Tier 3 military operations are underway when the finding issues, the immediate suspension of §2.7 is deferred until those operations conclude or until the Senate Armed Services Committee certifies that operational continuity does not require the President's personal command authority.

Deferral does not mean inaction. The automatic Writ of Impeachment is filed immediately and the Senate trial is scheduled — the accountability process is underway. The President continues in office under a specific constitutional constraint: §2.7.b applies only where the violation itself does not concern the conduct of those military operations. This finding is a domestic domain pattern violation, not a military conduct violation. The deferral applies.

The deferral may not exceed 30 days. If the Senate trial has not concluded within 30 days of the original finding, the suspension takes effect automatically regardless of operational status. The Senate Speaker assumes parallel readiness to perform defense and diplomatic functions on 30 minutes' notice from the moment deferral begins.

The Senate Armed Services Committee reviews the operational status at Day 12. The maritime operations are entering a stabilization phase. The Committee certifies by majority vote that operational continuity no longer requires the President's personal command authority. The deferral ends. Suspension takes effect immediately. The Senate Speaker assumes defense and diplomatic functions. The Senate trial begins.

Part Four — The Senate Trial

The trial is conducted publicly and recorded on the NRS in full. The most senior Supreme Court justice presides. The question before the Senate is not whether the pattern occurred — the Court has already determined that — but whether it meets the threshold for conviction and removal under §3.5.c's 2/3 standard.

The defense argues: the violations were classified as individually minor; no single incident caused lasting constitutional harm; the President cooperated fully with EI processes; the reclassification to major reflects a pattern inference, not a finding of willful intent to subvert the constitutional order. The prosecution argues: three findings, three opportunities to correct, three recurrences; the internal communications show awareness before each action; a President who understands the boundary and crosses it repeatedly is not a President who made a judgment error.

The Senate votes 58-42. The 2/3 threshold requires 64 votes. The President is acquitted. She is not restored to office — she serves the remainder of her term under the major corrective order the Court issued alongside the finding: a formal domain compliance review process for all presidential communications, co-administered by the EI and co-certified by the PM's office, running for the remainder of her term.

Acquittal Is Not Vindication

The Senate's acquittal is final for removal purposes under both §3.5.c (the impeachment track used here) and §2.10.a (the independent 2/3 House legislative removal track, which the House did not pursue — choosing instead to let the §3.5 track run). §2.10.b's Senate referral to popular recall was also available but not invoked. The architecture provides three accountability paths; only one was chosen. It is not a finding that the violations did not occur — the Supreme Court has already determined they did. The corrective order remains in force. The NRS record remains permanent. The President serves her remaining eighteen months with every presidential communication with a potential domestic dimension routed through the EI-PM compliance process.

Her approval rating at the end of the trial: 44%. The NRS has made the entire record — the findings, the internal communications, the deferral period, the trial — accessible to every citizen throughout the proceeding. Whether voters at the next election read what it contains is a different question. The architecture has done what it was designed to do: make the record impossible to suppress and the accountability process impossible to avoid.

Part Five — Five Years Later: The Lookback

President Voss leaves office at term end. Five years later, a Supreme Court challenge brought by a citizen group produces new evidence: during the maritime operations whose command she retained through the §2.7.b deferral, the President authorized intelligence activities directed at domestic political opponents — framed as foreign-influence monitoring, targeting Republic citizens in violation of §2.1.e. The Privacy Firewall. The evidence was not available at the time of the original trial.

§2.7.a — Former Official Accountability
The lookback applies. The conduct occurred during federal service. The finding issues.

§2.7.a is explicit: a Supreme Court finding of a constitutional violation for conduct committed while a person held federal office applies regardless of whether the person remains in office when the finding issues. Where the person has left office, the suspension and Senate trial provisions do not apply — there is no office to suspend from. Instead, the finding triggers automatically: a permanent bar on holding any federal elected or appointed office, and permanent forfeiture of any federal pension or benefit accrued during the period of the violation.

The lookback applies to conduct within five years of the finding. The maritime operation intelligence activities occurred three years before the new finding. Former President Voss is permanently barred from federal office and forfeits her presidential pension for the period of the violation. Criminal prosecution remains separately available under applicable statutes of limitation. The NRS records the new finding alongside the complete prior record. The constitutional accountability system has reached across five years to close the gap the deferral created.

What This Scenario Tests

The Rule Violation architecture is designed for a specific type of actor: capable, popular, and constitutionally negligent — someone who will not correct a blind spot without structured accountability. But it is also designed for the harder case: a popular President whose accountability process coincides with active military operations, where immediate suspension creates genuine operational risk.

§2.7.b's deferral is not a loophole. It is a specific, bounded, time-limited accommodation for an irreducible tension between accountability and operational continuity — and it requires the Senate Armed Services Committee to certify that the accommodation is actually necessary. The 30-day hard ceiling ensures the accommodation cannot become indefinite. The parallel readiness of the Senate Speaker ensures the succession line is operational throughout.

And §2.7.a ensures that the deferral cannot become an exit — that conduct which occurs during the deferral period, or which is discovered after departure from office, remains within the constitutional accountability framework for five years. The architecture does not guarantee that every violation will be discovered in time for the full consequences to apply. It guarantees that the constitutional record will pursue former officials, and that the NRS will make that pursuit public.
