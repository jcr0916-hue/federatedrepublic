The Weimar Scenario

November 1932. The Weimar Republic has days before Hindenburg appoints Hitler as Chancellor. The Nazi and Communist parties together hold a Reichstag majority. Article 48 has been invoked 109 times. The constitution is being amended to death. The Federated Republic is ratified instead — and runs for fifteen years against the exact conditions that destroyed its predecessor.

Historical Scenario — November 1932

This is a historical scenario, not a contemporary transition. It posits ratification of the Federated Republic in November 1932 — at the last constitutional moment before the Weimar Republic's destruction. The economic conditions, the political actors, the paramilitary violence, and the institutional decay of 1932 Germany are not altered. What changes is the constitutional architecture. The scenario asks: could a document specifically designed against Weimar's failure modes have held?

The answer is not inevitable. Architecture constrains — it does not determine. What this scenario traces is which specific provisions bite, when, and against whom.

The Weimar scenario is the foundational stress test for this document — not because it is the most dramatic to run, though it is, but because this constitution was designed against Weimar's failure modes. Every constitutional lawyer who has worked on post-authoritarian design has Weimar as a primary reference. The Basic Law was written against Weimar. This document was written against the same failure modes, through the Basic Law's lens, and further. Running the Federated Republic against Weimar itself closes the intellectual circle.

What Failed — Weimar's Specific Vulnerabilities

Article 48 — The Constitutional Suicide Mechanism

The President could rule by decree in a state of emergency, bypassing the Reichstag entirely. Between 1930 and 1932, Article 48 was invoked 109 times. Emergency governance became the normal mode of administration. When Hitler was appointed Chancellor in January 1933, the constitutional machinery for unilateral emergency rule was fully normalized. The exception had consumed the rule.

No Constructive Vote of No Confidence

Governments could be voted out without parliament simultaneously electing a replacement. Between 1919 and 1933, the Weimar Republic had twenty cabinets averaging less than nine months each. The anti-democratic parties understood this perfectly: permanent governmental instability was not a side effect of their opposition — it was the mechanism by which they made democratic governance appear impossible.

Article 76 — Amendment by Simple Supermajority of Members Present

Combined with the physical exclusion of Communist deputies from the March 1933 session and the SA's intimidation of Social Democratic members, this provision allowed the Enabling Act to pass with constitutional form. The constitution was amended to death in a single afternoon.

No Court with Jurisdiction Over Constitutionality

The Staatsgerichtshof's jurisdiction covered disputes between Reich and Länder — not review of legislation. There was no institution authorized to declare Article 48's systematic abuse unconstitutional. The courts applied the law as written. The law as written included Article 48.

The Context of Ratification — November 1932

The November 1932 elections have produced a Reichstag in which the Nazi and Communist parties together hold a majority — two parties dedicated to the destruction of the democratic republic, commanding more seats than all pro-democratic parties combined. The Papen government is ruling by Article 48 decree. Hindenburg is under sustained pressure to appoint Hitler as Chancellor. The SA controls the streets of major cities during election periods.

The ratification convention meets in Weimar — a gesture toward the Republic's founding that is also a frank acknowledgment of its failure. The ratification referendum passes at 54.1% on 81.3% participation. The Nazi and Communist parties boycott it. The 54.1% affirmative is thin. It is enough. The Federated Republic begins operating on a Monday morning in November 1932, against conditions it was built to resist.

Year 1 — The Architecture Meets Its First Tests

The Emergency Power Question

Within the first month, the new President faces pressure from several State governments to declare a national emergency. The economic situation is genuine crisis: unemployment at 30%, bank runs continuing, political violence in the streets of Hamburg and Berlin. The pressure to govern by decree is real.

§1.25 — Emergency Derogation
Joint declaration. Seven days. Two-thirds of both chambers. The President cannot act alone.

The critical difference from Article 48: §1.25.a requires a joint declaration from the President and Prime Minister. That declaration then requires ratification by a two-thirds vote of both chambers within seven days — or it lapses automatically. No emergency may exceed 90 days without a renewed two-thirds majority. The President's advisors draft an emergency declaration three times in the first eight months. Each time, the PM's refusal to co-sign stops it at the threshold. The Article 48 pattern cannot happen because no single actor holds Article 48's authority.

The democratic PM governs through statute, not decree. It is slower. The National Record System records every bill, every vote, every deliberation. The messiness is public — and this matters. The Weimar Republic's instability was partly hidden in the opacity of decree governance. Constitutional slowness, published on the NRS, is a different political signal than authoritarian efficiency claimed in secret.

The Nazi-Communist Majority — Constitutionally Inert

In February of Year 1, the NSDAP and KPD coordinate a no-confidence motion against the PM. Their combined Reichstag delegation exceeds any democratic majority. Under the Weimar constitution, this would have been constitutionally decisive. Under §2.3.b, it is constitutionally inert.

§2.3.b — The Constructive Vote

The House may remove the Prime Minister only by simultaneously electing a successor by absolute majority — a majority of the full seated membership, not merely those present. The NSDAP and KPD together have the votes to oppose the current PM. They do not have the votes to agree on a successor. Their coalition dissolves the moment they must name a replacement: the Communists will not legitimize a Nazi chancellor, the Nazis will not accept a Communist one, and neither will accept any democratic candidate.

The no-confidence motion fails constitutionally before it reaches the floor. The democratic PM governs. What the anti-democratic majority cannot do is what Weimar's architects never anticipated needing protection against: a combined majority that can destroy but cannot build is, under §2.3.b, a constitutional nullity for the purpose of government removal.

Year 2 — The Amendment Attempts

Having failed to remove the government through the confidence mechanism, the NSDAP shifts strategy. In early Year 2, the party introduces three constitutional amendment proposals targeting the provisions it finds most obstructive.

Amendment Attempt 1 — Emergency Powers Expansion

The proposal: remove the PM co-signature requirement from §1.25.a, allowing the President to declare emergencies unilaterally. The barrier: §15.4.a requires a 2/3 majority of both chambers plus ratification by 3/4 of State legislatures. The democratic coalition holds 54% of House seats. The amendment never achieves the threshold for introduction. It is recorded on the NRS as proposed and failed.

Amendment Attempt 2 — Habeas Corpus Suspension

The proposal: allow emergency declarations to suspend habeas corpus. The barrier: §15.3 — permanently unamendable provisions. The prohibition on torture (§1.2), the prohibition on slavery (§1.3), the right against arbitrary detention and habeas corpus (§1.13), the right to a public trial (§1.17), and the prohibition of retroactive laws (§1.18) cannot be amended by any supermajority under any conditions. The Supreme Court — whose justices were nominated through the Triad process and elected by national direct vote — rules the proposed amendment void before it reaches the States. A proposed amendment to a permanently unamendable provision is not a constitutional amendment. It is a null act.

§15.3 — Permanent Unamendability
Five rights that cannot be voted away. Not by a supermajority. Not by unanimous consent. Not ever.

The Enabling Act of March 1933 passed with constitutional form because the Weimar constitution had no provision it could not amend. Article 76 required a supermajority of members present — physical exclusion and intimidation manufactured that majority. The Federated Republic's answer is not a higher supermajority. It is a category of rights that no supermajority can touch. The courts enforce this. No parliamentary arithmetic changes the answer.

Amendment Attempt 3 — Electoral Postponement

The most revealing attempt: a proposal allowing the executive to postpone scheduled elections during a declared national emergency. The framers anticipated this specific mechanism precisely because it is how democracies historically end — not with a coup, but with an election deferred indefinitely. §1.25.e is unambiguous: no emergency declaration may postpone, suspend, cancel, or alter the schedule of any election. Electoral processes continue during any declared emergency without modification. Any order purporting to alter electoral schedules during a declared emergency is void, automatically, with no further proceeding required. The Supreme Court rules the proposal outside the amendment power entirely — it targets the electoral mechanism in a way that would hollow out every other constitutional provision, and the Court finds this falls within the constitution's protected structural core regardless of §15.3's enumerated list.

Year 3 — The Street Violence Crisis and the Emergency Declaration

The political violence of 1932 Germany does not stop because the constitution changed. The SA continues operating. Political assassinations occur. The paramilitary apparatus is real and dangerous. This is the stress test the emergency powers provisions were most immediately designed for.

§1.25 — What Emergency Powers Can and Cannot Do

In Year 3, following coordinated political violence in three States, the President and PM jointly declare the Republic's first national emergency. The declaration specifies everything §1.25.a requires: the nature of the emergency; the specific rights being limited (freedom of assembly in identified high-risk zones); the duration (90 days); and the geographic scope (the three affected States only, explicitly not national).

Five rights are untouched regardless of the declaration: the prohibition on torture, the prohibition on forced labor, habeas corpus and the 24-hour first appearance right, the right to a public trial, and the prohibition of retroactive laws. The SA cannot be mass-detained without individual judicial authorization. Every arrest is on the NRS within 24 hours. The emergency provides enforcement tools — not a blank check for political imprisonment.

§1.25.c operates throughout: the three Inspectorates continue with full constitutional authority. The Executive Inspectorate publishes weekly reports on emergency measure use — 847 individual enforcement actions documented in the 90-day period, each with judicial authorization attached. At Day 88, the joint renewal assessment is conducted. The two-thirds majority renews for a second 90-day period. There is no mechanism to extend indefinitely. Each renewal requires fresh democratic authorization from the same threshold that authorized the first.

Year 5 — The First Electoral Test

National elections in Year 5 produce a shifted House. The democratic coalition's share has grown modestly — constitutional stability, NRS-documented governance performance, and the NSDAP's visible constitutional failures have moved opinion in marginal States. The NSDAP remains the largest single party. It cannot form a government without partners who, having watched three years of constitutional operation, are unwilling to provide them.

The Electoral Record

The NRS's role in this outcome is not incidental. Every NSDAP constitutional violation attempt is recorded permanently. Every failed amendment proposal. Every no-confidence motion that failed the constructive test. Every enforcement action under the emergency declaration, with judicial authorization documented alongside it. The constitutional record is not propaganda — it is documentation. Voters in Year 5 are voting with access to a complete constitutional history of every actor in the system.

This is not the same as voters making wise choices. The NRS guarantees that ignorance of the constitutional record cannot be manufactured by anyone willing to suppress information. The Weimar Republic's collapse was partly enabled by the opacity of its crisis governance. The Federated Republic's crisis governance is on the record — for better and worse, in full.

Year 7 — The Rule Violation Finding

In Year 7, the President — pushing the boundaries of Tier 1 defensive authority to justify domestic intelligence gathering on political opponents, framed as foreign-influence monitoring — faces a Supreme Court Rule Violation finding under §2.1.e. The Privacy Firewall provision is explicit: any order for domestic surveillance directed at Republic citizens is a Rule Violation, triggering immediate suspension. The Court rules 6-3. The finding is published to the NRS.

§2.7 — Automatic Consequences

Suspension from duties, access revocation, Senate trial scheduled — all automatic upon the Court's finding. No further vote required. No executive cooperation required. No political deliberation about whether to act. The suspended President does not continue in office while allies calculate the cost of accountability. §2.7 removes that discretion by design.

The Senate convicts by a 2/3 majority. The President is removed. The Senate Speaker assumes the succession role pending a special election within 180 days under §2.6.a. The accountability mechanism has operated against the head of state. The NRS records every step. The precedent is set: the constitutional order removes those who violate it, regardless of their office or their popularity.

Year 12 — Fifteen Years in Context

By Year 12, the NSDAP has transformed — not into a democratic party, but into a constitutional actor. Unable to amend the provisions it found most obstructing, unable to manufacture governmental instability through destructive no-confidence votes, unable to use emergency declarations as a path to permanent authority, and watching constitutional accountability remove officials who overreach, the party's internal calculation has shifted. It competes within the constitutional framework because the alternative has proven constitutionally expensive and publicly documented.

This is not the triumph of liberalism. The NSDAP remains dangerous. Anti-democratic sentiment has not evaporated. Economic conditions have improved but not recovered. What has changed is the structural landscape: the constitutional architecture has made the specific path to power that worked in January 1933 constitutionally unavailable. Emergency rule by presidential decree — blocked at §1.25.a's joint declaration threshold. Legislative majority used to amend the constitution to death — blocked by §15.3's permanent provisions and the supermajority requirement. Governmental instability manufactured by destructive opposition — blocked by §2.3.b's constructive requirement. Electoral postponement — void under §1.25.e before any court even reviews it.

The Fifteen-Year Verdict

The Federated Republic in 1932 Germany does not produce a liberal utopia. It produces a constitutional democracy operating under severe stress, with documented violation attempts, documented accountability proceedings, documented emergency declarations used proportionally, and a National Record System that makes every actor's constitutional record permanently visible to every voter.

What it eliminates: the specific constitutional suicide mechanism that Weimar contained. The Enabling Act of March 1933 required Article 48's normalization, Article 76's single-session amendment process, the absence of judicial review, and the absence of any constructive vote requirement. The Federated Republic closes all four gaps simultaneously. The anti-democratic majority that existed in November 1932 retains its political power. It loses its constitutional path to total authority.

The scenario does not argue that constitutional design is sufficient. It argues that constitutional design is necessary — that the specific failure modes the Weimar Republic contained were not inevitable features of democracy but correctable design choices. Fifteen years against Weimar's conditions is evidence for that proposition. The proof requires the next fifteen years, and the sustained democratic will that no constitutional document can manufacture but that a well-designed one can make structurally easier to sustain.
