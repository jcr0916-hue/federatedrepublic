The JEC Table

Year 3. A Pacific trade agreement requires domestic implementation provisions the PM argues are within her domain. The President disagrees. The Joint Executive Council meets. No consensus. The SC rules in 30 days.

§2.14 Joint Executive Council §2.4 International Trade Authority §2.1 General Domain Default §2.1 Presidential Domain §2.2 PM Domestic Domain

The Republic's dual executive divides authority along a principled line: the President leads in foreign affairs and defense; the PM governs domestic affairs. The division is clean in theory and contested in practice wherever foreign commitments produce domestic obligations — which is to say, in almost every significant trade agreement ever negotiated.

The Pacific Compact was a trade agreement three years in the making. Its headline provisions — tariff schedules, market access terms, intellectual property frameworks — were unambiguously Presidential in character. The President had negotiated them under her §2.1 treaty authority, and the Senate had ratified the agreement's external terms by 2/3 majority.

The contested provisions were three clauses buried in Annex VII. Each required the Republic to enact domestic legislation: labour inspection standards for goods entering under the agreement's reduced tariff regime, a pharmaceutical data exclusivity period that departed from the Republic's current statutory scheme, and a digital services liability framework that affected the NRS Panel's operational independence. The PM argued all three required her written concurrence under §2.4. The President argued they were implementation details fully within her treaty authority.

The JEC Session — §2.14

The Joint Executive Council convened within five days of the dispute being formally lodged. The Council — the President's domain officers and the PM's Cabinet ministers meeting jointly — is the constitutional first forum for cross-domain disputes under §2.1. The session lasted eleven hours across two days.

§2.14 — The Council's Role
The JEC coordinates. It does not decide. Each executive retains full authority in their own domain.

The Council's purpose is coordination and the creation of a consultation record — not resolution by institutional vote. The President's Trade Minister and the PM's Finance Minister identified the pharmaceutical exclusivity clause as the clearest case: it required new domestic legislation departing from existing statute. That is exactly the category of provision for which §2.4 requires PM concurrence.

The labour inspection clause was less clear. The President's position was that it specified standards for goods entering the Republic under the agreement — a border measure falling within her trade authority. The PM's position was that enforcement required domestic regulatory infrastructure under her domain. No consensus on either characterisation was reached at the JEC level.

The digital services clause produced the most tension. The NRS Panel's operational independence is constitutionally guaranteed. A liability framework affecting that independence touched both the PM's domestic governance domain and the President's external treaty commitments simultaneously. The JEC published its session summary to the NRS within 14 days. The summary documented the disagreement without resolving it.

The SC Petition — §2.1

Having failed to reach JEC consensus on the labour inspection and digital services clauses, the President petitioned the SC under §2.1 for a domain determination. The PM's residual authority is presumptive pending the ruling — §2.1's default rule — which meant the contested implementation legislation could not proceed on Presidential authority alone while the petition was pending.

§2.1 — The Default Rule
Presidential authority is enumerated. PM authority is residual. Where domain is disputed, PM authority governs pending SC ruling.

The default rule is not a judgment on the merits. It is a constitutional traffic signal for the period between dispute and resolution. The President accepted it as designed — her position was that the SC would rule in her favour, not that the default rule was illegitimate. The ruling would come within 30 days.

The SC's ruling, delivered on Day 28, split the three clauses:

The pharmaceutical exclusivity clause: PM concurrence required. The clause requires new domestic legislation departing from existing statute — squarely within the §2.4 test. The President transmitted it to the House Speaker as ordinary legislation under §2.4's severability provision. Senate ratification of the remaining agreement proceeded independently.

The labour inspection clause: Presidential authority. Border entry standards for goods entering under a bilateral trade agreement are implementation of external market access terms, not domestic regulatory infrastructure. The domestic enforcement mechanism would be ordinary statute, but the standard itself was a treaty commitment.

The digital services clause: joint authority. Both executives have legitimate constitutional interests. The SC held that implementation of this clause requires both a PM-led legislative process for the domestic liability framework and Presidential treaty compliance certification. The two executives must act jointly — neither can act without the other's constitutional participation.

What This Scenario Tests

§2.4's narrow concurrence requirement, §2.14's JEC coordination function, §2.1's default rule, and the SC's 30-day domain determination process all operate in sequence. The scenario demonstrates that the dual executive's domain division is not a binary switch — it is a set of constitutional principles that produce different answers for different types of cross-domain obligations.

The JEC's failure to reach consensus is not a system failure. It is the system identifying the limits of coordination and escalating appropriately. The JEC's published record becomes the documentary foundation of the SC's analysis. The 30-day timeline ensures that trade agreement implementation is not delayed indefinitely while the domain question is resolved.

The joint authority outcome for the digital services clause is the most novel finding. The SC has created a new constitutional category — provisions that require both executives' constitutional participation — that neither §2.1 nor §2.2 explicitly anticipates. This is constitutional interpretation at the boundary of the dual executive's design.
