The Trade Concurrence

The President concludes a trade framework with three Pacific partners. Two of its implementation annexes require PM concurrence under §2.4. The PM withholds on one. How the severability provision works.

§2.4 International Trade Authority §2.1 Presidential Domain §2.2 PM Domestic Domain §2.14 Joint Executive Council §3.6 Treaty Ratification

The Pacific Framework Agreement was the Republic's first major multilateral trade instrument. Eighteen months of negotiation, 340 pages of text, eleven annexes. The President had led the negotiation under her §2.1 treaty authority. The agreement's external terms — tariff schedules, market access provisions, intellectual property frameworks, customs procedures — were unambiguously within the Presidential domain. The Senate ratified the external terms by 2/3 majority without significant controversy.

The implementation annexes were a different matter. Under §2.4, PM written concurrence is required for three specific categories of trade agreement provision: provisions imposing national treatment obligations within Republic territory; provisions requiring new domestic legislation to implement; and provisions creating direct obligations on domestic persons or entities. The agreement's external terms were Presidential alone. But three implementation annexes contained provisions that fell squarely within one or more of the §2.4 categories.

Annex IX and the Concurrence Request — §2.4

The President transmitted the three contested annexes to the PM with a formal concurrence request under §2.4. The JEC had flagged all three during pre-ratification review. Two of the annexes were uncontroversial: the customs infrastructure standards annex required new domestic regulations but fell within existing administrative frameworks, and the PM provided written concurrence within seven days. The pharmaceutical data exclusivity annex required new domestic legislation and the PM provided concurrence subject to a statutory framework that the House would need to pass within 12 months.

Annex IX was different. It created a digital services liability framework that — in the PM's constitutional assessment — affected the operational independence of the NRS Panel under §11.x. The liability standards as drafted would have required the NRS Panel to assess and potentially disclose certain categories of system records in response to foreign arbitration proceedings. The PM argued that the provision created obligations on domestic persons (the NRS Panel's administrators) and required domestic legislative action in a domain where the constitutional independence of the institution was explicitly protected.

§2.4 — The Withholding and Its Consequences
Where PM concurrence is withheld on a specific provision, that provision may be transmitted to the House Speaker as ordinary legislation. The remaining agreement terms proceed to Senate ratification independently.

The PM withheld concurrence on Annex IX. She published a written explanation to the NRS within 48 hours stating her constitutional grounds. The President disagreed with the analysis — she argued Annex IX's liability standards fell within her treaty implementation authority, not within domestic regulatory territory. The disagreement was genuine and substantive.

Under §2.4's severability provision, the President exercised her option to transmit Annex IX to the House Speaker as proposed domestic legislation, while the remainder of the Pacific Framework Agreement — including Annexes I through VIII and X through XI — proceeded to full Senate ratification. The framework was ratified. Annex IX was pending as a House bill.

The JEC Review — §2.14

The JEC convened a session on the Annex IX bill during its regular coordination cycle. The President's trade officers and the PM's Cabinet ministers reviewed three alternative drafting approaches that could achieve the trade agreement's objectives without the NRS Panel independence implications the PM had identified. The session produced a jointly agreed redraft that was transmitted to the House as an amendment to the pending bill.

The redrafted Annex IX passed the House and Senate with minimal debate. The President incorporated the agreed text into the official trade agreement record. The Republic notified its trading partners of the domestic implementation under the agreement's implementation timeline provisions. All eleven annexes were ultimately enacted, the last one taking slightly longer than the first ten.

What This Scenario Tests

§2.4's narrow concurrence requirement is designed for exactly this situation: the PM's legitimate interest in provisions that affect domestic regulatory architecture, balanced against the President's legitimate interest in implementing treaty commitments she negotiated. The key design feature is severability — the PM's withholding of concurrence on one provision cannot block the entire agreement. The agreement proceeds; the contested provision is handled through the domestic legislative process.

The JEC's role in resolving the Annex IX redraft demonstrates §2.14's coordination function working as intended. The executives disagreed, but their officers were able to develop a solution that satisfied both executives' constitutional interests. The coordination record was permanent and publicly visible on the NRS. The outcome — a slightly modified provision that achieved the same trade objectives without the NRS Panel implications — was better constitutional law than either the original draft or a forced override would have produced.

The distinction between the President's sole authority for external trade terms and the PM's concurrence authority for domestic implementation provisions is the constitutional line that this scenario tests in practice. Tariff schedules and market access terms require no PM involvement. Provisions that reach inside the Republic's institutional architecture and impose obligations on domestic bodies require it.
