States & Territories · §19.6 · §3.14 · §15.7 · §16.1 · Founding Sovereignty

The Prior Claim

Year 9. Halvern refuses to integrate its highway system with the federal network. Its legal argument is the most ambitious a State has ever made to the SC: it entered the Republic as a sovereign, it never gave up that sovereignty, and it should hold the same compact-level relationship as indigenous nations. Chief Justice Diallo writes for a unanimous Court.

§19.6 Transition Territories §3.14 Federal Legislative Competence §15.7 State Immutability §16.1 Indigenous Sovereignty Recognition §16.2 The Compact §4.5 Judicial Review

Halvern had been a semi-autonomous nation for three hundred years before the Republic. It had its own currency, its own diplomatic relationships with two neighboring territories, and the most extensive highway network in what would become the Republic's northern region. When the Republic was constituted, Halvern chose to enter as a founding State under what would become §19.6 — recognized directly, Senate seats allocated from Day Zero, no Territory period, no Provisional status, no Statehood Audit. It brought its infrastructure with it.

The Federal Infrastructure Alignment Act of Year 8 required all State highway systems to integrate with the Republic's National Transportation Network — a federal system for coordinating logistics, emergency routing, and infrastructure maintenance across jurisdictional lines. Integration meant two things: technical compatibility (State systems would share data with the federal network) and administrative coordination (State transportation departments would participate in joint federal-State management protocols). Halvern's legislature passed a resolution declaring the Act unconstitutional and refusing implementation. The Governor's statement was precise: "Halvern did not become a State by surrendering sovereignty. We brought our sovereignty with us."

The First Argument — Founding Sovereignty

Halvern's Position
§19.6 recognition is not constitutional subordination. A State that was never a Territory never accepted the Republic's framework as the source of its authority.

Halvern's Attorney General argued before the SC that the founding State distinction was constitutionally meaningful. States that went through the Territory and Provisional pathways explicitly accepted the Republic's framework as the source of their Statehood. They were assessed, found qualified, and admitted. Their Statehood is contingent in origin — contingent on the Republic's constitutional processes.

Halvern's Statehood was not contingent. It was recognized. §19.6 says founding States "do so as full States from Day Zero for the purpose of constituting the Senate." The recognition was for a specific purpose — Senate constitution — not for the unlimited purpose of transforming Halvern's pre-existing sovereign authority into constitutional authority held at the Republic's pleasure. Halvern's sovereignty predated the Republic. §19.6 recognized that sovereignty; it did not replace it with a constitutionally-derived grant that the Republic could withdraw or override through ordinary legislation.

The FIAA, under this argument, was a federal directive that Halvern's sovereign infrastructure answer to federal management. Halvern had never agreed to that. Its Senate vote in favor of Republic membership was not a sovereign waiver of all pre-existing authority over its own territory.

The Second Argument — Indigenous Parity

Halvern's Alternative Position
Article XVI recognizes pre-Republic sovereign entities as holding compact-level relationships with the federal government. Halvern is a pre-Republic sovereign entity.

The alternative argument was elegant in structure. Article XVI of the constitution explicitly recognizes indigenous nations as sovereign entities with a distinct, compact-based relationship to the Republic. The premise of that recognition is that these nations existed as sovereigns before the Republic and their sovereignty was not extinguished by the Republic's formation — it was explicitly preserved through the compact framework. Federal law applies to indigenous compact territories only to the extent the compact specifies.

Halvern also existed as a sovereign before the Republic. If the constitution's architecture is to make sense, the Attorney General argued, the category of "pre-Republic sovereign entity" must be applied consistently. Either pre-Republic sovereignty matters constitutionally — in which case Halvern should hold a relationship analogous to an indigenous compact — or it doesn't matter, in which case the entire rationale for Article XVI's special recognition collapses.

Halvern was not claiming to be an indigenous nation. It was claiming that the constitutional principle underlying Article XVI — that pre-existing sovereignty is preserved, not extinguished, by the Republic's framework — applied equally to it. The Republic's constitution was silent on this point for founding States. Silence, the Attorney General argued, was not prohibition. It was an implied reservation of what the constitution did not explicitly grant.

The Republic's Response

The Republic's Solicitor General, appearing before the SC on behalf of the LC and CC, offered a simple framing: Halvern's arguments were sophisticated, historically informed, and constitutionally wrong at every step. The SC should address them fully, because they would be raised again if not disposed of clearly.

On the founding sovereignty argument: §19.6 recognition has no content beyond what the provision says. It recognizes founding States "for the purpose of constituting the Senate and seating their legislators." It then immediately states that the annual Statehood Audit applies to all States from the first full cycle — "no founding status confers exemption from ongoing audit obligations or the consequences of audit failure." The provision that Halvern cited as the source of its retained sovereignty also explicitly denies founding States any special constitutional treatment going forward. If §19.6 had intended to reserve founding sovereignty, it would not have stated — in the same provision — that founding States face the same audit obligations as everyone else.

On the indigenous parity argument: the Solicitor General invited the SC to look carefully at the structure of Article XVI before accepting the analogy. The compact relationship is not a principle derived from pre-existing sovereignty in the abstract. It is a specific set of constitutional commitments made to specific nations as part of the Republic's founding moment. Those commitments are named. The nations are recognized. The compact process is specified. Nothing in Article XVI suggests that its framework applies to any entity that happened to be sovereign before the Republic. It applies to the indigenous nations it explicitly recognizes — and to them through the compact process, not by implication.

The SC's Opinion

Republic v. Halvern  ·  Year 9  ·  SC Opinion  ·  Chief Justice Diallo, for a unanimous Court

We address two constitutional arguments that, while distinct in their framing, share a common premise: that a political entity's sovereignty prior to the Republic's formation creates constitutionally meaningful entitlements within the Republic's framework. We hold that the premise is incorrect as applied to founding States, and that both arguments fail on that basis.

On the first argument: §19.6 does not preserve pre-Republic sovereignty. It recognizes founding State status for the limited purpose of constituting the Senate. The provision is express about what founding State recognition accomplishes — Senate representation and immediate legislative seating — and equally express that no founding status creates exemption from ongoing constitutional obligations. We read provisions as written. A provision that specifies its effect does not carry additional unstated effects.

More fundamentally, the premise of the sovereignty argument misunderstands what joining the Republic means. Halvern did not submit to the Republic. It chose to join. That choice was not coerced. But it was a choice with constitutional consequences that Halvern accepted when it ratified. The Republic's constitution is not an external constraint imposed on Halvern's sovereignty — it is the framework Halvern chose to operate within. A party to a constitutional framework does not retain sovereignty over the questions the framework addresses. §3.14 is one of those questions. Federal legislative competence, including the power to legislate on infrastructure of national significance, is squarely within the constitution's grant. Halvern's pre-Republic highway authority does not override a federal statute enacted under that grant.

On the second argument: the indigenous compact relationship is not a principle derived from pre-existing sovereignty in the abstract. It is a set of specific constitutional commitments to specific nations, made constitutive of the Republic itself. Article XVI's recognition is not a general proposition that pre-Republic sovereignty is preserved by default. It is a particular arrangement, specified in the constitutional text, applying to recognized nations through a defined process. We decline to extend that arrangement by implication to founding States. The constitution's silence on founding State sovereignty is not an implied reservation. It is the absence of a grant. What the constitution does not grant, the constitution does not give.

We note with care that this holding does not diminish the historical significance of Halvern's pre-Republic sovereignty, the legitimacy of its founding State status, or the genuine constitutional interests that States hold in their own governance. Those interests are protected — by §15.7's territorial integrity guarantee, by the Senate structure, and by §3.14's own limits on federal authority. What those protections do not include is immunity from federal legislation enacted within the Legislature's constitutional competence.

Republic v. Halvern · Year 9 · SC 2 · Chief Justice Diallo, for a unanimous Court. Affirmed in part, modified in part.

The Partial Win

The SC did not give the Republic everything it asked for. Having disposed of Halvern's sovereignty arguments, the Court turned to §3.14's anti-commandeering limit: the Legislature holds general authority to legislate for the peace, order, and good governance of the Republic, "but may not direct State institutions as instruments of federal administration."

The FIAA's technical integration requirement — data sharing, system compatibility, network access — was constitutional. The Republic could require Halvern's highway system to be technically connected to the national network. What it could not do was require Halvern's State transportation department to serve as the administrative organ of a federal system. The administrative coordination protocols in the FIAA — joint management boards, State participation in federal operational decisions, State employees performing federal network administration — crossed the anti-commandeering line. Halvern's State employees could not be directed to perform federal administrative functions.

The FIAA was modified. Technical integration stood. Administrative coordination was severed. The federal network would manage itself federally; Halvern's infrastructure would be technically compatible but administratively independent. The Republic got the network it needed. Halvern retained the administrative separation it had always had. Neither side fully won; the constitution drew the line between them.

What the Case Established

Republic v. Halvern settled three questions that had been open since the founding.

First: founding State recognition under §19.6 is entry into the constitutional framework, not reservation from it. Statehood is constitutionally defined regardless of how a State arrived at that status. The annual audit, federal supremacy, and the Legislature's general competence apply equally to founding States and to States that graduated from Territory status.

Second: Article XVI's compact relationship is specific to the nations it names and the process it specifies. It is not a general constitutional principle that any pre-Republic sovereign entity can invoke. The distinction between preserved sovereignty (indigenous nations, explicitly recognized) and exchanged sovereignty (founding States, who chose constitutional membership) is constitutionally fundamental.

Third: §3.14's anti-commandeering limit has real force. Federal technical standards are constitutional. Federal administrative conscription of State agencies is not. The Republic can require States to meet standards; it cannot require States to administer federal programs. The line between those two things is the line the constitution draws.
