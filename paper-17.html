Paper No. 17

The Infrastructure of Trust

On the Digital Infrastructure Commission — why the Republic's two most critical public systems share governance, why the pool mechanism is more transparent than confirmation alone, and why operational independence is not administrative detail but constitutional architecture.

Two systems make everything else in this constitution real. The National Record System is the mechanism by which government actions become permanently visible — the medium through which democratic accountability is technically enforced. The National Voting System is the mechanism through which citizens exercise the only power that matters in a democracy: the power to choose. Neither system can afford to be captured. Neither can afford to be corrupted. And neither, as it turns out, can afford to be governed separately from the other.

The original design separated them. The Electoral Commission — a five-member elected body — administered the NVS and all elections. The RSA Director — a statutory officer under Legislative Inspectorate oversight — administered the NRS. The separation was intuitive: elections administration requires one kind of expertise and independence, record-keeping requires another. Keeping them apart seemed to prevent dangerous concentrations of control over both systems.

The intuition was wrong in one important respect. Both systems require the same foundational competencies: cryptographic integrity, distributed custody, public auditability, tamper-evidence, resilience under degraded conditions. Governing them separately meant governing them to potentially different standards, reviewing them through potentially different processes, and maintaining two separate institutions where one with appropriate internal separation would serve better. The case for combining their governance is not about efficiency. It is about consistency of standards across the two systems that democracy most depends on.

The Architecture of the Commission

The Digital Infrastructure Commission§16.1 is governed by one elected Director and two panels of three administrators each. The Director chairs the Commission, votes on Commission-wide decisions, and sets technical standards and architecture policy across both systems. The NRS Panel operates the National Record System. The Elections Panel operates the National Voting System and administers all federal elections and referenda.

The design has two load-bearing features that require explanation: the election of the Director, and the operational independence of the panels.

The Director is elected by national ranked-choice vote to a single non-renewable eight-year term. The election serves several functions. It gives the Commission democratic legitimacy that does not flow from any political appointment — the Director answers to the electorate, not to the executives who might nominate them or the Senate that might confirm them. It creates accountability for the standards-setting function: a Director who allows both systems' cryptographic standards to degrade, or who tolerates access barriers in the NVS, faces electoral consequences. And it separates the Commission from the appointment politics that would otherwise surround any body governing the Republic's most sensitive infrastructure.

The election also reflects a principle articulated elsewhere in this document:§10 Principle III an independent body with real discretionary power over citizens' lives needs democratic legitimacy that does not flow from the political branches it is independent of. The Director's discretion — setting architecture policy, making final selections from the pool, casting deciding votes on deadlocked panels — is real. An elected mandate is the right source of legitimacy for it.

The Firewall Within

The most important provision in Article XVI may be §16.1.e: the operational independence of the panels. The NRS Panel has sole operational authority over the technical infrastructure, security, and system availability of the NRS — but not over its content. What is published to the NRS is constitutionally determined. The Panel may not withhold, delay, or restrict any constitutionally required record. The Elections Panel has sole operational authority over the conduct of elections, the administration of the NVS, and the certification of all electoral results. The Director and the opposite panel may not issue instructions on these core functions.

These protections are absolute. They cannot be modified by Commission policy or Director directive. They are not aspirational statements about the Commission's culture. They are constitutional prohibitions.

The reason is straightforward but worth stating. The NRS records election results certified by the Elections Panel. If the same director who controls the NRS could also direct the Elections Panel on how to certify results, one person would control both what gets certified as the electoral outcome and what gets permanently recorded as the constitutional fact of that outcome. This is the most dangerous concentration of institutional power in a democratic system. The operational independence provisions exist to make it structurally impossible, not merely institutionally frowned upon.

The Director's role is technical leadership of the combined infrastructure — shared cryptographic standards, consistent architectural requirements, the Commission's budget and institutional decisions. The Director votes on these. The Director cannot reach into either panel's operational core. A Director who attempts to is acting unconstitutionally regardless of whether the panel complies.

The Pool and What It Accomplishes

Panel administrators are appointed through the pool mechanism.§16.1.c Each executive maintains their own pool of nominees. They may nominate as many qualified people as they wish at any time. The JI reviews each nominee and publishes a qualification report to the NRS within thirty days. Pool membership is established by JI certification — no Senate confirmation is required to enter the pool. When a panel vacancy arises, each executive selects one from their pool for that panel's qualification track; the Director chooses between the two; the Senate confirms the Director's selection.

The mechanism looks complex. Its value is more visible when contrasted with what it replaces: a body where all members were directly elected. The argument for election is democratic legitimacy. The argument against it, for this specific body, is that panel administrators are technical experts whose value lies in their expertise rather than their electoral appeal. An election campaign for an NRS Panel administrator seat would reward communication skills and name recognition rather than cryptographic competence. The pool mechanism achieves accountability through a different chain: executive nominations are public (JI reports published to the NRS); citizens can assess whether their executive is nominating qualified people or political allies; senators can be held accountable for the confirmation vote; the Director can be held accountable for the selection. Every actor's decision is visible.

The JI's qualification report is the mechanism that makes this work. It is not a pass/fail determination — it is a structured assessment published permanently to the NRS, covering years of relevant experience, nature of the expertise, prior roles that might create conflicts of interest, and a conclusion on whether the nominee meets the constitutional qualification standard. Citizens can read these reports. Journalists do. The quality of each executive's pool is a matter of public record.

This is a stronger accountability mechanism than election for this role. Election accountability is binary and periodic — you hold officials accountable at the ballot box every several years. Pool accountability is continuous and specific — you can see every nomination, every JI assessment, every Director selection, every Senate vote. The granularity of accountability is higher, not lower, even though the administrators are not directly elected.

The Advisory Council

One independent review mechanism sits outside the Commission entirely: the Digital Infrastructure Advisory Council.§9.3 Every ten years, States nominate qualified technical experts to an Advisory Pool. The JI certifies qualifications and publishes reports. Five members are drawn by public lottery from the certified pool. They review both systems' architecture for compliance with constitutional requirements under §9.1 and §16.3, publish findings as recommendations, and appear before the Senate in a mandatory public session.

The Advisory Council is external to the Commission — its members are not Commission employees or nominees. The Commission must respond to each recommendation within 90 days, accepting, modifying, or declining with published reasons. The recommendations are not binding. The response obligation is.

The State nomination model was deliberately chosen over executive nomination. The executives already nominate to the panel appointment pools; giving them the Advisory Council nominations too would extend executive influence over a body meant to provide external review of a Commission the executives already help constitute. States have a direct stake in both systems — their laws are published to the NRS, their federal elections run through the NVS — without the institutional conflict that executive nomination would create. The Senate's mandatory public session on the findings completes the accountability chain: States nominate, citizens see the qualifications, the Council reviews independently, the Senate interrogates the findings on the States' behalf.

Why This Is the Right Design

The most fundamental question about any institutional design is: what failure mode does it prevent, and what failure mode does it risk? The DIC prevents the failure mode of fragmented governance standards across the two systems that democracy most depends on. It prevents the failure mode of an elected administrator whose re-election incentives conflict with the technical integrity of the systems. It prevents — through the operational independence provisions — the failure mode of concentrated control over both the records of government action and the administration of the elections through which citizens hold government accountable.

The failure mode it risks is the one that all combined governance structures risk: that the Director uses their position to build institutional influence over the panels in ways the operational independence provisions cannot fully prevent. The constitutional prohibitions are clear. The EI oversight of the Director is real. The Advisory Council provides external review. But institutional culture matters, and a Director who treats their Commission colleagues as subordinates rather than independent authorities would strain a design built on the assumption that the independence provisions will be respected.

The constitution cannot fully insure against that failure mode. What it does is make the failure mode visible. A Director who attempts to direct either panel's operational decisions acts unconstitutionally in public — every communication from the Director to a panel administrator about operational decisions is a potential constitutional breach that the EI can certify and the NRS records permanently. The transparency of the NRS, which this institution exists to protect, is also the transparency that constrains the institution's own actors.

That is not a coincidence. It is the design.
