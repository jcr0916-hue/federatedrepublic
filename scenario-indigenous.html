Scenario · §16.7 · Indigenous Nation Status Elections

The Four Elections

Year 0–4. Four elections that shape the Republic's relationship with its indigenous compact nations — and reveal what §16.7's menu of status options actually means in practice.

§16.7 §16.7.a §16.5 §20.1 §15.6 §2.14

The notification arrives at the same time for all of them — a formal document in each nation's primary language, published simultaneously to the National Record System on Day 37 of Phase 1, describing three options in plain language and attaching the relevant constitutional text. No Republic official accompanies the notification. No government minister presents the options in person. No federal liaison office exists to answer questions — only the published text of the constitution and the NRS address of the Judicial Monitor, which will receive formal election notices.

The constitution was explicit about what the notification must not include: any signal of preference, any assessment of which option is better, any implicit suggestion that one choice serves the Republic's interests more than another. The Republic sends the menu. The choice belongs to the nations.

The four nations who respond within Phase 1 are among dozens recognized under predecessor-government treaty relationships. Most take no action during Phase 1 — the Article XVI compact is the default, requiring no election to remain in force, and many nations need time to assess their options. Four do not wait.

The Menu and the Four Responses

The Miran League
Option (a) — Article XVI Compact · No Election Required
Deliberate affirmation of the existing compact after reviewing all alternatives. Published to the NRS on Day 89 of Phase 1.
The Kessai Peoples
Option (c) — Full Sovereign Independence · §16.7 · First step toward Associated Community
Independence election filed Day 134 of Phase 1. Sovereignty treaty with the Republic negotiated over 18 months. Associated Community compact negotiated separately in Year 3 once sovereignty is established.
The Ataro Confederacy
Option (b) — Territorial Integration · §16.7 → §15.6
Direct path from Article XVI compact to territorial integration — no intermediate sovereign status required. Integration agreement negotiated over 8 months with co-management covenant for ancestral lands.
The Venuwa Nation
Option (c) — Full Sovereign Independence · §16.7 · Landlocked
Independence election filed Day 203 of Phase 1. Treaty negotiations led by the Legat Consul over 22 months. Ratified by the Senate under §3.6.

The Miran League — A Deliberate Affirmation

The Miran League's governance council issues a formal statement on Day 89 of Phase 1: it has reviewed the §16.7 options, assessed each against its current circumstances, and elected to remain in the Article XVI compact relationship. The statement is published to the NRS. It is not an election — no election is required to maintain the default. It is something else: a deliberate, recorded affirmation of the compact after having genuinely considered the alternatives.

The Miran League's council notes that the FPIC protections, the land rights, and the government-to-government relationship of Article XVI represent more constitutional protection than any of the other options provide, and that the right to change status under §16.7 means the compact relationship is now a daily choice rather than a historical inheritance. A status chosen is different from a status inherited — even when the status is identical in content.

The Kessai Peoples — Independence First, Then a New Compact

The Kessai Peoples have maintained parallel governance structures for three generations — a traditional council for internal affairs and a representative assembly for external relations. They want a compact relationship with the Republic that treats them explicitly as equal sovereigns. But the constitution is clear on this point: Associated Community status under Article XX is available to independent entities entering a voluntary compact with the Republic. A nation cannot transition directly from Article XVI compact status to Associated Community status — the Associated Community framework requires sovereignty as a precondition, not as a destination.

The Kessai external relations assembly votes 71–12 to elect full sovereign independence under §16.7. The election is published to the NRS on Day 134 of Phase 1. The Judicial Monitor certifies the election within 14 days.

Constitutional Sequence
Why Both Executives Negotiate, Not Just One

An independence treaty under §16.7 is not assigned to either executive alone. Negotiations run jointly through the Council of Ministers under §2.14, with the Legat Consul holding primary authority over border and defense terms under §2.1 and the Civic Consul holding primary authority over domestic implementation terms under §2.5. The split tracks what an independence treaty actually contains: it creates a new international border, which is the Legat Consul's domain, and it unwinds infrastructure, services, and citizenship arrangements that were, until the election, ordinary domestic administration — the Civic Consul's domain.

The Kessai treaty negotiations are conducted by the Council of Ministers — the Legat Consul's domain officers and the Civic Consul's ministers meeting jointly. The Legat Consul's team leads on the border delimitation, defense arrangements, and recognition terms; the Civic Consul's team leads on the survival of existing infrastructure agreements, the status of social state benefits Kessai members had been receiving, and the citizenship arrangements for Kessai members who also hold Republic citizenship. Each session's summary is published to the NRS under §2.14 — neither executive may classify the fact or general subject of a negotiating session.

The treaty is ratified by the Legislature — 2/3 of both chambers, the threshold §16.7 sets for independence treaties specifically, higher than the Senate-alone threshold §3.6 sets for ordinary treaties — in Month 18. The Kessai Nation is recognized as fully sovereign. Their territory exits the Republic's constitutional framework entirely at the moment of ratification.

In Year 3, from their position as an established sovereign nation, the Kessai external relations assembly opens Article XX compact negotiations with the Republic. The compact that emerges treats them as the equal sovereign they are: bilateral rather than asymmetric, with explicit recognition that the Kessai hold sovereignty that precedes the compact. Their prior land protections are incorporated as compact terms. This second instrument is a different kind of treaty than the first — an Associated Community compact ratified by the Senate alone under §20.6, consistent with the ordinary §3.6 threshold, because Article XX governs the Republic's relationship with an already-sovereign nation rather than the act of creating one. The Kessai Peoples are now an Associated Community — but the path required two separate treaty processes, eighteen months apart, with full sovereignty in between, and each ratified by a different body under a different threshold.

Constitutional Finding

The Kessai pathway establishes that reaching Associated Community status from Article XVI compact status is a two-stage process requiring actual independence as an intermediate condition — not a procedural formality but a substantive change in the nation's relationship with the Republic. The independence treaty and the Associated Community compact are separate instruments, ratified by different bodies under different thresholds: the Legislature by 2/3 of both chambers for independence under §16.7, the Senate alone by 2/3 for the Associated Community compact under §20.6.

The Ataro Confederacy — Direct Territorial Integration

The Ataro Confederacy's situation is specific. Their territory has been substantially integrated into the surrounding Republic infrastructure for two generations — roads, utilities, schools, health services — to the point where maintaining a distinct governance structure is more burden than benefit for their members. Their confederacy council votes 84–31 to pursue territorial integration.

The territorial integration pathway does not require an intermediate sovereign status. Unlike the Associated Community framework — which is explicitly for independent entities choosing a closer relationship — territorial integration under §15.6 is a constitutional pathway from any status into the Republic's territorial structure. The Ataro petition directly under §15.6 from their Article XVI compact position, without the intermediate step of electing independence.

The integration agreement — which takes eight months to negotiate — contains specific provisions: the Ataro's ancestral lands within the Territory enter under a permanent co-management covenant; prior treaty rights are statutory obligations of the Republic; the confederacy council is recognized as the Territory's governance council during the Provisional Membership Period. The Ataro Confederacy is the first indigenous nation to use this direct integration pathway. Their territory enters the Territory framework in Month 14.

In Year 2, a federal social state infrastructure project proposes routing a water supply line through an area the Ataro covenant designates as ceremonial. The Civic Consul's office, reading the co-management covenant correctly, reroutes the project. No legal proceeding was required. The NRS record of the rerouting decision cites the integration agreement. The covenant functioned exactly as designed: not as a barrier to federal infrastructure, but as a requirement that federal infrastructure respect the terms of the integration that brought the Ataro into the Republic's political structure.

The Venuwa Nation — Landlocked Sovereignty

The Venuwa Nation's territory is 4,200 square kilometers of river valley, entirely surrounded by Republic territory on all sides. No Venuwa land touches an international border. The nearest foreign sovereign is 340 kilometers from the Venuwa's eastern boundary. The Venuwa governance council — a traditional assembly of forty-three elected elders — votes 38–5 to elect full sovereign independence under §16.7(d). The election is published to the NRS on Day 203 of Phase 1. The Republic has 24 months to negotiate a treaty.

The Legat Consul's office receives the Venuwa election notice and immediately faces a constitutional question with no precedent: how do you negotiate a sovereignty treaty with a nation that has no access to international territory, no port, no external border, and no existing trade relationship with any foreign sovereign? The constitutional text is clear — the Republic must negotiate in good faith; the geographic position does not affect the right; the practical arrangements are for the treaty to address.

Constitutional Sequence
The Same Joint Process, A Harder Negotiation

As with Kessai, the Venuwa treaty runs through the Council of Ministers under §2.14 — the Legat Consul's team on border and defense terms under §2.1, the Civic Consul's team on domestic implementation under §2.5. What makes Venuwa harder is not who negotiates but what there is to negotiate: a border delimitation team accustomed to coastlines and existing crossings has no template for a landlocked nation with no port and no prior international relationship of its own.

The Legat Consul's domain officers lead on the question with no precedent: how do you write transit rights for a nation that has never needed any, surrounded entirely by the territory of the government it is separating from? The Civic Consul's team, meanwhile, works the domestic side — the utilities, the social state benefits, the local administrative functions that have run through Republic systems for generations and now need either a clean handoff or a service agreement. The constitutional text is clear on the substantive right even where it specifies no procedure: the Republic must negotiate in good faith; the geographic position does not affect the right; the practical arrangements are for the treaty to address.

The treaty framework that emerges after 22 months of negotiation addresses six areas, split across the two domains. On the Legat Consul's side: (1) Transit corridors — the Republic grants the Venuwa permanent, irrevocable transit rights through designated Republic territory to any border of the Republic they designate; the corridors are published to the NRS as registered federal use rights and may not be revoked without Venuwa consent. (2) Trade — a customs and trade agreement treating the Venuwa Nation as a foreign sovereign for all trade purposes. (5) Diplomatic recognition — the Republic formally recognizes Venuwa sovereignty and commits to supporting the nation's application for recognition by international bodies. On the Civic Consul's side: (3) Citizenship — Venuwa members who hold Republic citizenship retain it; dual citizenship is constitutionally protected. (4) Infrastructure — existing Republic utilities within Venuwa territory continue under service agreements at rates equivalent to Republic citizens. Negotiated jointly by both teams: (6) Dispute resolution — the Supreme Court retains jurisdiction over disputes arising from the treaty by the express agreement of both parties.

The treaty is ratified by the Legislature — 2/3 of both chambers, the threshold §16.7 sets for independence treaties — in Month 22 of Phase 1. The Venuwa Nation formally exits all Republic constitutional frameworks at the moment of ratification. The NRS records the moment with a timestamp. The JM publishes a final Article XVI compact termination notice.

The Venuwa Nation's transit corridors are published to the NRS as permanent registered federal use rights. Their significance is not logistical — in the first year of independence, the corridor is used primarily for diplomatic travel and trade. Its significance is constitutional: the existence of the corridor is the proof that the right to independence was real, not merely formal. A sovereignty that cannot access the world is not sovereignty.

Year Three — What §16.7 Actually Produced

The Miran League's deliberate affirmation of the Article XVI compact has been cited in twelve subsequent legal proceedings involving Article XVI rights. The argument: the League's published review and reaffirmation establishes that the compact relationship is a continuous democratic choice, not an inherited constraint. The same FPIC protections apply regardless, but the Miran League's statement has changed the political framing — the compact is now described in public discourse as something nations choose rather than something they are assigned.

The Kessai Peoples' two-stage path has produced an important precedent: the sovereignty treaty and the Associated Community compact are separate instruments with separate ratification votes under §3.6. Nations considering Associated Community status from within Article XVI compact relationships must plan for a multi-year process with full independence as an intermediate stage — not a shortcut, but a genuine constitutional transition with a genuine interim period.

The Ataro Confederacy's direct integration pathway has been used by two subsequent nations. The co-management covenant model — specifying that ancestral lands within the Territory retain indigenous governance for internal resource decisions — has become a standard integration agreement term, incorporated by reference in later negotiations.

The Venuwa Nation's independence election is the constitutional stress test the provision was designed to survive. The landlocked geography that would have made independence practically impossible under any prior framework was addressed by the transit corridor requirement. The constitutional text was precise: geographic position does not affect the right. The Council of Ministers — the Legat Consul's team on the border question no one had faced before, the Civic Consul's team on unwinding generations of domestic administration — made that precision real.

What This Scenario Tests

§16.7's core design question is whether a constitutional menu of status options genuinely expands indigenous self-determination or merely adds process to outcomes that would have occurred anyway. The four elections suggest a genuine expansion — but the pathways are more complex than a simple menu implies.

The Kessai pathway establishes that Associated Community status is not a direct election from compact status. Independence must come first. The associated community relationship is available to sovereign nations choosing a closer relationship with the Republic, not to compact nations choosing a different kind of compact.

The Ataro pathway establishes that territorial integration does not require an independence intermediate step. The constitutional distinction matters: one framework (Associated Community) is for sovereigns entering the Republic's orbit; the other (territorial integration) is for compact nations choosing to join the Republic's structure.

The FPIC principle runs through all four elections. §16.7 prohibits the Republic from preferring or inducing any option. The Miran League's deliberate review and published reaffirmation is perhaps the purest expression of this: free, prior, and informed consent to remain in the relationship that was already in place. The Venuwa Nation's independence election demonstrates that the right was real because the obligation was enforceable — and because both executives negotiated in good faith through the Council of Ministers under constitutional compulsion, within the timeline, on terms the constitution required but could not specify.
