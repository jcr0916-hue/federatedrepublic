Military Authorization · Cessation · §2.2 · §12.1

The Default

Day 1. The President invokes immediate defensive authority following an attack on a treaty ally. Day 37. The Legislature has not acted. The constitution's answer is automatic.

§2.2 Military Authorization Framework §12.1 Authorized Purposes §12.2 Defense Authorization and Allocation §8.8 EI Annual Military Assessment

At 4:12 AM on Day 1, three Republic naval vessels were fired upon in international waters adjacent to the territory of Valdenmere, a treaty ally. President Asante-Owusu invoked immediate defensive authority under §2.2 within the hour. Notification to both chambers and the PM was transmitted at 5:47 AM — within the statutory notification period. Defensive operations began at 6:15 AM. The PM certified the initial defense allocation at 7:02 AM.

The authorized purpose — defense of a treaty ally within defined triggering thresholds — was stated in the NRS publication. The operation was within §12.1's first permitted purpose.

Days 1–37 — The Legislature Does Not Act

The Senate Armed Services Committee received the President's notification and convened within 24 hours. The Committee debated authorization for expanded operations — moving beyond immediate defense to sustained engagement in Valdenmere's territorial waters. The debate was inconclusive. A vote was scheduled, postponed, rescheduled. On Day 15, the House began parallel deliberations on whether a full legislative authorization was warranted.

On Day 37, neither chamber had passed a legislative authorization for sustained operations. Under §2.2, the burden was always on authorization, never on termination. The constitutional default on expiry was cessation of all operations except immediate defense within Republic and treaty-ally territorial waters.

§2.2 — Cessation Is the Constitutional Default
Legislative authorization expires unless affirmatively renewed. The burden is always on authorization, never on termination. Operations cease automatically on expiry.

The President issued a cessation order on Day 37. The PM's certification of expenditure was simultaneously rescinded for all non-defensive operations. The EI was notified. The NRS publication of the cessation order noted the basis: absence of legislative authorization beyond the immediate defensive period defined by statute.

The President did not characterize the cessation as a defeat. In a brief statement, she described it as the constitution operating as designed: the Legislature had not authorized an expansion of operations, and in the Republic's constitutional framework, the absence of legislative authorization means operations do not continue.

The Legislative Authorization — Day 52

Fifteen days after cessation, the House passed a full legislative authorization for sustained operations in Valdenmere's territorial waters, stating the authorized purpose — defense of a treaty ally — and specifying a ninety-day authorization period with a renewal mechanism. The Senate concurred the same day. The President issued a new deployment order within twelve hours. PM certification of the renewed defense allocation followed within four hours.

§12.1 — Authorization Must State Its Purpose
A Tier 3 Declaration must state which authorized purpose it serves. Declarations that cannot be linked to an authorized purpose are constitutionally void.

The House's authorization was explicit: defense of a treaty ally, not nation-building, not resource extraction, not ideological imposition. The authorization's stated purpose was the constitutional anchor for all subsequent operations. When the EI's annual military assessment reviewed the operation, it had a documented purpose statement against which to assess whether operations remained within scope.

What This Scenario Tests

§2.2's most important constitutional feature is not the authorization mechanism — it is the default. Military operations do not continue because no one has acted to stop them. They cease because no one has acted to authorize continuation. The fifteen-day gap between Day 37 and Day 52 is not a constitutional failure; it is the constitution working. The Legislature took fifteen additional days to deliberate and then authorized sustained operations. Those fifteen days of deliberation — without military operations providing political pressure — produced a more considered authorization than the political dynamics of ongoing operations might have permitted.

The PM certification function reinforces the default: no certification means no expenditure. The President could not fund operations through alternative mechanisms while waiting for legislative authorization. The financial architecture and the authorization architecture are aligned: both default to cessation.
