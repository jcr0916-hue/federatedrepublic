States & Territories · §15.2 · §15.3 · Mandatory Devolution

The Third Strike

Year 13. Corindal fails its Statehood Audit for the third consecutive year. There is no automatic devolution. Instead, citizens vote on two paths: a federal remediation commission or Provisional status. The constitution gives Corindal's own voters the decision.

§15.2 The Statehood Audit §15.3 Mandatory Devolution §9.1.a Legislative Monitor §15.4 Voluntary Devolution §4.5 Judicial Review

Corindal had been on Statehood Watch since Year 11. That year's audit found two failures: electoral district maps that did not meet the LM's compactness criteria, and a ballot certification process running outside Elections Panel technical standards. Year 12 resolved one failure and confirmed the other. Two consecutive findings of failure were published to the NRS, each accompanied by an Early Warning specifying the remaining compliance requirements and a deadline for Corindal's Remediation Plan.

Year 13's audit team spent six weeks in Corindal. The certification software in three of Corindal's fourteen districts remained outside NVS technical standards — the same finding as Year 12. On publication morning, the legislature passed emergency legislation mandating NVS-compliant software statewide. The LM reviewed the Act and published its Year 13 finding anyway: the audit assesses conditions during the audit period, not prospective legislative intent. Third consecutive failure.

What Three Failures Actually Trigger

§15.3 — Not Automatic Devolution
Three failures require a citizen vote. The constitution gives the decision to Corindal's voters — not to a federal administrative mechanism.

The common assumption — that three audit failures automatically convert a State to Territory status — is wrong. §15.3 requires something more deliberate: after three consecutive failures, the Elections Panel administers a citizen vote presenting Corindal's eligible voters with two paths. The first path is a two-year federal remediation commission — federal oversight of the specific compliance failures, with the State retaining its status throughout. The second path is Provisional status — a constitutional holding position that continues the audit cycle while the State works toward restoration.

Neither path is devolution. Devolution requires its own subsequent process. The constitution does not remove Statehood from citizens without their participation in the decision.

The SC Challenge

§15.3 — 30-Day Petition Window
Corindal petitions the SC within 30 days. The Court reviews only one question: are the audit findings factually sufficient?

Corindal's Governor filed an SC petition on Day 14. The petition argued that the Electoral Integrity Restoration Act — passed on publication morning — demonstrated Corindal's genuine willingness to cure, and that the LM's refusal to treat prospective legislation as a cure was an error of constitutional interpretation, not merely a factual finding.

The SC's jurisdiction under §15.3 is narrow: it reviews only whether the stated grounds are factually sufficient to establish three consecutive failures. It does not review the LM's interpretation of what constitutes a cure, the adequacy of the Remediation Plans, or the wisdom of the audit criteria. The Court ruled in 11 days: the Year 13 finding was factually sufficient. The certification software non-compliance existed during the audit period. Three consecutive failures were established. The citizen vote would proceed.

Elections Panel · Mandatory Vote Notice · Year 13 · Corindal

Pursuant to §15.3, the Elections Panel will administer a mandatory citizen vote in the State of Corindal within the period defined by statute. Corindal's eligible voters will be presented with two paths: (A) a two-year federal remediation commission addressing the LM's certified compliance failures, with full State status maintained throughout; or (B) Provisional status, retaining existing governing structure and continuing under the annual Statehood Audit cycle. The result of this vote does not constitute devolution. A subsequent process under §15.3 or §15.4 is required for any change in constitutional status.

The Vote and What Follows

§15.3 — Citizens Choose Provisional Status
54% of Corindal voters choose Provisional status over the federal remediation commission.

The vote ran for two days. Turnout reached 68%. The remediation commission option carried significant opposition — the prospect of two years of federal oversight over State election administration was unpopular across party lines. Provisional status won with 54%.

Corindal entered Provisional status. Its existing governing structure continued. The Statehood Audit cycle continued. Sitting senators completed their current terms; no new Senate elections would be held during Provisional status. Assembly members continued. Article I rights and social state protections continued without interruption. Constitutionally, Corindal looked almost exactly as it had the day before — except that its path back to full Statehood now ran through a clean audit rather than through ordinary political continuity.

§15.3 — Year 14: Clean Audit
Corindal passes the Year 14 audit. Full Statehood restored immediately. No additional vote required.

The Electoral Integrity Restoration Act, passed in haste on Year 13's publication morning, had been fully implemented by Year 14's audit cycle. The LM certified both components clean. Under §15.3, a State in Provisional status that passes the Statehood Audit has its full Statehood restored immediately — no citizen vote, no Senate ratification, no additional process. The audit passing is the restoration trigger, just as it is on the initial pathway from Territory to State.

Corindal's Senate seats returned to full electoral status. New Senate elections were held at the next federal electoral period. The constitutional system had done precisely what it was designed to do: pressure toward compliance, citizen participation in the critical decision, and an automatic restoration once the underlying failure was resolved.

The Design Choice

The Mandatory Devolution mechanism reflects a specific constitutional philosophy: the Republic does not unilaterally remove Statehood from citizens. Three consecutive failures create consequences — a forced citizen vote, Provisional status, and ultimately a path toward devolution if the State cannot recover. But each step requires either citizen participation or a passed audit. There is no point at which federal administrative action alone changes a State's constitutional status against its citizens' wishes.

If Corindal had chosen the remediation commission instead of Provisional status, the commission would have operated for two years under federal oversight. If Provisional status had continued for a year without a passed audit, citizens would have voted again — this time on the principle of pursuing devolution under §15.4. An affirmative vote would have opened negotiation and Senate ratification. A negative vote would have continued Provisional status. At every stage, Corindal's voters remained in the decision.
