The Expanded Court

Year 6. The Legislature passes a statute adding two SC seats — the first time 2/3 of both chambers have agreed to expand the court. The PM nominates both within 60 days. One seat is confirmed; the other expires unfilled at 18 months. The opposition attempts reversal under the same 2/3 threshold.

§4.3 SC Composition and Size §4.2.d PM Nomination and Senate Confirmation §3.2 Senate Powers

The court expansion debate began in Year 5, when a 6-3 SC majority issued three consecutive rulings narrowing the scope of the social state provisions. The rulings were constitutionally reasoned and technically defensible. They were also, to the governing coalition, politically inconvenient. The PM's coalition held narrow House and Senate majorities — enough to govern, not enough to expand the court. Under §4.3, adding SC seats requires statute passed by 2/3 of both chambers. The coalition's first expansion bill, introduced in Year 5, failed at 58% — short of the 2/3 threshold in both chambers. The governing coalition spent the following year building a broader argument: not that the rulings were wrong, but that a court of nine generating consistent 6-3 splits on foundational social state questions warranted structural review. By Year 6, a negotiated coalition of governing and cross-bench members produced the required supermajority.

The statute passed the House with 73% of full seated membership and the Senate with exactly 2/3. Under §4.3, adding SC seats requires statute by 2/3 of both chambers — the same threshold required to reduce seats. The design is deliberately symmetric: neither expansion nor reduction is easier than the other. Both require genuine cross-coalition consensus that cannot be achieved on coalition votes alone. The theory is that a court whose size changes should reflect broad constitutional agreement, not the transient advantage of a governing majority.

The Two New Seats — §4.3

§4.3 — Expansion Mechanics
New seats are assigned to the class with the fewest members. Must be filled within 18 months or expire.

Two new seats added to a nine-justice court. The smallest classes at the time of expansion both had three members. The JI drew public lots to assign one seat to Class B and one to Class C. The PM had 60 days from enactment to nominate for each seat under §4.2.d. Both nominations were submitted on Day 58.

The first nominee, Judge Ryo Hashimoto, was confirmed by the Senate 28-14 on Day 112. The second nominee, Judge Celestine Mwamba, had a published record that drew more sustained Senate scrutiny. Her confirmation hearing stretched across six weeks. On Day 163 of the 18-month window, the Senate voted 26-16 — two votes short of the 2/3 required.

The PM re-nominated within 30 days as required. The third attempt for the second seat — a different nominee, Judge Serrano Vives — was still pending confirmation on Day 540 of the 18-month window. Under §4.3, a new seat that is not filled within 18 months of enactment expires by operation of law. On Day 548, the JI published a notice to the NRS: the second new seat had expired unfilled. The court sat at 10 justices — one expansion, one expiry.

The Reversal Attempt — §4.3 Reduction

The opposition, having won the midterm elections in Year 7, now held a Senate majority but not a supermajority. They introduced a statute to reduce the court from 10 back to 9 seats — reversing the expansion entirely.

§4.3 — Reduction Mechanics
Reduction requires 2/3 of both chambers. Takes effect through natural attrition only. No sitting justice may be removed.

The opposition's statute passed the Senate with exactly 2/3 of full seated membership — the minimum required. It passed the House with 2/3 as well, in a remarkable multi-coalition vote that reflected how the court expansion had changed the politics. The statute designated the Class B expansion seat for reduction by attrition — the next vacancy in that class would not be filled.

Judge Hashimoto, the confirmed expansion justice, was in Year 2 of a 12-year non-renewable term. He would not voluntarily step down; there were no grounds for removal. The reduction statute was constitutionally valid and the designation was constitutionally proper — but the court would remain at 10 until Hashimoto's term expired, resigned, or until death or removal created the vacancy. That was a maximum of 10 more years away.

The court sat at 10 justices. With an even number, §4.3's tie rule applied: where the court is equally divided, the challenged ruling of the lower court or government body stands. In practice, the court rarely split evenly on the high-stakes cases — justices do not vote as predictable blocs. But on three occasions in the following four years, cases produced a 5-5 split. Each time, the lower court ruling stood.

What This Scenario Tests

§4.3's symmetric design is its central feature: expansion and reduction require identical thresholds — 2/3 of both chambers. Neither side can unilaterally change the court's size. A government that builds the broad consensus to expand has demonstrated that their concern is broadly shared. An opposition that builds the broad consensus to reverse has demonstrated that the reversal is similarly supported. The design prevents court size from becoming a tool of transient majorities without making structural change impossible.

The 18-month expiry rule functions as designed: a seat authorised by a supermajority but never filled cannot remain as a perpetual vacancy. The expiry closes that path. The PM had 18 months across three nominees to fill the second seat. She failed — the Senate's 2/3 threshold for confirmation proved as difficult to clear as the threshold for the expansion statute itself.

The tie rule's application to an even-numbered court is mild rather than destabilising. The court is not paralysed — it handles hundreds of cases, most of which produce clear majorities. The handful of 5-5 ties produce the constitutional default: the existing ruling stands. That is not nothing. It means the SC cannot overturn a lower court ruling on a tied vote — which is, arguably, the right outcome when the Republic's highest court cannot reach majority agreement.
