Constitutional Amendment · Non-Derogable Rights · §17.3 · Entrenchment

The Void Exception

Year 18. The Republic's fourth constitutional amendment is procedurally valid — 2/3 supermajority, state ratification, every threshold met. It also contains a 90-day habeas carve-out for security detentions. The SC applies the amendment and voids only the carve-out.

§17.3 Amendment Consistency and Limitations§1.11 Habeas Corpus§1.19.a Non-Derogable Rights§17.1 Amendment Paths

The Public Safety Consolidation Amendment was ratified in Year 18, becoming the Republic's fourth constitutional amendment. It passed both chambers by 2/3 supermajority and was ratified by 2/3 of States within the statutory window. Its stated purpose was to give the executive stronger tools during declared national security emergencies: the power to detain persons of interest for up to 90 days, with judicial review limited to the narrow question of whether the detention fell within the declared emergency's scope.

The amendment's drafters had anticipated constitutional challenges. They had included explicit language: "during a declared national security emergency, habeas corpus proceedings under §1.11 are subject to the extended judicial review timeline established by this amendment." The 72-hour production ceiling would yield to 90 days for security detentions. The amendment was procedurally valid in every respect.

§17.3 — The Unamendable Core
A procedurally valid amendment can still be void to the extent of extinguishment.

§17.3 is explicit: no constitutional amendment may extinguish a right designated non-derogable under §1.19.a. Any amendment purporting to do so is void to the extent of the extinguishment. Habeas corpus is on the §1.19.a list. Its protection — the right to be produced before a court within the constitutional period — is non-derogable precisely because its value derives from holding under the worst circumstances.

Fifteen civil liberties organizations and four State governments petitioned the SC within days of the amendment's ratification. The SC accepted the case immediately under its jurisdiction over constitutional challenges to amendments under §17.3.

The SC's Ruling — Partial Validity
The amendment survives. The habeas exception does not.

The Court's ruling was precise. §17.3 does not permit the SC to refuse to apply an amendment that has met its procedural thresholds. The SC would not strike the Public Safety Consolidation Amendment as a whole. The executive detention authority was valid and in effect. The emergency coordination provisions were valid. The modified judicial review framework — except for one element — was valid.

The exception: the 90-day timeline for producing detained persons before a court. To that extent, and only to that extent, the amendment was void. §1.11's habeas corpus protection, as a non-derogable right, could not be extinguished by amendment. Detained persons must be produced before a court within the constitutional period, regardless of the emergency's declared scope. The amendment's habeas exception was severed and declared void. The rest stood.

The amendment's sponsors called the ruling a judicial overreach. The constitutional scholars noted the SC had done exactly what §17.3 required: it had applied the amendment, recognized its validity in full, and excised only the specific text that purported to extinguish a non-derogable right. The Republic's first application of the entrenchment clause had produced a partial void — the most precise constitutional surgery the court had performed in eighteen years.
