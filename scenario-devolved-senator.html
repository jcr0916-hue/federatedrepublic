The Devolved Senator

Year 5. Korda State fails its third consecutive Statehood Audit. Devolution is voted. Its two senators keep their seats under §15.3 Path A. The constitutional tension of senators representing a Territory.

§15.3 Mandatory Devolution §15.2 The Statehood Audit §15.5 Re-Qualification Pathway §3.2 Senate Powers

Korda State's failure was slow, documented, and unsurprising by the time it became constitutional. Three consecutive annual Statehood Audits had found the same combination of failures: electoral processes that did not meet the functional threshold required by §15.2.e and Article I rights compliance gaps in three specific areas that the LI had cited in each of three successive Early Warning publications. Korda's government had published remediation plans after each warning. Two of the plans had been credible on paper. None had produced the required changes.

The third audit failure triggered the State referendum required by §15.3. Korda's citizens voted 58% to 42% in favour of proceeding to devolution rather than accepting a two-year federal remediation commission. The preference for devolution over federal oversight was itself a notable constitutional outcome — citizens choosing to accept Territory status rather than invite federal management of their domestic affairs. The NRS published the result alongside the full audit findings. Korda State became Korda Territory.

Path A — §15.3

§15.3 — The Path A Consequence
Sitting senators serve the remainder of their current elected terms. Senate seats are not vacated. No new Senate elections may be held until Statehood is restored.

Korda had two senators at the time of devolution: Senator Adaeze Obi, three years into a six-year term, and Senator Mikael Lindqvist, five years into a six-year term. Both kept their seats. Neither was required to resign. Neither faced a recall procedure, a constitutional challenge, or any mechanism for removal. They were senators of the Federated Republic, elected by Korda citizens, and they would serve until their terms ended.

This is the design choice at the heart of Path A. The alternative — immediate Senate seat vacation — would have stripped representation from Korda citizens who had done nothing to cause their State's failure. Those citizens had voted in a good-faith Senate election, sent two senators to represent them, and were now to be told their representation was voided by their government's constitutional failures. Path A rejects that outcome. The senators serve. The citizens retain their voice.

The constitutional tension surfaces immediately: Obi and Lindqvist are senators of a Territory. They vote on legislation, confirm judicial nominees, ratify treaties, and participate in every Senate function as full members — representing a jurisdiction that no longer holds State constitutional status. When the Senate debates statehood policy for existing Territories, they participate. When the Senate votes on federal appropriations for Territory administration, they vote for provisions that directly affect their own constituents' legal status.

The Practical Questions

Three specific situations illustrate the tension during Lindqvist's final year of service and Obi's three remaining years.

First: the Senate votes on a statute defining the minimum timeline for a Territory to complete the Statehood Audit and requalification pathway. Obi votes in favour of an amendment that would shorten the minimum timeline — directly beneficial to Korda Territory's restoration prospects. The vote passes 24-18. No constitutional provision prohibits Obi's participation in this vote. No conflict-of-interest rule under §12.4 applies to legislative votes on policy matters. The vote stands.

Second: the Senate considers a motion to establish a federal oversight commission for Territories that have failed three consecutive audits. Korda would be the first subject of such a commission. Both Obi and Lindqvist vote against. The motion fails 22-20. Whether two senators representing the directly affected jurisdiction should have been permitted to vote is debated in the press and among constitutional scholars. The constitution provides no procedure for their recusal on this question.

§15.3 — The No-New-Elections Rule
No new Senate elections may be held for the devolved State until Statehood is restored.

When Lindqvist's term ends in Year 6, Korda Territory has no Senate election. The seat goes vacant. When Obi's term ends in Year 8, the second seat goes vacant. For however long it takes Korda to complete the audit, the Provisional Membership Period, and return to Statehood, it holds no Senate seats. The devolution's impact on Senate representation is delayed, gradual, and ultimately complete — not through immediate disenfranchisement but through term expiry.

The Restoration — §15.5

Korda Territory completes its remediation under direct federal administration, passes the Statehood Audit in Year 9, and completes the Provisional Membership Period in Year 10. Restoration to Statehood is automatic under §15.3 — no vote required, no additional hurdle. On the day the LI certifies audit passage and the Provisional Membership Period ends, Korda is a State. Senate elections are held on the next National Election Day. New senators, representing a State that understands exactly what failure costs, take their seats.

What This Scenario Tests

§15.3 Path A is a deliberate constitutional choice to protect voter mandates from the consequences of government failures. The tension it creates — senators representing a Territory with direct interests in policy affecting that Territory — is the price of that protection. The constitution does not resolve that tension with a recusal mechanism because doing so would create a different democratic problem: senators who cannot fully represent their constituents.

The scenario demonstrates that the restoration pathway is the real constitutional mechanism. Territory status is temporary, not permanent. The audit and requalification pathway under §15.2 and §15.5 exists precisely for the situation Korda finds itself in — and the automatic restoration upon audit passage means that a Territory that fixes its failures returns to full Statehood without requiring a political grant of permission from the very legislature where its senators have been outvoted.
