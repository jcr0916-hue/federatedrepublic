The Capital Cases

Three constitutional questions on the right to life. Whether a specific method of execution violates §1.2. Whether federal abolition requires statute or amendment. Whether a federal statute binds state criminal law — and what happens when it doesn't.

§1.1 Right to Life §1.2 Prohibition of Torture §1.25.b Non-Derogable Rights §15.3.a Unamendable Provisions §4.3.b Standing

In Year 19 the Legislature passed the Capital Sentencing Act by a margin of 53-47 in the House and 51-49 in the Senate — the narrowest possible majority in the upper chamber. The Act authorized capital punishment for three categories of offense: terrorism resulting in fifty or more deaths, genocide, and high treason committed during active armed conflict as defined under Article XII. The method of execution specified was a three-drug lethal injection protocol. The Act required a unanimous jury recommendation at sentencing and mandatory appellate review before any sentence could be carried out.

The Act was contested in its passage, deeply contested after it, and has never ceased being contested. It produced two separate lines of constitutional litigation over the following four years. Both eventually reached the Supreme Court. Together they define the constitutional landscape of the right to life in the Federated Republic.

Part IThe Protocol

Taras Venn was convicted in Year 21 of a terrorist attack that killed seventy-one people. The unanimous jury returned a death recommendation. The mandatory appellate review was completed in Year 22. His legal team filed a constitutional petition before the Supreme Court raising two distinct challenges: first, that capital punishment is not constitutionally permitted under §1.1 as currently written; second, that the three-drug lethal injection protocol specified in the Capital Sentencing Act constitutes cruel, degrading, or inhumane treatment prohibited by §1.2.

The §1.1 Question

SC 9–0 · §1.1 Does Not Bar Capital Punishment

§1.1 provides that the State may not deprive any person of life except as the lawful consequence of a sentence imposed by a court of competent jurisdiction following conviction. The provision is silent on what sentences may include. It says nothing about capital punishment — it neither authorizes nor prohibits it. The legislature has general authority over criminal sentencing. A capital sentence imposed by a court following conviction and mandatory review is a sentence imposed by a court of competent jurisdiction following conviction within the plain meaning of the provision. The petition's first challenge fails.

The Court notes what this holding does and does not establish. It establishes that capital punishment is constitutionally permissible if authorized by the legislature. It does not establish that any particular method is constitutionally permissible. That question is governed by §1.2 and is addressed separately.

The §1.2 Question

The petition's second challenge requires the Court to do something it could not have done under the prior constitution: actually examine the method. The prior text of §1.1 included an explicit provision that a lawfully imposed capital sentence does not constitute cruel, degrading, or inhumane punishment within the meaning of §1.2. That text is gone. The legislature's authorization of the protocol is not a defense under §1.2, which states in terms that no legislative act may authorize or excuse a violation.

The Court received substantial expert evidence on the three-drug protocol. The first drug is a sedative intended to render the subject unconscious. The second is a paralytic agent that stops voluntary muscle movement, including the ability to signal distress. The third is potassium chloride, which induces cardiac arrest. The documented concern is that if the sedative is improperly dosed or metabolizes faster than anticipated, the subject may regain or maintain consciousness during the second and third phases — fully aware of what is happening, and completely unable to communicate it. The paralytic agent prevents any visible signs of distress. From the outside, the procedure appears peaceful regardless of the subject's actual experience.

SC 6–3 · Three-Drug Protocol Violates §1.2

The constitutional standard under §1.2 is whether a practice constitutes cruel, degrading, or inhumane treatment. These are disjunctive categories — each is independently sufficient. The Court does not need to find that a practice reaches some absolute threshold of severity; it must find that it falls within one of the three prohibited categories on the evidence presented.

The three-drug protocol as specified and administered creates a documented and non-trivial probability that the subject experiences conscious cardiac arrest while paralyzed and unable to communicate. On the evidence before the Court, this constitutes cruel treatment. The legislature's authorization of the protocol does not change this analysis. §1.2 states explicitly that no legislative act may authorize or excuse a violation. The Court takes that language at its word.

The Court does not reach the question of whether any method of capital execution can satisfy §1.2. That question requires a specific method before the Court with specific evidence. The holding is limited to this protocol as implemented.

The three dissenting justices do not dispute the constitutional standard. They dispute the evidentiary threshold. The majority's "non-trivial probability" standard, the dissent argues, will in practice make every capital method challengeable, because no method can be proven to eliminate all probability of conscious suffering. The legislature's authorization — which reflects a democratic judgment about acceptable risk — should carry significant weight in assessing whether the §1.2 threshold has been crossed. The dissent predicts the majority has begun a line of reasoning with only one end.

Venn's sentence cannot be carried out under the current protocol. The government commutes it to life imprisonment rather than develop and litigate a new method. The Capital Sentencing Act remains on the books. No execution has been carried out under it.

Part IIThe Statute

The Protocol ruling produced an immediate legislative response. In Year 23 a coalition introduced the Capital Punishment Abolition Act. The bill's text was simple: capital punishment is prohibited for all offenses under federal law, effective upon enactment. It passed the House 58-42 and the Senate 54-46. The PM certified it. No referendum was required. No constitutional amendment was sought.

Statute or Amendment?

§1.1 — The Legislature's Discretion
Neither is required — and either is sufficient

The constitution does not require capital punishment. §1.1 permits the legislature to authorize it; it does not compel them to. A legislature that authorized capital punishment by statute can repeal that authorization by the same process — simple majority in both chambers. The Abolition Act does exactly this: it repeals the Capital Sentencing Act's authorization. The constitution is indifferent to the legislature's choice in either direction.

An amendment would be necessary only if the goal were to make the prohibition immune to a future legislative majority that wanted to restore capital punishment. A statutory prohibition, however durable it feels politically, can be repealed by the legislature that passed it or any subsequent one. If abolitionists wanted permanent constitutional entrenchment, they would need an amendment — but they did not seek one, and under §15.1 they are entitled to govern by statute if they have the votes.

A legal challenge to the Abolition Act on constitutional grounds was filed within weeks of enactment, arguing that by authorizing capital punishment in §1.1 the constitution had protected it from ordinary repeal. The Supreme Court dismissed this challenge in a single paragraph: §1.1 authorizes the legislature to permit capital punishment; it does not require it. A permissive constitutional provision does not freeze the legislative choice it permits.

Part IIIThe States

The drafters of the Abolition Act had anticipated the federalism problem and attempted to resolve it directly. Section 4 of the Act included a federal preemption clause: "No court of the Republic, federal or state or territorial, may impose a sentence of death, and no authority of the Republic, federal or state or territorial, may carry out an execution, for any offense under any law."

Kailos and Ostmark both had capital punishment statutes predating the Republic's ratification, carried forward into the Republic's legal order under the continuity provisions of Article XIX. Both States argued that the federal Abolition Act could not reach their own criminal statutes — that criminal sentencing within a State's domestic jurisdiction was a matter of State law, and that the federal legislature lacked constitutional authority to preempt it. Both States continued to authorize capital punishment under their own criminal codes and announced they would not treat the Abolition Act's preemption clause as binding on state courts.

The Constitutional Gap

The constitution does not explicitly allocate criminal justice authority between the federal government and the States. Article XIII establishes the federal-state relationship and the devolution framework. It says nothing about which level of government has authority over criminal sentencing. The Republic's courts had, in prior cases, treated criminal law as a concurrent power — both federal and state legislatures could create criminal offenses within their respective jurisdictions. The question of whether federal law could preempt state criminal sentencing had never been squarely addressed.

The case arrived at the Supreme Court from Kailos, where a defendant had been sentenced to death under the State's criminal code after the Abolition Act took effect. His legal team argued the sentence was void under the preemption clause. The State argued the clause was unconstitutional.

SC 5–4 · Federal Preemption Clause Upheld

§1.1 establishes the right to life as a constitutional guarantee binding on the State — meaning all state actors, including state governments and state courts. The federal legislature's authority to legislate in furtherance of constitutional rights is broad. The Abolition Act's preemption clause is a valid exercise of that authority: it operationalizes the constitutional right to life by specifying the outer limits of what any government within the Republic may do in the name of criminal sentencing.

The concurrent nature of criminal law does not insulate state sentencing authority from federal limitation. Concurrent means both levels can legislate; it does not mean federal law cannot set floors and ceilings within which state law operates. The Republic's constitutional structure places federal constitutional rights above state law, and the federal legislature may legislate to enforce those rights against all levels of government.

Section 4 of the Abolition Act is constitutional. The sentence imposed on the defendant in Kailos cannot be carried out.

The four dissenting justices write at length. The majority has used §1.1's silence to construct federal supremacy over state criminal sentencing in a domain the constitution did not address. The concurrent nature of criminal law is precisely the point: it means both levels can legislate, and the federal legislature cannot simply declare that its preference becomes the only valid law. The correct mechanism for making a constitutional prohibition universal was amendment — adding a provision to Article I that explicitly bars capital punishment at all levels. The legislature chose statute instead and cannot achieve constitutional supremacy through that route.

The dissent's closing paragraph becomes widely quoted: "When a constitutional question produces a five-to-four ruling, the constitution has not answered it. The legislature has answered it for now. The constitution will be asked again."

The §1.2 Trajectory

Two justices in the majority write separately. Their concurrence does not address the federalism question. It addresses the dissent's implicit suggestion that a future legislature could restore capital punishment and sidestep the Abolition Act. The concurrence notes that the Protocol ruling has a logic that does not stop at the three-drug protocol. The question of whether any method of capital execution can satisfy §1.2 — the question the Protocol ruling explicitly declined to answer — remains open. If a future case presents a method the Court finds crosses the §1.2 threshold, the constitutional prohibition applies regardless of what any legislature — federal or state — has authorized. §1.2 is non-derogable under §1.25.b and unamendable under §15.3.a. The legislature can restore capital punishment by statute; it cannot restore a method that the constitution prohibits.

The concurrence ends with a question it does not answer: whether there exists a method of ending a human life that can, on evidence, be shown not to constitute cruel, degrading, or inhumane treatment. That question has not been put to the Court. When it is, the Court will answer it.

What This Scenario Tests

The three parts of this scenario test the same constitutional choice from three different angles. The removal of the capital punishment carve-out from §1.1 did not prohibit capital punishment. It subjected it to the same constitutional framework that governs everything else. Part I shows what that means in practice: the legislature's authorization of a method is not a defense under §1.2. The Court must examine the method on evidence. The carve-out's function was to prevent that examination; its removal made the examination mandatory.

Part II shows that the choice between statute and amendment is a choice about durability, not about authority. The legislature has authority to permit or prohibit capital punishment either way. Statute is reversible by a future majority. Amendment is not. The Abolition Act's proponents chose statute — which means the question remains politically live in a way it would not if they had sought and won a constitutional amendment.

Part III exposes the constitution's most significant gap in this area: it does not explicitly allocate criminal justice authority between federal and state governments. The 5-4 ruling resolves the immediate case but does not resolve the underlying question, as the dissent correctly observes. A future court with a different majority might reach a different conclusion on the federalism question. The correct path to permanent, universal prohibition — if that is the goal — is a constitutional amendment explicitly placing the prohibition in Article I, where it would be non-derogable and unamendable. Everything short of that remains contestable.

The §1.2 trajectory, flagged in the Part I dissent and taken up in the Part III concurrence, is the scenario's most significant unresolved thread. The Protocol ruling began a line of reasoning about method. That reasoning has a potential endpoint: if the Court eventually holds that no method of execution can satisfy §1.2, the prohibition becomes constitutional rather than statutory — binding on all governments, immune to legislative reversal, impossible to amend away. Whether the Court will reach that holding depends on what methods are presented to it and on what evidence. The constitution did not answer that question. It left it to be answered.
