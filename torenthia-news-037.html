The Rhondel Ferry Timetable, Explained (Sort Of)

The lake ferry out of Rhondel changed its winter schedule last week. Depending on whom you ask, this was the decision of the port authority, the Kelvant transport office, or nobody. All three answered our questions. None of the answers matched.

Corwin Hale Editor · Rhondel Year 13, Month 7

I have spent the better part of a week trying to learn why the lake ferry out of Rhondel now leaves at 6:40 in the morning instead of 7:15, and I am writing this column to report that I have failed, and that the failure is more instructive than the answer would have been.

The ferry matters here in the way small things matter. Rhondel sits on Kelvant's eastern lake shore, the coast guard command is stationed here, and a good number of people cross the water for work in a way that thirty-five minutes either direction genuinely rearranges. When the schedule moved, it moved without much notice, and the notice that did appear was posted in three places that each said something slightly different. One said 6:40. One said 6:45. The third, still, says 7:15, because nobody has taken it down.

Three Offices, One Ferry

So I asked. The port authority told me, politely, that the sailing time is set in coordination with the Kelvant transport office and that I should really ask them. The Kelvant transport office told me, also politely, that they set the annual framework but that operational timing is the port authority's to adjust within it. When I noted that this meant each office had just told me to ask the other, the transport office allowed that this was "a known area of overlap" and suggested the coast guard schedule might also be a factor, the lake being what it is in winter.

"Nobody is hiding anything. It is genuinely just that three people can each be a little bit in charge of the same ferry, and a little bit in charge is how a 7:15 becomes a 6:40 and stays that way for a week before anyone notices the signs don't match."

The line above is from a dockhand named Merek who has loaded that ferry for eleven years and has, as far as I can tell, a firmer grasp of the jurisdictional situation than any of the three offices I called. He was not complaining, exactly. He said the ferry runs, the crossing is safe, and the people who take it every day figured out the new time within about two days regardless of what the signs say. "The sign being wrong," he said, "has never once made the boat late."

Why I Am Telling You This

I am telling you this because it is the kind of thing that never becomes a headline and never becomes a hearing and never becomes anything except a mild, recurring, low-grade friction in the lives of a few hundred people who cross a lake for work. There is no villain in it. There is no scandal. There is just the ordinary seam where three legitimate authorities each hold a corner of one small thing, and the small thing wobbles a little in the middle where their hands overlap.

Most of governing is this. The Legislature will not vote on the Rhondel ferry. No Consul will hold a domain over it. It will go on being set by three offices in loose coordination, and it will go on mostly working, and the sign at the third posting will read 7:15 until someone who is a little bit in charge remembers that they are. The boat, in the meantime, leaves at 6:40. Set your morning accordingly.
