Constitutional Amendment · §15.1.c · Citizen-Led Path and Re-Vote

The Petition

The Digital Sovereignty Amendment achieves 77.2% affirmative support on 63.1% participation. The standard threshold fails — 1.9% short on participation. The re-vote provision triggers automatically. Eight months later the campaign runs again. 74.8% affirmative. 64.1% participation. Still 0.9% short. The amendment fails. The re-vote gave it a genuine second chance. The threshold held.

§15.1.c Citizen-Led Path §15.1.c Re-Vote Provision §15.2.a No Executive Veto §15.3.a Unamendable Provisions §16.1 Electoral Commission §1.9 Digital Privacy

The Digital Sovereignty Amendment is the Republic's first citizen-led constitutional amendment to reach a national vote. Fourteen months of petition organisation. Three hundred and twelve thousand signatures verified by the Electoral Commission. A national vote conducted through the National Voting System. The amendment proposes to expand §1.9 — the right to digital privacy — to include computational sovereignty: the right to control any automated process that operates on a citizen's behalf without their continuing informed consent, including algorithmic decision systems used by public authorities.

The campaign has substantial but uneven support. Digital rights advocates, civil liberties organisations, and significant portions of the technical community have organised for it. Opposition focuses on implementation: the amendment's language will require years of judicial interpretation, creating litigation over every government algorithm. It is a policy question, opponents argue, better addressed by statute than by constitutional text that will lock in ambiguity permanently.

The First Vote

First Vote — §15.1.c Standard Threshold
Required: 70% affirmative support with at least 65% citizen participation
──────────────────────────────────
Affirmative: 77.2% (exceeds 70% ✓)
Participation: 63.1% (falls short of 65% ✗ — short by 1.9%)
──────────────────────────────────
Standard threshold: FAILS
Re-vote trigger check: participation 60-65% ✓ and affirmative >75% ✓
Re-vote triggered — EC schedules second vote within 12 months

The amendment does not pass. 63.1% participation falls 1.9 points short. But both re-vote trigger conditions are met: participation is between 60% and 65%, and the affirmative vote exceeds 75%. The Electoral Commission confirms the arithmetic and schedules a second vote automatically. No institution decides whether to grant a second chance — the provision self-executes. The EC publishes the second vote date: eight months from the first result certification, clear of the 90-day minimum floor.

§15.1.c — What the Re-Vote Provision Does and Does Not Do
A second chance at the full threshold. Not a lower one.

The second vote uses the same standard as the first: 70% affirmative with 65% participation. The re-vote provision acknowledges that a near-miss with commanding affirmative support deserves a second opportunity — but the opportunity is to meet the actual threshold, not a relaxed version of it. The campaign has eight months to remobilize. What it needs is broader participation, not more intense support among existing supporters.

The campaign leadership understands the arithmetic. They achieved 77.2% affirmative on 63.1% participation. They need 65% participation on the re-vote. That is 1.9 percentage points more of the eligible electorate showing up. In absolute terms, roughly 900,000 additional eligible voters need to participate. The question for the next eight months is whether those 900,000 voters can be reached — and whether they will vote yes when they arrive.

The Eight Months

The campaign makes a strategic decision that will define the re-vote outcome. Rather than broadening its appeal to voters who sat out the first vote — many of whom were undecided or mildly opposed — it focuses on deepening engagement among supporters who did not turn out. The theory: the 37% who didn't participate in the first vote includes a large bloc of sympathetic non-voters, not just opponents who stayed home. Mobilize the sympathetic non-voters and the participation threshold closes.

The theory is partially correct. The campaign's ground operation is the most extensive in the Republic's citizen-amendment history. Participation does improve. But the sympathetic non-voter bloc is smaller than modelled. And the eight months of campaign activity, including significant media coverage of the implementation concerns raised by opponents, shifts the affirmative margin slightly. Some first-vote supporters have developed doubts. The intensive mobilization push reaches voters who are less committed than the campaign assumed.

The Re-Vote

Second Vote — §15.1.c Standard Threshold
Required: 70% affirmative support with at least 65% citizen participation
──────────────────────────────────
Affirmative: 74.8% (exceeds 70% ✓)
Participation: 64.1% (falls short of 65% ✗ — short by 0.9%)
──────────────────────────────────
Standard threshold: FAILS
Re-vote trigger: NOT AVAILABLE — same text may not trigger a further re-vote
Amendment fails — no further re-vote available on this text

74.8% affirmative. 64.1% participation. The campaign improved participation by 1.0 percentage point and lost 2.4 points of affirmative support. The re-vote came closer on participation than the first vote — 0.9% short instead of 1.9% — but the amendment still fails the standard threshold. And the re-vote provision is explicit: the same amendment text may not trigger a further second vote. The Digital Sovereignty Amendment, in this text, is finished. The NRS carries both results permanently.

The campaign leadership issues a statement acknowledging the result. They describe it as two near-misses that demonstrate substantial public support for the underlying principle, if not for this specific text. They announce their intention to draft a revised amendment — addressing the implementation clarity concerns that opponents raised — and begin a new petition process. That petition is a different amendment. This one has concluded.

What This Scenario Tests

§15.1.c's re-vote provision answers a specific question: what should happen when a citizen-led amendment achieves commanding affirmative support but falls slightly short on participation? The answer the provision gives is not "it passes anyway" and not "it fails without recourse." It gives the campaign a genuine second chance at the full standard. The re-vote is not a consolation path — it is a second opportunity to demonstrate the breadth the threshold requires.

The scenario's outcome shows that the re-vote is a real test, not a formality. The Digital Sovereignty campaign improved participation by 1.0 point across eight months of intensive work. It needed 1.9. The gap closed but did not close enough. The amendment finished with more participation than either vote achieved separately — 64.1% is higher than 63.1% — but the constitution requires 65% in a single vote, not a cumulative record across two attempts.

The affirmative margin's decline between first and second vote — from 77.2% to 74.8% — is the scenario's quieter finding. The campaign's mobilization strategy reached voters who were less committed than the first-vote electorate. Broadening participation sometimes means diluting intensity. The re-vote exposed something the first vote's commanding affirmative figure obscured: a meaningful portion of potential supporters had real concerns about the amendment's implementation. The alternative paths remain available. §15.1.b requires the Legislature's participation — but a campaign that can demonstrate 74% public support twice has a credible case for legislative engagement. The revised petition route is also available. What is not available is another automatic second vote on a text that has twice failed to meet the participation standard the constitution requires for amendments that bypass normal democratic deliberation entirely.
