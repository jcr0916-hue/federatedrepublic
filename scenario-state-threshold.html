Presidential Election · State Plurality Requirement · §7.2

The State Threshold

National Election Day, Year 4. Candidate Mara Solano wins 57.2% of the national RCV — a clear national majority. She wins 19 of 42 States by first-preference plurality. The threshold is 22. The Republic holds its first presidential runoff.

§7.2 Presidential Election §7.2.a National RCV §7.2.b State Plurality Requirement §7.2.f Presidential Runoff §7.2.e Elections Panel Certification

The presidential election architecture is built on two concurrent requirements that a candidate must satisfy to win outright: a national RCV majority and a first-preference plurality in a majority of the Republic's States. Both must be met. The design reflects a constitutional judgment that the President governs all of the Republic — not just the population centers where majorities concentrate — and that a winning coalition should demonstrate geographic breadth as well as national weight.

Mara Solano's campaign was a study in what happens when those two requirements pull in different directions. Her national coalition was enormous — 57.2% on the final RCV count, a margin that would have won any prior election. But her coalition was concentrated. She won urban States by large margins and lost rural States by large margins. The distribution of her support across the Republic's 42 States produced a map that satisfied the national majority test decisively and failed the State plurality test narrowly.

The Election Night Count — §7.2.b

The Elections Panel of the Digital Infrastructure Commission publishes State-by-State first-preference results as the foundation of the State plurality calculation under §7.2.b. These are not RCV-transferred results — they are the raw first-preference votes, the unambiguous first choice of each voter in each State.

§7.2.b — The State Plurality Test
A candidate wins by receiving more first-preference votes than any other candidate in a majority of States.

Solano's first-preference plurality by State: 19 of 42. Her opponent, Renfro Callahan, won 23 States by first-preference plurality despite receiving only 38.4% of the national RCV total. A third candidate, Yeva Marsh, won no States and received 4.4% nationally before elimination.

The threshold for the State plurality requirement is a majority of the Republic's 42 States — 22 States. Solano reached 19. She failed the State plurality requirement by 3 States.

The 60% escape valve under §7.2 does not apply. That provision waives the State plurality requirement only where the national RCV majority exceeds 60% of active ballots — a threshold designed to recognise that a genuinely dominant national mandate constitutes its own geographic legitimacy. Solano's 57.2% is a substantial national majority, but it does not reach 60%. The requirement is not waived.

No candidate has won outright. Under §7.2.f, a runoff is conducted between the two candidates with the highest national first-preference vote totals. Solano leads nationally on first preference; Callahan is second. The runoff is Solano versus Callahan.

The Constitutional Debate — §7.2.f

The result produces immediate legal challenge. Solano's supporters argue that the State plurality requirement produces an anti-democratic outcome: a candidate who won the national popular majority by nearly 19 points is being sent to a runoff. Constitutional scholars disagree on whether this constitutes a system failure or the system working as designed.

The case for the system: §7.2 was constructed deliberately to require geographic breadth, not just numerical weight. Solano's coalition, while large, is concentrated in a way that would have produced a President whose electoral mandate came almost entirely from a subset of States. The runoff does not prevent her from becoming President — it requires her to build a broader coalition to do so.

The case for the critics: the State plurality test uses first-preference votes only, not RCV-transferred totals. In several States Solano lost by first preference, she was the clear second-choice favourite. The test measures where voters ranked her first, not where they most preferred her over the other candidate. A test that ignores second and third preferences in a system built on ranked-choice voting creates an internal tension in the election architecture.

§7.2.f — The Runoff Design
The runoff is determined by national RCV only. No State plurality requirement applies.

This is the critical design choice in §7.2.f. The State plurality requirement is a first-round test that incentivises candidates to build geographically broad coalitions from the start. If neither candidate can satisfy it outright, the democratic default — whoever the nation prefers in a direct national contest — prevails in the second round.

The runoff between Solano and Callahan is a national RCV election. Yeva Marsh's voters receive new ballots and rank the two remaining candidates. Their second and third preferences from the first election are not transferred automatically — it is a fresh ballot. This gives Marsh's supporters — distributed across both rural and urban States — a direct role in the runoff outcome.

The Runoff — §7.2.a Applied to Two Candidates

The runoff is conducted on a date set by statute, held within the timeframe the Legislature established for post-threshold runoffs. With only two candidates, the RCV mechanism reduces to a simple majority: every valid ballot expresses a first preference for one or the other, and the candidate with more first-preference votes wins.

Marsh's voters, asked to choose between Solano and Callahan, split. Post-election surveys found her supporters divided roughly 55/45 in favour of Solano — a split that reflected Marsh's own urban-rural coalition straddling both candidates' bases. In the final count, Solano wins the runoff with 52.1% of active national ballots.

What This Scenario Tests

The State plurality requirement produces its first real test in a close election where the national majority winner fails to demonstrate geographic breadth. The scenario tests §7.2, §7.2.b, and §7.2.f simultaneously.

The 60% escape valve is specifically designed for landslide mandates — a 70% national winner cannot be meaningfully said to lack geographic legitimacy regardless of the State count. Solano's 57.2% is a genuine majority but not a landslide. The escape valve's design threshold is the judgment that below 60%, the State plurality test serves a legitimate constitutional function.

The runoff's no-State-plurality-requirement design reflects a deliberate choice: the geographic check is a first-round incentive, not an absolute constitutional requirement for every election. If voters cannot produce a candidate who satisfies both requirements outright, a direct national majority is the fallback. The runoff produces a President with a clear national mandate — and one whose victory, having come through the full constitutional process, carries unimpeachable legitimacy.
