Institutional Process · §8.9.a–d.i · Per-Incident Panel Draw

The Empty Seat

The first proceeding to trigger the per-incident State Designee Panel draw. Two States have empty pools. A drawn member declines within hours. A challenge is filed the following day. Both alternates are exhausted before the Panel is fully seated. The JI conducts a supplemental draw and the clock restarts. What the mechanism looks like under ordinary strain — at the moment it actually matters.

§8.9 State Designee Panel §8.9.a State Designee Pools §8.9.b Annual Addition Cap §8.9.c Pool Monitoring §8.9.d Panel Constitution §8.9.d.i Draw Challenge §8.9.e Ongoing Eligibility §8.9.f Activation and Meeting Parameters

The EI and JI jointly initiate a For-Cause removal proceeding against the Legislative Inspectorate’s Inspector General. The grounds are transmitted to the JI at 9:14 on a Tuesday morning. The 21-day review clock does not start yet — first, the JI must draw a Panel.

The Pool

Before the draw session opens, the JI’s systems pull the current national pool from the NRS. The combined pool has 831 eligible members across 48 States. Two States contribute zero: Darent, whose last eligible member withdrew six months ago and whose legislature has not added a replacement within the current year’s cap; and Vellen, whose enabling statute for the pool was passed four years ago but never funded or staffed.

Neither absence is a crisis. The draw pulls from the aggregated national pool. With 831 eligible members, the absence of two States reduces the probability of those States’ citizens being drawn, but does not impair the draw itself. The LI’s most recent Pool Status Report flagged both States. The flag is in the permanent NRS record. It has been there for two years.

§8.9.d — Draw Session · Activation + 3 Days
Public lottery, certified remotely by all three Inspectorates

The draw session is held by secure video platform under the parameters the Legislature has set by statute. The JI’s draw officer opens the session. A representative of each Inspectorate joins from their respective offices. Three citizens selected by jury protocols join from two different States. The session is streamed and recorded to the NRS in real time.

Seven names are drawn in sequence from the national pool — five members and two alternates. The draw takes eleven minutes. All three Inspectorates certify. The NRS entry is published before the session closes. Drawn members receive notification simultaneously with NRS publication.

The Declines

The 48-hour acceptance window opens the moment notifications go out. By hour fourteen, four of the five drawn members have confirmed. The fifth — Wren Calloway, a retired municipal administrator from the State of Orreth — sends a decline. Her reason is personal: she is the primary caregiver for a family member whose condition has worsened in recent weeks. She asks that her pool membership be extinguished rather than held for future draws.

First alternate Jae-won Suh accepts within the hour. The Panel now has five confirmed members. One alternate remains: Petra Holst.

NRS Entry — Draw Record — Activation + 3 Days State Designee Panel constituted by public lottery for proceeding [reference]. Five members drawn; two alternates. Draw certified jointly by all three Inspectorates. National pool at draw: 831 eligible members across 48 States. Calloway (Orreth) declined; first alternate Suh filled seat. Panel fully constituted — five members confirmed. 48-hour challenge window open.

The Challenge

On the second day of the challenge window, the named officer’s legal representative files a challenge under §8.9.d.i. The challenge names Jae-won Suh. It does not challenge the draw procedure or any of the other four members. It alleges that Suh, during a panel discussion at a governance conference three years prior, made public remarks characterizing the type of conduct alleged in this proceeding as “a textbook example of dereliction — the kind that should trigger immediate removal proceedings.” A transcript is attached.

The JI reviews the challenge. The remarks are documented. The question is whether they constitute a conflict of interest impairing impartiality under §8.9.d.i, or whether they reflect a general professional opinion that falls short of the standard.

The JI’s ruling, published to the NRS within 48 hours of filing: the challenge is sustained. The remarks were specific enough in their characterization that a reasonable assessment of impartiality could not exclude their influence. Suh’s seat is extinguished. The remaining alternate, Petra Holst, is notified.

Holst declines. She has accepted a short-term state advisory appointment that she cannot exit on the timeline the proceeding requires. The appointment does not technically trigger §8.9.e’s disqualifying positions — it is below the department director threshold — but she chooses not to serve. The decline is her right. The seat is extinguished.

The Panel now has four confirmed members. Both alternates are exhausted.

Constitutional Position — Activation + 5 Days

§8.9.d is explicit: if both alternates are exhausted before five members have accepted, the JI conducts a supplemental draw from the remaining eligible pool by the same procedure. The 21-day review clock has not started — it begins only from full Panel constitution under §8.9.f. The clock is also suspended during the pending challenge, which has now been resolved. No timeline has been violated. The JI opens a supplemental draw.

The Supplemental Draw

The supplemental draw is conducted the following morning by the same remote procedure. One name is drawn from the remaining eligible pool — 829 members, minus the seven already drawn and minus Calloway whose position has been extinguished. The draw is certified and published. The drawn member, Olamide Adeyemi of the State of Korveth, accepts within six hours.

What This Tests

The per-incident design moves pool health from a deferred administrative concern to an immediate operational one. Under a standing panel model, Darent and Vellen’s empty pools were a problem visible two years in advance, flagged in reports, and practically irrelevant until the next election. Under the per-incident model, those empty pools are a fact at the moment of every draw. They do not break the draw — the national aggregation absorbs them — but they are always present, always on the NRS, always attached to whoever holds the governor’s office.

The challenge mechanism tests a genuine design tension. §8.9.d.i exists to protect the proceeding from drawn members with documented conflicts. It is also the most obvious vector for delay: file a challenge, exhaust alternates, force a supplemental draw, restart the clock. The provision anticipates this. The 48-hour JI ruling deadline, the prohibition on frivolous grounds, and the clock-suspension rather than clock-restart all bound the tactic. In this scenario the challenge was legitimate and the JI sustained it. The delay — three days before the clock started — was the constitutional cost of getting a Panel that could actually function impartially.

What the scenario does not test — because it did not arise — is what happens if a named officer files challenges against multiple drawn members sequentially, each within the 48-hour window. That question is left to statute under the Legislature’s §8.9.f parameter-setting authority. The constitution provides the floor: no challenge on grounds of State of origin, professional background, or prior pool membership. The Legislature may tighten further.

The Pool Monitoring provision’s threshold — twenty national members before the LI must convene an emergency session — was not triggered. At 831 members the national pool is robust. The two absent States are a governance failure of those States, not a systemic pool health problem. The unglamorous work of pool maintenance is exactly that: unglamorous, distributed, and mostly invisible until the moment it isn’t.
