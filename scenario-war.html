The War Scenario

A coordinated surprise attack. The first Tier 3 Legislative Declaration of Defense. Emergency derogation at maximum scale — and what the unamendable provisions protect even then. Two occupied States. A war crime published to the NRS during active operations. A weapon that kills 40,000 civilians, authorized by Senate vote. Five years of war. Two years of reckoning. The question the scenario cannot fully answer: what does constitutional authorization make legitimate?

Seven Constitutional Periods
  1. The Attack — Day 1 to Month 1: Tier 1, Tier 2, the night session
  2. The Declaration — Month 1: Tier 3, emergency derogation, the unamendable line
  3. Occupation — Year 1–2: Two States fall, constitutional obligations to occupied citizens
  4. The Firewall Breaks — Year 2–3: Wartime surveillance, the EI finds it, the PM stays
  5. The Weapon — Year 3–4: Senate authorization, 40,000 civilian deaths, victory
  6. Liberation and Reconstruction — Year 4–5: Restoring constitutional governance in occupied territory
  7. Reckoning — Years 5–7: War crimes prosecutions, the NRS as courtroom, the question that holds open

The attack begins at 04:17 on a Wednesday in mid-autumn. Kesvar Dominion ground forces cross the Republic's northern border at three points simultaneously — coordinated with naval strikes on the southern coastal installations launched from Ranthik Alliance vessels operating under Kesvar joint command. Within minutes, the Republic's early warning systems confirm what their intelligence services had been unable to confirm in the preceding weeks: this is not an exercise or a provocation. It is an invasion.

The Republic at the moment of attack is fifteen years post-ratification and constitutionally mature. Its military is subordinated, its Inspectorates are functioning, its NRS is operational across forty-three independent node operators. It has a President in the third year of her six-year term and a PM whose coalition has governed for four years. What it does not have is any recent experience of what a major war does to a constitutional framework — not as a theoretical question but as a lived one, hour by hour, in the specific constitutional machinery of a document that was designed for peace and must now operate in conditions of existential emergency.

I. The Attack — Day 1 to Month 1

§2.1.a — Tier 1, 04:31
Fourteen minutes after first contact

The President invokes Tier 1 at 04:31 — fourteen minutes after the first border crossing is confirmed. The invocation authorizes immediate defensive military operations and triggers the automatic 96-hour Emergency Defense Allocation. The PM is notified simultaneously. The PM certifies the allocation at 04:38. Seven minutes between invocation and funding. This is the Tier framework operating at its design speed: the civilian PM's fund certification converts the presidential order into an operational military action in real time.

The Tier 1 invocation is published to the NRS at 04:47. The entry is timestamped, signed by the President's authentication key, and permanently recorded. The first entry in what will become the war's constitutional record exists before most citizens of the Republic have woken up to learn they are at war.

§2.1.b — Tier 2, 16:52
The Senate Armed Services Committee convenes at noon

By 08:00, the scale of the attack is clear: Kesvar has committed approximately 180,000 ground troops in the initial crossing, with air support and a naval blockade forming in the western approaches. This is not a border raid or a limited operation. It is an invasion designed to capture and hold Republic territory. Tier 1's defensive ROE is insufficient. The President transmits the classified briefing to the Senate Armed Services Committee at 10:15. The Committee convenes in emergency session at noon in the capital. The briefing runs four hours. The Committee authorizes Tier 2 at 16:52. The authorization is published to the NRS at 17:04. The PM certifies extended operational funding at 17:11.

The 12-hour gap between Tier 1 invocation and Tier 2 authorization is not a delay — it is the constitutional framework operating as designed. The President has Tier 1 authority for immediate defensive response without authorization. Extended offensive and counter-offensive operations require the Committee's knowledge and authorization. The Republic's military commanders have 12 hours of defensive operations under Tier 1 while the civilian constitutional process catches up. The gap is visible on the NRS: the timestamp of the authorization is there, and the timestamp of what military operations occurred before and after it.

II. The Declaration — Month 1

Tier 2 governs extended military operations with Senate Armed Services Committee authorization. It is designed for conflicts of limited scope and duration — a naval engagement, a counterterrorism operation, a border security crisis. What the Republic now faces is none of these. By Week 2, Kesvar forces have advanced 40 kilometers into the northern State of Arveld. By Week 3, a second State — the border State of Varentis — is under simultaneous pressure on its eastern flank from Ranthik Alliance forces. The Tier 2 framework is not built for a two-front war with existential territorial stakes.

§2.1.c specifies the conditions for a Tier 3 Legislative Declaration of Defense: "A declaration that the Republic faces an external military threat to its territorial integrity or the continued existence of its constitutional government." The President submits a formal Tier 3 request to both chambers on Day 18. The joint session convenes on Day 21. The debate runs two days.

§2.1.c — Tier 3, Day 23
The first Legislative Declaration of Defense in the document's history

The House votes 421-79. The Senate votes 94-6. The Legislative Declaration of Defense is enacted on Day 23. It is the first Tier 3 invocation in the constitutional framework's history. The declaration is published to the NRS within 24 hours — all 47 pages of it, including the classified annex whose existence is registered and whose content is sealed.

Tier 3 does not suspend the constitution. It does not give the President emergency powers outside the existing framework. What it does is authorize full military mobilization — conscription, wartime production allocation, the requisition of private assets for military use — and expand the President's operational military authority to include strategic offensive operations that would require a new Tier 2 authorization under normal circumstances. Every specific action under Tier 3 still requires PM fund certification. Every classified operation still requires NRS registration. The declaration changes the scope of authorized action. It does not change the accountability framework within which that action occurs.

The 79 House members and 6 senators who vote against the declaration publish individual statements to the NRS within 48 hours of the vote, as their right. Some argue that diplomatic options were not exhausted. Some argue the declaration's scope is too broad. Some argue that constitutional authorization should not precede a clearer statement of war aims. Their objections are on the record. The declaration is also on the record. Both exist permanently.

On Day 29, the President declares a national emergency under §1.25 and invokes emergency derogation of specific Article I rights in defined geographic areas. The declaration is precise in the way the constitution requires: it names each right being limited, specifies the geographic scope, states the duration (90 days, renewable), and identifies the review mechanism. What the declaration limits: freedom of movement within the two northern border States, requiring identity documentation for civilian transit; communication surveillance in the border States, authorized by warrant under an expedited judicial review process; association with organizations registered as linked to Kesvar or Ranthik governments, requiring registration of members with the LI.

§1.25.b — The Non-Derogable Line
What the emergency declaration explicitly does not touch

The §1.25 emergency declaration contains a section that is as important as what it limits: what it expressly does not limit. The President publishes a non-derogation certification alongside the declaration: the prohibition on torture and cruel treatment (§1.2) is not derogated. Habeas corpus (§1.13.c) is not derogated. No person held by the Republic's military or security services may be detained without a judicial process — including Kesvar prisoners of war. Non-retroactive criminal law (§1.18.a) is not derogated. The right to a public trial (§1.17) is not derogated for any person charged with a civilian crime.

The non-derogation certification is published to the NRS with the emergency declaration. It is a constitutional act of specific significance: the President is not merely declining to suspend those rights. She is publishing, permanently, a statement that she knows they cannot be suspended and that her government will not attempt to suspend them. The EI's weekly war compliance assessments — beginning in Week 4 and continuing for the duration of the emergency — audit every detained person's access to habeas corpus, every treatment of prisoners of war against the §1.2 prohibition, and every prosecution against the §1.18.a non-retroactivity standard. The assessments are published to the NRS weekly.

III. Occupation — Years 1 to 2

By Month 4, Kesvar forces have occupied the northern quarter of Arveld — approximately 800,000 citizens. By Month 8, Ranthik forces, operating in coordination with Kesvar, have seized the eastern districts of Varentis — an additional 1.4 million citizens. By the end of Year 1, approximately 2.2 million Republic citizens are living under enemy occupation. Their State governments have withdrawn to unoccupied territory. Their local institutions are partly dismantled, partly operating under enemy direction, and partly continuing in an informal underground form that the occupiers have not yet fully suppressed.

The JI receives a petition in Month 6 from a coalition of civil society organizations in unoccupied Arveld asking for a constitutional determination: what are the Republic's obligations to citizens under enemy occupation? The constitution was not designed with this question in mind. The Tier 3 declaration governs military operations to liberate occupied territory. The §1.25 emergency derogation governs rights limitations in defined areas. But neither provision addresses the affirmative obligations of the Republic to citizens whose rights it cannot currently protect because an enemy government controls their territory.

§14.1-analog — JI Determination on Occupied Citizens
Month 9: The constitution has obligations to people it cannot reach

The JI's determination runs 34 pages. Its core finding: enemy occupation does not suspend constitutional rights. The right to habeas corpus, to freedom from torture, to non-retroactive criminal law — these rights belong to the occupied citizens by virtue of their status as Republic citizens and Inhabitants. The Republic cannot enforce them during occupation. But it cannot disclaim the obligation. Two constitutional consequences follow from this finding.

First, the Republic's war aims are constitutionally bounded: liberating occupied territory is not merely a military objective — it is a constitutional obligation, the only means by which the Republic can fulfill its duties to its own citizens. A peace treaty that traded occupied territory for security would be constitutionally void as applied to those citizens: it would permanently deprive Republic citizens of their constitutional rights by trading their territory to a state that does not recognize those rights. The PM publishes this finding's implication in a statement to the House: no peace on terms that leave Republic citizens under permanent foreign governance.

Second, the Republic must document what is happening to occupied citizens even when it cannot prevent it. The EI's weekly war compliance assessments expand to include a section on reported conditions in occupied territories — compiled from refugee accounts, communications intercepts where available, and international observer reports. These are not verified facts; they are reported conditions, clearly labeled as such, published to the NRS. The documentation will matter enormously in Year 7.

The war in Year 1 and Year 2 is a war of attrition. Republic forces hold a defensive line south of the occupied territories, conducting limited counteroffensives to prevent Kesvar from deepening its position. Allied states — Seren, Aldric, and three others — provide logistical support, shared intelligence under treaty arrangements, and in the case of Aldric, two ground divisions that operate under Republic Tier 3 command authority by treaty arrangement. The PM's domestic authority covers the economic mobilization: production requisition, labor allocation, rationing frameworks enacted by emergency statute. GNR falls 24% from the Year 0 baseline by Year 2. The Severe Contraction Protocol activates at Month 14 — the first time in the document's history.

§10.1.d — Severe Contraction Protocol Activation
Month 14: The BNF floor reduces, but holds

The Severe Contraction Protocol activates when GNR falls more than 20% from the preceding year's baseline. Month 14's GNR certification triggers it. The BNF floor reduces proportionally — by the same percentage as the GNR decline, under §10.1.e. The MA Director certifies the reduced floor. The LI publishes its §10.1.f review within 30 days: the reduced floor is constitutionally valid; the federal supplementation obligation under §10.1.g activates for four States whose BNF compliance falls below even the reduced threshold.

The practical consequence: wartime rationing and production diversion have reduced material provision in the most affected States. The BNF — designed for peacetime economic volatility — is not designed to maintain pre-war living standards during a major war. It is designed to ensure that no Inhabitant falls below the minimum, even when the minimum itself reduces under the Contraction Protocol. The protocol works: the NRS records that every Inhabitant received minimum provision throughout the war, at the reduced threshold. The record does not make the hardship less real. It makes it documented and acknowledged, which is the minimum the constitution owes to citizens who sacrificed under it.

IV. The Firewall Breaks — Years 2 to 3

By Year 2, Month 4, the Republic's domestic security services have been operating under the expedited judicial warrant framework for communication surveillance for sixteen months. The framework is being used. Warrants are being issued. The EI's weekly assessments note surveillance volumes without finding systematic violations. Then the PM authorizes something the warrant framework does not cover.

The authorization is contained in a classified PM Order registered to the NRS in Year 2, Month 5 — existence public, content sealed. It establishes what is internally designated the Kesvar Heritage Community Assessment Program. Its stated purpose: to identify active Kesvar intelligence networks operating among the Republic's communities with Kesvar ethnic ancestry. The Republic has approximately 340,000 citizens and legal residents with documented Kesvar heritage — descendants of pre-war immigration waves over three generations. The program conducts systematic data collection on this population: travel records, communication metadata, financial transactions, association memberships, religious community affiliations.

The EI discovers the program at Month 18 of its operation through the same mechanism that found the Concordance scenario's surveillance program: the classified classification registry on the NRS shows an expanding body of material associated with a program whose operational scope, when compared against the warrant records in the public portion of the EI's surveillance audit, does not match. The EI requests access to the classified material. The PM's office objects. The Supreme Court rules — in a classified proceeding whose existence is public and whose outcome is published — that the EI's audit authority extends to classified operations. The EI reviews the files.

§12.3 constitutional violation — Year 3, Month 1
The finding is published during active combat operations

The EI's finding: the Kesvar Heritage Community Assessment Program constitutes domestic surveillance of Republic citizens on the basis of ethnic ancestry without judicial warrant or any constitutionally recognized basis for domestic intelligence collection. Of the 340,000 people in the program's scope, the EI can verify that approximately 8,400 have actual documented contacts with Kesvar government or intelligence entities that might constitute legitimate subjects of counterintelligence investigation. The remaining 331,600 are citizens and legal residents surveilled because of their ancestry. This is a constitutional violation of §12.3.

The PM does not contest the factual finding. She does contest its constitutional significance under wartime conditions — arguing that the existential nature of the threat justifies surveillance that the EI's peacetime framework does not contemplate. The EI's published response is brief: §12.3 does not contain a national security exception. Its value as a protection lies precisely in the fact that it does not. An Intelligence Firewall that admits exceptions defined by the government claiming the exception is not an Intelligence Firewall. The finding stands.

What happens next is constitutionally different from the Concordance scenario. The House does not convene a constructive no-confidence vote. The war is in its third year. The Republic's forces are conducting a major counteroffensive. Removing the PM through a constructive vote — which requires simultaneously electing a successor — in the middle of an active military campaign is a constitutional act the House majority declines to perform. The PM acknowledges the constitutional violation publicly and in writing to the House, published to the NRS. The surveillance program is redesigned within 60 days to require individual judicial warrants — imperfectly implemented in the first months but measurably compliant within 90 days. The PM remains in office. The constitutional violation is on the NRS. The House majority will answer for that decision in the next election.

The NRS has now recorded, during an active war: a constitutional violation against the PM for domestic surveillance of ethnic communities; an EI finding that the President's non-derogation certification is being honored for prisoner treatment; an EI finding that the Severe Contraction Protocol is being correctly applied; and the weekly reports of conditions in the occupied territories. The NRS is functioning under attack — Kesvar has targeted three NRS node operators with cyberattacks in Year 2, destroying the server infrastructure of one university operator. The RSA Director reroutes affected recording through internationally hosted nodes under the provisional contingency framework. The NRS remains continuously operational. The cyberattack is itself an NRS entry.

V. The Weapon — Years 3 to 4

The Republic's military research program, accelerating under wartime mobilization, develops a thermobaric area-effect weapon in Year 3 that has no peacetime analog in the document's scenario library. The weapon uses a fuel-air explosive design with a blast radius sufficient to destroy the hardened military command infrastructure in Kesvar's capital city — the command and control network through which Kesvar coordinates its occupation of Republic territory, its supply lines, and its operational planning. Without that infrastructure, military experts advise the President, Kesvar's ability to sustain its occupation forces would degrade within 8 to 14 weeks, likely producing a negotiated withdrawal within 6 months.

The weapon's civilian cost is unavoidable. Kesvar's military command infrastructure is located in the capital city's administrative district. The blast radius of sufficient yield to destroy the hardened facilities would also destroy approximately 30 city blocks of surrounding urban area. The Republic's military planners, using Kesvar population data available through pre-war open sources, estimate 38,000 to 44,000 civilian deaths as a consequence of the weapon's use. The President's senior advisors present a range of 40,000 as the central estimate.

The President seeks Tier 3 authorization for the weapon's use. §2.1.c's Tier 3 framework authorizes the President to conduct "military operations necessary to defend the Republic's territorial integrity and constitutional government." The President's legal counsel argues that the weapon's use falls within this authorization — that destroying Kesvar's command infrastructure is a military operation, that its use is necessary in the context of a war now entering its fourth year, and that the civilian deaths are an incidental consequence of a military operation, not its purpose. The Senate Armed Services Committee disagrees.

§2.1.c — Senate Armed Services Committee Refusal
Year 3, Month 8: The Committee votes 9-3 against authorization

The Committee's 9-3 vote against authorizing the weapon's use is the first time any authorized body has refused a Tier request since the document's ratification. The committee's majority opinion, published to the NRS in classified summary, holds that: (1) a weapon whose design ensures 40,000 civilian deaths as a known consequence is not a weapon whose use can be authorized as incidental under the Tier framework; (2) the distinction between intentional civilian harm and incidental civilian harm is a matter of constitutional significance under the Republic's treaty obligations and the non-refoulement principle of §1.27; and (3) the Tier framework does not authorize crimes against humanity.

The President disagrees with the Committee's legal analysis. Under §2.1.c, the Committee's refusal does not end the authorization question — it sends it to the full Senate. The President requests a full Senate vote on Tier 3 expansion to include the weapon. The Senate debates for three weeks. The debate is conducted in closed session but the arguments are published in summary to the NRS within 72 hours of the close of each day's proceedings, as the constitution requires for all legislative proceedings. Citizens can read, in near-real time, the arguments their senators are making for and against authorizing a weapon that will kill 40,000 people.

The Vote — Year 3, Month 11

The Senate votes 64-36 to authorize the weapon's use within the Tier 3 framework. The President uses the weapon eleven days later. A single strike on a clear autumn morning. Kesvar's military command infrastructure is destroyed. The civilian death toll is confirmed, through post-strike assessment and subsequent Kesvar documentation, at 41,200.

The strike is published to the NRS within 24 hours. The authorization vote — 64-36, with the recorded vote of every senator — is on the NRS. The legal arguments for and against authorization are on the NRS. The civilian death estimate that the President presented to the Senate is on the NRS. The actual toll, once confirmed, is added to the record. Everything that can be known about this event by those who authorized it is permanently accessible to everyone who will ever want to examine it.

Kesvar's occupation forces, without coordinated command authority, begin fragmenting within six weeks. Negotiated withdrawal from Arveld begins in Week 8. Withdrawal from Varentis follows in Month 4 after the strike. Kesvar signs a surrender framework in Month 7. The war ends.

VI. Liberation and Reconstruction — Years 4 to 5

The liberation of Arveld and Varentis is a military event that immediately generates constitutional obligations. The occupied States' governments have been operating in exile for three and a half years. Their institutions — courts, civil service, local government — are a mixture of what survived underground, what was co-opted by occupying authorities, and what was destroyed and must be rebuilt. The returning State governments face a Statehood Audit question that the document addresses in §13.3.b but did not design for post-occupation reconstruction: can a State that has been under enemy occupation for years pass a Statehood Audit?

§13.3.b — Post-Occupation Statehood Assessment
Year 4: Reconstruction audits for Arveld and Varentis

The LI conducts post-occupation constitutional status assessments for both States within 60 days of liberation. The findings are nuanced. Arveld's judicial institutions, partly destroyed and partly co-opted, require systematic replacement of judges appointed under occupation authority; the LI identifies 47 judicial appointments that cannot be recognized as constitutionally valid under Article IV's independence requirements because they were made by an occupying authority that had no constitutional standing in the Republic's framework. A judicial re-appointment process, using the interim nominating commission structure from the transition provisions, resolves the question within 8 months.

Varentis presents a harder problem. The Ranthik occupation was shorter but more systematic: Ranthik's administration replaced Varentis's entire civil service leadership with occupation appointees who made decisions about land registration, business licensing, and local taxation during the occupation period. The legal status of those decisions — are they void, voidable, or valid acts of a de facto authority that the affected parties relied on in good faith? — is a question the constitution does not answer directly. The Supreme Court, in a landmark ruling, holds that occupation decisions affecting private parties who acted in good faith are voidable but not void, and that Varentis must establish a Claims Court under §19.6.e to adjudicate which decisions will stand and which will be reversed. The Claims Court operates for three years.

The reconstruction period produces the Republic's most significant fiscal stress since the Severe Contraction Protocol activation. Rebuilding occupied States requires capital that wartime GNR contraction has constrained. The BNF floor remains at its reduced Contraction Protocol level through Year 5 before GNR recovery allows it to return to the pre-war baseline. The MA Director certifies the return to full floor compliance in Year 5, Month 9 — published to the NRS, alongside a comparison to the Year 14 baseline that shows how far the floor had fallen and over what period it was restored.

VII. Reckoning — Years 5 to 7

The peace does not end the constitutional accounting. Three separate reckoning processes run simultaneously in the post-war period, each engaging different constitutional mechanisms.

The first is straightforward: the Transitional Accountability Mechanism framework (§19.9) is extended by Senate resolution to cover documented Kesvar and Ranthik atrocities against Republic citizens during the occupation. The extension is legally defensible — §19.9 was designed for the transition period but the Senate's power to establish fact-finding mechanisms by resolution is not time-limited. The extended TAM publishes its findings over two years: systematic torture of detained Republic resistance members; execution of civilians suspected of aiding Republic forces; the destruction of two villages in Arveld as reprisals. The findings are published to the NRS. Kesvar's government initially denies them, then stops responding. Seventeen Kesvar and Ranthik commanders are named in the findings as responsible.

The second reckoning concerns Republic forces. The EI's weekly war compliance assessments from Years 2 through 4 documented three specific incidents in which Republic forces committed acts in violation of the non-derogable prohibitions: one instance of torture of a Kesvar prisoner of war; two instances of the killing of civilians who were not combatants, in circumstances where the commanders had information that they were not combatants. The EI's findings were published to the NRS during the war. Now they are evidence in federal criminal proceedings.

§4.3 — War Crimes Prosecution
Year 6: The Republic prosecutes its own

Three Republic field commanders face criminal prosecution in federal court under the §1.2 prohibition on torture and cruel treatment, the non-derogable status of which means no wartime authorization can create a defense. The cases are not politically popular. Two of the commanders are decorated war heroes. One received the Republic's highest military honor for actions in the counteroffensive that liberated Arveld. Their prosecution is demanded by the EI's findings and enabled by the NRS's war record — the findings were published during the war, the evidentiary record is comprehensive, and the constitutional prohibitions are absolute.

Two commanders are convicted. One is acquitted — the court finds that the available evidence does not meet the criminal standard for the specific intent required for the war crimes charged, even while noting that the EI's factual findings about the incident are accurate. The sentences are published to the NRS. The acquittal is published to the NRS. The distinction between EI factual findings and criminal conviction is itself a constitutional finding: information-generation and consequence-imposition are different things, operated by different actors under different standards. The EI found that something happened. The court determined criminal culpability. Both are right, and they are different questions.

The third reckoning is the one the constitution cannot fully resolve. The thermobaric weapon's use — authorized by 64 Senate votes, documented on the NRS, with 41,200 confirmed civilian deaths — is the subject of extensive public debate in Years 5 through 7. The post-war government does not prosecute the authorization. The Senate vote was constitutionally valid; the President's use of the weapon within the authorization was constitutionally valid; the PM's fund certification of the operation was constitutionally valid. What is also constitutionally valid is that the entire record — authorization, debate, use, casualty count — is on the NRS permanently. The constitutional accountability for those 41,200 deaths is not criminal. It is historical and democratic. The senators who voted 64-36 serve the remainder of their terms with the vote on their constitutional records. The President who ordered the strike serves the remainder of her term. The NRS does not expire.

What This Scenario Tests

Note: §12.2 (added in v136) now provides an explicit offensive operations authorization mechanism. The analysis of the War Scenario's collective defense operations, and the allied liberation questions it raised, directly motivated the addition of §12.2. The War Scenario predates that provision and reflects the constitutional gap that existed in v135. The Tier framework was designed for real conflicts and tested here at the extreme it was designed to reach. Tier 1 at 04:31 on Day 1. Tier 2 at 16:52. Tier 3 by Day 23. The escalation is visible on the NRS — each step documented, authorized, certified, and published before the next step was taken. The Senate Armed Services Committee's refusal of the thermobaric weapon authorization is the most constitutionally significant moment in the Tier framework's history: it demonstrates that the authorization requirement has real content, that the body charged with authorizing military operations will sometimes refuse. That the full Senate overruled the Committee's refusal by 64-36 does not diminish the Committee's authority — it demonstrates that the authorization framework has an internal appellate structure, and that the final decision was made by 100 elected senators whose votes are permanently recorded.

The non-derogable provisions held. Habeas corpus was maintained for every detained person throughout the war. The torture prohibition was violated once — by a Republic field commander, not by policy — and prosecuted. The wartime Emergency Derogation was specific, published, and limited to what §1.25 permits. The non-derogation certification alongside the declaration is the most constitutionally significant use of §1.25 in any scenario: the President publicly committed, in a permanent NRS record, that the unamendable rights would hold even during an existential war. That commitment constrained every subsequent decision-maker who might have sought to deviate from it.

The Intelligence Firewall broke under wartime pressure, as it had under the lesser pressure of the Concordance scenario's economic warfare. The pattern suggests that this is the provision most vulnerable to executive override under genuine threat conditions. What is also consistent across both scenarios: the EI found the violation through audit, published it during active operations, and the NRS record shaped post-war political accountability even where it did not produce immediate removal. The Firewall bends. It does not disappear. The record of the bend does not disappear either.

The thermobaric weapon question is the scenario the document cannot fully answer. Senate authorization was given. The President used the weapon within that authorization. 41,200 civilians died as a known consequence. These facts are constitutional — they occurred within the constitutional framework, through constitutionally authorized processes. Whether constitutional authorization is sufficient to make such an act legitimate is a question the NRS preserves forever and each generation must answer for itself. The constitution's answer is not that the authorization makes it right. Its answer is that everything that happened — the authorization vote, the arguments for and against, the strike itself, the casualty count — is on the permanent public record. The electorate that returns or removes the senators who voted 64-36 is the accountability mechanism. The NRS is what makes that accountability real rather than rhetorical.

The BNF survived a major war. The Severe Contraction Protocol worked as designed under conditions its designers hoped would never arise. The post-occupation Statehood reconstruction produced constitutional questions — about occupation-authority decisions affecting private parties, about judicial independence after occupation — that the document addressed imperfectly but workably through existing provisions extended to novel circumstances. The NRS never went down. The node network survived cyberattack. Forty-three independent node operators, distributed across the Republic's territory, provided the redundancy that a single government-operated system never could have. The war's full constitutional record exists: from Tier 1 at 04:31 on Day 1 to the final war crimes conviction in Year 6. It is readable by anyone. It always will be.
