Constitutional Pool · §8.13 · JIC Lottery · Advisory Council · §9.3

The Public Draw

The Digital Infrastructure Advisory Council decennial review is triggered. The JIC conducts a public lottery draw under §8.13. One State pool is empty. A draw challenge is filed and resolved.

§8.13 Constitutional Pool Framework §8.12 Joint Inspectorate Council §9.3 Advisory Council §9.3.b Advisory Pool Entry §9.3.d Advisory Council Constitution

Every constitutional pool operates through the §8.13 framework. The Digital Infrastructure Advisory Council's decennial draw is the most complex pool activation in the Republic's design: 42 States each maintaining their own Advisory Pool, the JIC constituting the Council by public lottery, and the draw itself certified jointly by all three Inspector Generals in a single public session that is published to the NRS in real time.

The Advisory Council exists to assess the Digital Infrastructure Commission's performance at the end of each ten-year period — to review how the NRS, the NVS, and the Elections Panel have functioned, and whether the constitutional framework governing them has produced what it was designed to produce. The Council's findings are advisory; the Commission Director cannot be removed by Council recommendation alone. But the Council's published findings are permanent NRS records, and the political weight of a critical decennial review has proven significant.

Pool Entry and Certification — §8.13

§8.13 — Pool Entry Stage
State-designated. Any person may be placed in a State's pool through the State's own process, subject to JI certification.

In the months before the draw, 39 of the 42 States had active Advisory Pools with between two and seven certified members each. Three States had pool sizes of one certified member. One State — Aldenmoor — had an empty pool. Its last certified member had withdrawn two years prior when she accepted a government appointment. Aldenmoor had not nominated a replacement.

Under §8.13, the JI published a pool deficiency notice for Aldenmoor two months before the draw, triggering the 30-day response obligation. Aldenmoor's legislature moved quickly — its pool nomination process required a joint committee resolution and Governor signature. A retired telecommunications engineer with 18 years of infrastructure experience was nominated, the JI completed its qualification review within 30 days, and Aldenmoor's pool had one certified member on draw day.

The draw session was scheduled for the first Tuesday of the tenth year. Under §9.3.d and §8.13, the JIC conducts the draw in a public session, certified jointly by all three Inspector Generals, published to the NRS in real time. The session was streamed publicly. Representatives of all 42 States were permitted to observe. Three citizens selected by jury protocols served as independent witnesses.

The Draw — §8.13

The standard JIC lottery procedure draws one name from each State's eligible pool. Of the 42 draw slots, 41 produced clean results. Aldenmoor's single pool member, the telecommunications engineer, was drawn — its only option. Two drawn members declined acceptance within 48 hours (one citing a newly diagnosed health condition, one accepting a judicial appointment that triggered the no-current-government-position eligibility rule). Their alternates, drawn in the same session, accepted.

§8.13 — The Draw Challenge
Rejected entrants may appeal to an Appellate Court within 14 days. The Legislature defines draw challenge procedures by statute.

The challenge came from Vandemeer State, whose pool member had been drawn but then — upon the JI's post-draw eligibility confirmation — found to have accepted a part-time advisory role with a State regulatory body 18 days before the draw. The pool eligibility requirement bars persons who hold current government positions. The JI certified the drawn member as ineligible after acceptance. Under §8.13, supplemental draws fill seats when alternates are exhausted.

Vandemeer's government argued that the advisory role was not a "government position" within the meaning of the eligibility requirement — it was an unpaid advisory role, not an appointment. The Appellate Court ruled within 14 days: the eligibility requirement covers any formal advisory appointment to a government body regardless of compensation. The draw challenge failed. Vandemeer's alternate member was confirmed as the Council member for that State.

The Advisory Council of 42 members was fully constituted on Day 19 after the original draw — within the five-day constitution window counting from the last supplemental draw. The JIC published the full certified membership list to the NRS before the session closed.

What This Scenario Tests

The §8.13 pool framework's four stages — entry, certification, selection, confirmation — are all exercised in this scenario, with complications at two stages: pool deficiency (entry) and post-draw eligibility failure (certification/selection). The scenario demonstrates how the framework handles non-standard outcomes without the process breaking down.

The draw challenge mechanism is the most frequently misunderstood element of the pool framework. It is not an appeal of the draw result — it is an appeal of the JI's eligibility determination. The Appellate Court reviews only whether the JI correctly applied the eligibility criteria. It does not substitute its own judgment about whether Vandemeer's member would have served well. The distinction matters: the pool's independence depends on the lottery mechanism being genuinely random, not overridable by parties who preferred a different outcome.

Aldenmoor's near-miss — an empty pool days before a decennial draw — is a warning about pool maintenance. The §8.13 deficiency notice mechanism exists precisely for this situation. States that neglect their pools risk either having no representation in a draw or having their pool size force them to have every draw select the same single member. The deficiency notice is designed to make that risk visible before it becomes irreversible.
