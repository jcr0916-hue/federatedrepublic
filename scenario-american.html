The American Republic — Thirty-Five Years

One Republic, one constitutional order, thirty-five years. Not a crisis scenario. The ordinary constitutional life of a large and complex democracy: recession, immigration pressure, State fiscal failure, climate displacement, an island community that leaves and returns, a caretaker period, and finally the economic contraction that tests everything simultaneously.

§6.1 Dual-Gate Immigration §6.4 Asylum Court §1.27 Non-Refoulement §10.1 Basic Needs Floor §2.3 Constructive Vote §2.3 Post-Dissolution Bar §7.2.f Presidential Run-Off Article VIII Former Official Lookback §13 Territories & Independence §15 Amendments

A single continuous narrative — one Republic, one set of citizens, one constitutional order — across thirty-five years. Not a transition scenario. Not a crisis scenario. The ordinary constitutional life of a large and complex democracy: recessions, immigration waves, State fiscal failures, an island community choosing independence and then choosing to return, and finally the economic contraction that tests everything simultaneously. The architecture is not heroic. It is procedural. It works slowly, messily, publicly, and — examined across thirty-five years — correctly.

Years 1–4 — The Day-Zero Inheritance

The Republic inherits the United States' existing federal structure, debt obligations, immigration backlog, and every active domestic and military commitment. §5.5's Day-Zero grandfather clause activates automatically: all persons holding voting rights on ratification day receive Citizen Voting Credentials without testing. The transition does not disenfranchise anyone. The naturalization queue — 1.2 million pending applications — is processed under the new Gate architecture; applicants who have already cleared what would have been Gate 2 federal standards receive expedited Gate 1 State certification on a streamlined track the Legislature defines by statute in the first session.

The first Monetary Authority Director is nominated within 90 days and confirmed by the Senate. The BNF calculation for Year 1 produces the first national floor — 6.2% of GNR, distributed to States quarterly. Eleven States immediately flag projected shortfalls in the housing component. the Legislature's response — a supplementary housing assistance statute passed in Month 8 — establishes the precedent for BNF gap remediation that every subsequent Legislature will follow or modify.

Years 5–9 — The First Recession and the Gate Architecture

A technology sector correction in Year 5 produces a regional recession in six States. California, facing a $47 billion shortfall, proposes reclassifying university funding as BNF education provision. The MA Director's assessment within 14 days: university education is not basic needs floor provision. The anti-evasion principle prohibits definitional expansion. The reclassification is void. California complies within 90 days. The NRS records the attempt, the finding, and the compliance — in that order, permanently.

The Central American Displacement Wave

In Year 6, climate and governance collapse in three Central American nations produces the Republic's first major immigration pressure — Gate 1 petition volume increases 340%. the Legislature's response produces three successive statutory Gate 2 modification attempts that test the constitutional architecture against real political pressure.

Three Attempts — One Architecture

Attempt 1: a "community ties assessment" designed to identify potential irregular migrants. The Electoral Inspectorate's preliminary disparity analysis finds a 2.8x differential approval rate against Central American applicants. The §6.1.b disparity presumption activates: the proposal is presumptively discriminatory absent a compelling non-discriminatory justification. It is withdrawn before floor consideration.

Attempt 2: extending maximum processing periods for applicants from countries with limited documentation infrastructure. Constitutional — it produces processing delays, not §1.8 disparities. Enacted. Its effects documented on the NRS. The Legislature has learned to distinguish between what the architecture permits and what it prohibits.

Attempt 3: State sponsorship financial liability for Central American applicants — seven years of public services costs, criminal penalties for officials whose sponsored applicants later violate law. The Supreme Court strikes it down 6-3: the liability is facially neutral but produces costs so differentially concentrated on Central American applicants as to constitute constructive discrimination by national origin under §6.1.b.

The Asylum Court's First Years

the Legislature establishes the constitutional Asylum Court in Year 3, within the two-year deadline §6.4.c requires. By Year 6, the Court is processing 180,000 claims annually. §1.27's non-refoulement protection — applicable regardless of immigration status or mode of entry — produces the Court's most significant early ruling: a group of Central American applicants who entered by irregular means are protected from removal while their claims are pending under §6.4.b, regardless of the manner of their entry. §6.4.d is explicit: irregular entry does not affect the right to claim protection. The Court's published ruling becomes the foundational precedent for every subsequent irregular-entry asylum claim.

Years 10–14 — West Virginia and the Devolution Question

West Virginia enters Early Warning in Year 10. Coal revenues declining faster than any MA projection. Administrative capacity contracting. BNF provision in two rural counties below floor. The Legislative Inspectorate's audit finds genuine structural crisis, not bad faith — a distinction that matters for what follows. The corrective order issues. Federal support engagement opens within 30 days.

By Year 12, the corrective order's specific requirements are met but the broader fiscal trajectory remains unsustainable. The Senate's Devolution Committee debates a Devolution Motion. West Virginia's senators participate fully — the constitutional architecture gives them no power to prevent the debate but full standing to make their case within it.

The Two-Vote Margin

The Devolution Motion reaches the Senate floor in Year 13. The count: 37 in favour, 15 opposed. The 3/4 threshold requires 39 votes. The Motion fails by two votes.

What happens next is the scenario's most constitutionally significant moment in this period — not the vote but its aftermath. The two senators who voted against devolution have committed their political credibility to the proposition that West Virginia is on a viable trajectory. By Year 14, BNF compliance is at 1.3x the floor — still the lowest of any State, still receiving federal supplementation, but improving. Economic diversification is visible on the NRS. The constitutional architecture did not save West Virginia's economy. It kept the State's citizens protected during the transition, created political accountability for the trajectory, and made the process publicly visible throughout.

Years 12–14 — The House Dissolution

In Year 12, a parliamentary crisis produces the Republic's first caretaker period. Following a PM resignation under the constructive vote mechanism, the House spends 58 days attempting to elect a successor — exceeding the statutory 60-day window by 2 days before the deadline triggers automatic dissolution. The Senate Speaker calls emergency elections within 30 days.

§2.3 — The fixed-term caretaker model
The majority party that failed to seat a PM cannot field candidates in the emergency election.

The Progressive Coalition held 52% of House seats when dissolution triggered. §2.3 bars any party or organized faction that held a majority of seats in the dissolved House from fielding candidates in the emergency election. The Progressive Coalition cannot stand. Its individual members may stand — but not as a recognized party organization.

The emergency election produces a fractured House with no clear majority — four parties each holding between 18% and 28% of seats. The new House seats a PM in 34 days: a centrist compromise candidate acceptable to three of the four factions. The Post-Dissolution Bar produced exactly what it was designed to produce: a clean-slate election that prevents the dissolution from simply restoring the conditions that caused it. The Progressive Coalition returns at the next regular election cycle, having sat out one House term.

Years 15–19 — The Guam Compact

Guam Territory's independence movement produces the Republic's first Article XX compact negotiation. The independence petition reaches threshold in Year 13. The independence referendum passes at 58.3% in Year 14. Formal independence takes effect in Year 15. §1.26 — which guarantees Article I protections to all residents of Associated Communities operating under compacts — is the provision Guam's negotiators cite most often during compact discussions. The right to protect their residents through the compact, rather than ceding it, is what makes the compact model attractive over full separation.

Within six months of independence, Guam initiates compact negotiations. The compact's contested element: the Republic proposes embedding existing military base access as a reciprocal compact term. Guam's negotiators object that this converts an equal partnership into an explicitly transactional arrangement. The resolution reflects the equal sovereignty principle: the compact specifies the Republic's service obligations without conditioning them on military access; the Operational Use Rights exist as a separately negotiated bilateral agreement. The compact is ratified in Year 16 by 71% of both chambers. Guam's observing delegate takes a seat in the House.

Years 17–18 — The Presidential Run-Off

The Year 17 presidential election produces the Republic's first run-off. Three strong candidates split the national first-preference vote: 38.2%, 34.7%, and 27.1%. The combined score calculation under §7.2.b produces results within 0.4% of each other for the top two candidates — inside the 0.5% threshold that triggers a head-to-head run-off under §7.2.f.

§7.2.f — The Run-Off
Head-to-head national RCV. The outgoing President's term expires before the result is certified.

The run-off extends into the period after the outgoing President's constitutional term expires. §2.6.a's succession line activates immediately at term expiration: the Senate Speaker assumes presidential authority — defense, diplomacy, all of it — in a caretaker capacity pending the run-off result. §2.6.c applies: this is a succession-based period, not an elected term. The run-off winner, once certified and sworn in, takes immediate effect. The 14-day certification window and 14-day Supreme Court review window run concurrently. The transition is constitutionally seamless despite the timing complication.

Years 20–25 — The Climate Immigration Crisis

The Republic's largest immigration pressure since founding: 40 million globally displaced by Year 22, extended climate displacement from Bangladesh, the Philippines, Vietnam, and Pacific Island nations. the Legislature's response produces three more Gate 2 modification attempts — and, for the first time, one that holds.

The Reform That Holds

Attempt 1: a "Climate Stability Country of Origin Assessment" rating source countries by stability index. The EI finds a 4.1x differential burden against South Asian, African, and Pacific applicants. §6.1.b's disparity presumption activates. Withdrawn before floor consideration.

Attempt 2: a "Community Integration Pathway Assessment" requiring employment offers and language assessments. Facially neutral. The EI's six-month implementation analysis finds 91% documentation compliance for high-income English-speaking source countries versus 34% for displacement source countries — not because they lack integration capacity but because the displacement event itself destroyed the documentation infrastructure. The Supreme Court rules 7-2: the Republic is using evidence of displacement to screen against displaced persons. Void.

Year 24: the Climate Displacement Track — a separately resourced processing pathway substituting a Republic-administered resettlement support program for the private sponsorship or employment offer requirement. Security screening and identity verification unchanged. The §6.1.d Federal Sponsorship Category is used for the first time at scale: a designated federal resettlement agency serves as Gate 1 sponsor for applicants whose displacement source country has no functioning State government capable of providing sponsorship documentation. The EI's disparity analysis after one year: approval rates by national origin converge toward parity. 340,000 approvals annually. The §1.27 non-refoulement bar — explicitly covering persons within the Republic's effective custody or control regardless of immigration status — is cited 2,100 times in Asylum Court proceedings during the peak displacement years, providing protection to persons who could not be returned to any safe destination.

Years 26–31 — The Guam Incorporation

In Year 26, a decade into its Associated Community compact, Guam's elected assembly petitions for Territory status with a Statehood pathway. 71% in favour. The constitution already provides the mechanism: §13.6 Voluntary Incorporation, which allows any sovereign entity to petition to join the Republic's constitutional framework as a Territory. The process requires a 2/3 vote of Guam's own assembly, a citizen referendum achieving simple majority with at least 50% participation, Judicial Inspectorate certification that Guam does not systematically violate any unamendable Article I provision, and acceptance by the Legislature by 2/3 of both chambers.

All four conditions are met by Year 28. The Judicial Inspectorate certifies compliance within 90 days of petition receipt — the compact relationship has already produced a decade of published annual compliance reviews, and the JI's task is largely confirmatory. The Legislature accepts by 71% of both chambers in Year 28. Guam's assembly votes to formally incorporate, confirmed by citizen referendum at 74%. The incorporation agreement specifies a 36-month transition period and the institutional adjustments required to meet §13.2.f's minimum territorial floor. Guam enters the Territory framework in Year 28. Every step voluntary. Every step democratic. Every step covered by the founding document.

The Amendment That Fills the Gap

The Guam incorporation tests something the amendment cases could not: whether the founding document anticipated the full arc of a sovereignty relationship — independence, compact, and voluntary return. §13.6 was designed precisely for this. The Republic's non-coercion clause in §13.6 — "the Republic may not compel, incentivise, or pressure any sovereign entity to initiate this process" — means Guam's petition carries unimpeachable democratic legitimacy. No Republic actor initiated it. No Republic actor accelerated it. The assembly's vote and the citizen referendum are the constitutional record of a community's unencumbered choice.

Guam's residents — who were guaranteed Article I protections throughout their entire compact period under §1.26 regardless of their formal status — are now on a trajectory to full Statehood. The Associated Community compact framework has produced its first voluntary return to the constitutional structure, and the founding document was ready for it. The NRS records the complete arc: independence petition, compact ratification, incorporation petition, JI certification, legislative acceptance, Statehood pathway.

Years 32–35 — The Economic Contraction

A global financial contagion originating in European sovereign debt markets reaches the Republic in Year 32. GNR declines 17% in Year 32, accelerating to 29% in Year 33. The Severe Contraction Protocol activates. The MA Director's certification is published at 9:07 AM on a Wednesday. By 10:00 AM it is the most-read NRS record in the Republic's history.

The State Variation

Thirteen States maintain above-floor provision through fiscal reserves. Twenty-four maintain exactly at-floor. Eleven fall below the reduced constitutional floor within six months. LI audit findings trigger Inspectorate reporting. Eight restore compliance within 90 days. Three — Ohio, Kentucky, and Mississippi — cannot. Their fiscal capacity is genuinely insufficient, not evasive. The MA certifies the Gap 14 triggering condition: federal supplementation is constitutionally required.

The Court Orders What the Legislature Wouldn't

the Legislature does not appropriate within the constitutional 60-day period. The Supreme Court accepts an emergency challenge within 24 hours of Day 61. Its ruling, 6-3: the supplementation obligation is constitutional, not discretionary. The Court orders the PM's office to identify emergency funding under existing appropriations authority and directs the Legislature to ratify or reject within 14 days. The Legislature ratifies — 287-156 in the House, 61-39 in the Senate — under the Court's timeline. Supplementation reaches the three States within 30 days. BNF provision is restored to the reduced constitutional floor. The citizens of Ohio, Kentucky, and Mississippi have been watching the NRS record of corrective orders, federal supplementation requests, and legislative deadlock. The architecture has done what it was designed to do: slowly, messily, publicly, and eventually correctly.

The Former Official Lookback — Year 33

During the contraction's peak, new evidence surfaces of a former MA Director — who served in Years 11–16 and left office seven years before the finding — having systematically understated BNF shortfall projections in reports to the Legislature, allowing States to defer remediation that would have left them better positioned for the contraction. The Supreme Court accepts the Article VIII former official accountability challenge.

The conduct occurred four years before the finding — within Article VIII's five-year lookback window. The Court rules: the knowing falsification of BNF compliance reports to the Legislature constitutes a constitutional violation during federal service. The former Director is permanently barred from federal elected or appointed office and forfeits the federal pension accrued during the period of the violation. Criminal prosecution proceeds separately. The NRS records the finding alongside the original BNF reports, the legislative sessions that relied on them, and the States that deferred remediation as a result. The lookback has closed a gap that departing from office had appeared to create.

The Dual Executive Under Contraction

The President's international creditor negotiations require demonstrating fiscal consolidation. The PM's constitutional obligations require maintaining the BNF floor. The Emergency Panel receives three domain challenges during the contraction period. The most significant: a G20 accord provision requiring independent international certification of Republic fiscal commitments that would overlap with the MA's constitutional function. The Panel rules within 48 hours: the President may commit to consolidation targets but may not commit to domestic social provision reductions that would conflict with constitutional floor obligations. The accord is renegotiated. The Republic's treaty partners understand the constraint — most have their own constitutional floor obligations and designed their treaty commitments with exactly this kind of constitutional limit in mind.

The Thirty-Five Year Verdict

The BNF floor held for 28 years before the contraction and held — at its constitutionally reduced level — during it. Twenty-three BNF evasion attempts documented over 28 years. Twenty-three corrected. The immigration disparity presumption eliminated six discriminatory proposals before they processed a single application, leaving the Legislature with only genuine policy choices: restrict equally or expand pathways equally. The §1.27 non-refoulement protection provided constitutional floor coverage to persons the immigration system could not otherwise reach. The constructive vote prevented governmental instability and required a positive mandate before removing a sitting PM. The dual executive domain separation handled contraction-period international creditor pressure without constitutional breakdown. The Article VIII lookback reached a former official whose conduct shaped the contraction's severity. The amendment architecture produced sixteen substantive amendments — gaps identified, debated, closed.

What the architecture could not produce: West Virginia's economic transformation. The climate crisis's root causes addressed. The 29% GNR decline prevented. Legislative inaction on fiscal warnings changed in real time. A former official's misconduct discovered before it affected outcomes. These are not constitutional failures. They are political and economic challenges that no constitutional framework can resolve. The constitution creates conditions for democratic self-governance — accountability, transparency, protected floors, a permanent public record. It does not guarantee the democracy will make correct choices, only that it will make them in the open, on the record, for all to see. Thirty-five years is not enough to judge. The NRS records everything. Whether the next generation reads what it contains is the question Year 35 cannot answer.
