Paper No. 11

The Watchmen

On the three Monitors — why accountability requires structural independence, not just goodwill. The cross-nomination model that ensures no branch selects its own auditor. The information-only mandate that makes findings credible. The lottery that protects the judiciary’s watcher. And the SC categorical review under §9.9 that protects the watchmen themselves.

~2,800 words

The strongest objection to any accountability institution is not that it might be wrong. It is that it might be captured. An oversight body whose legitimacy depends on the goodwill of those it oversees is not an oversight body. It is a permission slip.

Every government that has ever existed has faced the accountability problem: who watches the watchers? The standard constitutional answer is separation of powers — the legislature checks the executive, the courts check the legislature, the executive checks the courts. The separation is real and important, but it has a structural weakness that this document takes seriously. Separation of powers relies on each branch’s institutional self-interest to generate accountability. A legislature that wants to preserve its own authority will check an executive that is overreaching. But what happens when the legislature and executive agree? What happens when the courts are aligned with both? Self-interest as a check only works when interests diverge. When they converge, the separation produces nothing.

The three Monitors are this document’s answer to that problem. They are not a fourth branch of government. They have no authority to make law, issue binding orders, or impose sanctions. They have one constitutional function: to publish facts. Their power is entirely downstream — it operates through the accountability that follows from documented facts being visible to the People, the Legislature, and the courts. Understanding why that design is correct, and why the specific mechanisms that protect the Monitors were chosen the way they were, is the subject of this essay.

⁕

The Cross-Nomination Model

The three Monitors watch the three branches. The Legislative Monitor audits the Legislature. The Executive Monitor audits both executives and the Monetary Authority — which operates as a constitutionally mandated but statutorily defined independent institution under §12.1. The Judicial Monitor audits the courts. Each is headed by an Monitor General who serves a ten-year non-renewable term.

The selection mechanism for each Monitor General is the same in principle and different in application: the Monitor General is nominated by one of the branches it does not watch, and confirmed by a third. The LI’s Monitor General is nominated by the Supreme Court — the judiciary nominates the body that watches the legislature. The EI’s Monitor General is nominated by the Assembly — the legislature nominates the body that watches the executive. The JI’s Monitor General is selected by public lottery from a qualified pool — no political actor nominates anyone for the body that watches the courts.

The Assembly is entirely excluded from the LI’s selection because the Assembly’s seat allocation depends on the census, which the LM conducts. Giving the Assembly any role in selecting the office that determines its own composition is a direct conflict of interest the constitution removes categorically rather than managing through recusal or disclosure.

The most important property of this model is what it prevents, not what it produces. An Monitor General whose nomination depends on the body being watched has an obvious incentive problem: the watchdog that depends on the watched for its appointment will not bite the hand that placed it. Cross-nomination does not guarantee that the nominated Monitor General will be aggressive or accurate. It guarantees that the Monitor General’s institutional survival does not depend on the goodwill of the institution under review. That is a different and more achievable goal.

The ten-year non-renewable term compounds this effect. An Monitor General who cannot be reappointed has no career incentive to shade findings toward the preferences of the nominating institution. The non-renewable constraint also means the Monitor General has no reason to position for a post-service role within the branch they oversaw — a common failure mode of oversight institutions whose leaders treat the role as a stepping stone rather than a terminal commitment.

The stagger requirement — no two Monitor General terms may begin within eighteen months of each other — ensures continuous institutional knowledge across transitions. An incoming Monitor General inherits two colleagues with established institutional memory. The knowledge does not reset simultaneously.

The Lottery

The Judicial Monitor’s selection is the most unusual of the three. The LM Monitor General is nominated by the SC; the EM Monitor General is nominated by the Assembly. But the JM Monitor General is drawn by public lottery from a qualified candidate pool administered by the outgoing Monitor General, witnessed by the other two IGs and three citizens selected by jury protocols, and published to the NRS immediately. No political body nominates anyone.

The reason is structural. The LM watches the legislature; the EM watches the executive. Each of these Monitors can be nominated by a body with no stake in the outcome of its audits. But the JM watches the courts. Every body that might nominate the JM Monitor General — the Assembly, the Senate, the Legat Consul, the Civic Consul — has cases before the courts, has interests in judicial interpretation, has candidates for judicial seats whose confirmation the JM vets. There is no political body that is genuinely indifferent to who runs the judicial oversight mechanism.

The lottery solution removes the nomination from political actors entirely. Its legitimacy comes from a different source than the other two Monitors’ legitimacy: not from institutional endorsement, but from procedural purity. Anyone who meets the eligibility criteria is equally likely to be drawn. The draw is public, certified by multiple witnesses, and published before the session closes. The JM Monitor General’s independence is not guaranteed by the interests of whoever nominated them — it is guaranteed by the fact that no one did.

The JI’s distinctive selection method also explains the distinctive removal rule: the JM Monitor General can only be removed for cause. The other two IGs can be removed without cause by three-quarters of both chambers, a provision that exists for the rare circumstance where continued service is untenable regardless of conduct. That provision is not available for the JM, because a no-cause removal pathway for a lottery-selected official would effectively give the Legislature a veto over any draw result it dislikes. The lottery’s independence guarantee would be meaningless if the body being watched could simply vote out a drawn Monitor General whose existence was inconvenient.

The Information-Only Mandate

The Monitors publish facts. They do not issue orders. They do not impose sanctions. They do not make legal determinations. An Monitor finding that a government official has breached the constitution is a published statement of fact, not a judicial ruling. Courts determine legal liability. The Legislature imposes political consequences. The People draw electoral conclusions. The Monitors supply the factual record that makes all of those processes work.

This design is counterintuitive to many people encountering it for the first time. The standard expectation is that an oversight institution should be able to do something about what it finds. An auditor that can only report seems toothless. Why not give the Monitors binding authority?

The answer is institutional credibility. An institution that can both discover wrongdoing and punish it has a built-in incentive to shape its discoveries toward punishments it wants to impose. The prosecutor who wants a conviction starts with the conclusion. The auditor who wants a sanction starts with the finding that justifies it. Information-generation and consequence-imposition are not complementary — they corrupt each other when housed in the same institution.

The Monitor that cannot punish has no stake in any particular outcome of its findings. If the LM finds that a bill was passed in violation of the single-subject rule, the LI’s institutional interest is in getting the finding right. It gains nothing from a wrong finding, because it cannot use the finding to do anything except publish it. The consequence follows from the courts or the legislature interpreting the finding — neither of which is the Monitor’s problem. That alignment produces better information than any arrangement in which the information-gatherer benefits from a particular result.

The NRS publication requirement reinforces this. Every Monitor finding must be published permanently and free of charge. No official may suppress, delay, classify, or alter an Monitor Report before publication. The classification exemption is absolute: if an investigation involves classified material, the report describes the nature of the issue without reproducing classified content, but the investigation’s existence and conclusions are public. A government that routinely classifies everything inconvenient discovers it cannot classify its own oversight.

The minority report provision is the internal complement to the external publication requirement. Any staff member may publish a Minority Report directly to the NRS if they believe a finding has been improperly omitted or suppressed by the Monitor General. Staff cannot be disciplined for filing a Minority Report. This creates an internal whistleblower mechanism that operates at the level of specific investigative findings rather than at the level of general whistleblowing. An Monitor General who buries a finding faces the structural possibility that a staff member will publish what was buried. The knowledge that this is available — even if it is rarely used — changes the Monitor General’s incentive calculation.

Who Watches the Watchmen

The oldest problem in accountability design is recursive: every oversight institution needs its own oversight. The Monitors are independent, powerful, and — precisely because they cannot be easily removed — difficult to check. If an Monitor General becomes corrupt, captures the institution, or begins producing findings that serve particular interests, what mechanism prevents that from persisting for a decade?

The constitution’s answer has two parts. The first is structural: the Monitors watch different branches, and the cross-nomination model means each Monitor General was nominated by an institution with an ongoing interest in how the other two Monitors operate. An LM Monitor General who is shading findings to protect the legislature creates a problem for the SC, which nominated that Monitor General and whose credibility is associated with the nomination. The cross-nomination interests are not perfectly aligned with accountability — but they are not aligned with corruption either.

The second part is the Removal Grounds Review: the SC categorical verification mechanism that stands between any For-Cause removal proceeding against an Monitor General and the legislature’s vote to remove. A legislature that wants to remove an Monitor General for political reasons — because the Monitor General’s findings are embarrassing, accurate, and inconvenient — must first transmit the grounds to the JM, which draws a fresh Panel from the national pool by public lottery. The Panel has one function: determine whether the stated grounds fall within the constitutional categories for For-Cause removal. If the stated grounds are “this Monitor General keeps finding things we do not like,” that is not a constitutional category. The Panel says so publicly. The NRS records the finding. The Legislature can still vote to remove — the Panel’s finding is not a veto — but any legislator who votes to remove an Monitor General whose removal was found unsubstantiated owns that vote permanently.

The Panel is constituted fresh for each proceeding precisely to prevent any standing panel from being identified and pressured in advance. Five members drawn at random from a pool of citizens who have no ongoing institutional relationship with any Monitor cannot be captured before the proceeding begins. The challenge mechanism (a drawn member can be challenged for documented conflict of interest) closes the manipulation vector of drawing a convenient panel through structural means rather than targeting individuals.

The result is an accountability mechanism for the accountability mechanism that is structurally independent of the body being protected and the body initiating the removal. The Monitors cannot control who is on the Panel. The Legislature cannot control who is on the Panel. The Panel’s legitimacy comes entirely from the lottery and the transparency of the draw.

What the Monitors Cannot Do

The Monitors are not a guarantee against government misconduct. They are a guarantee that government misconduct will be documented, visible, and attributed. These are not the same thing, and the gap between them deserves to be named honestly.

A government determined to act improperly can do so even under continuous Monitor scrutiny. It can provide false information to Monitors. It can comply with the letter of disclosure requirements while violating their spirit. It can accept an adverse finding, publish a response contesting it, and proceed anyway — accepting the NRS record as the price of the action it wants to take. An Monitor that publishes a finding is not imposing a consequence. It is creating a record. What becomes of that record depends on what other actors — courts, legislatures, electorates — choose to do with it.

This is not a design failure. It is a design choice that reflects a specific theory of democratic accountability: that the most reliable long-term check on government misconduct is an informed citizenry with access to accurate information, not an administrative body with the power to impose consequences. The latter can be captured. The former is distributed across millions of people with diverse interests, none of whom can be captured simultaneously.

The Monitors are the mechanism by which that theory is operationalized. Their value is not in the consequences they impose — they impose none — but in the information they generate and protect. Every finding is permanent. Every suppressed finding can become a minority report. Every removal proceeding is itself documented and published. The institution is not powerful in the traditional sense. It is credible in a way that powerful institutions rarely are, precisely because it has no stake in its own findings beyond getting them right.

The constitutional preamble says the document does not limit government because it distrusts the people who serve within it, but because no document can select only the trustworthy. The Monitors are the institutional expression of that premise. They are not there because the government will be dishonest. They are there because, across a long enough timeline, some part of it inevitably will be, and when that happens the record should exist, should be accessible, and should not be suppressible by the actors whose conduct it documents.

That is what the three Monitors are for. Not to stop bad governments. To make them visible.

Paper No. 11

The Watchmen

On the three Monitors — why accountability requires structural independence, not just goodwill; the cross-nomination model; the information-only mandate; and six structural improvements that show what constitutional maintenance looks like when it works.

The strongest objection to any accountability institution is not that it might be wrong. It is that it might be captured. An oversight body whose legitimacy depends on the goodwill of those it oversees is not an oversight body. It is a permission slip.

Every government that has ever existed has faced the accountability problem: who watches the watchers? The standard constitutional answer is separation of powers — the legislature checks the executive, the courts check the legislature. The separation is real and important, but it has a structural weakness this document takes seriously. Separation of powers relies on each branch's institutional self-interest to generate accountability. But what happens when the legislature and executive agree? What happens when the courts are aligned with both? Self-interest as a check only works when interests diverge. When they converge, the separation produces nothing.

The three Monitors are this document's answer to that failure mode. They are constitutionally independent, selected by processes that structurally exclude the bodies they audit, and mandated to observe and publish rather than to enforce and compel. The design is unusual enough to require explanation.

The Cross-Nomination Model

Each Monitor is constituted through a process that excludes the body being watched from selecting its watchers.§9.2 The SC nominates the Legislative Monitor General — the Legislature that the LM audits is entirely excluded. The Assembly nominates the Executive Monitor General — the executives that the EM audits have no nomination role, and the 2/3 Senate confirmation threshold prevents the governing coalition from confirming without broad Senate consensus. The JM Monitor General is selected by public lottery from a pool of former judges — the JM has no role in constituting or certifying its own candidate pool.

The cross-nomination logic is not complicated: each Monitor's Monitor General is selected by a body that has no interest in the audit outcome. The SC has no stake in how the Legislature is audited. The Assembly has no stake in how the executives are audited at the level of structural accountability. The lottery has no stake in anything. The selection mechanism is designed to produce auditors whose tenure does not depend on the approval of those they audit.

The Information-Only Mandate

The Monitors publish findings. They do not issue binding orders, impose sanctions, or reverse decisions.§9.6 This design looks fragile if you assume publication is just announcement. A government that wants to suppress an inconvenient finding could simply ignore it and wait for the news cycle to move on.

But Monitor findings are published to the NRS. They are permanent. They cannot be deleted, altered, or reclassified. A finding that a Civic Consul exceeded constitutional authority does not expire. It is there when the next election is held, when the next government takes office, when the constitutional scholar writes the account. The NRS converts the Monitors' information-only mandate from a design limitation into a constitutional strength. The finding does not need enforcement power because it has permanence — and permanence turns out to be more durable than enforcement.

There is a deeper reason for the information-only design. An oversight body with enforcement power is not just an oversight body. It is a fourth branch of government with coercive authority and the institutional interest in using it that any bureaucracy develops. Such bodies begin as watchdogs and gradually become actors, developing relationships with those they oversee and accumulating influence that serves institutional interests. The Monitors have nothing worth capturing in this sense. Capturing an Monitor does not change what it has already published. It does not alter the existing record.

The Lottery and the Judicial Watcher

The JM Monitor General is selected by public lottery from a pool of former judges.§9.4.a The pool is maintained by the Elections Panel, certified by the LM and EM — the JM has no role in determining who is in its own candidate pool. The lottery is conducted by the JMC in a public session. The outgoing JM Monitor General does not participate.

The lottery is the right mechanism for the judicial watcher specifically because judicial independence is the value being protected. A JM Monitor General confirmed by the Senate — even by supermajority — has gone through a political process that produces political debts. A JM Monitor General selected by lot from a publicly certified pool owes nothing to anyone. The independence that makes the JM valuable comes precisely from the inability of any political actor to select the individual who holds the role.

The Removal Grounds Review

The IGs themselves must be protectable from politically motivated removal. §9.9 addresses this directly. For-cause removal of any Monitor General requires the charging party to first file stated grounds with the SC; the SC confirms within 14 days whether those grounds constitute a valid statutory removal category. If confirmed, duty suspension activates immediately and the Legislature may proceed to a removal vote. The SC is reviewing constitutional category only — not factual merits. If not confirmed, the process closes and may not be reinitiated on the same or substantially similar grounds for six months. For-cause removal of any Monitor General requires a substantiated finding from the SC — from the combined national pool, with no current federal office. The Panel reviews only whether the stated grounds correspond to an enumerated constitutional category. It does not investigate independently or assess credibility. A substantiated finding authorizes the Legislature to proceed to a 2/3 removal vote.

The design excludes any single federal actor from controlling the removal process. No executive nominates the Panel. No Legislature selects it. No Monitor General from another Monitor controls it. The State pools, the lottery, and the constitutional category review together produce a removal process that is genuinely independent of the political actors most likely to want an inconvenient Monitor General removed.

Six Structural Improvements

The original Monitor design was sound in its architecture. It had gaps — points where the execution did not fully deliver what the architecture intended. Six targeted corrections address those gaps, and what they reveal about constitutional maintenance is as important as what they fix.

The first three addressed appointment and coordination. The JM Monitor General's original selection had the JM maintaining its own candidate pool — a structural inversion of the cross-nomination principle the rest of the design correctly implements. The fix: the eligible class is now former Republic judges certified by the LM and EM, with the JMC conducting the lottery. The JM has no role in selecting who watches it. The EM Monitor General originally required only a simple Senate majority for confirmation — weaker protection than the LM Monitor General despite auditing the executives directly. The fix: 2/3 supermajority, matching the LM Monitor General threshold. The three Monitors were already performing joint functions without a formal institutional home. The fix: the Joint Monitor Council§9.11, which formally houses all jointly certified constitutional functions and provides a standing forum for cross-jurisdictional coordination.

The second three addressed what happens when mechanisms fail rather than gap in appointment. Monitor General term expiry now terminates constitutional authority automatically — no holdover, no further proceeding required, any post-expiry act void.§9.1.d Unexpected mid-term vacancies are filled by pool-sourced Acting IGs — the longest-tenured eligible pool member, with both executives notified, ineligible for the permanent appointment, returning to the pool when done. The institutional compromise protocol§9.11.a addresses the hardest case: not a compromised individual but an entire Monitor operating in bad faith. Two Monitors petition the SC; an Acting Monitor General from the affected body's pool designates immediately; the SC rules within 14 days. And the judicial removal track§3.9.a creates a path through political gridlock that protects a compromised independent officer — two Monitors certify, the SC suspends, the Senate has 60 days, or the matter goes to public election at 60%.

What the Six Fixes Share

Each fix addressed a gap where an assumption broke down. The JM's independence was assumed to follow from the lottery, but the lottery drew from a pool the JM controlled. The EM's independence was assumed to follow from cross-nomination, but the confirmation threshold allowed coalition-only confirmation. The JMC's authority was assumed to follow from individual Monitor General independence, but without a formal structure the joint functions had no institutional weight. The term expiry problem assumed IGs would leave when their terms ended. The acting mechanism assumed internal deputies would provide adequate independence during vacancies. The institutional compromise protocol assumed that individual Monitor General replacement was sufficient for institutional capture.

None of these was a failure of vision. All were the natural consequence of building something complex and finding the points where execution didn't fully deliver. The pattern — assumption failure at a specific mechanism level, targeted fix at that level — is what constitutional maintenance looks like when it is working as it should. The Monitor design is more durable after six fixes than it was before. The fixes are the argument for the design, not against it.
