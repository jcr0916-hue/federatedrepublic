Sunderland Vessel Holds Lake Varda Position for Six Hours. Coast Guard Tracked It. Then It Left.

A naval vessel crossed the contested midline before dawn and withdrew at mid-morning. The Legat Consul's diplomatic note was on the NRS before the vessel cleared the line.

Mira Kael Rhondel Bureau · Rhondel — Year 12, Month 10

The vessel appeared on coast guard tracking at 0347 on Tuesday, approximately 400 meters past the administrative midline, moving at low speed on a course parallel to the Torenthian shore. It held that position for six hours and twenty-three minutes. At 1010 it turned and withdrew into Sunderland-controlled waters. No engagement occurred. No warning was issued. The coast guard tracked it for the duration and logged every position reading.

By the time Rhondel woke up, the Legat Consul's diplomatic note was already on the NRS.

"We know exactly where it was at every minute. We always know. Knowing and doing are different problems."

The person who said that asked not to be named. They have worked the lake for eleven years and have watched the midline become, in their words, a line that Sunderland crosses when it wants to and respects when it doesn't. This is the third time in Year 12 that a Sunderland naval vessel has crossed into Republic-claimed waters. It is the third diplomatic note. The math is not complicated.

What the Coast Guard Did

The coast guard command in Rhondel operates under standing emergency protocols that have been in place since Year 11. Under those protocols, a vessel crossing the administrative midline triggers immediate logging, real-time reporting to the command in Rhondel and to the LC's office in Verentum, and continuous tracking for the duration of the incursion. Engagement — verbal, signaling, or otherwise — requires separate authorization. That authorization was not requested on Tuesday and was not given.

Coast guard officials declined to comment on what specific instructions were in place. The LC's office referred questions to the diplomatic note published to the NRS, reference NRS-Y12-2847, which it described as the Republic's full and formal response. It has nothing further to add at this time.

In Rhondel, the people who live closest to the water have a different view of what Tuesday meant. The fishing cooperative near the northern harbor suspended operations between 0600 and 1100 — standard practice when a foreign naval vessel is in proximity and the coast guard's posture is elevated. They have done this seven times in the past eighteen months. The cooperative's coordinator, Dael Sovern, said she expects to do it again.

"At some point someone has to tell us this stops," she said. "We keep waiting for that conversation."

The Political Dimension

The incident arrived in the same week that Director of Foreign Affairs Ines Carrow addressed the Verentum Foreign Policy Forum with remarks many observers read as a de facto campaign address. Among the positions she staked out was a call for a multilateral coordination framework with Marisvia and Seronne to formalize refugee burden-sharing before the lake situation became a bilateral crisis. She did not address what such a framework would do about Sunderland vessels in contested waters.

Senator Tobias Vael of Harren, the first declared LC candidate, said through a spokesperson on Tuesday that he would have more to say about the lake this week. When asked what specifically, the spokesperson said to watch for it. Vael had said in his declaration speech in Casten last month that the Republic's coast guard was "funded like an afterthought." He has not walked that back.

From the LC's office, nothing beyond the NRS record. Rehn has eleven months left in a term he cannot extend. Whatever his diplomatic note says, he is the one who has to mean it.
