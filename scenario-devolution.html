State Governance · §13.3 · Mandatory Devolution

The Third Audit

The State of Korrath fails its Statehood Audit for the third consecutive year. Early warnings were published. Grants were withheld. A remediation plan was submitted and found inadequate. The Senate votes 76-24 on the Motion of Devolution. Korrath challenges at the Supreme Court within thirty days. The three-year process, and what follows it.

§13.3.a Devolution Triggers §13.3.b Early Warning — Years 1–2 §13.3.c Devolution Vote — Year 3 §13.3.d Effect of Mandatory Devolution §13.3.e Constitutional Challenge §13.1 Territory Status

Korrath is a mid-sized State of 2.3 million citizens. It entered the Republic in Year 4 of the transition, passing its Phase 2 Statehood Audit on the second attempt. It struggled from the beginning: a legacy of underfunded public infrastructure, a revenue base heavily dependent on a single extractive industry in structural decline, and a state judiciary that produced a pattern of §1.8 equality rulings that the LI's published findings described as "systematically insufficient without reasonable legal basis."

Year 1 — The First Failure

Korrath fails its Year 11 annual Statehood Audit on two grounds: fiscal insolvency (its projected revenue ratio falls below the constitutional threshold without federal assistance beyond the Basic Needs Floor) and Article I non-enforcement (the LI documents twenty-three §1.8 cases in which Korrath's state courts dismissed equality claims on procedural grounds the LI characterizes as pretextual).

The LI publishes an Early Warning Report to the NRS and distributes it to every eligible voter in Korrath. The Korrath state government and the Domestic Affairs Council jointly publish a Remediation Plan within the statutory period. The Plan addresses fiscal restructuring through a mineral rights revaluation and addresses Article I enforcement through a mandatory judicial training program. The LI reviews the Plan and publishes a finding: the fiscal component is adequate; the judicial training component is insufficient — training does not address the structural procedural barriers identified in the LI's findings.

The PM does not take action within the statutory period following the LI's inadequacy finding. Federal grant withholding activates automatically. Basic Needs Floor distributions to Korrath residents continue unaffected — the MA classifies them and protects them explicitly.

Year 2 — The Second Failure

Korrath's Year 12 audit fails again. The fiscal position has improved marginally — the mineral rights revaluation produced partial results — but remains below the threshold. The Article I non-enforcement finding is repeated and expanded: forty-one cases, the same pretextual procedural pattern. The Korrath government has amended its judicial procedures statute, but the LI finds the amendments have not changed the pattern of outcomes.

§13.3.b — Second Year Warning
Grant withholding continues. The countdown cannot be reset.

The critical provision of §13.3.a: the countdown resets to zero only if Korrath passes an audit in an intervening year. It has not. Year 2 is the second consecutive failure. Federal grant withholding continues. The LI's published findings for Year 2 note — for the first time in the Republic's history — that a third consecutive failure will trigger the Senate Devolution Vote under §13.3.c. The finding is published to the NRS. Every eligible voter in Korrath receives it.

The Korrath government appeals to the Senate to intervene — to pass legislation overriding the LI's Article I non-enforcement determination. The Senate's legal counsel publishes an advisory opinion: the Senate may not override LI factual findings. The LI's non-enforcement determination is a published statement of observed fact, not a legal order. The only path that resets the counter is a passed audit.

Year 3 — The Vote

Korrath fails its Year 13 audit for the third consecutive year. The fiscal position has not reached the constitutional threshold. The Article I non-enforcement pattern continues. The LI publishes its Year 3 finding with a mandatory statement under §13.3.c: the three-consecutive-failure trigger has been met. The Senate must vote on a Motion of Devolution.

The Senate debate takes eleven days. Korrath's two Senators argue that devolution is disproportionate — a state with genuine structural challenges, not a state acting in bad faith. Other Senators respond that the three-year process provided two years of early warning, federal support, remediation planning, and every institutional tool available. The question before the Senate is not whether to help Korrath — that help has been ongoing — but whether a State that has failed to meet minimum governance standards for three consecutive years should retain the constitutional rights of Statehood.

The Challenge

Under §13.3.e, the affected State has thirty days to petition the Supreme Court for expedited review on a single narrow ground: whether the LI's audit findings, as a factual matter, meet the constitutional trigger standards of §13.3.a. Korrath files on day eighteen. The challenge argues that the Article I non-enforcement findings do not meet the constitutional standard — the LI documented procedural dismissals, but a pattern of procedural dismissals does not constitute "verified failure to enforce Article I protections" under §13.3.a.

The SC rules within the expedited statutory period. The majority opinion is careful and narrow: the Court reviews facts, not policy. The LI documented twenty-three cases in Year 1 and forty-one in Year 2 in which equality claims were dismissed on procedural grounds the LI characterizes as pretextual. The Court's review is limited to whether this factual record meets the constitutional trigger standard. It does. The challenge is dismissed.

Korrath as a Territory

The devolution takes effect on the date the Senate Motion passes. Two Senate seats are vacated immediately. Korrath's House seats are retained proportionally — its citizens continue to have federal legislative representation. Basic Needs Floor distributions continue without interruption. The Domestic Affairs Council assumes administrative oversight. The LI conducts an immediate post-devolution baseline audit.

§13.3.d — What Devolution Does and Does Not Do
Statehood lost. Rights intact. The path back is open immediately.

Every Korrath citizen retains full Article I rights. Devolution removes Statehood — it does not diminish the Individual Sovereignty Floor. Korrath residents vote in House elections. They participate in national elections. Their Basic Needs Floor distributions continue. What they lose is Senate representation and the governance autonomy that comes with Statehood.

Under §13.2, Korrath may begin the Statehood Audit process again immediately. There is no waiting period. The path to restored Statehood is available from the first day of Territory status. What is required is passing three annual audits — one each year, with no consecutive failures. The three-year process that produced devolution is the same three-year process that produces restoration. The constitution's remedial logic is symmetric: the same standard that was not met produces Territory status; meeting it produces Statehood again.

What This Scenario Tests

§13.3's mandatory devolution process is the most significant sanction in the constitution short of the individual accountability mechanisms. It is also — deliberately — slow. Three consecutive audit failures, each preceded by Early Warning Reports, each triggering remediation obligations, each activating federal support tools. A state that reaches year three has had two years of explicit constitutional notice that the next failure will produce this outcome.

The scenario tests the process at its most uncomfortable point: a State that has genuine structural challenges, not bad faith. Korrath's fiscal problem was not manufactured — it inherited an economy in structural decline. Its Article I enforcement problem was a pattern of judicial procedure, not explicit discrimination. The constitution's response to this is not sympathy or flexibility in the threshold — it is the three-year process itself. Two years of early warnings, federal resources, and remediation opportunities constitute the constitution's accommodation for difficult circumstances. The threshold for the third year is the threshold. Korrath didn't meet it.

The SC challenge under §13.3.e is the design's judicial backstop — the narrow review that ensures devolution isn't applied to a state whose audit findings were factually wrong. The Court does not review whether devolution is a proportionate response. It reviews whether the factual record meets the trigger standard. That narrow scope is intentional: it keeps the Court out of governance policy and limits it to factual verification.
