The Devolution

Year 5. Aldenmoor State fails its third Statehood Audit. Citizens vote for devolution. Its senators keep their seats. The constitutional logic of holding democratic mandates through failure.

§13.3 Mandatory Devolution §13.2 The Statehood Audit §13.2.e Minimum Democratic Legitimacy §13.3.e Constitutional Challenge §13.5 Re-Qualification Pathway

Aldenmoor's three-year audit record was a study in incremental failure. After the first Early Warning, its government published a remediation plan that addressed the electoral process failures directly but left the Article I compliance gaps — specifically in due process protections in administrative detention — without a credible timeline. After the second warning, the plan was updated to add a timeline. The timeline passed without implementation. After the third audit failure, the LI's certification was joined by both the EI and JI confirmations that §13.3 requires before devolution can be initiated. The constitutional machinery moved to the referendum stage.

Aldenmoor's citizens voted 61% to 39% in favour of devolution over the federal remediation commission alternative. The vote was not, in most analyses, a vote in favour of Territory status. It was a vote against the specific composition of the proposed remediation commission — a body that Aldenmoor's political leadership had successfully characterised as federal interference. The constitutional choice was between two paths to the same destination. Citizens chose the path without direct federal governance, accepting the implications of Territory status rather than ceding administrative control.

Path A — The Senators — §13.3

Aldenmoor had two sitting senators at the time of devolution. Under §13.3, sitting senators serve the remainder of their current elected terms. Senate seats are not vacated. No new Senate elections may be held for the devolved State until Statehood is restored.

§13.3 — Why Senators Keep Their Seats
Senate seats are not vacated. Citizens who elected those senators are not stripped of their representation because their government failed.

The design choice is explicit. Aldenmoor's senators were elected by citizens who voted in good faith. Those citizens did not fail the Statehood Audit — their government did. Stripping them of Senate representation would compound the consequence of a government failure onto individuals who had no individual role in producing it. Path A holds the democratic mandate intact: senators serve until their terms expire, then the seats go vacant until restoration.

Senator Kofi Larbi had two years remaining in his term. Senator Yuki Tanabe had four. Both remained in the Senate, voting on all matters as full members. The constitutional tension this creates — senators representing a Territory rather than a State — is real. It is also the price of not punishing voters for their government's failures.

The Audit Challenge — §13.3.e

Aldenmoor's government filed a constitutional challenge within 30 days of devolution taking effect under §13.3.e. The challenge had a narrow ground: the LI's audit findings were, in Aldenmoor's submission, factually insufficient to establish three consecutive failures on the electoral process criteria. The government argued that the audit had applied a higher standard to its electoral processes than the constitutional minimum required.

The SC reviewed only the factual basis of the audit findings — not the policy merits of devolution, not the State's economic circumstances, not whether devolution was proportionate. Its ruling, issued on Day 14, found the LI's findings factually sufficient. The three consecutive failures were documented with specific evidence in each audit cycle. The challenge failed. Devolution stood.

Territory Status and the Restoration Path — §13.5

Aldenmoor Territory's first year under §13.1 brought Article I protections and social state access without interruption — the constitution's guarantee that devolution does not punish citizens for their government's failures applies to rights and social protections as well as to electoral representation. The federal tax exemption applied immediately. The Domestic Affairs Council exercised oversight without replacing the elected State government, which continued to govern under State law.

What This Scenario Tests

§13.3 Path A is the constitution's answer to the democratic tension in mandatory devolution. The remediation ladder — three consecutive audits, Early Warnings, published plans, citizen referendum — ensures the process is proportionate and documented before any status change occurs. The Path A consequence — senators keep seats, no new elections until restoration, Article I protections continue — ensures the citizens of a devolved State are not punished for their government's failures beyond the loss of State constitutional status itself.

The §13.3.e challenge demonstrates that devolution is not beyond judicial review. The SC can find that the audit findings are factually insufficient. It can order a stay pending ruling. What it cannot do is review the policy judgment that a State failing three consecutive audits should face consequences. The factual threshold is reviewable; the constitutional design is not.

The restoration pathway under §13.5 is the other half of the mandatory devolution design. Territory status is not punitive in perpetuity. The moment Aldenmoor passes the Statehood Audit and completes the Provisional Membership Period, it is a State again — automatically, without requiring a political grant of permission. The devolution is a consequence; the restoration is a right.
