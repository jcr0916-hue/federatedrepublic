The Question Nobody Running Can Answer

Three candidates for Legat Consul spent this week being asked, in one form or another, who is going to balance Elin Thoss. It is the only question the rooms want. It is also a question about an office none of them is running for — and how each of them handled that is the most useful thing this race has shown us yet.

Lenne Akar TNB Caldenmere · Political Correspondent · Calden — Year 13, Month 9

I have covered four campaign stops in six days, in three States, and the question has come in every accent this network broadcasts to. A grain co-operative in western Orath asked it politely. A hall in Calden asked it twice. In Rhovane a man who described himself as having voted in every election since the Founding stood up and asked it without preamble: who is going to stop her?

He did not mean the lake. Nobody has meant the lake for eleven days.

And here is the difficulty, which I want to state plainly before we get to how the candidates answered, because the answer to the question is a fact about the Constitution and not an opinion about any of them: the Legat Consul cannot stop her. Not on this. Not on anything the Civic Consul does inside her own domain.

Two Offices, Two Domains, No Overlap

The Republic's executive is two people, and the Constitution divides their authority by subject rather than by rank. Under §2.1 the Legat Consul holds military command, foreign affairs, treaty negotiation, and the recognition of foreign governments. Under §2.5 the Civic Consul holds the domestic residual — which is to say everything else the executive does. Neither is senior to the other. Neither confirms, removes, reviews, or vetoes the other.

The fiscal-equalization mechanism the new Civic Consul has just sent back to the Legislature is domestic. So is the queue underneath it. So are the ministries she named last week. A Legat Consul — Vael, Carrow, Mak, whoever wins — will have precisely as much formal authority over the §12.8 review as any other citizen of the Republic, which is to say the authority to have an opinion about it.

What the two offices share is a table, not a hierarchy. §2.14 seats them jointly in the Council of Ministers for matters that cross their domains, and that body, in the Constitution's own words, coordinates positions and makes recommendations; it does not exercise executive authority. There is a mechanism, in §2.14.b, for one Consul to ask the other to act inside the other's domain — and it says in terms that neither may compel the other, and that the responding Consul decides whether and how to act.

That is the whole of it. Ask, publish, and be told no if the answer is no.

Three Answers

Senator Tobias Vael was asked in Calden on Tuesday and said it outright. The office does not reach domestic policy, he said; anyone who tells you they will govern the Civic Consul from the Legat Consul's chair is telling you something the Constitution does not permit, and you should ask yourself what else they will tell you. He then spent nine minutes on the actual domain — the Sunderland file, the standing of the crossing, what a treaty framework would require — to a room that had come for something else.

It is the same instinct we saw when he owned the tributary error in public rather than let it be dug out of him. It costs him something every time. It is also, on the evidence of six days in the field, the only answer given this week that was entirely true.

Director Ines Carrow was asked in Orath and did not correct the premise. She said the Republic will need judgment at the top through what is coming, that she has spent a career being the person in the room who says the uncomfortable thing, and that voters should consider carefully what kind of counterweight they want in the executive. Every clause of that is defensible on its own. Together, in a room asking who stops her, they are a promise the office cannot keep — and the room heard a promise.

Her campaign, asked afterward whether the Director was claiming authority over domestic policy, said she was not, and pointed to §2.14. That is a correct citation to a provision about coordination, and it is not what the room heard.

Assembly member Seren Mak refused the frame in a different way, which was to talk about the border for eleven straight minutes and decline to discuss the Civic Consul at all. Asked directly a second time, he said the Legat Consul's job is the Republic's external security and that he intended to run for that job and no other. Whether that is discipline or evasion depends on the listener; both readings were audible in the room.

Pell Sandris, who has been running a campaign of procedural argument that the other three have largely ignored, was the only one to name the mechanism that actually answers the question, and did it in a hall in Caldenmere to perhaps forty people. If you want the Civic Consul balanced, he said, you are not looking for a Legat Consul. You are looking for an Assembly.

"Two hundred and fifty-one members can end her government on any afternoon they choose, without asking me, without asking any of us. That is the check. It is not a person. It is a vote you already control, and it belongs to the people you elected last cycle — and to the ones you will elect next."

Why the Wrong Question Is Still Worth Something

It would be easy to file this as an electorate confused about its own constitution, and there is some of that. But I think the confusion is doing something more interesting than being wrong.

The Republic has just watched an office change hands in fourteen minutes by a vote most of the country did not know was coming, held by a chamber it does not think about much between elections. It went looking, reasonably, for the counterweight — and the constitutional answer is not a person at all. It is §2.6 itself: an absolute majority of the Assembly, at any time, for any reason, with a successor named in the same breath. The check on the new Civic Consul is the identical mechanism that produced her, and it is held by the same 500 members, several of whom are already saying aloud that the coming fight about the equalization formula is exactly the kind of thing that moves votes.

People are asking the Legat Consul candidates because a race is what is in front of them, and because a person is easier to imagine than a procedure. What they are actually asking about is the Assembly. The answer to who stops her was decided at the last general election and will be revisited at the next one, and it was never going to be on this ballot.

Three candidates got asked a question about an office they are not seeking. One said so, one let it stand, one walked around it, and the fourth — in front of forty people — answered it correctly. We will find out in the spring which of those the Republic rewards.
