Voting Rights · §7.1 · NVS Access

The Harder Ballot

Year 7. Korda State introduces an in-person verification requirement that effectively reduces NVS access in rural districts by 40%. Citizens challenge under §7.1's prohibition on making voting more difficult.

§7.1 The Right to Vote §7.3 Federal Elections §11.2 NVS Properties and Voting Rights §9.1.a The Legislative Monitor

Korda State's Voting Integrity Act required voters to present government-issued biometric credentials at designated verification terminals before the NVS would accept their ballot. The terminals were installed at 847 locations statewide. Rural districts received one terminal per fifteen hundred square kilometers. Urban districts received one per four square kilometers. Drive times to the nearest terminal in rural Korda ranged from twelve minutes to four hours and twenty minutes.

The Act passed Korda's legislature 61-39. The State's Attorney General certified it as a fraud prevention measure. Within three weeks of the Act taking effect, the Elections Panel published a NVS access report showing a 41% reduction in submitted federal ballots from Korda's rural districts compared to the prior electoral period.

The Constitutional Challenge — §7.1

Three civic organizations and seventeen individual citizens petitioned the SC under §7.1. The petition was direct: the Voting Integrity Act made voting more difficult. The constitutional text was equally direct: no law, rule, administrative decision, or practice that reduces access to the franchise is constitutionally valid.

§7.1 — The Test Is Simple
Government at every level is prohibited from taking any action that makes voting more difficult, less accessible, or less secure.

Korda's defense was that the Act made voting more secure. The SC's ruling engaged directly with the tension between the accessibility and security requirements in §7.1: "The state holds an affirmative obligation to make voting as accessible as possible consistent with security. Consistent with security does not mean security at any cost to accessibility. The state must demonstrate that the security benefit is genuine, proportionate, and that no less restrictive means achieves the same purpose. A 41% reduction in rural ballot submission is not consistent with the affirmative accessibility obligation."

The Act was void. Korda had 90 days to submit a revised verification framework that the Elections Panel would certify as NVS-compatible. The Elections Panel administered the next federal electoral period under the prior access protocols in Korda while the revised framework was developed.

The LM's Compliance Finding

The LM published a compliance notice noting Korda's Act as a constitutional violation certified by the SC. The notice specified the 41% access reduction figure and Korda's 90-day remediation window. It did not impose any sanction — the LM's mandate is information and compliance, not enforcement. The sanction was the SC ruling itself: the Act was void.

§7.3 — No State May Reduce NVS Access
No State may take any action that reduces NVS access, functionality, or availability within its territory.

§7.3's NVS protection provision is the companion to §7.1's rights guarantee. §7.1 states the principle; §7.3 makes it operational by specifying that NVS access is the constitutional floor and no State may reduce it. The Elections Panel's access report — showing the 41% rural reduction — was the evidentiary foundation for the SC's proportionality analysis. The NVS data is constitutionally transparent: access reductions are visible in the published record before, during, and after any electoral period.

What This Scenario Tests

§7.1 creates a constitutional test that is simple to state and difficult to evade: does this action make voting more difficult? The Korda Act made voting more difficult for rural citizens. That the Act also made voting more secure does not save it — §7.1 requires the state to achieve security without reducing accessibility, not to choose between them. The burden is on the government to show that its security measure is proportionate and that no less restrictive means achieves the same purpose.

The scenario also demonstrates the constitutional relationship between §7.1, §7.3, and the Elections Panel. The Panel's NVS access reporting is not advisory — it is the constitutional measurement system that makes §7.1's guarantee enforceable. A state that reduces NVS access produces a visible, documented, NRS-published record of that reduction. The constitutional rights violation is not abstract; it is quantified and permanent.
