Official Benefits · §10.7 · EI Certification

The Benefits Audit

Year 5. The PM requests a dedicated aircraft and an expanded twelve-person security detail. The EI certifies that one is operationally justified. The other is not.

§10.7 Compensation of Public Officials §8.1.b The Executive Inspectorate §9.1 National Record System

PM Kwame Asante filed a formal accommodation request in Year 5 following two security incidents — one involving a credible threat to his family residence, one involving an attempt to access his scheduled travel itinerary. The request had two components: an expanded personal security detail of twelve officers (up from four), and a dedicated government aircraft for all domestic and international travel.

Under §10.7, official benefits and accommodations beyond compensation reflect the functional requirements of the office, not the status of the officeholder. Any departure from ordinary professional standards requires a demonstrable operational need certified by the EI, and only to the minimum extent that need requires.

The EI Assessment

The EI published its assessment 21 days after the formal request. The assessment was binary — each component either met the §10.7 standard or it did not.

Security Detail — Operational Need Certified
Demonstrable operational need: certified. Minimum extent: eight officers, not twelve.

The EI reviewed the two security incidents, the PM's travel pattern, and the threat assessment provided by the EI's own security division. It certified that an expanded security detail was operationally justified given the documented and credible threats. It did not certify twelve officers. Its assessment — published to the NRS — found that eight officers was the minimum extent required to address the documented threats while maintaining operational flexibility. Twelve exceeded the minimum.

The PM accepted the eight-officer certification. The four additional requested officers were not assigned.

Dedicated Aircraft — Operational Need Not Certified
No demonstrable operational need for a dedicated aircraft. Existing government fleet arrangements meet the functional requirement.

The EI's aircraft assessment reviewed the PM's travel schedule, the availability of the Republic's shared government fleet, and whether the PM's travel pattern had produced scheduling conflicts or security compromises attributable to shared fleet use. It found one minor scheduling conflict in the prior year that had been resolved within two hours. It found no security compromise attributable to shared fleet use. The assessment concluded that a dedicated aircraft exceeded what the PM's functions genuinely required and that the shared fleet arrangement met the functional requirement.

The PM did not challenge the assessment. The NRS published both assessments — the certified security detail and the declined aircraft — permanently as part of the Republic's official record of executive accommodation decisions.

The Public Record

Citizens and press organizations read the EI's published assessment. Several commentators noted that the PM had accepted the EI's partial denial without public objection — and that this acceptance was itself constitutionally significant. The EI's role is certification, not enforcement. A PM who rejected the EI's assessment and obtained the aircraft anyway would face a constitutional compliance breach finding. A PM who accepted it demonstrated that the §10.7 mechanism worked without requiring further enforcement.

What This Scenario Tests

§10.7 tests two constitutional principles simultaneously. The first is the functional benefits rule: accommodations reflect what the office requires, not what the officeholder's status might suggest. The second is the EI certification mechanism: the EI is the independent body that determines whether any claimed operational need is genuine and proportionate. Neither executive can self-certify their own accommodation needs.

The minimum extent requirement is the provision's constitutional discipline. Even where operational need is genuine — the security detail was certified — the accommodation cannot exceed what the need genuinely requires. This prevents the legitimate exception (security threat) from becoming the general rule (expanded security as a status marker). The permanent NRS record of each certification makes the distinction between legitimate operational need and status-based preference visible across administrations.
