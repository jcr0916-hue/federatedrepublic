The Amendment Architecture

On how the document changes, and why six provisions cannot change at all.

~2,160 words
A constitution that cannot be changed is not a gift to future generations. It is a burden on them — the founding generation's final act of arrogance, the insistence that their judgments about what a just government requires will remain correct in perpetuity regardless of what subsequent generations learn, experience, or decide. The democratic tradition is rightly suspicious of such insistence. The people who will live under a constitution should be able to change it.

This document can be changed. Almost all of it. By two different paths, both requiring substantial democratic consensus, neither requiring the consent of the executive. Almost all of it — but not quite everything. Six provisions cannot be changed by any process. The case for both parts of that design — the openness and the limits — is the case for the amendment architecture.

Three Paths, One Principle

✦

The document provides three amendment pathways — Path A (Parliamentary), Path B (Parliamentary and Popular), and Path C (Citizen-Led) — designed on the same principle: constitutional change should require more than a legislative majority but less than practical impossibility.

Path A — Parliamentary Amendment requires a 3/4 supermajority of the full seated membership of both chambers in a single concurrent vote. No popular referendum. No executive signature. Every vote is published to the NRS within 24 hours. The 3/4 threshold is the highest legislative supermajority in the constitution — near-unanimous agreement across both chambers required. No executive plays any role at any stage. No executive can. Path B — Parliamentary and Popular Amendment sets a lower legislative threshold — 2/3 of both chambers — but requires the People to ratify. A national popular vote must follow within 180 days, achieving 55% affirmative support with at least 55% citizen participation. The EC and LI certify jointly. Neither stage alone is sufficient. Path B exists because some amendments command broad popular support without commanding near-unanimous legislative agreement — and citizens deserve a constitutional route to ratify them directly.

Path C — Citizen-Led Amendment requires 70% affirmative support on at least 65% participation. It is the less familiar mechanism and the more significant innovation. It exists because Path B is the alternative when Path A's near-unanimous threshold is unavailable: it retains legislative buy-in at a lower bar while adding direct popular ratification, responsive to partisan incentives, capable of blocking amendments that command wide popular support if those amendments threaten incumbent political interests. The Direct Democracy Path allows citizens to bypass the State legislature layer entirely — to amend their constitution directly, provided they can demonstrate not just majority support but supermajority support combined with broad participation. The 70% threshold ensures this is not a simple majority mechanism. The 65% participation requirement ensures that a supermajority of voters is also a substantial fraction of citizens, not a supermajority of a thin turnout.

The participation safety valve is worth examining carefully. If participation falls between 60% and 65% but the affirmative vote exceeds 75%, the Electoral Commission schedules a single second vote on the same amendment text. If the second vote again fails to meet the participation threshold, the amendment fails entirely. This provision prevents the participation requirement from becoming a veto by abstention — a mechanism by which opponents of an amendment could suppress the result by discouraging turnout rather than winning the argument. A 75% affirmative vote is an extraordinary democratic mandate. If the reason 64% of citizens voted rather than 65% is that voting access failed in certain regions, that shortfall does not represent the democratic judgment that the participation threshold is meant to measure.

The absence of executive veto is the third element of the design and deserves explicit statement. Neither the President nor the Prime Minister holds any authority over constitutional amendments. An amendment ratified by the required threshold takes immediate effect when the Inspectorates certify it. The executives are bound by what their citizens and States have decided. This is the correct design for a democracy in which the constitution governs the executives rather than the executives governing the constitution.

Why Three Paths

✦

The three paths are not redundant. They serve different constitutional functions and will be used in different circumstances, reflecting different sources of democratic authority and different kinds of amendment.

Path A — the parliamentary supermajority — is the appropriate mechanism for amendments that affect the federal structure itself: changes to Senate representation, to the balance between State and federal authority, to the regional architecture of presidential elections. These are amendments where the States, as constitutional actors with their own standing, have a direct stake in the outcome. The 3/4 requirement ensures that amendments affecting the federal structure can only succeed if they command genuinely broad support across both chambers. The threshold does not prevent the change — it ensures the change has the kind of consensus that makes it durable.

Path B — the combined legislative and popular vote — is the appropriate mechanism for amendments that command wide public support but face institutional resistance in the Legislature. The lower legislative threshold (2/3) reflects a deliberate judgment that some amendments should not require near-unanimous legislative agreement if the People are prepared to ratify them directly. Neither stage alone is sufficient — the Legislature must still act, and the People must still affirm.

Path C — the citizen-led path — is the appropriate mechanism for amendments that reflect citizen judgment about what the constitution should protect or guarantee: the expansion of rights, the clarification of ambiguous provisions, the correction of design errors that citizens have identified over time. A State legislature that resists an amendment expanding individual rights may have institutional incentives that diverge from the citizens it represents. Path C allows citizens to act on their own constitutional judgment without requiring legislative buy-in at all.

The three paths also create a constitutional redundancy that protects against each path’s vulnerabilities. If near-unanimous legislative agreement is unavailable for a broadly supported amendment, Path B or C remains. If citizens seek to change the constitution in ways that reflect a temporary movement rather than durable consensus, Paths A and B’s higher thresholds and participation requirements provide structural resistance. No single path is necessary; no single obstacle is fatal. Together, they do.

The Unamendable Provisions

✦

Six provisions cannot be changed by any process — the prohibition on torture (§1.2), the prohibition on slavery and forced labor (§1.3), the right to First Appearance and Habeas Corpus (§1.13), the right to a public trial (§1.17), the prohibition on retroactive criminal laws (§1.18), and the prohibition on refoulement (§1.27). They are permanent. They are not subject to the Path A 3/4 parliamentary threshold. They are not subject to the Path B combined threshold. They are not subject to the Path C 70% citizen threshold. They cannot be removed, qualified, or altered by any constitutional process this document provides.

This is the most significant constraint in the amendment architecture and it requires the most careful defense.

The defense begins with what the six provisions share. They are not policy preferences elevated to permanent constitutional status. They are not economic arrangements, not institutional designs, not spending commitments. They are the minimum conditions under which a person can be said to live under law rather than under arbitrary power. A government that can torture its citizens is not a constitutional government — it is a government that has used constitutional forms to sanction violence against the people it is supposed to serve. A government that can enslave, detain without charge, convict without public trial, or punish for acts that were legal when committed is not governing its citizens. It is preying on them.

These five prohibitions define the threshold below which a government ceases to be the kind of government this document establishes. They are not the most expansive rights in the document — Article I contains many more protections with broader scope. They are the most foundational: the ones whose removal would not merely reduce the rights citizens enjoy but would transform the nature of the government itself. A document that could be amended to permit torture would not be an amended version of this constitution. It would be a different document with a different theory of what government is.

The democratic objection to unamendable provisions is serious and deserves a direct answer. If citizens can amend almost everything, why can they not amend these? The answer is that democratic authority derives its legitimacy from the premise that the people exercising it are free and secure in their persons. A democratic majority that votes to permit torture of a minority has not exercised democratic authority — it has used democratic forms to deny the conditions that make democratic authority legitimate. The five unamendable provisions are the conditions of legitimate democratic authority, not constraints on it. They cannot be removed by democratic majorities for the same reason that democratic majorities cannot vote to eliminate elections: the vote would be using the mechanism to destroy the mechanism's own conditions of validity.

The alignment between §15.3.a's unamendable provisions and §1.25.b's non-derogable emergency rights is not coincidental. These are the same six provisions. What the document declares cannot be removed by amendment, it also declares cannot be suspended by emergency. The two provisions together define the absolute floor of the Republic's obligations to its citizens — the rights that hold regardless of the constitutional weather. An amendment that cannot alter these rights and an emergency declaration that cannot suspend them arrive at the same place from different directions: the Republic that cannot protect its citizens' basic humanity in an emergency has not declared an emergency. It has committed atrocities under emergency's name.

The Close

✦

The document that trusts citizens with the full truth of how their government operates trusts them to change it. Almost all of it, through two paths that require genuine democratic consensus without requiring practical unanimity. It trusts them enough to allow them to expand rights beyond what the founding generation provided, to correct institutional designs that time reveals as flawed, to adapt the constitutional framework to circumstances that the founding generation could not anticipate.

It does not trust any democratic majority to remove the conditions under which democratic authority is legitimate. Not because democratic majorities cannot be trusted in general — the entire document is built on the premise that they can. But because the specific question "should we permit torture?" is not a question democratic authority is competent to answer. It is the question that determines whether what follows is democratic authority or something that has borrowed democratic authority's forms while abandoning its foundations.

The amendment architecture closes Volume II where Volume I's first provision began. Article I opens with the Individual Sovereignty Floor — the foundational statement that the Republic's authority exists to protect the people within it, not to predate on them. Article XV closes with the six provisions that cannot be changed because they are the minimum expression of that protection. The constitutional circle is complete: the document begins by establishing what citizens are owed and ends by declaring that the core of what they are owed cannot be taken away by any process.

The wager this document makes — that citizens trusted with the full truth of how their government operates will make reasonable decisions with it — includes the wager that they will make reasonable decisions about the document itself. The amendment architecture is the mechanism by which that wager is honored, and the five unamendable provisions are the acknowledgment that some questions are prior to the wager. You cannot bet on democratic judgment to answer the question of whether democratic judgment is legitimate. Some things must be settled before the betting begins.
