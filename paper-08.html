The Amendment Architecture

On how the document changes, and why five provisions cannot change at all.

~2,160 words
A constitution that cannot be changed is not a gift to future generations. It is a burden on them — the founding generation's final act of arrogance, the insistence that their judgments about what a just government requires will remain correct in perpetuity regardless of what subsequent generations learn, experience, or decide. The democratic tradition is rightly suspicious of such insistence. The people who will live under a constitution should be able to change it.

This document can be changed. Almost all of it. By two different paths, both requiring substantial democratic consensus, neither requiring the consent of the executive. Almost all of it — but not quite everything. Five provisions cannot be changed by any process. The case for both parts of that design — the openness and the limits — is the case for the amendment architecture.

Two Paths, One Principle

✦

The document provides two amendment pathways — the State Convention Path and the Direct Democracy Path — designed on the same principle: constitutional change should require more than a parliamentary majority but less than practical impossibility.

The State Convention Path requires ratification by 3/4 of State legislatures. It is the more familiar mechanism, drawing directly from the federal amendment tradition. It ensures that constitutional change has geographic breadth — that amendments reflect the support of communities distributed across the Republic rather than a majority concentrated in its most populous areas. The Legislative Inspectorate coordinates the ratification process, receives and verifies State votes, certifies the threshold, and records the amendment to the National Record System. No executive plays any role. No executive can.

The Direct Democracy Path requires a national vote achieving 70% affirmative on at least 65% participation. It is the less familiar mechanism and the more significant innovation. It exists because the State Convention Path has a specific vulnerability: State legislatures are political bodies, responsive to partisan incentives, capable of blocking amendments that command wide popular support if those amendments threaten incumbent political interests. The Direct Democracy Path allows citizens to bypass the State legislature layer entirely — to amend their constitution directly, provided they can demonstrate not just majority support but supermajority support combined with broad participation. The 70% threshold ensures this is not a simple majority mechanism. The 65% participation requirement ensures that a supermajority of voters is also a substantial fraction of citizens, not a supermajority of a thin turnout.

The participation safety valve is worth examining carefully. If participation falls between 60% and 65% but the affirmative vote exceeds 75%, the Judicial Inspectorate may certify the amendment valid if it finds that the participation shortfall resulted from systemic access failures rather than genuine civic disengagement. This provision prevents the participation requirement from becoming a veto by abstention — a mechanism by which opponents of an amendment could suppress the result by discouraging turnout rather than winning the argument. A 75% affirmative vote is an extraordinary democratic mandate. If the reason 64% of citizens voted rather than 65% is that voting access failed in certain regions, that shortfall does not represent the democratic judgment that the participation threshold is meant to measure.

The absence of executive veto is the third element of the design and deserves explicit statement. Neither the President nor the Prime Minister holds any authority over constitutional amendments. An amendment ratified by the required threshold takes immediate effect when the Inspectorates certify it. The executives are bound by what their citizens and States have decided. This is the correct design for a democracy in which the constitution governs the executives rather than the executives governing the constitution.

Why Two Paths

✦

The two paths are not redundant. They serve different constitutional functions and will be used in different circumstances.

The State Convention Path is the appropriate mechanism for amendments that affect the federal structure itself — changes to Senate representation, to the balance between State and federal authority, to the regional architecture of presidential elections. These are amendments where the States, as constitutional actors with their own standing, have a direct stake in the outcome. The 3/4 requirement ensures that amendments affecting the federal structure can only succeed if they command genuinely broad support among the States. A constitutional change that a bare majority of States want but a quarter oppose is a change that creates legitimate constitutional grievance among the States in the minority. The threshold does not prevent the change — it ensures the change has the kind of consensus that makes it durable.

The Direct Democracy Path is the appropriate mechanism for amendments that reflect citizen judgment about what the constitution should protect or guarantee — the expansion of rights, the clarification of ambiguous provisions, the correction of design errors that citizens have identified over time. These are amendments where the States-as-political-bodies may have institutional interests that diverge from the citizens' interests. A State legislature that resists an amendment expanding individual rights is a State legislature whose institutional incentives may not align with the citizens it represents. The Direct Democracy Path allows citizens to act on their own judgment in those cases.

The two paths also create a constitutional redundancy that protects against each path's vulnerabilities. If State legislatures are captured by interests opposed to a genuinely popular amendment, the Direct Democracy Path remains. If a temporary popular movement seeks to change the constitution in ways that lack genuine geographic breadth, the State Convention Path requires that breadth. Neither path alone would provide both protections. Together, they do.

The Unamendable Provisions

✦

Five provisions cannot be changed by any process — the prohibition on torture (§1.2), the prohibition on slavery and forced labor (§1.3), the right to First Appearance and Habeas Corpus (§1.13), the right to a public trial (§1.17), and the prohibition on retroactive criminal laws (§1.18). They are permanent. They are not subject to the 3/4 State Convention threshold. They are not subject to the 70% Direct Democracy threshold. They cannot be removed, qualified, or altered by any constitutional process this document provides.

This is the most significant constraint in the amendment architecture and it requires the most careful defense.

The defense begins with what the five provisions share. They are not policy preferences elevated to permanent constitutional status. They are not economic arrangements, not institutional designs, not spending commitments. They are the minimum conditions under which a person can be said to live under law rather than under arbitrary power. A government that can torture its citizens is not a constitutional government — it is a government that has used constitutional forms to sanction violence against the people it is supposed to serve. A government that can enslave, detain without charge, convict without public trial, or punish for acts that were legal when committed is not governing its citizens. It is preying on them.

These five prohibitions define the threshold below which a government ceases to be the kind of government this document establishes. They are not the most expansive rights in the document — Article I contains many more protections with broader scope. They are the most foundational: the ones whose removal would not merely reduce the rights citizens enjoy but would transform the nature of the government itself. A document that could be amended to permit torture would not be an amended version of this constitution. It would be a different document with a different theory of what government is.

The democratic objection to unamendable provisions is serious and deserves a direct answer. If citizens can amend almost everything, why can they not amend these? The answer is that democratic authority derives its legitimacy from the premise that the people exercising it are free and secure in their persons. A democratic majority that votes to permit torture of a minority has not exercised democratic authority — it has used democratic forms to deny the conditions that make democratic authority legitimate. The five unamendable provisions are the conditions of legitimate democratic authority, not constraints on it. They cannot be removed by democratic majorities for the same reason that democratic majorities cannot vote to eliminate elections: the vote would be using the mechanism to destroy the mechanism's own conditions of validity.

The alignment between §15.3.a's unamendable provisions and §1.25.b's non-derogable emergency rights is not coincidental. These are the same five provisions. What the document declares cannot be removed by amendment, it also declares cannot be suspended by emergency. The two provisions together define the absolute floor of the Republic's obligations to its citizens — the rights that hold regardless of the constitutional weather. An amendment that cannot alter these rights and an emergency declaration that cannot suspend them arrive at the same place from different directions: the Republic that cannot protect its citizens' basic humanity in an emergency has not declared an emergency. It has committed atrocities under emergency's name.

The Close

✦

The document that trusts citizens with the full truth of how their government operates trusts them to change it. Almost all of it, through two paths that require genuine democratic consensus without requiring practical unanimity. It trusts them enough to allow them to expand rights beyond what the founding generation provided, to correct institutional designs that time reveals as flawed, to adapt the constitutional framework to circumstances that the founding generation could not anticipate.

It does not trust any democratic majority to remove the conditions under which democratic authority is legitimate. Not because democratic majorities cannot be trusted in general — the entire document is built on the premise that they can. But because the specific question "should we permit torture?" is not a question democratic authority is competent to answer. It is the question that determines whether what follows is democratic authority or something that has borrowed democratic authority's forms while abandoning its foundations.

The amendment architecture closes Volume II where Volume I's first provision began. Article I opens with the Individual Sovereignty Floor — the foundational statement that the Republic's authority exists to protect the people within it, not to predate on them. Article XV closes with the five provisions that cannot be changed because they are the minimum expression of that protection. The constitutional circle is complete: the document begins by establishing what citizens are owed and ends by declaring that the core of what they are owed cannot be taken away by any process.

The wager this document makes — that citizens trusted with the full truth of how their government operates will make reasonable decisions with it — includes the wager that they will make reasonable decisions about the document itself. The amendment architecture is the mechanism by which that wager is honored, and the five unamendable provisions are the acknowledgment that some questions are prior to the wager. You cannot bet on democratic judgment to answer the question of whether democratic judgment is legitimate. Some things must be settled before the betting begins.
