Pool Nomination · Transparency · §9.13 · §2.4

The Visible Field

Year 4. The President nominates a Director of Intelligence from the pool. The pool has eleven certified candidates. The nominee ranked ninth by published qualifications. The Senate asks why.

§9.13 Constitutional Pool Framework §2.4 Presidential Domain Operations §9.4.b SC Candidate Registry §9.1.b The Executive Inspectorate

When President Calloway nominated Marcus Vreeland as Director of Intelligence in Year 4, the Elections Panel published the nomination to the NRS within the hour. That publication included, as the constitution requires, the full pool record: eleven certified candidates, their eligibility certifications, and the publicly stated qualification criteria. Vreeland appeared ninth in experience-years. He had led a regional intelligence analysis unit for six years. The top three candidates had led comparable operations for twelve, fifteen, and eighteen years respectively.

The President has full discretion to nominate anyone from the pool. The constitution does not require the most qualified candidate. It requires a pool-certified candidate. Vreeland was pool-certified. The nomination was constitutionally valid from the moment it was published.

The Senate Intelligence Committee — Week Three

Senator Yuki Osei-Tanabe opened the confirmation hearing with a question that would have been impossible in any prior appointment system: "The pool has eleven certified candidates. You are not the most experienced among them. Can you tell this Committee why the President chose you?"

The question was not procedurally improper. The pool record was public. The committee had read it. The nominee's answer — that President Calloway had selected him for his operational philosophy on intelligence oversight and cooperation with the EI, which differed from the candidates with longer field records — was also on the record.

§9.13 — The Pool Makes the Choice Visible
Pool entry, certification, and records are public NRS records. The nominating body's discretion is preserved. Its accountability to the public is constitutionally guaranteed.

Two of the more senior pool candidates appeared before the Senate Intelligence Committee as outside witnesses — not to oppose Vreeland but to explain their own qualifications. The committee's published report noted that the President's choice reflected a legitimate policy judgment about intelligence oversight philosophy rather than an assessment that Vreeland was unqualified. The report recommended confirmation. The Senate confirmed 71-29.

The exchange that no prior system had permitted — a nominee explaining the President's choice among publicly known alternatives — had happened. Not because the constitution required an explanation, but because the pool made the question articulable and the NRS made the answer permanent.

The Second Face of Power — Made Visible

Political scientists had long described appointment power's "second face": not the visible act of choosing, but the invisible shaping of who was considered plausible. In systems without a public pool, this second face was unchallengeable — the executive's private consideration set was unknowable.

In Year 4, a Senate committee asked a question the pool made possible: why this one and not those ten? The President retained full discretion. The nominee was confirmed. But the discretion was exercised in public, against a known field, with an explanation that is now permanently part of the NRS record of the Republic's intelligence leadership history.

What This Scenario Tests

§9.13's pool framework does not constrain appointment discretion — it constitutionalizes visibility. The President nominated a pool-certified candidate and the nomination was valid. What changed is that the Senate, the press, and the public could see the full field and ask why this choice rather than another. The EI plays no role in the nomination itself; the pool's transparency is self-executing through the NRS. The accountability is democratic, not institutional.

The scenario also illustrates the pool's effect on the confirmation process. A 71-29 confirmation of the ninth-ranked candidate would have been uncontroversial in a system without the pool record. In a system where the pool is public, it required a public explanation. The explanation was sufficient. The confirmation reflected a Senate exercising genuine judgment rather than simply validating an executive choice it could not evaluate.
