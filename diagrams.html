How a Law Is Made

Every law originates in the Assembly and passes through multiple constitutional gates before enactment. Click any stage to explore the provisions behind it.

1
Bill Introduced in the Assembly
All legislation originates in the Assembly. Every bill must address a single, clearly defined subject — unrelated riders nullify the entire act. The full bill text must be published to the NRS for the minimum exposure period before any final vote.
§3.4 Legislative Standards
2
Assembly Passage
The Assembly votes. An absolute majority of the full seated membership — not merely those present — is required to pass.
§3.7 — Standard Passage
Vote Outcome
✓ Passes — proceeds to Senate
✗ Fails — bill dies; sponsor may re-introduce next session
3
Senate Review
The Senate must take up every Assembly-passed bill within the statutory period. It may pass, amend, decline, or allow Deemed Approval to trigger. Senate amendments must be germane — limited to the subject matter of the original bill. The LM publishes a germaneness analysis for any amended bill returned to the Assembly.
§3.1 Legislature · §3.7 Legislative Process · §9.1.a LM Germaneness Analysis
Senate Options
✓ Passes — proceeds to Legislative Review Period
↩ Amends — returns to Assembly for acceptance or rejection
✗ Declines — bill dies
⏱ No action in statutory period — Deemed Approved
4
Legislative Review Period — Civic Consul
The Civic Consul reviews every bill during the statutory review period. The Civic Consul may sign, remain silent (Deemed Enacted), issue a Fiscal Objection, or issue a Suspensive Veto. The Civic Consul must give the LM's germaneness analysis due consideration where one was published.
§2.7 Civic Consul Legislative Instruments
Civic Consul Options
✓ Signs or silent — bill proceeds to enactment
📊 Fiscal Objection — requires MA assessment; Assembly may override by simple majority
↩ Suspensive Veto — returns to Legislature; 2/3 both chambers to override
5
Consular Review (Security Domain Bills Only)
The Legat Consul may veto only bills raising a genuine constitutional concern within the Legat Consul's domain — military operations, intelligence, treaty obligations, or border security. A general or speculative connection to security is not sufficient. The SC reviews any veto within 48 hours on petition from either Speaker.

✗ Vetoes — 2/3 both chambers to override; SC reviews within 48hrs

Enactment and NRS Publication
The enacted law is published to the National Record System — permanently, tamper-evidently, and publicly accessible to every Inhabitant at no cost. All prior versions, votes, Monitor findings, and veto records are indexed alongside it.
§10.1 — National Record System Mandate

State Pathways

The three constitutional paths available to States and Territories — from Territory to Statehood, voluntary devolution, and the independence process.

Step 1 — Entry Territory Status

A population petitions the Senate for Territory status. The Senate votes by simple majority. Article I rights apply immediately upon petition filing regardless of vote outcome. The Territory receives social state protections under §12.1 and holds Assembly seats but has no Senate representation.

§15.1 — Territorial Compact
Step 2 — Evaluation Statehood Audit

The Legislative Monitor conducts a Competency Baseline audit assessing fiscal sustainability, rule of law, institutional capacity, and infrastructure. A score of 60% or above passes. Below 60% begins a technical assistance period with the Development Assistance Corps.

§15.2 — Requirements for Statehood
Criteria: fiscal integrity (§15.2) and full Article I legal compliance (§15.2)
Step 3 — Provisional Period Provisional Membership Period

Following a successful referendum, the new State enters a Provisional Membership Period (Legislature sets duration, 365–900 days) — a constitutional probationary period with full rights but monitored institutional development. At the end of the Legislature-defined period, full Statehood is confirmed automatically on a clean audit.

§15.2.c — Provisional Membership Period
Final — Full Membership Statehood Confirmed

The State receives two Senate seats, retains proportional Assembly seats, accesses social state systems under §12.1, and is subject to federal taxation. All constitutional rights and obligations of full Statehood apply.

§15.2.c — Statehood is automatic at end of clean Provisional Period; no further vote required
Step 1 — Transparency Fiscal Incidence Study

The State formally requests a Fiscal Incidence Study from the Monetary Authority. The MA must publish the study within 120 days. The study shows: the value of the federal income tax exemption to residents, the value of federal benefits received above the social state floor, the net fiscal position, and the projected effect on Republic-wide social state sustainability.

§15.4 — Fiscal Transparency Requirement (120-day MA deadline)
Step 2 — Optional but Recommended Negotiated Agreement

The State may request a formal negotiated devolution agreement with the Republic before the vote — covering infrastructure continuity, DAC assistance, military installation arrangements, and Provisional Membership Period terms. If parties cannot agree within 18 months, a default framework activates automatically so the process cannot be blocked by non-negotiation.

§15.4 — Negotiated Devolution Pathway / §15.4
180-day public campaign period required after study publication
Step 3 — Democratic Authorization 60% of State Voters

60% of State voters must approve the voluntary devolution petition. The ballot must specify the proposed restructuring — subdivision, merger, conversion to Territory status, or other arrangement — and must include the net-contributor disclosure if the State is a net contributor to federal redistribution.

§15.4 — Voluntary Devolution Vote
Step 4 — Effect Territory Status

Senate seats are vacated. Assembly seats are retained proportionally. Federal income tax exemption begins the following fiscal year. Social state protections continue without interruption. Article I rights remain fully in force. The path to Statehood restoration is available immediately — no penalty or waiting period applies.

§15.4 — Effect of Voluntary Devolution
Step 1 — Petition National Citizen Petition

A citizen petition must gather 10% of eligible voters nationally through the National Voting System — not just the residents of the proposing territory. This requires building a national coalition for independence, not merely a local majority. The petition is certified by the Judicial Monitor.

§15.7 State Immutability and Territorial Integrity
Step 2 — Framework Pre-Referendum Agreement

Before any referendum is held, a framework independence agreement must be negotiated and published to the NRS at least 90 days before the vote. The agreement must address: citizenship status, social state transition provisions, military installations (§14.5), debt arrangements, and diplomatic relationship. The Judicial Monitor certifies the agreement is complete. If no agreement is reached within 18 months, a default framework activates — the process cannot be blocked.

§15.4 Voluntary Devolution and Merger
70% national vote required — all eligible Republic voters, not only the departing territory's residents
Step 3 — National Referendum 70% National Supermajority

The referendum is held nationally with a minimum 65% participation requirement. 70% of all eligible voters must approve — a threshold that requires not just the departing territory's support but genuine national consensus. The ballot references the published framework agreement.

§15.7 — 70% threshold / 65% participation minimum
Step 4 — Transition 24–60 Month Transition Period

Formal independence takes effect no sooner than 24 months and no later than 60 months after referendum certification. During the transition, the territory remains part of the Republic for all constitutional purposes. Social state-equivalent provisions continue as treaty obligations the Legislature must authorize. The independence treaty is negotiated by the Legat Consul (diplomatic domain) and requires Senate ratification.

§15.4
Step 5 — Citizenship Citizenship Continuity

Republic citizens residing in the former territory retain Republic citizenship — it is irrevocable and continues regardless of the territory's independence. Citizens who choose to renounce Republic citizenship may petition the Elections Panel; renunciation is voluntary and irrevocable. The Legislature may not impose more than a two-year waiting period.

§5.1.e — Post-Independence Citizenship and Voluntary Renunciation

Key Constitutional Distinctions

Asylum is constitutionally independent The asylum track's foundation is §1.20, a non-derogable Article I right under §1.19.b. No legislation can extinguish the right to claim and have determined an asylum claim. The dual-gate system cannot be used to block asylum proceedings.
Credentials are always federal Whether granted through the dual-gate process or the Asylum Court, residency credentials are issued by the federal government and are valid in every State. No State may create a separate residency tier.
Aggregate limits apply to Gate 1/2 only The Legislature may set aggregate limits on immigration applications processed — these apply to the dual-gate track. Asylum proceedings are not subject to aggregate caps. · §6.1
Discrimination is always reviewable §1.8 non-discrimination applies throughout both tracks. Gate 1 denials are not subject to federal review on the merits, but §1.8 discrimination claims always have a federal court pathway. · §6.3

Military Authorization Framework

Military force operates under five constitutional principles. The Legat Consul may act immediately in defence — but all sustained operations require legislative authorization, Civic Consul fund certification, and a stated authorized purpose. Cessation is the constitutional default.

1
Immediate Defensive Force

Legat Consul acts immediately in response to an active or imminent attack on Republic territory, citizens abroad, or treaty allies.

Must notify both chambers and the Civic Consul within the period defined by statute. Strictly bounded to defensive response.

§2.2 No prior authorization
↓
2
All Other Force — Legislative Authorization Required

Any military force beyond immediate defence requires legislative authorization. Authorization is granted for a period the Legislature defines; it expires unless affirmatively renewed.

Cessation is the constitutional default. The burden is always on authorization, never on termination. Operations cease automatically on expiry.

§2.2 Legislature defines timelines

Civic Consul Fund Certification — Throughout All Operations

Military expenditure requires Civic Consul certification at all times. Immediate defensive operations carry a self-executing allocation for the statutory period; beyond that, Civic Consul certification is required or expenditure is void. No Declaration takes effect without Civic Consul certification. Civic Consul may certify, condition, or decline.

§2.14.a — Conflict Resolution During Active Operations

Amending the Constitution

Three paths to constitutional amendment — parliamentary via State ratification, parliamentary via popular ratification, and citizen-led. No executive may veto an amendment. All amendments require genuine democratic consensus.

§17.2 — No Executive Veto Neither the Legat Consul nor the Civic Consul holds any veto authority over constitutional amendments. An amendment takes immediate effect the moment the LM or Elections Panel certifies ratification and publishes it to the NRS.

§9.6 — The Information-Only Mandate: What All Three Can and Cannot Do
✓ Publish facts to the NRS Every finding, report, and discrepancy notice is published permanently and freely accessible to all Inhabitants. No official may suppress, delay, classify, or alter a Report before publication.
✗ Issue binding orders Monitor findings are not legal determinations. They state observed facts. Courts, the Legislature, and the People decide what to do with them.
✓ Publish Annual State of the Republic Reports Every year, automatically, on a constitutionally fixed date — regardless of whether any official requests it. Content includes compliance rates, investigation summaries, constitutional strains, and a risk register.
✗ Arrest, prosecute, or enforce No arrest authority. No prosecutorial function. No enforcement power of any kind. Their sole constitutional output is published factual information.
✓ Receive minority reports from staff Any staff member may publish a Minority Report directly to the NRS if they believe a finding has been improperly omitted or suppressed. Staff cannot be disciplined for doing so — the protection is absolute.
✗ Classify their reports Monitor Reports are permanently exempt from classification. If an investigation involves classified material, the Report describes the issue's nature without reproducing classified content, and flags it for the relevant executive.

The Social State

The Republic's constitutional commitment that every Inhabitant is entitled to conditions of life consistent with human dignity — implemented through three mandatory systems and backstopped by the National Endowment.

System 1
Healthcare Insurance
Every Inhabitant enrolled from birth. No premium. No exclusions based on pre-existing conditions. Portable across all States.
§12.1
System 2
Social Assistance
Income floor ensuring every Inhabitant can meet basic material needs. Right-to-work referral within 30 days. MA certifies adequacy annually.
§12.1
System 3
Compulsory Education
Free through secondary level. State-funded, constitutionally guaranteed. No child may be excluded on financial grounds.
§12.1
Funding Architecture
Normal Operation
All three systems funded through the ordinary annual budget. The Legislature appropriates; the MA certifies that the appropriation meets the constitutional dignity standard. If any system falls short, the MA publishes a compliance gap and the Legislature must address it.
Severe Revenue Contraction
When the MA certifies a Severe Revenue Contraction, the National Endowment activates automatically — disbursing from its constitutionally maintained reserve to backstop all three systems. Emergency reduction of social state access is constitutionally prohibited. §12.1
Monetary Authority Certification Role
The MA independently certifies: (1) that healthcare insurance is financially solvent; (2) that social assistance meets the constitutionally required adequacy standard; (3) whether a Severe Revenue Contraction exists that triggers Endowment activation. The MA cannot be directed by either executive in its certification function — these findings are constitutional facts, not political judgments.

Supreme Court — Appointment Process

Justices are nominated by the Civic Consul from the open SC Candidate Registry and confirmed by the Senate by 2/3 supermajority. Nine justices serve staggered 12-year non-renewable terms.

Open Registry No political branch controls who enters the pool. Any citizen with 15+ years legal practice or 10+ years judicial service may apply to the JM's Candidate Registry. The Registry is public, permanent, and freely accessible on the NRS.
Expert Voice — No Veto A SC Candidate Registry of five senior judges chosen by lot publishes advisory assessments of registered candidates. Transmitted to the Senate before any confirmation vote. Advisory only — the Board holds no nomination authority.
Civic Consul Nomination + 2/3 Senate Confirmation The Civic Consul nominates from the JM-certified Registry. The Senate confirms by 2/3 supermajority of full seated membership. The 2/3 threshold requires broad Senate consensus — the Civic Consul cannot pack the Court without extraordinary legislative support.

Mid-Term Vacancies and Special Provisions
Mid-Term Vacancy — Special Nomination and Confirmation (180 days) Death, resignation, removal, or expulsion triggers the §4.4 appointment process with compressed timelines: Civic Consul nominates within 45 days; JM assessment within 30 days; Senate confirmation within 90 days. Total ceiling: 180 days. The replacement serves only the remainder of the original 12-year term. · §4.4
Temporary Associate Justice During the vacancy, the most senior Appellate Court judge (by continuous service) is automatically designated as Temporary Associate Justice within 5 days — no nomination or confirmation required. They hold a full vote in all cases. They cannot stand as a candidate for the seat they are temporarily filling. · §4.4
Emergency Panel — Immediate Defensive Authority Scope Four justices drawn by rotation form a standing Emergency Panel each two-year cycle. The Panel has sole jurisdiction over constitutional challenges to immediate defensive Legat Consul's authority during active operations and must rule within 48 hours. Ruling is binding on the full Court unless overruled within 72 hours. · §4.5
Non-Renewable Terms Every term — initial or subsequent — is non-renewable. A justice confirmed to an initial short class term (4 or 8 years) is eligible to be nominated again; if confirmed, they serve a full new 12-year term. A justice who has served a full 12-year term may not be nominated again. · §4.2

The Constitutional Pool Framework

Eleven constitutional pools operate through the same framework under §9.12. The table shows each pool across six dimensions: who fills it, what qualifications are required, who vets applicants, how someone is chosen from the certified pool, and what confirmation is needed. The Elections Panel maintains all pools administratively; the appropriate Monitor certifies qualifications.

1
Entry
Who may join the pool? Self-nominated, State-designated, or executive-nominated. Never requires Senate confirmation.
2
Certification
JM reviews and publishes a qualification report within 30 days. EM reviews financial disclosure for offices with significant financial authority.
3
Selection
JMC public lottery (independence-critical roles) or deliberate nomination from pool (accountability-critical roles). All draws publicly certified and NRS-published.
4
Confirmation
Where required, Senate confirms the selected individual only — never pool entry. Some pools require no confirmation at all.

States & Federation

The Republic's constitution gives States significant autonomy — but within explicit, enforceable limits. This diagram maps what States control, what the federal government controls, and the contested boundary where they interact.

State Authority
Gate 1 Immigration Sponsorship
States sponsor applicants at their own discretion — but not on §1.6 discriminatory grounds. §6.1
Assembly District Maps
States draw their own maps subject to five constitutional criteria; LM reviews for compliance. §3.3
Senator Recall
States may establish recall mechanisms by their own law; the Republic treats a recalled seat as vacant. §3.5
State Court Systems
States may maintain their own court structures; constitutional questions always escalate to federal courts. §4.1
Electoral Frameworks
States may offer early voting, mail-in ballots, and alternative methods — as long as all connect to the NVS. §7.3
Voluntary Devolution
A State may restructure its relationship with the Republic through referendum and Senate ratification. §15.4
The Boundary
Redistricting
State draws; LM reviews; Elections Panel draws an interim map where the State's map fails. §3.3
Immigration Sponsorship
State controls Gate 1; federal government controls Gate 2 and credential issuance. §6.1–6.2
Statehood Audit
LM audits fiscal integrity; JM audits Article I compliance. Pass both → automatic Statehood. §15.2
Federal Property
Federal government holds functional use rights over a registered footprint; State retains sovereignty beyond it. §18.1
Amendment Ratification
Path A amendments require 2/3 of States to ratify; States hold direct constitutional power over amendment. §17.1
Federal Authority
Removal from the Republic
Only the federal government may remove individuals. State law cannot authorize removal. §6.2
Residency Credentials
Federal credentials are valid throughout the Republic; no State may create a secondary residency tier. §6.1
NVS Administration
No State may reduce NVS access, functionality, or availability within its territory. §7.3, §11.2
Article I Rights
Article I rights apply in full within all State territory. State law cannot abridge them. Article I
Territorial Integrity
A State may not secede by ordinary statute or State referendum alone. §15.7
Local Government Protection
States may not systematically defund local governments to achieve de facto centralization. §13.3

What States Cannot Do

Set immigration policy or conduct removal proceedings §6.1–6.2
Apply Gate 1 sponsorship criteria that violate §1.6 §6.1
Reduce NVS access or impose additional voting burdens §7.3
Draw district maps using partisan registration or racial composition as a primary criterion §3.3
Divide directly into two States — the portion must become a Territory first §15.7
Systematically defund local governments to centralize functions at State level §13.3
Abridge Article I rights within their territory for any reason Article I
Block asylum proceedings by refusing Gate 1 sponsorship §6.3
