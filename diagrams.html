Every institution is checked by at least one other. No institution controls its own oversight. Parliament is the most direct democratic link — the PM serves at its confidence. Every major appointment to an independent body is publicly assessed before nomination or election.

The Life of a Law

From introduction to enactment — every checkpoint, every veto point, every override mechanism.

1
Bill Introduced in the House
Any member may introduce a bill. The Single-Subject Rule applies immediately — a bill may address only one subject. The Speaker reviews for compliance; a bill violating the single-subject rule is returned to the sponsor before it can proceed.
§3.3.b Single-Subject Rule
2
House Passage
The bill is debated and voted upon. Standard passage requires an absolute majority — a majority of the full seated membership, not merely those present. Budget bills and constitutional implementation bills have the same threshold.
§3.3.a — Absolute majority of full membership
Vote Outcome
✓ Passes — proceeds to PM review
✗ Fails — bill dies; sponsor may re-introduce next session
3
PM Review Window
The PM has a statutory review period (Parliament-defined, not more than 21 days) to consider the bill. The PM may: sign (enact immediately), take no action (deemed enacted at window's close), issue a Suspensive Veto, or issue a Fiscal Objection.
§2.4 — PM Legislative Objection
PM Options
✓ Signs or silent — bill enacted
⏸ Suspensive Veto — bill suspended one full session; House may override by absolute majority next session
📊 Fiscal Objection — House overrides by simple majority; MA assessment required
4
Senate Review
The Senate must act on every House-passed bill within 30 days. The Senate may pass, amend, or decline. Senate amendments return the bill to the House. Failure to act within 30 days results in Deemed Passed — the bill proceeds without Senate action.
§3.2.c — Senate Must Act on Every House Bill
Senate Options
✓ Passes — proceeds to Presidential review
↩ Amends — returns to House for concurrence
✗ Declines — bill dies (unless House overrides by 2/3)
⏱ No action in 30 days — Deemed Passed
5
Presidential Review (Security Domain Bills Only)
For bills touching the President's constitutional domain — defense, diplomacy, national security — the President has a veto. The Presidential Veto requires a 2/3 override by both chambers. Bills outside the President's domain proceed directly to enactment. The President may not expand the definition of their domain to capture domestic legislation.
§2.1.f — Presidential Veto (domain-scoped)
Presidential Options
✓ Signs — enacted
✗ Vetoes — requires 2/3 both chambers to override
→ Not in domain — bill proceeds to enactment directly
6
Enactment and NRS Publication
The enacted law is published to the National Record System simultaneously with enactment. The publication is immutable — neither the law nor the record of its passage can be altered without a new legislative act. Citizens may read the full text of every law, every amendment, and every parliamentary vote on the NRS.
§9.1 — National Record System Mandate
7
Post-Enactment Challenge
Any citizen with a concrete injury from an enacted law may petition the Supreme Court. The Optional Referendum also allows citizens to challenge any enacted law by gathering the required petition threshold — Parliament cannot prevent a referendum that meets the constitutional requirements.
§4.3 Judicial Scope / §11.1 Optional Referendum
Challenge Paths
⚖ Supreme Court — may strike as unconstitutional
🗳 Optional Referendum — citizens may repeal by majority vote

State Pathways

The three constitutional paths available to States and Territories — from Territory to Statehood, voluntary devolution, and the independence process.

Step 1 — Entry Territory Status

A population petitions the Senate for Territory status. The Senate votes by simple majority. Article I rights apply immediately upon petition filing regardless of vote outcome. The Territory receives Basic Needs Floor distributions and holds House seats but has no Senate representation.

§13.1 — Territorial Compact
Step 2 — Evaluation Statehood Audit

The Legislative Inspectorate conducts a Competency Baseline audit assessing fiscal sustainability, rule of law, institutional capacity, and infrastructure. A score of 60% or above passes. Below 60% begins a technical assistance period with the Development Assistance Corps.

§13.2 — Requirements for Statehood
Threshold: 60% Competency Baseline score required to proceed
Step 3 — Democratic Authorization NVS Referendum

Residents vote on Statehood via the National Voting System. A simple majority of residents who have held a Citizen Voting Credential for the statutory minimum period approves Statehood. The Electoral Commission certifies the result.

§13.2.d — Statehood Referendum
Step 4 — Provisional Period 730-Day Provisional Membership

Following a successful referendum, the new State enters a 730-day Provisional Membership Period — a constitutional probationary period with full rights but monitored institutional development. At 730 days, full Statehood is confirmed.

§13.2.e — Provisional Membership Period
Final — Full Membership Statehood Confirmed

The State receives two Senate seats, retains proportional House seats, participates in the BNF funding formula, and is subject to federal taxation. All constitutional rights and obligations of full Statehood apply.

§13.2.f — Full Statehood
Step 1 — Transparency Fiscal Incidence Study

The State formally requests a Fiscal Incidence Study from the Monetary Authority. The MA must publish the study within 120 days. The study shows: the value of the federal income tax exemption to residents, the value of federal benefits received above the BNF, the net fiscal position, and the projected effect on Republic-wide BNF sustainability.

§13.4.b — Fiscal Transparency Requirement (120-day MA deadline)
Step 2 — Optional but Recommended Negotiated Agreement

The State may request a formal negotiated devolution agreement with the Republic before the vote — covering infrastructure continuity, DAC assistance, military installation arrangements, and Provisional Membership Period terms. If parties cannot agree within 18 months, a default framework activates automatically so the process cannot be blocked by non-negotiation.

§13.4.e — Negotiated Devolution Pathway / §15.5.a Default Framework
180-day public campaign period required after study publication
Step 3 — Democratic Authorization 70% Supermajority Vote

Residents who have held a Citizen Voting Credential for a minimum of 10 years vote on voluntary devolution. 70% must approve. The ballot must include the net-contributor disclosure if the State is a net contributor to federal redistribution — residents vote with full knowledge of the fiscal consequences for the Republic.

§13.4.a & §13.4.c — Vote and Net-Contributor Transparency
Step 4 — Effect Territory Status

Senate seats are vacated. House seats are retained proportionally. Federal income tax exemption begins the following fiscal year. Basic Needs Floor distributions continue without interruption. Article I rights remain fully in force. The path to Statehood restoration is available immediately — no penalty or waiting period applies.

§13.4.d — Effect of Voluntary Devolution
Step 1 — Petition National Citizen Petition

A citizen petition must gather 10% of eligible voters nationally through the National Voting System — not just the residents of the proposing territory. This requires building a national coalition for independence, not merely a local majority. The petition is certified by the Judicial Inspectorate.

§15.4 — Territorial Integrity and Independence
Step 2 — Framework Pre-Referendum Agreement

Before any referendum is held, a framework independence agreement must be negotiated and published to the NRS at least 90 days before the vote. The agreement must address: citizenship status, BNF transition distributions, military installations (§17.3.e), debt arrangements, and diplomatic relationship. The Judicial Inspectorate certifies the agreement is complete. If no agreement is reached within 18 months, a default framework activates — the process cannot be blocked.

§15.5.a — Framework Agreement Requirement
70% national vote required — all eligible Republic voters, not only the departing territory's residents
Step 3 — National Referendum 70% National Supermajority

The referendum is held nationally with a minimum 65% participation requirement. 70% of all eligible voters must approve — a threshold that requires not just the departing territory's support but genuine national consensus. The ballot references the published framework agreement.

§15.4 — 70% threshold / 65% participation minimum
Step 4 — Transition 24–60 Month Transition Period

Formal independence takes effect no sooner than 24 months and no later than 60 months after referendum certification. During the transition, the territory remains part of the Republic for all constitutional purposes. BNF-equivalent distributions continue as treaty obligations Parliament must authorize. The independence treaty is negotiated by the President (diplomatic domain) and requires Senate ratification.

§15.5.b–d — Independence Transition Framework
Step 5 — Citizenship Citizenship Continuity

Republic citizens residing in the former territory retain Republic citizenship — it is irrevocable and continues regardless of the territory's independence. Citizens who choose to renounce Republic citizenship may petition the Electoral Commission; renunciation is voluntary and irrevocable. Parliament may not impose more than a two-year waiting period.

§5.1.e — Post-Independence Citizenship and Voluntary Renunciation
