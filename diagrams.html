How a Law Is Made

Every law originates in the House and passes through multiple constitutional gates before enactment. Click any stage to explore the provisions behind it.

1
Bill Introduced in the House
All legislation originates in the House. Every bill must address a single, clearly defined subject — unrelated riders nullify the entire act. The full bill text must be published to the NRS for the minimum exposure period before any final vote.
§3.1.c Single-Subject Rule · §3.1.d Bill Exposure
2
House Passage
The House votes. An absolute majority of the full seated membership — not merely those present — is required to pass.
§3.3.a — Standard Passage
Vote Outcome
✓ Passes — proceeds to Senate
✗ Fails — bill dies; sponsor may re-introduce next session
3
Senate Review
The Senate must take up every House-passed bill within the statutory period. It may pass, amend, decline, or allow Deemed Approval to trigger. Senate amendments must be germane — limited to the subject matter of the original bill. The LI publishes a germaneness analysis for any amended bill returned to the House.
§3.2.c Senate Must Act · §3.2.i Senate Amendment Authority · §8.1.a LI Germaneness Analysis
Senate Options
✓ Passes — proceeds to Legislative Review Period
↩ Amends — returns to House for acceptance or rejection
✗ Declines — bill dies
⏱ No action in statutory period — Deemed Approved
4
Legislative Review Period — PM
The PM reviews every bill during the statutory review period. The PM may sign, remain silent (Deemed Enacted), issue a Fiscal Objection, or issue a Suspensive Veto. The PM must give the LI's germaneness analysis due consideration where one was published.
§2.4 Suspensive Veto · §2.5 Legislative Review Period
PM Options
✓ Signs or silent — bill proceeds to enactment
📊 Fiscal Objection — requires MA assessment; House may override by simple majority
↩ Suspensive Veto — returns to Legislature; 2/3 both chambers to override

Presidential Review (Security Domain Bills Only)
The President may veto only bills raising a genuine constitutional concern within the President's domain — military operations, intelligence, treaty obligations, or border security. A general or speculative connection to security is not sufficient. The SC reviews any veto within 48 hours on petition from either Speaker.
§2.1.f Presidential Veto — domain-scoped · §2.1.f.i SC Review

✓ Signs, or bill not in domain — enacted
✗ Vetoes — 2/3 both chambers to override; SC reviews within 48hrs

The enacted law is published to the National Record System — permanently, tamper-evidently, and publicly accessible to every Inhabitant at no cost. All prior versions, votes, Inspectorate findings, and veto records are indexed alongside it.

State Pathways

The three constitutional paths available to States and Territories — from Territory to Statehood, voluntary devolution, and the independence process.

Step 1 — Entry Territory Status

A population petitions the Senate for Territory status. The Senate votes by simple majority. Article I rights apply immediately upon petition filing regardless of vote outcome. The Territory receives Basic Needs Floor distributions and holds House seats but has no Senate representation.

§13.1 — Territorial Compact
Step 2 — Evaluation Statehood Audit

The Legislative Inspectorate conducts a Competency Baseline audit assessing fiscal sustainability, rule of law, institutional capacity, and infrastructure. A score of 60% or above passes. Below 60% begins a technical assistance period with the Development Assistance Corps.

§13.2 — Requirements for Statehood
Criteria: fiscal integrity (§13.2.a) and full Article I legal compliance (§13.2.b)
Step 3 — Provisional Period Provisional Membership Period

Following a successful referendum, the new State enters a Provisional Membership Period (Legislature sets duration, 365–900 days) — a constitutional probationary period with full rights but monitored institutional development. At the end of the Legislature-defined period, full Statehood is confirmed automatically on a clean audit.

§13.2.c — Provisional Membership Period
Final — Full Membership Statehood Confirmed

The State receives two Senate seats, retains proportional House seats, participates in the BNF funding formula, and is subject to federal taxation. All constitutional rights and obligations of full Statehood apply.

§13.2.c — Statehood is automatic at end of clean Provisional Period; no further vote required
Step 1 — Transparency Fiscal Incidence Study

The State formally requests a Fiscal Incidence Study from the Monetary Authority. The MA must publish the study within 120 days. The study shows: the value of the federal income tax exemption to residents, the value of federal benefits received above the BNF, the net fiscal position, and the projected effect on Republic-wide BNF sustainability.

§13.4.b — Fiscal Transparency Requirement (120-day MA deadline)
Step 2 — Optional but Recommended Negotiated Agreement

The State may request a formal negotiated devolution agreement with the Republic before the vote — covering infrastructure continuity, DAC assistance, military installation arrangements, and Provisional Membership Period terms. If parties cannot agree within 18 months, a default framework activates automatically so the process cannot be blocked by non-negotiation.

§13.4.e — Negotiated Devolution Pathway / §15.5.a Default Framework
180-day public campaign period required after study publication
Step 3 — Democratic Authorization 60% of State Voters

60% of State voters must approve the voluntary devolution petition. The ballot must specify the proposed restructuring — subdivision, merger, conversion to Territory status, or other arrangement — and must include the net-contributor disclosure if the State is a net contributor to federal redistribution.

§13.4.a — Voluntary Devolution Vote
Step 4 — Effect Territory Status

Senate seats are vacated. House seats are retained proportionally. Federal income tax exemption begins the following fiscal year. Basic Needs Floor distributions continue without interruption. Article I rights remain fully in force. The path to Statehood restoration is available immediately — no penalty or waiting period applies.

§13.4.d — Effect of Voluntary Devolution
Step 1 — Petition National Citizen Petition

A citizen petition must gather 10% of eligible voters nationally through the National Voting System — not just the residents of the proposing territory. This requires building a national coalition for independence, not merely a local majority. The petition is certified by the Judicial Inspectorate.

§15.4 — Territorial Integrity and Independence
Step 2 — Framework Pre-Referendum Agreement

Before any referendum is held, a framework independence agreement must be negotiated and published to the NRS at least 90 days before the vote. The agreement must address: citizenship status, BNF transition distributions, military installations (§17.3.e), debt arrangements, and diplomatic relationship. The Judicial Inspectorate certifies the agreement is complete. If no agreement is reached within 18 months, a default framework activates — the process cannot be blocked.

§15.5.a — Framework Agreement Requirement
70% national vote required — all eligible Republic voters, not only the departing territory's residents
Step 3 — National Referendum 70% National Supermajority

The referendum is held nationally with a minimum 65% participation requirement. 70% of all eligible voters must approve — a threshold that requires not just the departing territory's support but genuine national consensus. The ballot references the published framework agreement.

§15.4 — 70% threshold / 65% participation minimum
Step 4 — Transition 24–60 Month Transition Period

Formal independence takes effect no sooner than 24 months and no later than 60 months after referendum certification. During the transition, the territory remains part of the Republic for all constitutional purposes. BNF-equivalent distributions continue as treaty obligations the Legislature must authorize. The independence treaty is negotiated by the President (diplomatic domain) and requires Senate ratification.

§15.5.b–d — Independence Transition Framework
Step 5 — Citizenship Citizenship Continuity

Republic citizens residing in the former territory retain Republic citizenship — it is irrevocable and continues regardless of the territory's independence. Citizens who choose to renounce Republic citizenship may petition the Electoral Commission; renunciation is voluntary and irrevocable. The Legislature may not impose more than a two-year waiting period.

§5.1.e — Post-Independence Citizenship and Voluntary Renunciation

Key Constitutional Distinctions

Asylum is constitutionally independent The asylum track's foundation is §1.27, an unamendable Article I right. No legislation can extinguish the right to claim and have determined an asylum claim. The dual-gate system cannot be used to block asylum proceedings.
Credentials are always federal Whether granted through the dual-gate process or the Asylum Court, residency credentials are issued by the federal government and are valid in every State. No State may create a separate residency tier.
Aggregate limits apply to Gate 1/2 only The Legislature may set aggregate limits on immigration applications processed — these apply to the dual-gate track. Asylum proceedings are not subject to aggregate caps. · §6.1
Discrimination is always reviewable §1.8 non-discrimination applies throughout both tracks. Gate 1 denials are not subject to federal review on the merits, but §1.8 discrimination claims always have a federal court pathway. · §6.3

Military Authorization Framework

Every military operation fits one of three tiers. Each requires greater authorization and greater accountability. The President's authority is strictly bounded — broader operations require legislative consent.

Active, Imminent, or Ongoing Attack

An attack on Republic territory, Republic citizens abroad, or treaty allies. The authorization tier that applies depends on the operation's nature and duration — not the executive's preference.

1

Immediate Defensive Response

President alone · No prior authorization required
Self-executing
Scope
Defensive operations only. Does not authorize offensive operations into foreign territory beyond the immediate threat. Expires automatically when the immediate threat is neutralized.
Notification
President must notify both chambers and the PM within 12 hours of invoking Tier 1. The Legislature defines by statute when sustained Tier 1 operations require transition to Tier 2.
Limits
Cannot be used to initiate a new conflict. Cannot be extended beyond neutralizing the immediate threat without Tier 2 authorization. Cannot authorize domestic emergency powers.
💰
Automatic 96-hour Emergency Defense Allocation — self-executing, not subject to PM Fiscal Objection or certification requirement. Uncertified spending beyond 96 hours is void and triggers pay freeze. · §2.1.d
2

Senate Armed Services Authorization

Senate Armed Services Committee · Simple majority
Within 96 hours · Covers 30 days
When Required
Any offensive military operation, or any defensive deployment that has transitioned beyond neutralizing an immediate threat. Required within 96 hours of the operation commencing.
Authorization Process
The Senate Armed Services Committee — a constitutionally designated standing committee with security clearance — votes by simple majority following a classified presidential briefing. Authorization covers 30 days.
Transition to Tier 3
Operations beyond 30 days require a Tier 3 Legislative Declaration. If the Legislature fails to pass a Declaration within those 30 days, all operations except immediate territorial defense must cease.
💰
PM must certify funds within 96 hours of Committee authorization. PM may issue a Fiscal Objection on specific solvency grounds. · §2.1.d · §2.1.b
3

Legislative Declaration of Defense

Full Legislature · 2/3 concurrent majority of both chambers
Renewable every 180 days · Maximum 5 years
When Required
All military operations beyond 30 days from initial deployment. Simultaneously authorizes extended operations and triggers the Emergency Defense Allocation under §12.2.
Declaration Requirements
Must state the §12.1 authorized purpose being served. Must be renewed by 2/3 majority every 180 days. Failure to renew automatically terminates authorization and all expenditure. Maximum 10 renewals (5 years), then mandatory 90-day cessation.
Scope Limit
Cannot be used to authorize domestic emergency powers not directly connected to the external military operation. The Declaration covers only what is stated — it does not expand presidential domestic authority.
💰
PM certification concurrent with Legislative Declaration. No Declaration takes effect without PM certification of funds. · §2.1.d · §2.1.c
Automatic Termination Tier 1 expires when the immediate threat is neutralized. Tier 2 expires after 30 days if no Tier 3 Declaration is passed. Tier 3 expires if not renewed every 180 days — renewal failure triggers automatic cessation of all operations and expenditure, with no presidential override.

§12.1 — Authorized Purposes for Military Force (Tier 3 only)

✓ Border protection and territorial integrity Defense of the Republic's recognized territory and sovereign borders.
✓ Defense of treaty allies Operations required by ratified mutual defense treaties within defined triggering thresholds. · §3.2.e
✓ Prevention of genocide or civilizational collapse Systemic intervention at the scale of a World War II-type event. A Legislative determination — not a Presidential one.
✗ Nation-building Operations to reconstruct or reorganize foreign political systems.
✗ Resource extraction Operations to secure access to foreign natural resources.
✗ Ideological imposition Operations to spread a political system, religion, or set of values.

§2.1.h — Conflict Resolution During Active Operations

Step 1 — Objection President must file a specific written objection within 6 hours of the PM's order, published to the NRS.
Step 2 — 12-Hour Window Both executives attempt to resolve the conflict. If resolved, the agreed order is published and governs.
Step 3 — SC Expedited Review If unresolved after 12 hours, either executive may petition the SC for emergency domain determination. SC rules within 24 hours.
Interim Rule Pending SC ruling, the President's order governs only for operations directly connected to the active military situation. All other government functions follow the PM's order.
The key asymmetry: The President's domain is bounded and defined — defense, diplomacy, intelligence, trade agreements. The PM's domain is residual — everything else. A question about which executive has authority almost always resolves to the PM unless the President can point to a specific defense, intelligence, or foreign affairs nexus.

Amending the Constitution

Three paths to constitutional amendment — each requiring different sources of authority. No executive may veto an amendment. Some provisions cannot be amended at all.

§15.2.a — No Executive Veto Neither the President nor the Prime Minister holds any veto authority over constitutional amendments. An amendment takes immediate effect the moment the Inspectorates certify ratification and publish it to the National Record System.
A Legislative Path §15.1.a
Both Chambers — Simultaneously ¾ supermajority of the full seated membership of both chambers in a single concurrent vote.
No Popular Vote Required Legislature acts alone. No citizen referendum. No executive signature needed.
Recorded Vote Every member's position published to the NRS within 24 hours. No anonymous constitutional amendment votes.
Takes effect: Upon LI certification and NRS publication.
B Legislative and Popular Path §15.1.b
Stage 1 — Legislature ⅔ supermajority of the full seated membership of both chambers simultaneously.
Stage 2 — National Popular Vote 55% affirmative support with at least 55% citizen participation. Must be held within 180 days of the parliamentary vote.
Certified by: Electoral Commission and Legislative Inspectorate jointly.
Lower legislative threshold than Path A, but requires the People to ratify. Neither stage alone is sufficient.
C Citizen-Led Path §15.1.c
National Vote — No Legislature Required 70% affirmative support with at least 65% citizen participation. Legislature plays no role.
No Second Vote A vote that fails either threshold fails. A new petition may be filed immediately; the failed vote’s NRS record is permanent public evidence of prior support.
Highest threshold — broadest mandate The only path that bypasses the legislature entirely.
Takes effect: Upon EC certification and NRS publication.
🔒 §15.3.a — The Unamendable Floor
Article I — Can only expand, never contract No amendment may reduce any right below the floor established in Article I. The Individual Sovereignty Floor is a one-way ratchet — rights may be added or expanded, never removed or weakened.
§1.2 — Prohibition on Torture Permanently unamendable. May not be altered, qualified, or removed by any process, under any circumstances.
§1.3 — Prohibition on Slavery and Forced Labor Permanently unamendable. No declaration, statute, or constitutional process may reinstate slavery or compelled labor.
§1.13 — Habeas Corpus and First Appearance Permanently unamendable. The right to challenge detention before a court cannot be suspended or removed.
§1.17 — Right to a Public Trial Permanently unamendable. No secret criminal proceedings.
§1.18 — No Retroactive Criminal Laws Permanently unamendable. No person may be convicted of an act that was not criminal when committed.
§1.27 — Non-Refoulement Permanently unamendable. The prohibition on returning any person to persecution is an absolute right of the same character as the prohibitions on torture and slavery.
Everything else — Amendable by one of the three paths All other provisions — including the structure of the government, the amendment process itself, the judiciary, the fiscal framework — may be changed through Paths A, B, or C.
The amendment process itself is amendable. A future supermajority could change the thresholds, add paths, or restructure the process — provided it does so through one of the three current paths and does not touch the permanently unamendable provisions.

§8.6 — The Information-Only Mandate: What All Three Can and Cannot Do
✓ Publish facts to the NRS Every finding, report, and discrepancy notice is published permanently and freely accessible to all Inhabitants. No official may suppress, delay, classify, or alter a Report before publication.
✗ Issue binding orders Inspectorate findings are not legal determinations. They state observed facts. Courts, the Legislature, and the People decide what to do with them.
✓ Publish Annual State of the Republic Reports Every year, automatically, on a constitutionally fixed date — regardless of whether any official requests it. Content includes compliance rates, investigation summaries, constitutional strains, and a risk register.
✗ Arrest, prosecute, or enforce No arrest authority. No prosecutorial function. No enforcement power of any kind. Their sole constitutional output is published factual information.
✓ Receive minority reports from staff Any staff member may publish a Minority Report directly to the NRS if they believe a finding has been improperly omitted or suppressed. Staff cannot be disciplined for doing so — the protection is absolute.
✗ Classify their reports Inspectorate Reports are permanently exempt from classification. If an investigation involves classified material, the Report describes the issue's nature without reproducing classified content, and flags it for the relevant executive.

Supreme Court Elections

Nine justices elected directly by citizens, organized into three staggered classes. No political branch nominates anyone. The ballot is open to any qualified citizen on the Judicial Inspectorate's Registry.

Open Candidacy No political branch controls ballot access. Any citizen on the SC Candidate Registry may stand. The Registry is maintained by the Judicial Inspectorate.
Expert Voice — No Veto A Judicial Selection Board of five randomly selected senior judges publishes an advisory assessment of all candidates. Advisory only — the Board cannot remove anyone from the ballot.
Ranked Choice Election Citizens vote by national Ranked Choice Vote. The winner is the candidate who achieves a national RCV majority. The Electoral Commission administers and certifies.

The elected justice takes the Official Oath administered by the Supreme Court. Recorded on the NRS within 24 hours. The justice serves a single non-renewable 12-year term from this date.

Mid-Term Vacancies and Special Provisions
Mid-Term Vacancy — Special Election (180 days) Death, resignation, removal, or expulsion triggers a 180-day special election. Full open-candidacy process applies with compressed timelines (registration day 30, EI day 60, Board day 90, JI day 120, election day 180). The replacement serves only the remainder of the original 12-year term. · §4.2.e
Temporary Associate Justice During the vacancy, the most senior Appellate Court judge (by continuous service) is automatically designated as Temporary Associate Justice within 5 days — no nomination or confirmation required. They hold a full vote in all cases. They cannot stand as a candidate for the seat they are temporarily filling. · §4.2.g
Emergency Panel — Tier 1 Scope Four justices drawn by rotation form a standing Emergency Panel each two-year cycle. The Panel has sole jurisdiction over constitutional challenges to Tier 1 presidential authority during active operations and must rule within 48 hours. Ruling is binding on the full Court unless overruled within 72 hours. · §4.2.f
Non-Renewable Terms Every term — initial or subsequent — is non-renewable. A justice who serves a 4-year Class A initial term, then wins election to the same or another seat, serves a full new 12-year term that is also non-renewable. A justice who serves their 12-year term cannot stand again. Term limits are built into the non-renewable structure. · §4.2
