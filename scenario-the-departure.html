The Departure

Year 14. The Vaskeld Nation gives notice that it is leaving the Republic. As an Associated Community, its right to go is unconditional — no vote of the Legislature, no permission, no cause required. What follows is not a fight over whether Vaskeld may leave, but over everything that must be untangled before it can: shared infrastructure, cross-border obligations, and the twelve thousand Vaskeld members who are also, by birth, citizens of the Republic.

§20.4 Associated Community Withdrawal §20.5 Compact Obligations §16.2 Founding Status Election §5.1 Citizenship Continuity §20.3 Non-Derogable Floor

When the Republic was constituted, the Vaskeld Nation faced the choice every recognized indigenous nation faced during the Transition Window under §16.2: default into Associated Community status, or integrate as a Territory with the Statehood pathway open. Vaskeld chose the default. It had governed itself for longer than the Republic had existed, and it wanted the relationship of one sovereign to another — inside a compact, not inside the constitutional structure. For fourteen years that relationship held. Vaskeld sent no members to the Assembly and paid no federal tax; in return it received none of the Republic's social-state guarantees. What crossed the border was defined entirely by the compact: a shared hydroelectric grid, a customs arrangement, mutual recognition of professional credentials, and a standing agreement on emergency response.

Then a new Vaskeld council, elected on a platform of full independence, filed a notice of withdrawal.

The Right That Cannot Be Refused

The first thing the Republic's lawyers established was what they could not do. §20.4 is one of the few provisions in the constitution that grants an unconditional power to a body outside the Republic: an Associated Community may withdraw from its compact relationship by its own governance decision, on twelve months' notice, with no requirement of Republic consent and no cause required. There is no ratification vote. There is no supermajority. There is no reviewing body that can find the withdrawal improper. The Legat Consul cannot veto it and the Senate cannot block it.

This is deliberate, and it is the structural expression of §16.1's central claim — that indigenous sovereignty is acknowledged, not granted. A sovereignty the Republic could refuse to release would not be a sovereignty that preceded the Republic; it would be a permission the Republic extended and could revoke. The unconditional exit is what makes the Associated Community a genuine compact between sovereigns rather than a subordinate status dressed up in the language of one.

So the Republic did not contest the departure. It contested nothing about the departure. The twelve-month clock started on the day Vaskeld's notice was published, and the only question left was what the border would look like when it stopped.

What §20.4 Does Not Resolve

An unconditional right to leave is not an unconditional right to leave on any terms one likes. §20.5 provides that the obligations running between the Republic and an Associated Community are whatever the compact says they are — and a compact does not evaporate the moment one party gives notice. The hydroelectric grid did not stop being shared. The customs arrangement did not lapse. For twelve months, the compact remained in force, and its wind-down terms — not the fact of withdrawal — became the entire substance of the negotiation.

Three knots had to be untied. The power grid served communities on both sides of the border and could not be severed without leaving Republic towns dark; the compact's own terms required continuity of supply during any transition, and Vaskeld could not unilaterally cut it off without breaching an obligation that survived its notice. The customs and credentials arrangements needed replacement instruments or an orderly lapse. And the emergency-response agreement — which had twice sent Republic teams into Vaskeld during floods — had to be either renewed as a treaty between the now-fully-separate parties or allowed to end.

None of this constrained the right to leave. All of it constrained the speed and shape of leaving. The distinction is the whole lesson of the case: the constitution makes the exit absolute precisely so that the exit itself is never the bargaining chip, which forces both parties to negotiate the disentangling in good faith rather than holding departure hostage to terms.

The Twelve Thousand

The hardest question was not infrastructure. It was people. Roughly twelve thousand Vaskeld members had been citizens of a predecessor government at Day Zero, which made them citizens of the Republic under the transition — and §5.1 provides that citizenship, once held, cannot be stripped. Vaskeld's withdrawal changed the status of Vaskeld's territory; it did not and could not change the status of Vaskeld's people as Republic citizens.

This produced a population that, after departure, would live in a fully sovereign foreign nation while holding Republic citizenship — able to enter the Republic freely, to vote in Republic elections, to carry every Article I right in their dealings with the Republic. What they would lose was Article I as a constraint on Vaskeld's own government, since a sovereign Vaskeld outside the compact is bound only by the non-derogable floor that §20.3 preserved while the compact ran, and after full separation, by nothing in the Republic's constitution at all.

The council had run on independence without quite reckoning with this: that it could take Vaskeld out of the Republic, but it could not take the Republic out of twelve thousand Vaskeld citizens. The final separation agreement did not resolve the tension so much as name it — a formal acknowledgment that dual status would persist, that the Republic would maintain a consular presence, and that any Vaskeld member who wished to relocate into the Republic as a full resident retained the absolute right to do so.

Month Twelve

The compact ended on schedule. The grid was re-engineered into two interconnected but independently governed systems under a new bilateral supply treaty. Customs and credentials lapsed into a thinner treaty arrangement. The emergency-response agreement was renewed, sovereign to sovereign. On the day the compact terminated, the Republic's territorial extent contracted by the area of the Vaskeld Nation — and because §20.4 withdrawal is not a reduction imposed by the Republic but an exit taken by a sovereign under a right the Republic acknowledged from the founding, no constitutional amendment was required and none was sought.

Vaskeld left as it had arrived: a sovereign, dealing with the Republic as an equal.

What the Case Established

That the Associated Community exit right under §20.4 is exactly as unconditional as it reads — no consent, no cause, no veto, no reviewing body — and that this is a feature of acknowledging prior sovereignty, not a gap in the Republic's authority. That §20.5 leaves the terms of separation to the compact, so that an absolute right to leave coexists with a real obligation to leave in an orderly way. And that §5.1 citizenship continuity means a nation can withdraw its territory from the Republic without withdrawing its people's citizenship — producing dual status that no separation agreement can erase, only manage.

The founding choice under §16.2 gave Vaskeld this door. §20.4 guaranteed the door could always be opened. The rest of Article XX made sure that opening it did not bring the roof down on either side.
