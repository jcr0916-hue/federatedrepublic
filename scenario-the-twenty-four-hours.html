Scenario · §2.16 · The Executive · Incapacity

The Twenty-Four Hours

The Council of Ministers determines that the Civic Consul is temporarily unable to exercise authority. He disagrees. Under §2.16 he does not need to persuade a court, a chamber, or a doctor — he publishes a declaration and, twenty-four hours later, he is Consul again. The provision hands him that door on purpose. It also lets him through it exactly once.

§2.16 Executive Incapacity §2.16(3) Restoration & the 90-Day Brake §2.16(4) Declination §2.5(6) Acting Succession §2.6 Constructive Removal §10.1 NRS Permanence

Every constitution has to answer a question it would rather not: what happens when the person holding an office cannot do the job, and will not say so?

Most answers are bad. Give the decision to doctors and you have made a medical board into a constitutional organ. Give it to a court and you have asked judges to rule on a mind. Give it to the legislature and you have built a second removal power with a lower threshold than the real one, which is to say you have built a coup with a paperwork requirement. Give it to nobody and the Republic is run for six weeks by a man who cannot remember the meeting.

§2.16 refuses all four. Its answer is stranger and it is worth watching work.

The Setup
Nobody in this scenario is lying, and nobody is certain.

Civic Consul Aurel Vasko is fifty-eight. On a Tuesday in the ninth month he is forty minutes late to a Council session, arrives without the folder, and asks the Minister for Transport a question she answered on the previous Thursday. It is not dramatic. It would not be worth a paragraph if it had happened once.

It has now happened, in some form, four times in eleven days. Twice in private. Once in front of eleven people. Once, badly, in a corridor with a journalist in it who did not write it up because she was not sure what she had seen.

Vasko says he is tired. He may well be tired. He is fifty-eight, the Varda file has been on his desk since the spring, and he has not had a week off in two years. Everything he says about it is plausible and none of it is checkable, because tired and something else look identical from across a table.

The Council of Ministers has eleven members in office. Nine of them have now seen at least one of the four episodes. None of them is a doctor. All of them have to decide whether the domestic executive of the Republic is being exercised by a man who is exercising it.

Panel One

§2.16(2) — The Determination
The Council may determine incapacity by majority of members then in office, provided no fewer than five are in office.

They meet on the Thursday. The vote is seven to four.

Read the clause carefully, because it is doing something precise: this function is a constitutional function of the Council and is not an exercise of executive authority. That sentence is the whole reason the mechanism is safe. The Council of Ministers is not a cabinet in the ordinary sense — it does not hold executive power and cannot take any by finding a Consul unable to use his. The Ministers are not seizing the office. They are making a finding of fact about a person, in the same way a clerk certifies a signature, and the finding does not put a single unit of power into their hands.

Where does the power go? §2.5(6). The order of succession, untouched by the Council, decided in advance, by a document that had never heard of Aurel Vasko.

NRS-Y41-1187 · Determination of Incapacity · Council of Ministers
FILED: Year 41, Month 9, Day 14, 16:22
BY: Council of Ministers, sitting as a constitutional function under §2.16(2)(a)
MEMBERS IN OFFICE: 11    PRESENT: 11    THRESHOLD: majority of members in office (6), minimum 5 in office — MET
VOTE: 7 – 4

DETERMINATION: The Council determines that the Civic Consul is temporarily unable to
exercise the authority of the office.

GROUNDS AS RECORDED: Four observed episodes of disorientation between Month 9 Day 3 and
Month 9 Day 14, particularised in the annex.

EFFECT: Acting authority vests by constitutional operation under §2.5(6) upon publication.
This determination is not an exercise of executive authority (§2.16(2)(a)).

Notice what is not in that filing. No diagnosis. No medical annex. No physician's name. The Council did not determine that Aurel Vasko is ill — it has no competence to determine that and the Constitution never asks it to. It determined that he is unable to exercise authority, which is a question about an office, not a body, and which eleven people who sat in the room with him are better placed to answer than any specialist in Valdrath.

Panel Two

§2.16(4) — The Declination Chain
A person upon whom acting authority would fall may decline within 48 hours. The person last in the order may not.

Acting authority falls first on the Speaker of the Assembly, who does not want it. She is nine weeks from an election in a district she holds by four hundred votes, and the last thing she needs is to spend those weeks being the woman who took Vasko's chair. She publishes a declination on the Friday morning. It costs her nothing: a declination is irrevocable and does not prejudice the person's eligibility for any future succession event — the Constitution explicitly refuses to punish her for it.

It falls to the next officer designated by statute, who declines on the Friday evening for reasons he does not give and does not have to.

It falls to the third, who says nothing at all. On Sunday at 16:22 his forty-eight hours expire and he becomes Acting Civic Consul by silence. §2.16(4)(c): a person who does not publish a declination within 48 hours is deemed to have accepted. There is no sequence of events in which the office is empty because nobody answered the phone.

And underneath all of it, §2.16(4)(d), four words that make the whole chain a floor rather than a slope: the person last in the order of succession may not decline. The music cannot stop. Somebody is always holding the chair, whether they want it or not, because a Republic that lets its succession run out the bottom has not written a succession provision — it has written a queue.

Panel Three

§2.16(3)(a) — The Restoration
An incapacity ends when the Consul publishes a restoration declaration to the NRS. Full authority returns 24 hours after publication.

On Monday morning Aurel Vasko publishes four sentences.

NRS-Y41-1194 · Restoration Declaration · The Civic Consul
FILED: Year 41, Month 9, Day 18, 09:05
BY: Aurel Vasko, Civic Consul

I declare that the incapacity determined under §2.16(2) has ended.

TRANSMITTED TO: Speaker of the Assembly; Speaker of the Senate; the Legat Consul;
the officer then holding acting authority.

FULL AUTHORITY RETURNS: Year 41, Month 9, Day 19, 09:05 (24 hours after publication).

That is the entire procedure. No hearing. No court. No vote. No doctor. No permission. The man the Council found unable to exercise authority ends that finding by saying so, and on Tuesday morning at five past nine he is Civic Consul again with every power he had on the Wednesday before.

The first reaction to this is that it is absurd — that the provision hands the incapable man the key to his own cell. Sit with it a moment longer.

Who else could hold that key? A court, and you have asked judges to adjudicate a mind. A medical board, and you have made physicians a branch of government. His accountability chamber, and you have created a removal power that needs no successor named, no absolute majority of full seated membership, no constructive vote — a §2.6 with the safety off. Every alternative to the Consul decides is worse than the Consul decides, and most of them are worse in a way that is permanent.

And the twenty-four hours are not decoration. They are the only delay in the provision, and they exist so that the Speakers, the other Consul, and the acting officer — all of whom must be transmitted the declaration — have one full day to look at the man and decide what to do about it. The clause does not tell them what to do. It gives them a day and their own judgement, which is all the Constitution ever gives anybody.

Panel Four

§2.16(3)(b) — The Second Determination
On the same or substantially similar grounds within 90 days, the Consul may not end the incapacity by declaration alone.

On Day 26 it happens again, in a Council session, in front of everybody, and it is not subtle and it is not tiredness.

The Council determines incapacity a second time. The vote is ten to one.

Aurel Vasko publishes a second restoration declaration on Day 27. It does nothing.

Because the second determination rests on the same grounds as the first, and it came inside ninety days, §2.16(3)(b) has changed the lock. The incapacity now ends only when the Assembly — his accountability chamber, the body that elected him — so resolves by absolute majority of its full seated membership. Two hundred and fifty-one of five hundred. On the record. With their names on it.

This is the hinge of the whole provision, and it is worth being precise about what just happened.

The brake did not make it harder for Vasko to come back. It made it somebody else's decision — and specifically, the decision of the only body in Valdrath that already had the power to end him under §2.6 and has spent four weeks declining to use it. The Assembly can no longer watch. It must now either resolve that he is able, by absolute majority, in public — or leave him sitting in an incapacity it has the votes to end.

There is no third option. There is no we are looking into it. §2.16 has taken a medical question that nobody could answer and converted it, deliberately and by design, into a political question that somebody must.

§2.16(5) — The Ninety Days
An incapacity that has continued for 90 days ends by constitutional operation — unless a removal process has been initiated.

And here is the second wall, behind the first.

Suppose the Assembly does nothing. Suppose two hundred and fifty-one members cannot be found to say he is able, and nobody wants to be the one who says he is not, and the whole thing is simply allowed to sit — which is, on the evidence of every legislature that has ever existed, the most likely outcome of all.

Then on Day 90 the incapacity ends by constitutional operation and Aurel Vasko is Civic Consul again, and nobody voted for that, and everybody let it happen.

Unless. Before expiry, a removal process under §2.6 has been initiated — in which case the incapacity continues until that process concludes.

Read what that does to the Assembly's calendar. The chamber has ninety days to make an actual decision about an actual man. If it wants him gone it must do the honest thing and move a constructive vote of no confidence, naming a successor, in the open. If it wants him back it must resolve that he is able. And if it does neither — if it does what legislatures do — the man returns to full power on Day 91 by operation of the document itself.

§2.16 will not let the Republic park a Consul. Indefinite incapacity is not on the menu. You may remove him, you may restore him, or you may have him back on Day 91; and the one thing you may not do is keep him in a drawer.

What the provision is actually for. §2.16 never once asks whether Aurel Vasko is ill. It has no view. It does not want one. The word medical does not appear in it, and the omission is the design: the moment a constitution starts diagnosing people, it has acquired a power that has no natural limit and no honest owner.

What it does instead is build a sequence of forced decisions with names attached to each one. Eleven Ministers must vote, on the record, with their grounds particularised. A Consul must sign his own name to the claim that he is able. Officers must decline in writing within forty-eight hours or be deemed to have accepted. And if it happens twice, five hundred Assembly members must stop watching and resolve — by absolute majority of full seated membership — one way or the other, inside ninety days, or the question answers itself in the Consul's favour.

Every one of those documents is on the NRS forever (§10.1). The determination with its seven-to-four vote. The declination the Speaker filed nine weeks before an election. The silence of the officer who let his forty-eight hours run out. The restoration declaration that worked, and the one that did not. In twenty years, when somebody asks what the Republic knew and when, the answer will be sitting there in filing order, and nobody will have to reconstruct anything.

The trade, stated plainly: §2.16 accepts that a genuinely incapable Consul gets one free return — twenty-four hours, his own signature, no one's permission — in exchange for never letting anyone else hold the key to the office. It is a real cost. It bought a rule that a bad-faith Council cannot use to take an executive, because the door only locks when the Council is right twice and the accountability chamber agrees, and if everyone simply looks away the man goes back to work.

That is not a provision that trusts anybody. It is a provision that has read the file on all of them.
