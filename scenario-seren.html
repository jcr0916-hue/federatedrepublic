Scenario · Formation to Year 30

The Republic of Seren

Three former colonial territories — Anglophone Akoro, Francophone Maurel, and Lusophone Tessaba — federate on the West African coast. Thirty years: offshore gas, a Sahel migration crisis, traditional authority against federal jurisdiction, a presidential removal attempt, and the sea rising past the seawall.

§13.6 Voluntary Incorporation §14.1 Indigenous Compact §6.1 Dual-Gate Immigration §1.27 Non-Refoulement §10.4 MA Director + Board §2.10 Presidential Removal Article VIII constitutional violations §12.3 Intelligence Firewall §8.9 State Designee Panel §15.4.a 70% Independence §1.25.e Election Protection §9.1 NRS

The ratification ceremony is held simultaneously in three cities: Tema in Akoro, Abidjan-sur-Mer in Maurel, and Porto Sereno in Tessaba. Three former colonial territories — divided by the accident of which European power drew which coastline — sign a founding document in English, French, and Portuguese. The simultaneous ceremony is constitutionally significant. Under Article XIX, all three territories ratify as co-equal founding polities. There is no senior partner. The NRS logs all three instruments at the same timestamp, 09:00 GMT, on the first day of Year Zero.

The Republic is born from a shared exhaustion. Akoro has cycled through five governments in twelve years, each promising structural reform and delivering structural stasis. Maurel's last president won an election certified by no independent observer and governed for six years through emergency decree. Tessaba, the smallest and poorest of the three, ran a functional if threadbare parliamentary democracy — but one too small to survive the commodity shocks that arrived every four to seven years. The federation's promise is not ideology. It is institutional infrastructure none of the three could build alone.

Year Zero — The Foundation Documents

The constitution's transition architecture (Article XIX) requires four phases before the Republic is fully operational. The founding congress — drawn from the elected assemblies of all three territories by proportional delegation — spends fourteen months negotiating what Phase 1 alone requires: the existing legal systems of Akoro (common law), Maurel (civil law), and Tessaba (civil law with customary law overlay) must produce a constitutional compatibility assessment. The Judicial Inspectorate panel is stood up with three IGs — one nominated by each territory's outgoing judicial body, confirmed jointly. It publishes its compatibility assessment in Month 9: the three systems are compatible at the constitutional level, with 23 areas where harmonization statutes will be required within Phase 3. The assessment is 847 pages. The NRS publishes all 847 pages the day the panel transmits it.

The Language Question
Three official languages, one federal record system

The constitution requires the NRS to maintain a complete and authoritative record. The founding congress negotiates for three months on what "authoritative" means when three languages share official status. The resolution — reached in Month 11 and enshrined in the first federal statute — is procedurally elegant and practically demanding: all federal executive orders, court decisions, and legislative acts must be published to the NRS simultaneously in all three languages within 72 hours. The authoritative text for constitutional interpretation is the one in which the provision was originally drafted and subsequently ratified. For the constitution itself, all three ratification texts are simultaneously authoritative — any provision whose meaning differs across translations must be resolved by the Supreme Court in the language that most narrowly limits government power. This last clause will be invoked eleven times in thirty years.

The Traditional Authority Compact Negotiations

Akoro has forty-seven registered chieftaincies. Maurel has nineteen paramount chieftaincies and a network of village councils with statutory authority over land allocation that predates colonial administration. Tessaba has the Sereno Compact — a 200-year-old agreement between the Sereno fishing confederacy and successive governing authorities guaranteeing fishing rights and coastal governance within twelve nautical miles of the Porto Sereno coast. All three intersect with Article XIV.

The Article XIV compact negotiations run concurrently with Phase 1 and extend into Phase 2. The constitutional requirement — demonstrated continuity of governance predating colonial legal frameworks, distinct legal tradition, and active exercise of governance authority — is met by forty-one of Akoro's forty-seven chieftaincies, by all nineteen of Maurel's paramount chieftaincies, and by the Sereno Confederacy. The six Akoro chieftaincies that do not qualify are those that were created by the colonial administration as administrative convenience structures — their records show this clearly. The JI panel's finding on all of this is published to the NRS in Month 16. Three chieftaincy councils contest the finding. The Supreme Court hears all three appeals by Month 22 and upholds the JI panel's determination in each case, applying the "precolonial continuity" standard strictly.

The Sereno Compact — §14.3.a
A 200-year document meets a new constitution

The Sereno Confederacy's negotiating position is specific and non-negotiable: the 1823 Sereno Compact — under which the Portuguese colonial administration recognized confederacy governance authority within the twelve nautical mile zone — remains in force, superseded only by express written agreement of the Confederacy itself. The constitutional compact negotiations produce exactly that: a new framework agreement that incorporates the 1823 Compact by reference, updates the twelve nautical mile zone to align with the Republic's federal maritime boundary, and adds a Confederacy veto right over federal infrastructure development within that zone. The Confederacy's delegate signs the framework agreement on Day 1 of Phase 3. The NRS logs both the 1823 Compact and the new framework agreement simultaneously. The 200-year document is now part of the federal constitutional record.

Years 1–6 — First Cohabitation

Phase 3 elections produce a divided Republic. The House seats a PM from Akoro's Progressive Alliance — Kwame Asante-Boateng, a constitutional lawyer who led the Akoro ratification campaign. The presidential election produces a different result: Isabelle Marchetti-Kouassi, Maurel's former finance minister, wins a six-way RCV field by a margin of 4.2 percentage points. Asante-Boateng leads the domestic agenda. Marchetti-Kouassi holds the military, intelligence, and foreign affairs brief. They have never been in the same political coalition. The dual executive architecture will spend its first five years doing exactly what it was designed for.

The first genuine domain dispute arrives in Year 2. Offshore gas exploration in the Maurel continental shelf requires federal certification — the President's domain under the military-adjacent foreign affairs authority, because the exploration blocks overlap with a maritime boundary dispute with a neighboring state. The PM argues the revenue framework is domestic economic policy and belongs in the DAC. The Emergency Panel (§4.2.e) receives its first ever referral in Year 3 when the President's office issues an exploration moratorium order simultaneously with the PM's office issuing a licensing framework notice. The Panel rules within 24 hours: the licensing framework is domestic economic policy (PM's domain); the moratorium tied to the maritime dispute resolution is foreign affairs (President's domain). The two offices must coordinate their timing. The Panel's ruling is nine pages. It is published to the NRS and becomes the first precedent in the Republic's cohabitation jurisprudence.

The Gas Revenue Architecture

By Year 4, the offshore blocks are producing. The MA Director — elected in the Phase 3 national election, the first time any democratic polity has put a central bank governor to a direct popular vote — certifies the first GNR figure with offshore gas revenue included. The certification produces an immediate political dispute. Akoro's finance committee argues the gas revenue should be excluded from GNR for BNF floor calculation purposes, on the grounds that offshore extraction revenue is inherently volatile and its inclusion will lock in a BNF floor that cannot be sustained during low-price periods. Maurel's bloc argues inclusion. Tessaba abstains, observing that its own fiscal contribution to GNR is primarily subsistence agriculture and artisanal fishing, and that whatever formula is chosen will disadvantage Tessaba either way.

§10.4.a — The Elected Director's First Test
Director Amara Diallo certifies inclusion

Director Amara Diallo — elected on a platform of "constitutional literalism about the BNF floor" — certifies gas revenue included in GNR in Year 5. Her reasoning is published to the NRS in full: the constitution defines GNR as gross national revenue; offshore extraction revenue is revenue; excluding it requires statutory authority the Legislature does not currently hold. The PM's suspensive veto on the GNR certification methodology statute fails — the House overrides within the constitutional window. The BNF floor includes gas revenue from Year 5 onward. Diallo has made an enemy of the Akoro finance committee and every politician in the Republic who was hoping for fiscal headroom. She has also established that the MA Director's constitutional literalism is not negotiable by political pressure. The NRS records her certification, the veto, the override, and the reasoning for all three.

Years 7–12 — The Sahel Wave

The rains fail across the Sahel for the fourth consecutive year in Year 7. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change's Year 8 report identifies a 340-kilometer southward shift in the Sahel's aridity boundary — a shift that has rendered permanently unviable the agricultural systems of four countries north of the Republic's border. The displacement begins slowly: an estimated 180,000 people in Year 7, accelerating to 620,000 in Year 8, then 1.4 million in Year 9. The Republic's northern border — shared between Akoro and Maurel — receives the majority.

The dual-gate immigration system (§6.1) was designed for managed flows. What arrives at the northern border in Year 9 is not a managed flow. It is a population movement of a kind the Republic's founding generation could model but hoped not to see for another two decades. The constitutional architecture handles it provision by provision.

§1.27 — The Non-Refoulement Question
When the gate cannot process the volume

Gate 1 requires State sponsorship. Gate 2 requires federal certification. Both require individual processing. In Year 9, when 1.4 million people are moving, individual processing is not operationally possible at the pace movement demands. The Republic's borders cannot be physically closed without placing people in conditions that violate §1.27's non-refoulement protection — the constitutional guarantee that no person may be returned to a place where their life, physical integrity, or freedom is under serious threat. The Supreme Court receives three emergency petitions in Year 9, all from human rights organizations, all making the same argument: the operational failure to process is a de facto removal order, and §1.27 prohibits it. The Court rules within 72 hours: the Republic may not return or physically exclude any person whose §1.27 claim is plausible pending processing, regardless of processing capacity. A person whose life is at risk from climate-driven agricultural collapse in a country with documented food insecurity at crisis levels has a plausible §1.27 claim. The border remains open pending processing. The processing system must be rebuilt.

The emergency processing framework that follows is built under the PM's domestic emergency authority (§2.2.e) and ratified by both chambers within 30 days. It establishes mass-registration camps with NVS biometric enrollment, a federal Gate 2 certification track for climate displacement claims, and a State sponsorship pool allowing States to collectively sponsor registered individuals who complete the federal certification. All of this is constitutionally novel. None of it was written into the constitution. All of it is consistent with what the constitution authorizes.

By Year 11, 2.3 million people have entered the Republic through the emergency registration framework. 1.9 million have completed Gate 2 federal certification. Of those, 1.4 million have State sponsors — spread across Akoro's northern States, Maurel's agricultural regions, and even Tessaba's coastal zone, where the Sereno Confederacy signs a separate compact extension offering Gate 1 sponsorship for fishing community members with documented maritime livelihoods. The 500,000 who have completed Gate 2 but lack State sponsors are placed in a federal sponsorship category the Legislature creates under §6.1.d, designating federal agricultural development agencies as sponsors for individuals willing to participate in an experimental land stewardship program in Akoro's underutilized northern territories.

What the immigration architecture produced

The dual-gate system was never designed for a climate migration emergency. It was designed to give States sovereign authority over community composition while protecting individuals from federal discrimination. In the emergency, the architecture's flexibility — States can set their own sponsorship criteria; federal certification categories can be created by statute — proved more important than its original purpose. The statistical disparity presumption (§6.1.b) had an unexpected application: when Year 10's Gate 2 denial rates showed a 340% disparity by country of origin, the federal court system had a constitutional tool to identify and challenge discriminatory administration of the climate displacement track. Six regional certification offices were placed under remediation orders within eighteen months of the disparity presumption being invoked. The NRS recorded the denial patterns, the disparity analysis, and the remediation orders in a public file that became the basis for the Legislature's Year 12 certification reform statute.

The Intelligence Firewall Tested

The President's intelligence apparatus — operating under the President's foreign affairs and national security domain — develops intelligence that some of the individuals in the migration flow are affiliated with a militant organization operating in the Sahel. The intelligence assessment is credible and specific: named individuals, documented affiliations, operational timelines. The President's office issues an executive order in Year 10 directing the Directorate of Intelligence to compile surveillance records on the named individuals, who are by this point Republic residents in the federal processing camps.

The order is recorded on the NRS at 11:43 PM on a Thursday. By 8:00 AM Friday, the EI has published a factual finding: the individuals named in the order are residents of the Republic, and surveillance of Republic residents is prohibited under §12.3 regardless of their immigration status, affiliation, or the national security basis invoked. There are no exceptions. The order is void. The President contests the EI's finding, arguing that §12.3 applies to citizens and that the named individuals are not yet citizens. The Supreme Court takes the case on emergency basis and rules within 48 hours: §12.3 prohibits domestic intelligence operations against "Republic citizens" — but §1.1 establishes that all persons within the Republic's jurisdiction hold individual sovereignty protections. The Court reads §12.3 in light of §1.1: domestic surveillance of any person within the Republic's jurisdiction is prohibited, regardless of citizenship status. The order is confirmed void. The President's Inspectorate finding is issued the following week. The named individuals are investigated through criminal law enforcement channels — a process subject to §1.13's judicial oversight requirements — rather than intelligence surveillance.

Years 13–18 — The Chieftaincy Crisis

The Maurel paramount chieftaincies have coexisted with the federal constitutional order for twelve years. The arrangement has worked because the traditional governance authority — land allocation, dispute resolution, ceremonial functions — operated in domains the federal legislature had not yet legislated. In Year 13, it legislates. The National Land Registry Act establishes a federal land titling system intended to secure property rights for smallholder farmers who have never held formal title. The problem: in Maurel, the paramount chieftaincies hold customary authority over land allocation that predates any written title system. Under the chieftaincy compact negotiated in Phase 2, the federal government cannot impose a land governance framework within chieftaincy territorial boundaries without the relevant paramount chief's written consent.

Sixteen of nineteen paramount chieftaincies refuse consent. The Land Registry Act cannot be implemented in sixty-three percent of Maurel's territory. The PM's office — which spent three years shepherding the Act through the Legislature — publishes a written objection to the chieftaincy refusals on the NRS. The chieftaincy councils respond with their own NRS publication: a 200-page legal brief arguing that the Act's land titling framework conflicts with their compact rights and that the PM's published objection is itself a violation of the non-interference principle in §14.2.a.

§14.3.e — The Inspectorate Jurisdiction Limit
The legislative Inspectorate finds it cannot help

The PM requests a Legislative Inspectorate audit of whether the chieftaincy refusals constitute a constitutional violation — specifically, whether a compact holder's refusal to consent to federal legislation constitutes an abuse of compact authority. The Legislative Inspectorate publishes its response within 30 days: it does not have audit jurisdiction over the internal governance decisions of compact-holding traditional authorities under §14.3.e. The compact refusals are internal governance decisions, not federal acts. The LI can audit federal compliance with the compact framework; it cannot audit the chieftaincy councils' exercise of their compact rights. The PM must use the compact negotiation mechanism — the Article XIV framework agreement — not the audit mechanism. The LI's finding is four pages. It is published to the NRS and ends three months of political argument about whether the audit approach was viable.

The resolution takes four years. The PM's office and the chieftaincy council of each of the sixteen refusing paramount chieftaincies negotiate individually. The final framework — agreed in Year 17 — creates a dual-track land registry: federal title coexists with chieftaincy allocation authority, with a conflict resolution protocol that gives chieftaincy allocation priority for land within the customary boundary and federal title priority for land in the federal infrastructure corridor. Eleven paramount chieftaincies sign the framework agreement in Year 17. Five do not. Their territories remain outside the Land Registry system indefinitely, which is constitutionally permissible. The NRS records every negotiation round, every draft framework, every objection, and every final agreement.

Years 19–22 — The Presidential Removal

President Marchetti-Kouassi's successor — elected in Year 13 after Marchetti-Kouassi completes her single six-year term — is General (retired) Emmanuel Tché-Adjobi, a former Akoro defense force commander who ran on a platform of "security federalism" and won by eighteen points. His first four years are uncontroversial. His fifth year is not.

In Year 18, the intelligence service — operating under the President's domain — begins a surveillance program targeting domestic political organizers in Maurel who are coordinating opposition to Tché-Adjobi's proposed constitutional amendment to extend the presidential term to eight years. The program involves metadata collection, not content surveillance. The President's office argues metadata collection is not "domestic intelligence operations" within the §12.3 prohibition. The EI disagrees. The Supreme Court, in an expedited ruling, agrees with the EI: metadata collection of Republic residents for the purpose of monitoring domestic political activity is domestic intelligence in violation of §12.3 regardless of whether content is captured. The Inspectorate finding is issued. Tché-Adjobi is suspended from office pending Senate trial under Article VIII.

Article VIII + §2.10 — The Two Tracks Considered
The House faces a choice it has never made before

The House Accountability Committee spends six weeks analyzing whether to let the Article VIII Inspectorate reporting and political accountability run — Senate trial, 2/3 supermajority to convict, published Inspectorate findings already in effect — or to pursue the §2.10.a legislative removal track simultaneously, which would require its own 2/3 majority and a Statement of Grounds. The Committee recommends the Inspectorate reporting and political accountability alone. Its reasoning, published to the NRS: the Article VIII finding is already made; the constitutional violation is already established; the Senate trial will determine whether Tché-Adjobi is removed; adding a §2.10.a track would create two parallel proceedings with different standards and risk confusing both. The House accepts the recommendation 312-89. The §2.10.a track is not invoked.

The Senate trial runs for eleven days. The defense argument is narrow: metadata collection targeting non-citizens would be lawful; the Maurel opposition organizers include twelve individuals who have not completed citizenship — their Gate 2 federal certification included residency but not naturalization. The prosecution's response: the §12.3 prohibition, as interpreted by the Supreme Court following the Year 10 ruling, applies to all persons within the Republic's jurisdiction regardless of citizenship status; and in any event, the remaining organizers are citizens, and the surveillance program did not segregate by citizenship status before collection. The Senate convicts, 72-18. Tché-Adjobi becomes the first president in the Republic's history to be removed through the constitutional accountability system. The Deputy Senate Speaker — who is Tessaba's senior senator — assumes the succession role pending a presidential by-election.

The by-election is held within 90 days under §16.5. Candidate registration, campaigning, and the election itself occur in eleven weeks. The Electoral Commission's administration of the by-election — compressing what would normally be a six-month process into eleven weeks — is studied by constitutional scholars for years afterward. The NVS handles it. Every step is on the NRS. The new president, Adaeze Okonkwo-Ferreira, takes office in Month 10 of Year 21. She is the Republic's third president and the first to have been born inside its borders — she was six years old when the ratification ceremony happened simultaneously in three cities.

Years 23–26 — The MA Director Election and the Veto

Director Amara Diallo's seven-year non-renewable term expires in Year 22. The election to replace her is the Republic's most contested since the founding presidential race. Three candidates reach the NVS ballot after JI vetting — a monetary economist from Akoro, a former IMF regional director from Maurel, and a development finance specialist from Tessaba who has never held a central banking position. The Tessaba candidate, Dr. Félicité Andrade, wins the RCV count in the second round, becoming the first Tessaba-origin federal official to hold a constitutional administrative position above the State level.

Andrade's first significant action as Director is a veto of the board's Year 24 BNF floor certification. The board has certified the floor at 6.8% of GNR. Andrade vetoes on methodology grounds: the board's GNR calculation uses a three-year rolling average that, in her view, understates the current-year figure because it includes two years of suppressed gas revenue during a maintenance shutdown period. Her preferred calculation produces a 7.4% floor. She files the Supreme Court methodology challenge within 36 hours of the veto — well within the 48-hour constitutional window under §10.4.d.

§10.4.d — The Methodology Challenge

The Supreme Court accepts the challenge on a 6-3 vote. The majority opinion, published in Year 24, establishes the first constitutional precedent on MA methodology disputes: the question before the Court is not which methodology is economically correct, but whether the board's chosen methodology falls within the range of constitutionally permissible approaches to GNR calculation. The Court rules 5-4 that the three-year rolling average is constitutionally permissible even if it produces a figure that understates current-year capacity during recovery periods. The veto lapses. The 6.8% certification stands. Andrade publishes a 47-page Director's statement to the NRS explaining her continued disagreement with the methodology and the grounds on which she believes the rolling average will produce systematic BNF undercounting in economies with volatile extraction revenue. The statement is not legally binding. It is part of the permanent constitutional record. The Legislature reads it and commissions a statutory review. In Year 26, a new GNR calculation statute addresses extraction revenue volatility through a specific smoothing mechanism. Andrade's veto failed in Year 24 and changed the statute by Year 26.

Years 27–30 — The Sea and the Vote

The Sereno Confederacy's twelve nautical mile coastal zone has been flooding since Year 15. The Republic's federal climate infrastructure program has built three seawall extensions within the zone — each negotiated under the framework agreement, each approved by the Confederacy's council. By Year 27, the rate of inundation has outpaced the seawall program. The Confederacy's permanent coastal settlements — forty-three fishing villages housing 280,000 people — are projected to be uninhabitable within eighteen years at current trajectories.

The Confederacy's Year 28 petition to the federal government is remarkable in its constitutional precision. It requests three things: designation of an inland territory in northern Akoro as the Confederacy's new sovereign compact zone; federal funding for the physical relocation of the forty-three villages over a twelve-year period; and constitutional amendment to §14.1 to allow compact zones to be relocated rather than territorially fixed. The petition cites §14.3.d — the Republic may provide support for the development of internal rights enforcement mechanisms — and extends the principle: the Republic may provide support for the physical continuity of the governance structure itself, where that structure is under threat from forces neither the Republic nor the compact holder created.

§15.1.b — State Convention Amendment
The first climate-driven constitutional amendment

The State Convention amendment path requires a petition from 2/3 of State assemblies, followed by a Convention, followed by either 3/4 of both federal chambers or a 70% popular vote. Twenty-one of thirty-one State assemblies petition within six months of the Confederacy's request — the fastest State Convention petition in the Republic's history. The Convention convenes in Year 29 in Porto Sereno, in the flooding city. The amendment language is narrow and specific: §14.1 is modified to allow compact zones to be established in a location other than the traditional territorial boundary where the original territory is subject to documented irreversible environmental change, upon written consent of the compact holder and approval by 2/3 of both federal chambers. The amendment passes the Convention with 91% support. The federal chambers ratify in the same session, 88% in the House and 84% in the Senate. No popular referendum is required at the 3/4 threshold. The NRS logs the amendment on the last day of Year 29. The Sereno Confederacy begins the relocation planning process. The 1823 Compact — now 207 years old — remains in force in the new compact zone. The NRS records note it is the first compact in human history to survive a territory's physical disappearance.

The Tessaba Independence Movement

The smallest founding territory has run a quiet independence movement since Year 14. The movement's complaint is principled rather than aggrieved: Tessaba joined the federation as an equal partner but holds 4% of GNR, 8% of House seats (by population), and 11% of Senate seats. Its policy preferences — consistently more cautious on gas revenue inclusion in BNF calculations, more supportive of traditional authority compact expansion, more oriented toward the Lusophone regional bloc than the Anglophone or Francophone ones — are consistently in the minority. The independence movement's argument is not that the Republic has wronged Tessaba. It is that Tessaba might govern itself better than it is governed within the Republic.

The independence referendum is held in Year 29, timed to coincide with the State Convention that was meeting in Porto Sereno. The Tessaba territory-level vote: 71% yes, with 79% participation. The national vote: 43% yes, with 58% participation — below both the 70% affirmative threshold and the 65% participation requirement of §15.4.a. The national vote fails on both counts. The independence declaration issued by Tessaba's assembly within hours of the territory-level result is constitutionally void the moment it is issued. The Supreme Court confirms this within two days.

§15.4.a — What 71% in one territory cannot produce

The 70% national vote requirement was the most debated provision in the founding congress's negotiations. Tessaba's ratification delegation tried to insert a territorial majority exception — if 70% of the departing territory votes yes, independence should be available regardless of national sentiment. The provision as ratified contains no such exception: the threshold is national, not territorial. Tessaba accepted this in Year Zero because the alternative was not ratifying. In Year 29, Tessaba's 71% territorial majority produces exactly what the constitution says it produces: a powerful advisory signal that 71% of Tessaba's population supports independence, and no constitutional authority to achieve it. The NRS records the vote, the territory-level result, the national result, and the Supreme Court's confirmation. The 43% national yes vote — representing a significant minority of the Republic's population — is also part of the permanent record. Tessaba's independence movement now has a documented democratic claim. It does not have a constitutional path forward without building national majority support for a question that most people outside Tessaba have never seriously considered.

President Okonkwo-Ferreira's response to the Tessaba result is widely praised for its constitutional literacy. She does not characterize the territory-level vote as a failure. She notes that 71% democratic support for any proposition is a significant political fact requiring a serious political response, even when it cannot produce the constitutional outcome the voters sought. She establishes a Tessaba Governance Commission — staffed jointly by the federal government and the Tessaba territory assembly — to produce a report within two years on what structural changes within the existing constitutional framework might address the policy representation concerns driving the independence movement. The report is due in Year 31. It is not in this scenario's window. But the NRS records the Commission's establishment, its mandate, and its timeline.

The Thirty-Year Verdict

Three territories that could not individually survive commodity shocks, climate pressure, and migration crises built an institutional infrastructure that handled all three simultaneously — imperfectly, publicly, correctively. The BNF floor held through a Sahel migration emergency that was not in any founding scenario analysis. The §1.27 non-refoulement guarantee produced a constitutional command to process rather than exclude, and the Republic built the processing system the command required. The traditional authority compact framework — negotiated slowly, case by case, chief by council — produced the flexibility that let the Sereno Confederacy survive the physical loss of its territory. The §12.3 Intelligence Firewall caught a president who believed metadata was not surveillance, and the constitution disagreed. The presidential removal process worked: the Inspectorate finding, the Senate trial, the conviction, the by-election, the handover — eleven months from finding to new president, on the record, without interruption to governance.

What the architecture could not produce: Tessaba's political satisfaction within a federation it joined as the smallest partner. The Sahel's rains returning. The 280,000 people of the Sereno coast living where their grandparents lived. A gas revenue methodology that resolves cleanly across commodity cycles. These are not constitutional failures. They are the problems that constitutions create conditions to address — and that democracies must address on their own terms, with their own choices, in their own time. The NRS at Year 30 contains forty-seven million records: executive orders, court decisions, legislative acts, Inspectorate findings, compact agreements, migration registrations, NVS election results, MA certifications, and the Director's 47-page dissent. Future citizens of the Republic of Seren — including those born in the Sahel and those born in a fishing village that no longer exists — will be able to read all of it. What they will do with what they read is the question Year 30 cannot answer.
