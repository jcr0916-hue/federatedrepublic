## Fix 1 — §8.5 For-Cause Removal Cannot Simultaneously Be a Rule Violation (§8.5 addition)

**Gap identified by:** State Designee Panel scenario test — Test 2 of session.

**The gap:** The constitution provides a For-Cause removal process for Inspectorate IGs
(§8.5.a: 2/3 both chambers + State Designee Panel verification). It does not address
whether a procedurally valid For-Cause removal — one that achieves the constitutional
threshold and follows the State Designee Panel process — can simultaneously constitute
a Rule Violation for the voting members if the underlying motivation was retaliatory.

If the Rule Violation track can reach voting members who completed the §8.5.a process
correctly, the removal power becomes functionally unusable: every For-Cause removal
creates Rule Violation exposure for the majority that voted for it. This chilling effect
would effectively entrench IGs beyond the removal standard the constitution intended.

**Proposed addition to §8.5.a:**

  "A removal of an Inspector General that achieves the constitutional threshold of this
   section and has been preceded by the State Designee Panel verification process of §8.9
   is not, solely by reason of the political motivation of any voting member or members,
   a Rule Violation under §2.7 for those members. Motive is not justiciable where the
   constitutional process is complete. This provision does not limit the Rule Violation
   accountability of any member for conduct outside the removal vote itself — including
   false statements in the stated grounds, fabrication of evidence presented to the
   State Designee Panel, or coercion of other members' votes — but the act of voting
   for removal at the constitutional threshold, on stated grounds that were presented to
   the Panel, is constitutionally protected legislative conduct."

**Rationale:** This mirrors the constitutional protection accorded to legislative votes
in most constitutional democracies — the Speech or Debate Clause analogue. Voting
within a constitutionally prescribed process at the constitutionally required threshold,
on stated grounds, is a legislative act that the constitution protects from collateral
judicial attack through the Rule Violation mechanism. The accountability for the removal
is political (the NRS record, the State Designee Panel's unsubstantiated finding
published alongside the vote) not judicial. The constitution has already built in the
accountability mechanism — making the removal vote itself a Rule Violation would
collapse the accountability framework it establishes.

**What this does NOT change:**
- The State Designee Panel process remains required
- The 2/3 threshold remains required
- False statements in stated grounds remain Rule Violations
- Fabrication of evidence presented to the Panel remains a Rule Violation
- Coercion of other members remains a Rule Violation
- Political motivation alone — where process is otherwise complete — is not justiciable
