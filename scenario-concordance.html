The Concordance Scenario

Five sovereign states ratify together. A continental neighbor views the union as encirclement and the constitutional framework as an ideological threat. Thirty-five years of constitutional durability under sustained external pressure — economic warfare, two military crises, one Intelligence Firewall violation, and the hardest test the document faces: whether the constitution survives not by defeating its enemies but by remaining itself under their pressure.

Thirty-Five Years — Seven Periods
  1. The Convention and Founding — Five sovereign states, one ratification (Years 0–4)
  2. The First Response — Maritime exclusion zone, Tier 1, first Tier 2 (Years 4–8)
  3. The Economic War — Trade sanctions, GNR under pressure, the Intelligence Firewall tested (Years 8–13)
  4. The Violation — EI finding, constitutional violation, constructive no confidence (Year 13)
  5. Détente — Maritime treaty, economic recovery, constitutional maturation (Years 14–20)
  6. Accession and Second Crisis — New States, encirclement pressure, second military crisis (Years 20–28)
  7. Resolution — Internal transformation, treaty holding, Year 32 assessment (Years 28–35)

The five governments that meet in Palavan City in Year 0 are not obvious constitutional partners. Palavan is an archipelago democracy with a history of strong executives and weak institutions. Thalaran is a constitutional monarchy whose royal family has agreed to step aside from governance but retains deep cultural authority. Mekoran has been a single-party state for forty years and is ratifying partly because its reformist faction has concluded that constitutional constraint is the only mechanism that will prevent the hardliners from returning to power. Sulakor is a diverse federal sultanate of twenty-three ethnic groups, two official religions, and a history of inter-ethnic tension that has always been managed rather than resolved. Nusaran is the vast archipelago — seventeen thousand islands, the largest Muslim-majority population in the region, a governing coalition that has held for twelve years and believes the constitutional framework is the only path to institutional durability beyond its own electoral survival.

What they share is geography, history, and a common assessment of the threat they face. The People's Compact of Zhuan occupies the continental north. It has claimed maritime jurisdiction over the Concordance Sea — the body of water between the five founding states and Zhuan's southern coast — for decades, on the basis of historical maps that no independent authority has verified and that the founding states have collectively refused to recognize. Zhuan's claims, if enforced, would give it effective control over the trade routes that connect Palavan's eastern islands to Nusaran's western ports, through which approximately 40% of the five states' collective trade moves. The founding states have managed this threat bilaterally for decades, each making separate accommodations to Zhuan's pressure. The union is, among other things, a decision that separate accommodation is no longer tenable.

The constitutional convention runs twenty-two months. The document that emerges from it is not a compromise between five different constitutional traditions. It is a constitution — one document, applied uniformly, with the multi-founder adaptation clause of §19.1 governing the founding mechanics. The five founding states become the Republic's first five States on Day Zero. Their previous governments, laws, and institutions are subject to the transition provisions. Their militaries will integrate. Their currencies will converge on a single monetary authority. Their citizenship systems will unify under §5.5.a's Grandfather Clause. The founding states are not retaining sovereignty in reserve. They are ratifying.

I. The Convention and Founding — Years 0 to 4

§19.1's multi-founder adaptation clause specifies that where a polity is constituted by more than one sovereign founding entity, the Statehood Audit proceeds simultaneously for all founding States during Phase 2, with the joint Inspectorate panel assessing each against the minimum democratic legitimacy and fiscal integrity criteria. All five founding states must pass their audits before Phase 3 elections can proceed. The clause also specifies that if any founding State fails its Statehood Audit, it enters the Remediation Plan process while the remaining founding States proceed to Phase 3 — it does not become a State immediately but retains a constitutional pathway through §13.3.b.

Mekoran is the audit concern. Forty years of single-party rule have left a judicial system that is institutionally independent in statute but culturally dependent in practice — judges appointed under the prior system have internalized a relationship to the executive that will take time to change. The joint Inspectorate panel issues an Early Warning at Month 14. The Remediation Plan requires structural judicial appointment reform: an independent commission composed of sitting judges selected by lottery, senior lawyers, and a civil society representative. The reform is enacted within the Remediation period. Mekoran passes its audit at Month 22. All five founding States pass. Phase 3 elections proceed.

§19.1 — Multi-Founder Military Integration
Year 2: Five militaries become one — the fund certification test

The most constitutionally demanding element of multi-founder ratification is not the judicial reforms or the citizenship unification. It is the military. Five armed forces, each with its own command structure, logistics chain, operational doctrine, and national tradition, must be integrated under the dual executive framework of Article II — subject to the President's Tier authorization and the PM's fund certification. The integration is not merely organizational. It requires each military to internalize a civilian constitutional relationship that most of them have never had in the same form.

The fund certification mechanism does the work that no ceremony could. Military payroll, logistics contracts, and operational funding all flow through the PM's domestic authority within the first year. Units that submit spending claims through the new constitutionally recognized channels receive payment. Units that continue through prior national command channels receive nothing. The pattern that appeared in the China Scenario over eighteen years appears here in compressed form: administrative momentum, not symbolic authority, produces subordination. By Year 2, all five militaries are functionally integrated under the Tier authorization framework. The senior officers know which civilian has the authority to certify their payroll. That knowledge shapes behavior faster than any cultural transformation program could.

The first President is elected in Year 2 — a retired Thalaran admiral whose foreign policy credentials and regional security expertise were the decisive factors in a Republic-wide ranked-choice election. The first PM is elected through the House majority, which is itself a coalition of reformist parties from all five founding States. The coalition is fragile from the beginning: the parties agree on the constitutional framework but disagree about everything else. This fragility will matter in Year 13.

By Year 4, the Republic has a functioning NRS — not without difficulty. The distributed node infrastructure required by §9.1.b must be built across the five founding States and their thousands of islands, some of which have no reliable electrical grid. The RSA Director, confirmed in Year 1, certifies in Year 4 that the node network has achieved full §9.1.b compliance. The provisional arrangements of the first three years — international server nodes certified by the joint Inspectorate panel under the transitional framework — are converted to permanent charter. The NRS that results is the most geographically distributed in any scenario the document has faced. The physical distribution turns out to be a constitutional advantage: no military action against any subset of the Republic's territory can take the NRS offline.

II. The First Response — Years 4 to 8

Zhuan's government has watched the constitutional convention with controlled alarm and the ratification with uncontrolled alarm. The alarm is partly strategic — five previously manageable bilateral relationships have been replaced by one constitutional actor that negotiates as a unit — and partly ideological. The Republic's constitutional framework is, from Zhuan's perspective, a direct challenge to its own domestic political legitimacy. A government that maintains authority through claimed civilizational superiority and managed information cannot view with equanimity a neighboring polity of 680 million people operating under a document that requires transparency, independent oversight, and democratic accountability for every exercise of power.

Zhuan's response is calibrated: pressure short of provocation, designed to demonstrate the costs of union without triggering the formal military response that would consolidate the founding states' political commitment to the constitutional framework. In Year 4, Month 6, Zhuan announces a Maritime Coordination Zone covering the northern Concordance Sea — an area that overlaps with the Republic's claimed exclusive economic zones in three founding States. The Coordination Zone does not claim sovereignty. It asserts a Zhuan right to inspect all commercial vessels transiting the zone for "security compliance." The commercial implications are immediate: shipping companies begin routing around the zone, adding four to six days to transit times for vessels moving between Palavan and Nusaran.

§2.1 — Tier Authorization
Year 4, Month 7: The President invokes Tier 1

The President invokes Tier 1 defensive operations within 48 hours of the Maritime Coordination Zone announcement — positioning Republic naval vessels as escorts for commercial shipping in the Republic's claimed EEZ within the zone. Tier 1 requires no additional authorization and triggers an automatic 96-hour Emergency Defense Allocation. The PM certifies the allocation within 24 hours.

The Tier 1 invocation is published to the NRS, as required. It is the first formal military action of the Republic's existence. The President's accompanying statement is precise: the Republic does not recognize the Coordination Zone, considers the inspection regime to be a violation of the Republic's sovereign rights over its EEZ, and will maintain a naval presence in the zone as long as the assertion of inspection authority persists. The statement is diplomatic in tone and constitutionally meticulous in form. The NRS record shows: an executive order, a fund certification, a public statement of rationale, all within 72 hours of the triggering event. Transparency is itself a signal — the Republic's government is not going to handle this quietly.

Zhuan does not challenge the Republic's naval escorts directly. It escalates through a different route: trade. By Year 5, Zhuan has imposed a series of technical barriers on Republic exports — phytosanitary certification requirements for agricultural products that effectively close the northern market to Sulakor's palm oil exports, and port access restrictions in Zhuan's southern ports that increase transit costs for Nusaran's manufacturing exports by approximately 22%. These are not formally sanctions. They are regulatory measures, each of which Zhuan can defend as legitimate domestic policy.

The effect on the Republic's GNR is measurable within two quarters. Trade with Zhuan and through Zhuan-controlled ports has represented approximately 28% of the five founding States' combined prior-year economic activity. As that activity contracts, the GNR formula reflects it. By Year 6, the Republic's GNR has fallen 11% from its Year 2 baseline. The BNF floor is still being met — the MA Director certifies compliance in Year 6 — but the trajectory is concerning. The Severe Contraction Protocol activates at a 20% GNR decline. The Republic is not there yet. But the direction is visible.

§2.1.b — Tier 2 Authorization
Year 6: First Senate Armed Services Committee authorization

In Year 6, a Zhuan naval vessel fires warning shots at a Republic fishing vessel operating within the Republic's claimed EEZ but inside the Coordination Zone. No one is killed. The fishing vessel retreats. The incident is documented by the Republic's naval escorts and published to the NRS within 24 hours.

The President invokes Tier 2 the same day: extended defensive operations including rules of engagement authorizing Republic naval vessels to interpose between Zhuan vessels and Republic civilian vessels within the Republic's claimed EEZ. The Senate Armed Services Committee convenes within 24 hours. The classified briefing on the incident is transmitted to the Committee. The Committee authorizes within 72 hours. The PM certifies funds within the same window. Republic naval vessels begin active escort operations with authorized defensive ROE.

Zhuan does not fire again. The Tier 2 invocation changes the calculus: previously, Zhuan could treat Republic naval presence as symbolic. An authorized Tier 2 with active ROE is not symbolic. The incidents in the Coordination Zone de-escalate. The trade restrictions do not.

III. The Economic War — Years 8 to 13

By Year 8, the Republic and Zhuan have settled into a stable pattern of low-level military tension and sustained economic pressure. The Coordination Zone exists; the Republic's naval escorts operate within it; Zhuan's vessels conduct inspections at the margins of the zone but not of escorted Republic civilian vessels. The trade restrictions have become permanent features of the economic relationship. GNR has fallen 16% from its Year 2 baseline and stopped declining — the Republic's trade routes have partially rerouted through third-country ports, Nusaran's manufacturing sector has diversified toward domestic consumption markets, and Sulakor has opened new export markets in the western archipelago trade routes. But partial recovery is still contraction. The MA Director's Year 8 certification notes that the BNF is being met — by a narrower margin than in Year 4.

The economic pressure produces political pressure inside the Republic. The House coalition fractures at its weakest point: Mekoran's parliamentary delegation, whose constituencies are the most exposed to the northern trade route disruption, begins advocating for separate bilateral negotiations with Zhuan. The constitutional framework does not prohibit this — it is politics, not a constitutional violation, to argue for a different policy. But the argument exposes the founding logic of the union: the five states ratified together precisely because bilateral accommodation had failed. A Mekoran that negotiates separately is not defecting from the constitution; it is arguing for a constitutional policy that the rest of the House majority views as a betrayal of the founding premise.

The PM — whose coalition has held through three years of economic pressure partly through the discipline of shared adversity — decides in Year 10 to act. What happens next is the constitutional crisis the document was built to handle, and almost fails to handle.

§12.3 — The Intelligence Firewall
Year 10–13: What the PM authorized

§12.3 draws a bright line: the Republic's intelligence and security services may not conduct surveillance, infiltration, or information collection operations targeting Republic citizens or legal residents. Military intelligence functions are confined to external operations. Domestic security operations require judicial warrant, are subject to the procedural protections of Article I, and are reported annually to the LI in aggregate form. Any intelligence operation that crosses the line between external intelligence and domestic surveillance of citizens is a constitutional violation.

The PM, in a classified PM Order recorded to the NRS as a classified document (with existence public, content sealed), authorizes what is described as a "Zhuan Influence Mapping Initiative." The stated purpose is to identify Zhuan intelligence assets operating within the Republic — a legitimate external counterintelligence function. The initial scope is what the PM described. What the operation becomes over three years is something different.

By Year 13, the Mapping Initiative has produced surveillance files on approximately 4,400 individuals. Of these, the EI's audit finds: 340 have documented contacts with Zhuan diplomatic or commercial entities that might constitute intelligence relationships. The remaining 4,060 are journalists who have written critically about the PM's economic management, academics who have published analyses of Republic-Zhuan relations that were critical of the government's military posture, opposition politicians from Mekoran who had argued for bilateral negotiation, and civil society representatives who had publicly opposed elements of the PM's security policy.

The EI does not discover this through a tip or a leak. It discovers it through the classified classification registry — the NRS entry for every classified document must be created within 24 hours of classification, even if the content is sealed. The registry shows a large and growing body of classified material associated with the Mapping Initiative. The EI requests access to the classified content under its audit authority. The PM's office initially objects. The Supreme Court rules within 14 days that the EI's audit authority over executive operations extends to classified material subject to appropriate security protocols. The EI reviews the classified files. The finding is published — in a version that protects the identities of individuals where disclosure would cause harm — within 30 days of access being granted.

IV. The Violation — Year 13

The EI's finding is one of the longest constitutional violation determinations in the document's history. It runs to 87 pages of published findings and 340 pages of classified appendices registered on the NRS. The published version states its conclusion in the first paragraph: the Zhuan Influence Mapping Initiative, as implemented over Years 10 through 13, constitutes a sustained constitutional violation of §12.3 — the Intelligence Firewall — through the domestic surveillance of Republic citizens and legal residents on the basis of their speech, political association, and profession, without judicial warrant or any constitutionally recognized basis for domestic intelligence collection.

The PM does not contest the factual finding. The PM contests the characterization: the surveillance, she argues, was of individuals with documented or suspected Zhuan connections, conducted in the interest of national security under conditions of active economic warfare and military tension. The EI's finding does not accept this characterization. The standard is not what the PM believed she was doing. It is whether the surveillance was of Republic citizens or legal residents. The files document that it was. The Intelligence Firewall does not have a national security exception. Its value as a constitutional protection lies precisely in the fact that it does not.

Article VIII constitutional violation — §2.3.b Constructive No Confidence
Year 13, Month 7: The mechanism works under maximum pressure

The Inspectorate finding triggers Article VIII. The PM is suspended from duties pending the constitutional consequence determination. The Deputy PM assumes caretaker status under §2.2.h. The EI's finding is on the NRS. Every citizen of the Republic can read the 87-page published version. The Mekoran parliamentary delegation — whose members were among those surveilled — calls for immediate removal.

The House faces the hardest version of the constructive no-confidence requirement. The PM's coalition has held through years of external pressure. Removing her now, under Zhuan's sustained economic warfare, risks appearing to Zhuan as a constitutional vulnerability — proof that the Republic's institutions crack under pressure. The argument for inaction is available: hold the coalition together, address the violation through a lesser consequence, avoid giving Zhuan the satisfaction of watching the Republic's government collapse.

The argument fails, for a reason that reflects the constitutional design. The Intelligence Firewall violation is not a policy disagreement. It is a documented breach of a specific constitutional provision that the document places beyond executive modification. The PM authorized surveillance of people for their speech and political association. The NRS records this. Every subsequent political calculation made within the House occurs in the knowledge that the record exists and is permanent. The question for each House member is not whether the violation occurred — the EI has answered that — but whether they are willing to stand in front of their constituents in the next election and explain why they kept the PM in office after she surveilled journalists and opposition politicians.

The constructive no-confidence motion names a successor: the PM of Thalaran's founding coalition, a former civil liberties lawyer who has spent three years in opposition to the surveillance program without knowing the classified details that the EI has now published. She is not the most experienced foreign policy operator in the House. She is the most credibly constitutional candidate available. The motion passes 312-198. The new PM is elected simultaneously. The transition of government authority occurs within the constitutional framework, under conditions of active external military pressure, without incident.

Zhuan's government responds to the political transition with a statement calling it "evidence of constitutional instability." The statement is published. The Republic's NRS publishes, on the same day, the complete record: the EI finding, the constitutional violation determination, the constructive no-confidence motion, the simultaneous election of the successor PM, and the transition of authority — all documented, timestamped, and permanently available. The NRS record is the Republic's answer to Zhuan's characterization. What Zhuan is calling instability the NRS demonstrates to be exactly the opposite: an institution that identified its own government's constitutional violation, published the finding, and changed the government within the constitutional framework without military intervention, institutional breakdown, or suspension of any Article I right. The transition took 23 days from EI finding to new PM sworn in.

V. Détente — Years 14 to 20

The new PM's first act is to declassify the Mapping Initiative files — not the intelligence assessments of the 340 individuals with documented Zhuan connections, which remain classified for legitimate national security reasons, but all materials relating to the surveillance of journalists, academics, opposition politicians, and civil society representatives. The declassified files are published to the NRS. The 4,060 individuals whose files were created learn, for the first time, that they were surveilled. Most of them are ordinary citizens who held views the prior government found inconvenient. The declassification is a constitutional act, not merely a political one: it restores to the surveilled individuals the knowledge of what was done to them, which is the minimum the constitution owes anyone whose rights were violated by their own government.

The new PM's second act is to open treaty negotiations with Zhuan on the maritime boundary question. This is not a capitulation to Zhuan's pressure — the negotiations proceed on the basis that the Republic's EEZ claims are valid and non-negotiable, and that the Coordination Zone has no legal basis. What the PM is offering is a bilateral framework for managing disputes that replaces the current standoff with an agreed process. Zhuan has never had a reliable interlocutor for this offer because the five founding states each approached it differently. The Republic speaks with one voice.

§12.5 — Treaty Ratification
Year 17: The Concordance Sea Maritime Framework Treaty

Three years of negotiation produce a treaty that neither side would describe as a victory. The Concordance Sea Maritime Framework Treaty establishes: a joint dispute resolution panel for maritime incidents; an agreed EEZ boundary map that Zhuan has agreed not to contest (without acknowledging the legal basis for the Republic's claims); a commercial vessel transit protocol that eliminates the inspection requirement for Republic-flagged vessels in exchange for a Republic commitment to notify Zhuan of military vessel transits in a defined northern zone; and a trade normalization schedule that will gradually remove the technical barriers Zhuan imposed in Years 5 through 8.

The treaty is submitted to the Senate for ratification under §12.5. The Senate has 14 days to act or the treaty lapses if the Legislature cannot convene within the statutory period. The Senate debates for 11 days. The treaty is ratified 82-18. It is published to the NRS — in full, translated into all official languages of the founding States — within 24 hours of ratification. Zhuan ratifies through its own process three weeks later. The Coordination Zone does not formally cease to exist, but Zhuan ceases to assert inspection authority within it. Republic naval escorts continue to operate in the zone under Tier 1, which the President maintains at lower intensity as a background presence rather than an active escort mission.

The economic recovery that follows the treaty is real but incomplete. Trade with Zhuan resumes on the treaty schedule, but the Republic's traders have spent seven years developing alternative routes and markets. They do not simply revert. GNR reaches its Year 2 baseline again by Year 18, then exceeds it. The BNF floor, which had been met at declining margins during Years 8 through 14, is now met with comfortable headroom. The MA Director's Year 18 certification is the most unambiguous in the Republic's history.

The constitutional violation's political legacy plays out across two election cycles. In Year 15 — the first national elections after the PM transition — the parties associated with the surveillance program lose significant House seats. In Year 17, the former PM herself stands for election to the House as a backbencher from her home constituency in Palavan. She wins — her constituents return her. But the NRS record of the constitutional violation is part of every subsequent campaign against her. She never leads a government again. The record does not erase her political career. It constrains it permanently and publicly.

VI. Accession and Second Crisis — Years 20 to 28

By Year 20, the Republic is a functioning constitutional polity of 680 million people, with a seven-year-old maritime treaty, a recovering economy, and a constitutional performance record that has become widely known in the region. The Statehood Audit process, the NRS transparency architecture, the dual executive model, and — most prominently — the Inspectorate reporting mechanism's treatment of the PM's surveillance program have been studied in detail by every government in the region that is watching the Republic's trajectory.

Two governments approach the Republic in Year 20 about accession. The first is a small archipelago state to the east of Palavan — a former colonial territory that achieved independence forty years ago and has spent that time building democratic institutions that its constitution cannot reliably protect against executive capture. The second is a mainland state to the south of Thalaran, whose government has concluded, after watching the PM's removal, that the constitutional accountability mechanisms are worth the sovereignty they cost.

Both governments apply for Statehood Audit assessment under §13.2.b. The joint Inspectorate panel — its mandate extended by Senate resolution to cover accession assessments as well as transition oversight — conducts both audits simultaneously. The eastern archipelago passes its audit in Month 18 of the accession process and enters the Provisional Membership Period. The mainland state takes longer: its judicial independence provisions are structurally sound but its census data is twelve years out of date, creating a House seat allocation problem under §3.1.b that requires resolution before accession can proceed. The LI works with the acceding state to conduct a population survey. The state achieves Statehood in Year 24.

§13.2 — Accession Under External Pressure
Year 21: Zhuan's encirclement argument

Zhuan's government interprets the accession applications as a deliberate encirclement strategy. The maritime treaty has been in effect for four years and has produced the expected modest normalization of trade relations. The accession of two additional states — one to the east of Palavan, extending the Republic's maritime presence, and one to the south of Thalaran, extending its land border — changes the strategic geography in ways the treaty did not contemplate.

Zhuan's formal response is a diplomatic note delivered to the Republic's President asserting that the accession of additional states violates the "spirit" of the Concordance Sea Maritime Framework Treaty. The Republic's President publishes the note to the NRS — because all diplomatic communications received by the executive are required to be registered, and because the President judges that Zhuan's claim should be visible rather than handled quietly — and responds that the treaty governs the maritime boundary and commercial relations in the Concordance Sea and has no provisions addressing the Republic's constitutional processes for admitting new States. The accession processes continue.

Zhuan's military response to the accession is the most serious incident in the Republic's thirty-five year history. In Year 23, Zhuan conducts twelve days of naval and air exercises in the Concordance Sea, including areas that the Maritime Framework Treaty expressly designated as shared commercial lanes. The exercises involve approximately 140 surface vessels and significant air support — the largest Zhuan military exercise in the Concordance Sea in recorded history. No weapons are fired. No Republic vessel is directly threatened. The exercises are designed to communicate Zhuan's displeasure with the accession process without crossing any line that would trigger the Republic's treaty-based collective defense obligations.

§2.1 — Second Tier 2 Authorization
Year 23: Republic mobilization without escalation

The President invokes Tier 2 on Day 3 of the exercises — the point at which the exercise pattern makes clear this is not a routine transit but a deliberate presence. The Senate Armed Services Committee authorizes within 48 hours. The Republic mobilizes its naval forces to a full defensive posture in the Concordance Sea — not interposing between Zhuan's exercising forces, which would risk an incident, but establishing a clear Republic presence that makes any escalation by Zhuan a deliberate choice rather than an incremental one.

The PM simultaneously invokes the treaty's dispute resolution panel — the first time the panel has been used since ratification. The complaint is specific: the exercises are conducted within designated commercial lanes, creating navigational hazards for commercial vessels and constituting a violation of the commercial transit protocol. The panel convenes within its required 30-day period. Zhuan participates — because not participating would acknowledge that the treaty doesn't constrain it, which is a more damaging admission than accepting the panel's jurisdiction and arguing the exercises were lawful.

The exercises end on Day 12, one day before the panel is scheduled to issue preliminary findings. The Republic's naval forces stand down from Tier 2 within 72 hours of the exercises concluding. The President's stand-down order is published to the NRS, as required. The panel publishes its findings regardless: the exercises were in technical violation of the commercial lane protocol. The finding is on the record. Zhuan does not acknowledge the finding, which also goes on the record.

The second crisis does not produce a second Intelligence Firewall violation. This is not because the pressure to extend surveillance is absent — there are voices within the security apparatus, in Year 23 and Year 24, arguing that the exercise period justifies expanded domestic intelligence collection to identify potential Zhuan assets. The PM declines. The EI's annual audit in Year 24 finds no domestic surveillance activity outside the warrant framework. The institutional memory of Year 13 is doing constitutional work: the security apparatus knows that the EI will audit, that the NRS will record, and that the prior PM's experience with the consequence of crossing the Intelligence Firewall is permanently on the public record.

VII. Resolution — Years 28 to 35

The resolution of the Republic-Zhuan tension does not come through further negotiation or military deterrence. It comes through Zhuan's internal political transformation — a process the Republic had no role in producing and cannot take credit for.

Beginning in Year 26, Zhuan experiences a period of internal political contest between its security apparatus and a technocratic faction that has concluded that the economic costs of confrontational regional policy are unsustainable. The contest is not visible from the outside for two years. What becomes visible in Year 28 is the result: a new leadership configuration in Zhuan that is still authoritarian, still committed to territorial claims in the Concordance Sea, and still ideologically hostile to the Republic's constitutional framework — but is willing to treat the maritime treaty as a stable baseline rather than a transitional arrangement pending a more favorable regional power balance.

The Republic's NRS has thirty-five years of records by the time the Year 32 constitutional assessment is published. The assessment is the joint work of the three Inspectorates. It is 340 pages long in its published version. Its summary findings occupy four pages.

§8.11 — Year 32 Constitutional Performance Assessment (Summary)

The Republic has maintained continuous NRS operation for 32 years. The node network — distributed across seven States and their island territories — has never experienced a compliance failure. No military action by any external actor has disrupted NRS recording. The transparency architecture the constitution requires functions and is, after 32 years, genuinely taken for granted: citizens file petitions to the NRS, challenge NRS entries in court, and read classified registration records as a matter of routine. The infrastructure whose absence was a constitutional vulnerability in Year 0 has become invisible through success.

The Intelligence Firewall violation of Year 13 is the most significant constitutional failure in the assessment period. It is also the constitutional mechanism's most significant success. The EI identified the violation through audit, not through whistleblower disclosure. The Inspectorate finding was published within the constitutionally required period. The constructive no-confidence mechanism operated as designed — the House elected a successor PM simultaneously with removing the prior PM, and the transition occurred within 23 days without military intervention, institutional breakdown, or suspension of any Article I right. The constitutional violation is on the NRS permanently. The former PM returned to political life and was elected to the House by her constituents. The constitution did not end her career; it constrained it by making its record permanent. This is the correct outcome: accountability without prohibition, transparency without destruction.

The BNF was met in every fiscal year of the assessment period. The Severe Contraction Protocol came within 4 percentage points of activation during the economic warfare period of Years 8 through 14. It did not activate. The MA Director's annual certifications during that period show the floor being met at declining margins — the constitutional record of a society under economic stress maintaining its minimum commitments. The record is neither celebratory nor alarming. It is accurate. A constitution that required the floor to be met without difficulty would be setting a floor that could always be met without difficulty. The floor that the Republic maintained during sustained economic warfare is constitutionally more meaningful than a floor maintained during prosperity.

Military subordination to civilian constitutional authority is complete. The Tier authorization framework has been invoked nine times over 32 years — four Tier 1 invocations, five Tier 2 authorizations, and zero Tier 3 legislative declarations of defense. Each invocation is on the NRS with its authorization record, its duration, its cost certification, and its stand-down order. The Republic has been in genuine military confrontation with a nuclear-armed continental power for the entirety of its existence. It has not been attacked. It has not attacked. The deterrence produced by the Tier framework — visible, documented, constitutionally authorized — appears to have been effective. Whether it would have remained effective if a different government had been in office during Year 23 is not a question the assessment can answer. It is a question the NRS record allows future historians to examine.

The Republic is seven States at Year 32. The two acceding States are functioning constitutional actors with their own House and Senate delegations, their own Statehood Audit compliance records, and their own NRS node contributions. The founding premise — that five states separately vulnerable are constitutionally stronger together — has been tested over 32 years of sustained external pressure and has not been falsified. Whether the premise will be validated over the next 32 years belongs to those who will live it.

What the Concordance Scenario Tests

This scenario is the only one in the scenarios library that places the Republic under sustained, genuine external military and economic threat from a hostile great power over a period long enough for institutional drift to become a realistic concern. Most constitutional failures under external threat are not dramatic: they are incremental — a surveillance program that exceeds its mandate, an emergency derogation that becomes habitual, a treaty process that is bypassed because the executive judges speed to be more important than ratification. The scenario tests whether the constitution's mechanisms can interrupt those incremental failures before they become permanent.

The Intelligence Firewall violation is the scenario's central constitutional event. It demonstrates three things simultaneously. First, that the violation occurred — the PM made a choice the constitution prohibits, and the security apparatus implemented it. External pressure does not excuse this; it explains the political logic but does not change the constitutional analysis. Second, that the mechanism worked — the EI found the violation through audit, published the finding, and the constitutional consequence followed without requiring any extraordinary action by any actor. Third, that the recovery was genuine — the successor PM's first acts were to declassify the surveillance files and open treaty negotiations, demonstrating that the constitutional change produced a different policy, not merely a different face on the same policy.

The dual executive framework's performance under sustained military pressure is the scenario's most important structural finding. The President manages the Tier authorization framework across 32 years and three different administrations — the Tier record is consistent because it is constitutionally structured, not because any particular president was wise. The PM manages the economic response, the treaty negotiation, and the domestic political consequences of external pressure — including the consequences of the Year 13 violation. The two executives operate in their respective domains without collapsing into each other. No President attempts to use military authority to override the PM's treaty negotiations. No PM attempts to use fiscal authority to constrain the President's Tier invocations. The domain separation works because it is structural, not because it is gentlemanly.

The NRS's role in this scenario cannot be overstated. Every Tier invocation is on the record. Every treaty is on the record. The Intelligence Firewall violation is on the record — in 87 published pages that any citizen of the Republic could read on the day of publication and can still read today. Zhuan's diplomatic notes asserting that the accession process violates the spirit of the maritime treaty are on the record alongside the Republic's response. The Year 23 naval exercises and the dispute panel's finding are on the record. The consequence of this permanent, transparent record is that the Republic's government is constitutionally accountable to its own history in ways that no government operating without such a record can be. The accountability is not merely to current voters or current courts — it is to the permanent document of what was done, when, by whom, and with what constitutional authorization. Thirty-five years of that document makes the Republic constitutionally legible in a way that most polities never achieve.
