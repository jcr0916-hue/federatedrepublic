Monitor General Removal · §9.14 · SC Categorical Review

The Grounds Review

Year 5. The Assembly initiates removal proceedings against the EM Monitor General. A concurrent resolution of 1/3 of the Assembly files stated grounds with the SC under §9.14. The SC has 14 days to determine whether the grounds constitute a statutory removal category. The EM Monitor General challenges the categorization.

§9.14 Constitutional Officer Removal — Standard Track §9.5 Monitor General Removal §10.1 National Record System

In Year 5, a concurrent resolution signed by 38% of Assembly members — well above the 1/3 threshold — was filed with the SC under §9.14. The stated grounds alleged that EM Monitor General Priya Nair had systematically failed to audit the Civic Consul's intelligence authorization records, constituting dereliction of the EM's constitutional mandate under §9.1.b.

The SC's categorical role under §9.14 is narrow. It does not assess whether Nair actually neglected her duties. It does not evaluate the credibility of the allegations. It determines only whether the stated grounds — systematic failure to audit within the EM's mandate — constitute at least one statutory removal category as defined by the Legislature. The Legislature had defined the removal categories in statute: dereliction of constitutional mandate was category three.

§9.14 — Day 14
Grounds confirmed. Duty suspension activates immediately.

The SC published its finding on Day 14. The stated grounds were confirmed as falling within statutory category three. Nair's duty suspension activated immediately on publication — by constitutional operation, not by further proceeding. The most senior deputy assumed acting authority.

Nair's legal team noted immediately that the SC had confirmed only that the stated grounds fit a statutory category, not that any dereliction had actually occurred. This was correct — and precisely the design. The categorical review is a gatekeeping mechanism, not a finding of fact. The factual determination would come from the Legislature.

The Removal Vote

With grounds confirmed, the Legislature had the authority to proceed to a removal vote under §9.14 Standard Track. The vote required 2/3 of both chambers simultaneously. The Assembly moved quickly; the Senate scheduled a hearing. In the Senate proceedings, the factual record was examined — the EM's audit logs, the Civic Consul's intelligence authorization records, and Nair's documented audit decisions over six years.

The Senate vote fell 59-41 — short of the 2/3 threshold needed for removal. Nair was reinstated immediately. The §9.14 grounds confirmation had been correct: the stated grounds fit the statutory category. But the Legislature had evaluated the facts and found them insufficient for removal at the 2/3 threshold. The categorical gate opened; the factual determination closed it.

What This Scenario Tests

§9.14 separates two questions that removal proceedings tend to conflate: does the stated ground constitute a cognizable basis for removal, and did the officer actually do what is alleged? The SC's categorical review answers the first. The Legislature's removal vote answers the second. Keeping them sequential — categorical gate, then factual determination — prevents the Legislature from removing an officer on grounds that aren't constitutionally recognized, while preserving the Legislature's full factual authority once the grounds are confirmed.

The scenario also demonstrates the protection built into the 2/3 threshold. A majority of the Senate may have believed Nair had been lax — the 59-41 vote suggests it. But 2/3 did not find the factual case sufficient for permanent removal of a constitutional officer. The high threshold is the protection; the §9.14 categorical review is the gate. Both do different work.
