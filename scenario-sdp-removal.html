Inspectorate Accountability · §8.5.a · §8.9.f–i · IG For-Cause Removal

The Record

The Legislative Inspectorate Inspector General is accused of systematic dereliction — consistently incomplete compliance findings that fail to flag known NRS irregularities. The EI and JI transmit the grounds. The State Designee Panel reviews the documentary record and finds grounds unsubstantiated. The Legislature proceeds to the removal vote anyway. The finding and the vote are both on the NRS permanently. What the constitution does when its accountability mechanisms disagree.

§8.5.a For-Cause IG Removal §8.9 State Designee Panel §8.9.f Activation §8.9.g Scope of Review §8.9.h Effect of Finding §8.9.i SC Review §9.1 NRS Immutability

Inspector General Adara Fenn has led the Legislative Inspectorate for seven years. She is two years from the end of her ten-year non-renewable term. Her annual State of the Republic Reports are thorough in some domains and, her critics argue, conspicuously vague in one: the NRS integrity audit. The NRS publishes every law, every vote, every executive order — and the LI’s annual audit is supposed to verify that publication is complete, accurate, and timely. For three consecutive years, Fenn’s NRS integrity sections have read the same way: procedurally compliant, no systemic irregularities identified.

The EI disagrees. Its own annual review of executive NRS obligations has flagged, in each of the same three years, a pattern of delayed publication entries in the legislative record — bills whose passage records appeared on the NRS between 48 and 72 hours after the constitutional 24-hour requirement, with no notation of the delay and no correction entry. The EI’s findings are on the NRS. Fenn’s findings, which do not mention them, are also on the NRS. The discrepancy has been public for three years. The House Accountability Committee has raised the issue twice in session. Fenn’s office has responded both times that the delayed entries fall within acceptable administrative variance.

The Grounds

In Year 8 of Fenn’s term, the EI and JI jointly initiate a For-Cause removal proceeding under §8.5.a. The stated grounds are documented dereliction of mandate: that Fenn’s consistent failure to flag the publication delay pattern in three consecutive NRS integrity audits, in the face of the EI’s contemporaneous findings to the contrary, constitutes a knowing failure to perform a core function of the LI’s mandate.

The documentary record transmitted to the State Designee Panel is substantial: Fenn’s three NRS integrity sections; the EI’s three corresponding findings; the two House Accountability Committee transcripts; Fenn’s written responses; and the underlying NRS publication logs showing the delayed entries.

§8.5.a — IG For-Cause Removal · Day 0
EI and JI transmit grounds to the JI — draw triggered

Transmission of the grounds and documentary record to the JI activates the proceeding and triggers the draw under §8.9.d. The JI has five days to constitute a Panel by public lottery from the national eligible pool. The EI assumes pool monitoring duties for the duration of the proceeding, consistent with §8.9.c’s exception for active LI IG removal proceedings. The LI IG is suspended from duties by operation of §8.5.a. The 21-day review clock will begin from the date the Panel is fully constituted — five members having accepted service and any draw challenges resolved.

The Panel’s Review

The Panel meets in closed session on Days 4, 9, and 14. It does not conduct witness interviews. It does not commission expert analysis of acceptable variance in NRS publication timelines. It requests no additional documentary submissions from the initiating body. The Panel’s function under §8.9.g is categorical: does the documented conduct constitute “documented dereliction” within the constitutional meaning of that category for LI IG For-Cause removal?

The Panel’s working question is narrow. Dereliction in the constitutional sense requires a documented failure to perform a function of the mandate — not a methodological disagreement about how that function is best performed. The LI’s NRS integrity audit mandate is real. The question is whether Fenn’s published findings constitute a failure to perform it, or a performance of it that the EI disagrees with.

The record the Panel reviews does not show Fenn’s office ignoring the delayed entries. It shows her office reviewing them and classifying them as within acceptable administrative variance. The EI classified the same entries differently. Both classifications are documented. Neither is facially absurd. The publication delays are real; the question of whether they constitute “systemic irregularities” requiring notation is a question of professional judgment about which reasonable Inspectorates can disagree.

The Panel’s finding is published on Day 19.

State Designee Panel — Day 19 — NRS Publication

Finding: Grounds unsubstantiated.

The Panel has reviewed the documentary record transmitted by the initiating body. The stated grounds allege documented dereliction of mandate. The Panel’s review is categorical: does the submitted record establish that the stated grounds constitute at least one of the enumerated constitutional categories for For-Cause removal applicable to the Inspector General of the Legislative Inspectorate?

The submitted record establishes that the named officer published NRS integrity findings that differ materially from contemporaneous findings of the Executive Inspectorate on the same subject matter. The submitted record does not establish that the named officer failed to perform the NRS integrity audit function — it establishes that she performed it and reached a different conclusion than the EI. Professional disagreement between Inspectorates about audit methodology and classification standards does not, on the face of the submitted record, constitute documented dereliction within the constitutional category.

The Panel makes no finding as to whether the named officer’s methodology was correct, whether her classifications were appropriate, or whether her continued service is advisable. These are not matters within the Panel’s categorical review function. This finding becomes permanent public record alongside any subsequent legislative vote.

The Vote

§8.9.h is unambiguous: a finding of grounds unsubstantiated does not bar the Legislature from proceeding to a removal vote. The House and Senate Accountability Committees have been watching the proceeding. Three years of EI findings, two committee inquiries, and a Panel finding that — in the view of a legislative majority — applied the constitutional categories too narrowly. The House Speaker announces the removal vote will proceed.

Fenn petitions the Supreme Court for review of the Panel’s finding under §8.9.i within 14 days of NRS publication. Her petition argues the Panel’s categorical determination was constitutionally erroneous: that the difference between the EI’s findings and hers, sustained over three years and never corrected despite public notice, crosses the line from methodological disagreement into documented dereliction. The Court accepts the petition and schedules argument.

§8.9.i — SC Review · Day 33
Court reviews Panel’s categorical determination — not the Legislature’s vote

The petition does not stay the legislative vote. Fenn’s duty-suspension under §8.5.a continues. The Court’s jurisdiction under §8.9.i is narrow: it reviews whether the Panel correctly applied the constitutional categories to the submitted record. It does not review the underlying policy question of whether Fenn should be removed, or whether the Legislature is acting wisely in proceeding past the Panel’s finding.

The Court rules 5-2. The majority: the Panel’s categorical determination was within the permissible range of applications of the dereliction standard to the submitted record. The Panel identified a genuine ambiguity — the distinction between failed performance and disputed performance — and resolved it in the manner §8.9.g requires: by declining to evaluate policy merit and confining itself to whether the record establishes categorical dereliction. The Court does not hold that the Panel’s resolution was the only permissible one, only that it was not constitutionally erroneous. The petition is denied.

The House votes 289-211 for removal. The Senate votes 68-32. Both exceed the 2/3 threshold required by §8.5.a. Fenn is removed.

NRS Entry — Legislative Vote — Day 47 House vote: 289-211 (2/3 threshold: 267). Senate vote: 68-32 (2/3 threshold: 67). Inspector General Fenn removed by For-Cause vote. State Designee Panel finding (Day 19): grounds unsubstantiated. SC review (Day 40): Panel determination upheld. All three records published at this entry and indexed together permanently.

What This Tests

The most important thing this scenario tests is not whether Fenn deserved to be removed. That question is permanently unresolved — the Panel found the grounds unsubstantiated on the documentary record, the Court upheld the Panel, and the Legislature removed her anyway. All three positions are on the NRS. Future constitutional scholars will argue about which was right.

What the mechanism tests is the design question at the heart of §8.9: what does a verification panel accomplish if the Legislature can proceed past its finding? The scenario shows the answer. The Panel’s unsubstantiated finding didn’t block the removal — but it forced the Legislature to own the removal in a specific way. Every member who voted for removal did so with the Panel’s published finding on the record. The NRS entry indexing the Panel finding alongside the vote is not incidental. It is the accountability mechanism the constitution chose in place of a hard gate.

The two-IG joint finding mechanism that §8.5.a previously used would have required the EI and JI to affirmatively endorse the grounds before the Legislature could vote. The SDP replaces institutional endorsement with civic verification. The difference is not just procedural. It removes the EI and JI from the role of evaluating whether their peer should be removed — a role that puts institutional solidarity in structural tension with honest assessment — and places that evaluation in the hands of citizens with no stake in the outcome.

The Panel’s finding in this scenario was unsubstantiated. The Legislature disagreed and had the constitutional authority to proceed. The disagreement is now permanent. That is what §8.9.h was designed to produce: not a block on legislative judgment, but a documented counterpoint to it.

What the NRS now shows — permanently

Three years of EI findings flagging NRS publication delays. Three years of LI findings classifying the same delays as within acceptable variance. Two House committee inquiries. A State Designee Panel review finding grounds unsubstantiated. A Supreme Court ruling upholding the Panel’s categorical determination. A 289-211 House vote and a 68-32 Senate vote removing the Inspector General despite the unsubstantiated finding. Each record indexed alongside the others.

Future legislators considering a For-Cause removal of an Inspector General will search the NRS. They will find this case. They will see what it looks like when the Legislature proceeds past an unsubstantiated Panel finding. That is the accountability the mechanism provides — not a veto, but a permanent record that makes the choice visible.
