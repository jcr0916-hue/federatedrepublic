The Grounds Review

Year 5. The Assembly initiates removal proceedings against the EM Monitor General. The SC confirms the grounds in 14 days — but grounds confirmed is not removal. The Senate votes. It falls short. The Monitor is reinstated. The JMC publishes.

§9.9 Constitutional Officer Removal §9.7 The JMC §9.7.a Institutional Compromise Protocol §9.5 Monitor Operations §10.1 National Record System

In Year 5, a concurrent resolution signed by 38% of Assembly members — well above the 1/3 threshold — was filed with the SC under §9.9. The stated grounds alleged that EM Monitor General Priya Nair had systematically failed to audit the Civic Consul's intelligence authorization records, constituting dereliction of the EM's constitutional mandate under §9.1.b. The resolution was filed on a Thursday. Nair's duty suspension, if the SC confirmed grounds, would activate immediately on publication of the SC's finding.

The SC's categorical role under §9.9 is narrow and deliberately so. It does not assess whether Nair actually neglected her duties. It does not evaluate the credibility of the allegations. It determines only whether the stated grounds — systematic failure to audit within the EM's mandate — constitute at least one statutory removal category as defined by the Legislature. The Legislature had defined the removal categories in statute. Dereliction of constitutional mandate was category three.

The SC's Fourteen Days

§9.9 — Day 14
Grounds confirmed. Duty suspension activates immediately by constitutional operation.

The SC published its finding on Day 14. The stated grounds were confirmed as falling within statutory category three. Nair's duty suspension activated immediately on publication — by constitutional operation, not by further vote or proceeding. The most senior EM deputy assumed acting authority. The finding was published to the NRS as a permanent record.

Nair's legal team noted immediately that the SC had confirmed only that the stated grounds fit a statutory category, not that any dereliction had actually occurred. This was correct — and precisely the design. The categorical review is a gatekeeping mechanism, not a finding of fact. It ensures that removal proceedings cannot be opened on grounds the constitution does not recognize. The factual determination would come from the Legislature.

§9.9 — The Judicial Track Alternative
The LM and JM had a second option they chose not to use.

The Standard Track — 1/3 Assembly initiates, SC reviews grounds, Legislature votes — is one of two removal paths under §9.9. The Judicial Track requires two Monitors to jointly assess and report for-cause grounds, then petition the SC directly; the SC rules within 14 days; if confirmed, the Senate has 60 days to vote at 3/5, or the question goes to a national ballot.

The LM and JM had discussed the Judicial Track in the weeks before the Assembly resolution. They decided against it — their assessment was that the factual record was genuinely ambiguous, and the Judicial Track's lower Senate threshold (3/5 rather than 2/3) felt disproportionate to the ambiguity of the case. They issued a joint statement noting the Assembly had chosen to pursue the Standard Track and that the JMC would monitor the proceedings. This statement was published to the NRS the same day the Assembly resolution was filed.

The Senate Trial

With grounds confirmed, the Legislature had the authority to proceed to a removal vote. The vote required 2/3 of both chambers simultaneously. The Assembly moved to schedule immediately. The Senate held extended hearings spanning eleven days, examining the EM's audit logs, the Civic Consul's intelligence authorization records, and Nair's documented audit decisions over six years. Nair testified for two full days.

The factual record was genuinely contested. Nair's position was that her audit team had reviewed the intelligence authorization records and found them properly categorized — not that she had failed to audit them. The Assembly's position was that the categorization itself was the problem: records that should have been audited as executive orders had been classified as intelligence assessments, placing them outside the EM's ordinary audit cycle. The constitutional question was whether Nair's scoping decision was a legitimate judgment call or a dereliction of mandate.

§9.9 — The Vote
Senate: 59-41. Short of 2/3. Monitor General reinstated.

The Senate vote fell 59-41. Fifty-nine senators found the factual case sufficient; forty-one did not. But 2/3 of the full seated Senate was 60. One vote short. Nair was reinstated immediately. Her acting deputy stepped aside. The removal proceedings were closed.

The §9.9 grounds confirmation had been correct in its limited sense: the stated grounds fit the statutory category. A systematic failure to audit, if proved, would be dereliction of constitutional mandate. The Senate had evaluated the facts and found them insufficient at the 2/3 threshold. The categorical gate opened; the factual determination closed it.

After Reinstatement

Nair returned to full duties the following Monday. The JMC met the same week. The LM and JM had, throughout the Senate proceedings, continued their own mandates without interruption — Monitor independence is constitutional and does not pause for proceedings against a colleague. What the JMC now turned to was the question of whether the removal proceedings themselves constituted obstruction of the EM's mandate.

JMC Joint Assessment · Post-Reinstatement Publication

The JMC has reviewed the circumstances of the removal proceedings initiated against EM Monitor General Nair under §9.9. The factual record examined during the Senate trial does not establish that Monitor General Nair failed to audit within her constitutional mandate; it establishes that she made a scoping judgment with which the Assembly majority disagreed. A disagreement with a Monitor's scoping judgment is not dereliction of constitutional mandate. The §9.9 Standard Track was appropriately used — the constitutional mechanism functioned as designed. No further assessment is warranted at this time. Monitor General Nair has resumed full constitutional authority. This finding is published to the NRS permanently.

The scenario tests two things simultaneously. First, the categorical gate: §9.9 ensures that removal proceedings cannot be opened on unconstitutional grounds, but it does not predetermine the factual outcome. Second, the 2/3 threshold: it protects constitutional officers from bare-majority political pressure while preserving the Legislature's genuine removal authority. Fifty-nine senators wanted Nair removed. The constitution required sixty. That arithmetic is the protection.
