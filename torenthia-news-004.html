Rehn Tells Senate River Negotiations Will Not Be Surrendered at the Table

Legat Consul offers careful warning as Caldris water talks enter critical phase; final address on foreign affairs before term closes

Petra Vend Political Correspondent · Verentum — Year 12, Month 11

Legat Consul Casimir Rehn addressed the Senate's Foreign Relations Committee on the state of the Toren River treaty renewal Monday, offering what observers described as measured language with an unmistakable edge — a combination that has become familiar in his final year.

"The Republic does not negotiate from a posture of dependency," Rehn said, in a prepared statement read into the NRS record. "Downstream by geography. Not by nature."

The address came three weeks after the Senate passed a resolution directing the LC's office not to accept any treaty terms reducing guaranteed minimum flow below a defined threshold — a resolution the Legat Consul's office acknowledged without formally endorsing. Rehn did not mention the resolution by name. He did not need to.

"Treaty negotiation is an executive function. Ratification is a legislative one. That boundary exists for a reason, and it will be honored on both sides of it."

The statement drew a brief silence in the chamber before the Committee chair moved to questions.

Two Files, One Problem

The current negotiations have been complicated by Caldris's position as an economic stakeholder in Sunderland — a fact the EM noted in a fiscal risk assessment published last month and the Legat Consul referenced obliquely when asked whether the water talks and the eastern situation were being handled as separate files.

"Nothing on our northern border exists in isolation from what is happening on our eastern one," Rehn said. "Anyone conducting these negotiations as if that were otherwise would not be conducting them for long."

The treaty's renewal deadline falls in Year 13. Rehn's term ends before it. He has said publicly that he does not intend to seek the appointment renewal that would allow him to serve beyond Year 12's close. The question of who next holds the office — and what posture they bring to a table Rehn is preparing — is one that every declared LC candidate is now being asked to answer.

The Line

Rehn declined to name the flow threshold the Republic considers a red line, saying that doing so publicly would amount to conducting the negotiation in the press. Members of the Committee who pushed on the question received, in different phrasings, the same answer: the Republic knows its position, the LC's office knows its position, and Caldris knows the Republic's position.

"Caldris will know our position," Rehn said, at the close of his statement. "They have always known our position. The question is whether that knowledge, this time, produces a different result."

The Committee recessed without a vote. The NRS record of the address is public.

In eleven months, whoever replaces Rehn will inherit the river file. They will also inherit whatever Caldris decides to do with the knowledge he described. In the chamber on Monday, that fact was present in the room without being spoken — a quality that has marked a number of Rehn's final-year appearances. He is, at this point, very good at endings.

Petra Vend is The Torenthian's political correspondent, based in Verentum. She covers the Legat Consul's office, the Senate, and national electoral politics.
