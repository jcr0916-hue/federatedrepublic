The Laboratory of Democracy

One Floor.
Many Governments.

The Federated Republic's constitution sets a floor — rights, healthcare, housing, education, accountability — and then steps back. States decide everything above it. The result is something no existing federal system quite achieves: genuine structural diversity among states, none of it at the expense of citizens.

The Design Choice

Why Structural Diversity is a Feature

Brandeis's "laboratory of democracy" has always had a dark side in existing federal systems — states experimenting downward, using autonomy to deny rights, suppress wages, exclude people. The Republic's constitutional floor changes this. Healthcare, housing, food security, and education are guaranteed regardless of which party controls the state legislature. A hostile Assembly cannot eliminate the BNF. It cannot gerrymander its own districts. It cannot accept corporate money. What states can do is decide everything above that floor: how to organize the executive, whether to recruit immigrants actively or passively, how far to push the BNF above 5%, how strong to make local government. Those are genuine choices with genuine consequences — and citizens are protected from the worst-case outcomes of each experiment.

The Founding Moment

Every Territory Chose Its Government

Every state that formed from a territory had a constitutional convention — a moment when real people sat down with a blank page above the constitutional floor and decided what kind of government they wanted to be. That founding act is constitutionally significant: the choices made — unicameral or bicameral, parliamentary or presidential, appointed or elected judiciary, strong local government or centralized state — are not inherited from a predecessor system. They are deliberate. They belong to the community that made them. Those choices create path dependencies that shape political culture for generations, and they carry a weight that purely inherited structures do not — because the people who made them are still alive, and their reasoning is on the permanent public record.

What the NRS Changes

No State Has a Memory Hole

Every state government action required to be published is permanent, unalterable, and publicly accessible from the moment it is recorded on the National Record System. State legislators' votes, state contracts, civil servant refusal reports, regulatory decisions — all of it is there, forever, searchable by anyone. The effect on political culture across all states — regardless of their other differences — is consistent: there is no comfortable private record. State governments in the Republic are not more honest than governments elsewhere because their officials are better people. They are more accountable because their records cannot disappear.

§13.7 — State Structural Autonomy
"Every State of the Republic retains the right to organize its internal government as it sees fit, provided it meets the minimum democratic legitimacy conditions of §13.2.e. This Constitution prescribes no required structure for State governments... The diversity of State governance structures is a constitutional feature, not a constitutional problem."
