Paper No. 14

The Writ

On the six permanently unamendable provisions and the rights no emergency can suspend. What it means constitutionally for something to be truly beyond revision — and why those six, not others.

~2,600 words

The strongest objection to placing any provision beyond democratic revision is democratic: who are the framers of a constitution to bind future majorities in perpetuity? The answer this constitution gives is not comfortable. It is: some things that majorities might choose to do would end the constitutional order rather than revise it. Those things are placed beyond revision not despite democracy but as its precondition.

This constitution contains two categories of absolute protection. The first is the emergency floor: certain rights cannot be suspended even when the state has declared a genuine emergency. The second is the unamendable floor: six provisions cannot be changed by any constitutional amendment process, regardless of the size of the majority that wants to change them. Both floors exist, and understanding why requires understanding what each is designed to prevent.

The Emergency Floor

⁕

Emergency powers are constitutional in every stable democratic system because genuine emergencies — natural disasters, armed conflicts, pandemics — sometimes require the government to act faster and with more authority than ordinary constitutional procedures allow. The question is not whether emergency powers should exist. It is which rights the government retains the power to suspend when emergencies are declared, and which rights cannot be suspended under any circumstances.

This constitution permits emergency derogation of most Article I rights when a properly declared emergency meets the constitutional requirements: joint executive declaration, legislative confirmation within seven days, a specified scope and duration, automatic expiry without renewal, and Inspectorate continuity throughout. The emergency powers are real. They are not unlimited.

Five rights are non-derogable. They cannot be suspended even by a properly declared, legislatively confirmed, temporally limited emergency:

Non-Derogable Rights (§1.25.b) — cannot be suspended by emergency declaration
§1.2Prohibition on torture and cruel treatment — no exception for national security, military operations, or emergency.
§1.3Prohibition on slavery and forced labor — no emergency justifies compelled servitude.
§1.13Habeas corpus — every detained person has the right to challenge detention before a judge within 48 hours.
§1.17Right to a public trial — no secret proceedings, no secret verdicts.
§1.18No retroactive criminal laws — no person may be convicted for an act that was lawful when committed.

These five rights share a constitutional logic. They are the minimum conditions under which a person retains legal personhood — the ability to be a subject of law rather than merely an object of state power. A person who can be tortured for the state’s benefit, enslaved, detained without judicial review, tried in secret, and convicted retroactively for past lawful conduct has no meaningful constitutional standing. The state has reduced them to a thing that can be processed without recourse.

The non-derogable floor does not reflect a judgment that these five rights are more important than other Article I rights. It reflects a specific constitutional theory about what emergency powers are for. Emergency powers exist to allow the state to respond to external threats — threats that come from outside the constitutional order. The non-derogable rights protect against threats from within the constitutional order: from a state that has turned its emergency powers against its own citizens. Emergency powers that extend to these five rights are not emergency powers that protect the constitutional order. They are powers that the state can use to dismantle it.

The practical history that underlies this floor is not hypothetical. Democratic states have, during genuine emergencies, tortured, detained without review, tried in secret, and applied retroactive criminal law to their own citizens. In every documented case, the emergency was used as justification for conduct the government could not have authorized through ordinary means. The non-derogable floor does not assume bad faith in any particular government. It assumes that institutional incentives — the incentive to use available power — operate regardless of intent, and structures the constitution accordingly.

The Unamendable Floor

⁕

The unamendable floor goes further. Six provisions cannot be changed by any constitutional amendment, regardless of the path, regardless of the majority. Path A requires three-quarters of both chambers. Path B requires two-thirds of both chambers plus popular ratification. Path C requires seventy percent affirmative with sixty-five percent participation. None of these paths can amend the six unamendable provisions. A unanimous vote of every legislator and every citizen cannot change them.

Permanently Unamendable Provisions (§15.3.a) — beyond revision by any process
§1.2Prohibition on torture and cruel treatment.
§1.3Prohibition on slavery and forced labor.
§1.13Habeas corpus.
§1.17Right to a public trial.
§1.18No retroactive criminal laws.
§1.27Non-refoulement — the Republic cannot return any person to persecution, torture, or deprivation of life.

The unamendable list largely matches the non-derogable list, with the addition of non-refoulement (§1.27). Non-refoulement extends the constitution’s prohibition on state-imposed physical violation of persons beyond the Republic’s citizens to anyone who would be returned to such violation by the Republic’s action. Its inclusion reflects the drafters’ conclusion that the logic underlying the other five provisions — that the state may not physically violate persons as instruments of its purposes — does not stop at the border.

Also unamendable: the directionality of Article I as a whole. No amendment may reduce any existing Article I right below its current floor. Rights may be expanded; they cannot be contracted. This provision means that even the non-unamendable Article I rights — freedom of expression, freedom of religion, freedom of association, equality before the law — cannot be diminished by constitutional amendment. They can only be added to.

The Democratic Objection

⁕

The strongest challenge to unamendable provisions is democratic in character. In a constitutional republic, ultimate sovereignty rests with the People. The People, through their representatives or directly, have the authority to change the rules under which they are governed. To place provisions beyond democratic revision is to assert that the framers of a constitution know better than any future generation of citizens what those citizens should be permitted to choose. That assertion is difficult to defend in principle.

This objection deserves to be taken seriously rather than dismissed, because it identifies a genuine tension. Constitutional designers who place too many things beyond revision make the constitution brittle — it cannot adapt to circumstances the framers did not anticipate. Constitutional designers who place nothing beyond revision make the constitution contingent — it survives only as long as each successive majority finds it convenient. Both failures are real, and the challenge is finding the line.

This constitution’s answer to the democratic objection is not that the framers know better than future generations. It is that certain provisions are not policy choices that future generations should be free to revise. They are the conditions under which constitutional governance exists at all.

Consider what it would mean to amend the torture prohibition. A constitutional amendment to permit torture as an instrument of state interrogation would not be a policy revision — it would be a categorical change in what kind of polity the Republic is. A state that can legally torture is not a constitutional republic in any meaningful sense. It is a state that has decided its purposes justify treating persons as instruments. The constitutional document that permitted this revision would have created something that cannot legitimately describe itself as a constitution in the tradition this document belongs to.

The same analysis applies to each of the six. Habeas corpus is not a procedural preference. It is the mechanism by which the state is required to justify detention to an independent judiciary. A state that can detain without this requirement has removed the most basic constraint on arbitrary state power over persons. Retroactive criminal law is not a technicality. It is the prohibition on the state deciding, after the fact, that conduct it previously permitted was actually criminal — a power that enables unlimited persecution of any group by retrospective criminalization of whatever they were doing. Slavery is not an economic policy. It is the reduction of persons to property.

The unamendable floor does not restrict future generations from revising policy. It restricts future majorities from using constitutional amendment to end the constitutional order. That distinction is the answer to the democratic objection.

Why These Six and Not Others

⁕

The obvious question the unamendable floor raises is the one this essay has been building toward: why these six, and not others? Freedom of expression, for example, is a fundamental right that democratic theorists consider essential to the functioning of democratic government. Why is the expression right not unamendable? Why is equality before the law not unamendable?

The answer requires distinguishing between two types of fundamental rights. The first type is rights that define the minimum conditions for personhood — the ability of a person to exist as a subject of law rather than an object of state power. The six unamendable rights all belong to this category. Torture, slavery, detention without judicial review, secret trial, retroactive criminal law, and forced return to persecution: these are the mechanisms by which states eliminate a person’s legal standing entirely, treating them as something to be processed rather than someone with rights.

The second type is rights that define the conditions for free and equal participation in democratic governance. Freedom of expression, freedom of association, freedom of religion, equality before the law: these rights are fundamental to what it means to live in a free society, but they are rights that reasonable people have disagreed about in detail and that different constitutional systems implement differently. Freedom of expression does not mean the same thing in every democratic constitution. Equality before the law is interpreted differently by different courts in different contexts. These rights are protected in this constitution — strongly, with Article I’s one-directional floor — but they are not placed beyond revision because reasonable revision of their scope does not end the constitutional order. It disputes how the constitutional order is implemented.

The six unamendable provisions cannot be reasonably revised in scope without ending the constitutional order. There is no reasonable version of “some torture is permitted” that is compatible with a constitution that treats persons as subjects rather than objects. There is no reasonable version of “some slavery is permitted”. There is no reasonable version of “detention without judicial review is sometimes acceptable” that does not effectively remove the constraint on arbitrary state power over persons.

The line is sharp because the distinction is sharp. Rights that define the minimum conditions for personhood are placed beyond revision because their removal is not a revision of the constitutional order — it is the abolition of it. Rights that define the conditions for free participation in democratic governance are protected by the strongest available guarantees short of unamendability — because reasonable people can and do disagree about their implementation in ways that do not threaten the constitutional order itself.

The Writ and What It Guarantees

⁕

The habeas corpus writ — the legal instrument that requires a state to bring a detained person before a judge and justify the detention — is historically the oldest written guarantee in this tradition. Its inclusion among the unamendable provisions is not accidental. Habeas corpus is the procedural form of the substantive guarantees: the mechanism by which a person can force the state to account for what it is doing to them. Without it, the other protections are aspirational. With it, they are enforceable.

The writ represents the constitutional minimum: the thing a person can demand when the state has them in its custody. You have the right to appear before a judge. You have the right to know the reason for your detention. You have the right to challenge that reason. No emergency can take that from you. No amendment can take that from you. No majority, however large, can vote it away.

This is the constitutional logic of the unamendable floor in miniature. It does not bind future generations because the framers know better. It binds them because without these six provisions, there is nothing here worth binding.
