Presidential Runoff Campaign · Companion to The 0.3

Twenty-One Days

A conduct complaint on Day 3. A foreign interference finding on Day 11. An EC ruling on Day 15. The constitutional machinery of a presidential runoff under pressure.

§7.2.f — The Runoff §18.2.a — Conduct Standards §18.2.b — Media Standards §18.2.c — Foreign Interference §18.2.d — Incumbent Advantage §10.3.a — Public Financing §16.3 — NVS Properties §16.4 — Voting Rights

Setting: Year 12 — 21 days following the §7.2.f runoff trigger  ·  Characters: Senator Webb, Governor Holt, EC Chair Nwofor  ·  See also: The 0.3 (election night and SC challenge)

Background

The Elections Panel of the Digital Infrastructure Commission's finding had been on the NRS for six hours when Commissioner Nwofor called the staff meeting. A 0.29-point combined score margin. A mandatory runoff under §7.2.f. Twenty-one days to administer a head-to-head national election between two sitting officeholders, both of whom would spend the campaign period exercising the constitutional powers of their current offices while also running for a different one.

The conduct rules had been written for normal campaigns. A normal campaign was six months, at minimum. The EC would now be applying them in three weeks, with no recourse to the appeal timelines the conduct framework assumed. Everything would have to move faster than the provisions were designed to move — without anyone being able to say the provisions didn't apply.

Constitutional Context · §7.2.f

The runoff is a head-to-head national RCV contest. No regional score. No third candidate. Both the Article XVIII electoral finance rules and the §18.2 campaign conduct standards apply in full. The EC administers the runoff on the same NVS infrastructure as any federal election, with the same public auditability requirements under §16.3. The 21-day clock runs from the day of the SC ruling that confirmed the runoff.

Day 3 · The Advertisement

The Webb campaign released a thirty-second digital advertisement at 6:14 AM on Day 3, timed to appear before the morning news cycle. The advertisement showed archived footage of Governor Holt at a state legislature podium, voice-over stating: "Dana Holt voted against expanding social state healthcare coverage for 340,000 Ohioans. Now she wants to administer the national social state system."

The claim was technically accurate. In Year 8, the Ohio state legislature had debated two competing social state expansion bills. Holt had voted against the first version — a version her own party later withdrew and replaced with a substantially different bill that Holt had championed and voted for. The final version passed and expanded coverage for 340,000 Ohioans. The advertisement used footage from the vote against the first bill.

The Holt campaign filed a conduct complaint with the EC at 8:47 AM the same morning. §18.2.a

The Legal Question · §18.2.a

The §18.2.a standard is narrow by design: prohibition on "demonstrably false statements of material fact about a candidate's recorded votes — specifically, claiming a vote was cast when it was not, or was not cast when it was." The provision explicitly protects characterization, emphasis, and context as political expression. The vote did happen. Holt did vote against the first bill. The conduct standard does not ask whether that vote was fairly contextualized — it asks only whether the vote was accurately described as having occurred.

The EC convened an emergency panel of three commissioners. Under the compressed runoff timeline, the EC's standard 30-day review process was constitutionally impossible — it would have concluded after the election. The EC published a procedural notice to the NRS acknowledging the complaint and committing to a ruling within 72 hours. §16.5.a

Day 5 · The EC Ruling

The EC panel ruled at 11:22 AM on Day 5 and published the full reasoning to the NRS within the hour.

Elections Panel of the Digital Infrastructure Commission · Conduct Ruling · Day 5

The complaint alleges that the Webb campaign's advertisement violates §18.2.a by misrepresenting Governor Holt's voting record. The Panel has reviewed the advertisement, the archived legislative record, and the campaign's response.

The constitutional standard covers demonstrably false statements of material fact about recorded votes. The recorded vote is not in dispute: Governor Holt cast a vote against a social state expansion bill in Year 8. That vote is accurately described as having occurred. The advertisement does not claim a vote was cast when it was not, or was not cast when it was.

Whether the advertisement fairly represents the context of that vote — including the subsequent bill that achieved the same expansion with the candidate's support — is a question of political characterization. The constitution protects political characterization as expression. The EC does not adjudicate the fairness of campaign arguments; it adjudicates the factual accuracy of vote records. The complaint is not sustained.

The Webb campaign's advertisement may continue. This ruling and the complaint record are published to the NRS permanently. §18.2.a

The Holt campaign issued a statement calling the ruling "technically correct and politically shameless." The Webb campaign issued a statement calling it "a vindication of honest campaigning." Commissioner Nwofor issued no statement — the ruling was the statement, and it was already on the NRS.

Day 8 · The Incumbent Problem

On Day 8, Senator Webb held a scheduled Senate Armed Services Committee hearing on military readiness. The hearing was a regular committee function — scheduled months before the election, on the public calendar, fully within his constitutional role. It was also, unavoidably, a high-profile platform that put his face on every major news broadcast the same day his runoff campaign released new advertising in rural swing regions.

The Holt campaign filed a second complaint, alleging that Webb had used his official Senate committee role for campaign purposes in violation of §18.2.d.

The EC panel reviewed the hearing record within 48 hours. Finding: no violation. The hearing had been scheduled before the runoff trigger. Webb had conducted the hearing on its stated subject without any campaign references. The committee staff who prepared the materials had no involvement with the Webb campaign. The fact that a sitting officeholder's official functions generated media coverage during a campaign period did not constitute incumbent advantage — it constituted the existence of an incumbent.

Design Note · §18.2.d

The incumbent advantage provision prohibits using "official resources — staff, communications infrastructure, government platforms" for campaign purposes. It does not prohibit incumbents from performing their constitutional functions during a campaign. The provision's target is deliberate exploitation of official resources, not the unavoidable visibility of officeholders doing their jobs. The EC's ruling made this distinction explicit and published it to the NRS — a precedent for future runoff administrations.

Day 11 · Foreign Interference

The JI's digital monitoring system flagged the pattern on the evening of Day 11. A coordinated network of social media accounts — later traced to a foreign state-adjacent proxy entity — had been amplifying Webb campaign messaging in three regional language communities, targeting voters in areas with significant diaspora populations. The accounts were not posting original content. They were systematically resharing Webb campaign advertisements with engagement patterns consistent with coordinated inauthentic behavior.

The JI published its monitoring finding to the NRS at 11:47 PM on Day 11. §18.2.c

By constitutional obligation, the EI opened an investigation within 12 hours. The Webb campaign issued a statement at 6:30 AM on Day 12: they had not solicited, coordinated, or accepted any foreign assistance; they had not been contacted by the entity identified by the JI; they were cooperating fully with the EI investigation. The statement was published to the NRS campaign record by 7:00 AM.

Constitutional Mechanism · §18.2.c

Foreign interference is a constitutional violation committed by the foreign actor, not automatically by the candidate whose campaign benefits. §18.2.c specifies that candidates who knowingly accept foreign assistance are disqualified from the election cycle. Unknowing benefit — a foreign actor amplifying publicly available campaign material without candidate contact or coordination — does not trigger disqualification. The EI investigation's purpose was to determine whether knowing acceptance had occurred. The JI's role was complete at publication of the monitoring finding; enforcement was the EI's mandate.

Day 15 · The EI Finding

The EI published its investigation finding on Day 15, four days before the runoff. The finding was comprehensive: no evidence of campaign coordination with the foreign entity, no evidence of knowing acceptance of foreign assistance, no evidence of contact between the Webb campaign and the identified proxy. The foreign interference was documented, real, and conducted without candidate involvement.

The EI finding also made a second, separate determination: the foreign entity's conduct constituted a §18.2.c violation and would be referred to the Republic's foreign affairs office for diplomatic response. That determination was not about the candidates. It was about the foreign actor.

Executive Inspectorate · Investigation Finding · Day 15

Investigation confirms: coordinated foreign amplification of Webb campaign content occurred between Days 4 and 11, originating from a proxy entity linked to a foreign government. No evidence of candidate knowledge, coordination, or acceptance of assistance. Webb campaign is cleared of §18.2.c violation. Foreign actor's conduct is separately referred.

The NRS record of this investigation — including all evidence, methodology, and findings — is published in full and is permanent. No subsequent action by either campaign or government can alter or remove this record. §18.2.c · §9.1.b

The EI finding landed four days before the election. The Webb campaign had been cleared. The foreign interference had been documented. Both facts were on the NRS permanently, side by side. Voters could read both. What they concluded from both was up to them.

Day 18 · The NVS and the Last Push

Early voting under the NVS had been open since Day 2. By Day 18, 38.4% of eligible voters had cast runoff ballots. The EC published a daily NVS integrity report confirming ballot secrecy, system auditability, and zero technical exceptions. §16.3 · §16.4

Both campaigns spent Days 17 through 20 on the ground. Webb's organizers focused on turnout in the five regions he'd won in the initial election. Holt's focused on voter registration and CVC verification in areas where credential renewal had lagged — the EC had flagged three counties on Day 14 where CVC renewal infrastructure was running below normal capacity, and the EC had dispatched mobile verification units to each. Every eligible voter who wanted a ballot could get one. That was constitutionally non-negotiable.

Neither campaign spent a dollar outside their public financing allocation in the runoff period. The §18.3.a baseline funding applied to both equally. Neither had an advantage from the spending cap. For twenty-one days, they were constitutionally equal in resources.

Day 21 · What the Machinery Did

The runoff closed at 8:00 PM. The NVS published certified counts in real time. By midnight, 81.3% of eligible voters had participated — higher than the initial election. Commissioner Nwofor certified the result at 4:47 AM on Day 22.

Looking back across the twenty-one days: two conduct complaints filed, two ruled upon within 72 hours. One foreign interference event detected, investigated, and publicly resolved before Election Day. Three counties where CVC access had lagged, remediated before the vote. One clear candidate disqualification threshold — knowing foreign assistance — never crossed. One NRS record that captured everything that happened, in sequence, permanently.

The conduct provisions had been written for six-month campaigns. The EC had applied them in three weeks. Every ruling had been faster than the normal process allowed — and every ruling had followed the normal standard. The compressed timeline hadn't changed what the constitution required. It had only changed how quickly the institutions were required to deliver it.

Commissioner Adaeze Nwofor · Post-Election Report to the JI

The runoff administration identified three structural gaps in the conduct framework that assume normal campaign timelines: the 30-day standard review for conduct complaints, the 90-day post-election finance audit, and the CVC renewal cycle. All three were managed by expedited procedure. None required deviation from the constitutional standard — only from the statutory timeline. The Legislature should address all three in statute before the next election cycle that could produce a runoff.

The NRS record is complete. Every complaint, ruling, finding, and certification is published. The process was visible throughout. That visibility was not incidental. It was the mechanism. §9.1 · §16.3

What This Scenario Tests

§18.2.a — Conduct Standards

The vote-record accuracy standard is narrow by design: it covers false claims about whether a vote occurred, not unfair characterization of what a vote meant. The EC's Day 5 ruling illustrates exactly where this line falls — and why drawing it narrowly protects political expression while still prohibiting fabrication.

§18.2.c — Foreign Interference

Foreign interference can occur without candidate involvement. The constitutional violation is committed by the foreign actor; disqualification requires knowing acceptance by the candidate. The EI's investigation and NRS publication resolved the ambiguity before Election Day — exactly what the transparency infrastructure was designed to enable.

§18.2.d — Incumbent Advantage

Officeholders cannot be expected to stop performing their constitutional functions during a campaign. §18.2.d targets deliberate use of official resources for campaign purposes, not the visibility that comes with holding office. The distinction matters: the provision is a guard against abuse, not an impossible standard of campaign-period invisibility.

§16.3 · §16.4 — NVS and Voting Rights

The NVS operates continuously from Day 2. CVC access is a constitutional obligation — when the EC identifies access failures, remediation is not discretionary. The three-county mobile unit deployment was not exceptional; it was what the constitution required.

§10.3.a — Equal Public Financing

Both candidates operate under the same public financing cap. The runoff doesn't reset spending allocations but does create a compressed period where equal resources matter more, not less — there's less time to overcome a financial disadvantage through organizing. Equal baseline funding during the runoff is constitutionally load-bearing.

Statutory Gap Identified

The conduct framework assumes 30-day review timelines that a 21-day runoff makes impossible. The EC managed by expedited procedure without deviating from constitutional standards — but the gap between the constitutional standard and the statutory timeline is real. Commissioner Nwofor's report to the JI flags it for legislative action. The constitution identified the problem by requiring the runoff; the Legislature is responsible for closing the gap.
