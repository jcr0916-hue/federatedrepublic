Emergency Governance · Consular Incapacity · §2.6 · Acting Consular Authority

Twenty-One Days

Day 1. The Legat Consul is incapacitated following emergency surgery. The EM and LM certify jointly. The Senate Speaker assumes Acting Consular Authority. Twenty-one days later, the Legat Consul returns.

§2.6 Consular Incapacity§2.6.a Consular Succession§2.8 Emergency Governance Protocol§3.5 Senate Speaker

The Legat Consul's emergency surgery on Day 1 was not planned. The circumstances were both medically severe and constitutionally triggering: she was unconscious, under general anaesthesia, and — in the assessment of her attending physicians — unable to exercise Legat Consul's authority for a period of unknown duration. The Chief of Staff notified both the EM and LM simultaneously within 30 minutes of the surgery beginning.

The constitutional clock under §2.6 began at the moment the EM and LM received notification. Their joint assessment of Consular incapacity was required within 6 hours. The two Monitors worked in parallel — the EM assessing whether the medical situation constituted an inability to exercise Legat Consul's authority, the LM confirming the constitutional preconditions for an Acting Consular Authority transfer.

The Joint Certification — §2.6

§2.6 — Joint Certification
If both Monitors certify incapacity, they publish a joint assessment to the NRS. Acting Consular Authority transfers immediately to the Senate Speaker.

At Hour 4, the EM and LM published a joint assessment to the NRS. The assessment was brief and procedural: the Legat Consul was medically incapacitated, unable to exercise Legat Consul's authority, and the constitutional conditions for an Acting Consular Authority transfer had been met. The joint assessment appeared on the NRS at 14:23. Senate Speaker Amara Diallo assumed Acting Consular Authority at 14:23.

The transfer was automatic — not a decision by any political actor, not a vote, not a negotiation. The joint assessment published; the authority transferred. Diallo's Senate seat was not vacated; she held both roles simultaneously for the duration of the acting period. No special Legat Consul election was triggered. The acting period did not constitute a succession event under §2.6.a.

The practical implications of the authority transfer materialised within hours. Three pending Consular actions — a foreign policy communication requiring Consular signature, a treaty notification deadline, and a military deployment authorisation that had been under review — were now Diallo's responsibility. She consulted the Legat Consul's domain officers, who briefed her on the status of each. The foreign policy communication was signed within 24 hours. The treaty notification met its deadline. The military deployment authorisation was deferred — Diallo declined to approve a significant military action without the Legat Consul's direct involvement.

The Acting Period — Days 1 Through 21

The surgery's complexity meant the Legat Consul's recovery required more time than initially projected. On Day 8, her physicians issued an updated prognosis: she would be capable of reviewing written materials by Day 14 and capable of resuming full Legat Consul's authority by approximately Day 21. Diallo continued to exercise Acting Consular Authority throughout, coordinating with the Legat Consul's domain officers on routine matters and holding significant decisions — particularly military and foreign policy — for the Legat Consul's return where constitutionally possible.

§2.6 — The 90-Day Reassessment Threshold
Where Acting Consular Authority extends beyond 90 days without restoration, the Monitors must reassess. On joint certification of permanent incapacity, §2.6.a succession activates.

The 21-day recovery put the acting period well within the 90-day threshold. Had the recovery extended beyond 90 days, the EM and LM would have been required to publish fresh findings within 7 days of the threshold — assessing whether the incapacity had become permanent. On a finding of permanent incapacity, §2.6.a succession would activate: the acting period would convert to full succession, Diallo's Senate seat would be vacated, and the Deputy Speaker would assume the Speaker role.

That threshold was not reached. On Day 21, the Legat Consul published a restoration declaration to the NRS. The declaration was the Legat Consul's own statement that she was resuming full Legat Consul's authority. Under §2.6, this ends Acting Consular Authority immediately. At the moment of NRS publication, Diallo's acting authority lapsed and the Legat Consul resumed full authority.

What This Scenario Tests

§2.6's design is tested in its simplest form: incapacity, transfer, recovery, restoration. The 6-hour certification window ensures the constitutional vacuum is brief. The automatic transfer on joint certification ensures no political actor can delay or contest the moment of transfer. The restoration declaration mechanism gives the Legat Consul herself the primary role in returning to authority — she declares, the NRS publishes, the authority returns.

The Senate Speaker's simultaneous role retention during the acting period reflects the design choice that Acting Consular Authority is temporary and does not require the Speaker to permanently leave the Senate. A Speaker who resigned their Senate seat every time they assumed acting authority would face a structural disincentive to serve in a role the constitution needs to be reliably filled. Path A applies: the Speaker holds both roles for the duration, then returns to the Senate role alone when the Legat Consul is restored.

Diallo's decision to defer the military deployment authorisation demonstrates the constitutional design working at the political level. She had full Acting Consular Authority over military matters under §2.1.a. She chose not to exercise it for a significant new deployment, preferring to hold it for the Legat Consul. The constitution did not require that deference — it permitted it. The distinction between what authority Acting Consular Authority grants and what a person in that role should actually exercise is a political judgment the constitution deliberately leaves to the individual.
