Legislative Monitor · Critical Failure · §9.6 · §9.14

The Critical Finding

Year 11. The LM's annual legislative audit publishes a Critical Failure finding on fiscal accuracy — the Legislature's revenue projections have systematically overstated collections across a majority of budget cycles. The remediation plan requirement activates. The Legislature resists.

§9.6 Monitor Operations and Reporting §9.1.a Legislative Monitor §9.11.a Institutional Compromise Protocol §10.1 National Record System

The LM's Year 11 annual legislative audit found what earlier audits had flagged as Deficiencies: the Legislature's revenue projections had systematically overstated collections in seven of the prior nine budget cycles, by an average of 6.2%. In Year 11, the pattern crossed the threshold for Critical Failure classification — a majority of cycles, sustained, with no remediation plan from any prior Deficiency finding.

The Critical Failure finding was published to the NRS on a Wednesday. Under §9.6, the Legislature was required to publish a remediation plan with actions and timelines within the period defined by statute — 90 days. Failure to publish a remediation plan was itself a constitutional compliance breach, certified by the LM.

§9.6 — The Remediation Requirement
The Legislature cannot ignore a Critical Failure finding.

The Assembly leadership initially argued the LM's methodology was flawed — that revenue projections inherently involve uncertainty and that the Critical Failure threshold was set too low. The argument was constitutionally unavailable. §9.6 does not permit the Legislature to dispute the finding's methodology; it requires a remediation plan. The LM's findings are not subject to legislative approval or revision. They are published permanently to the NRS regardless of legislative response.

On Day 87, the Assembly published a remediation plan. The plan committed to: independent revenue forecasting methodology review within 60 days; MA co-certification of revenue projections for the next three budget cycles; and LM verification of each cycle before tabling. The LM published its assessment of the plan's adequacy. The NRS held both.

When the Auditor Fails — §9.11.a

This scenario demonstrates the LM functioning correctly. Three Monitors operating independently, publishing findings continuously, creating a permanent NRS record of government performance. The Critical Failure classification activated a mandatory legislative response — the NRS record of both the finding and the remediation plan is permanent.

§9.11.a exists for the harder version: what if the LM itself is the institution operating in bad faith? Where two of the three Monitors jointly certify that the third is operating in demonstrable bad faith, they petition the SC for confirmation and immediately designate an Acting Monitor General from the compromised Monitor's candidate pool. The Monitor cannot audit itself out of capture. The other two can.

What This Scenario Tests

The annual audit cycle is more responsive than a scheduled periodic review. The LM's Year 11 Critical Failure finding was possible because the Monitor had been tracking the pattern continuously — not waiting for a scheduled moment to look. The NRS record of prior Deficiency findings gave the Critical Failure classification its context. The remediation requirement has teeth because the LM can certify non-compliance as a constitutional breach. The Legislature cannot ignore the finding; it can only respond to it.
