Executive Accountability · §2.1.i · Presidential Officer Removal Denied

The Notice

The President issues a Notice of Intended Removal for the Secretary for Defense. The Senate has thirty days to approve, deny, or do nothing. On day twenty-two, the Senate votes to deny. The Secretary stays. The President cannot resubmit for twelve months. What an executive officer does when she cannot be fired — and what the President does when he cannot fire her.

§2.1.i Presidential Officer Appointments and Removal §2.1.b Tier 2 Armed Services Authorization §3.5 Impeachment Scope §9.1 NRS Publication

President Cormac Hale is in Year 4 of his six-year term. His Secretary for Defense, Petra Okafor, has served since Year 1 — nominated by the President, confirmed by the Senate 61-39. She is one of the most effective and publicly respected officers in the administration, a career defense administrator with thirty years' institutional knowledge and a reputation for speaking plainly to both the Senate and the press.

That reputation is the problem. Secretary Okafor has testified before the Senate Armed Services Committee six times in Year 3 and four times already in Year 4. In every hearing, she has described the Republic's defense posture in terms the President's office characterizes as inconsistent with his policy positions. She has not contradicted any classified operational order. She has not disclosed protected information. She has stated, clearly and repeatedly, that the Republic's current military deployment strategy carries risks she would not recommend accepting if the decision were hers alone. She believes the President's posture exposes the Republic's northern commitments under the defense treaty framework to calculated test.

President Hale disagrees with her assessment. He has also concluded that her public disagreement — plainly stated, NRS-documented, and widely reported — has become untenable for the coherent conduct of his defense policy. He wants her gone.

The Notice

Under §2.1.i, the President may remove any Senate-confirmed Presidential Officer by submitting a written Notice of Intended Removal to the Senate, published simultaneously to the National Record System. The Senate has thirty days to respond. If the Senate approves by simple majority, removal takes effect immediately. If the Senate does not vote within thirty days, removal is deemed approved on Day 31. If the Senate votes to deny by simple majority within thirty days, the officer remains and the President may not resubmit a removal notice for that same officer for twelve months.

The Notice is published on a Monday morning. It states three grounds: policy misalignment, loss of confidence, and impairment of the executive's ability to conduct unified defense policy. The Notice is forty-one words of operative text. It is the first Notice of Intended Removal to be issued for a Secretary for Defense since the constitution came into force.

§2.1.i — Thirty-Day Clock
What each outcome produces

Senate approves (or does not vote by Day 31): Removal takes effect. The President nominates a replacement. The Senate confirms. Okafor is out.

Senate denies by Day 30: Okafor remains in post. The President cannot resubmit a Notice for Okafor for twelve months. For the remainder of that twelve months, the President must work with a Secretary for Defense he has publicly notified his intention to remove — and whom the Senate has publicly protected from that removal.

Days 1–22

The Senate Armed Services Committee convenes within forty-eight hours. Secretary Okafor testifies for three hours. She is asked directly whether she can continue to perform her duties effectively given the President's Notice. She says she can and will, that her constitutional obligation is to the Senate-confirmed role and the Republic's defense posture, not to any particular policy alignment with the President. She does not resign.

The committee's deliberations are brief. Its recommendation to the full Senate — deny the removal — passes 9-2 and is published to the NRS on Day 11.

The full Senate debate takes nine days. The arguments against denial are principled: the President is the domain head of the defense executive; the §2.1.i framework includes Senate participation precisely to ensure removals are not arbitrary, but the President's authority to form his own team is real and the Senate should not become a personnel board for Presidential Officers. The arguments for denial are also principled: Secretary Okafor has done nothing unconstitutional; her testimony to this chamber has been accurate, on the record, and has never been contradicted by any classified briefing; the Notice's stated grounds amount to removal for honesty. The Senate has a legitimate interest in protecting honest testimony from executive retaliation.

Senate Vote — Day 22
Full seated Senate membership: 100
Denial threshold: simple majority of full seated membership = 51
──────────────────────────────────
For denial (Okafor stays): 54
For approval (Okafor removed): 38
Abstentions: 8
Denial — Secretary Okafor remains in post. Twelve-month bar activates.

The Twelve Months

Secretary Okafor returns to the Pentagon the following morning. The Notice of Intended Removal is permanently on the NRS. The Senate's denial vote is permanently on the NRS. Both documents are public. Every journalist, foreign government, defense contractor, and allied military attache knows the President wants her gone and the Senate prevented it.

The twelve months that follow are constitutionally unprecedented. The President cannot remove her. She cannot be directed to resign — she declines. She continues attending Cabinet sessions. She continues testifying to the Senate when called. She continues issuing the Secretary's mandatory quarterly report on force readiness, each one published to the NRS, each one representing her assessment rather than the President's preferred framing.

Month 2 Secretary Okafor testifies on the treaty framework. Her assessment of northern deployment risk is unchanged. The President's office releases a simultaneous statement contradicting her characterization. Both documents are published to the NRS. Foreign defense attaches, it is later reported, begin routing inquiries to Okafor's office rather than the President's national security staff.
Month 5 A Tier 2 authorization request is submitted by the President to the Senate Armed Services Committee under §2.1.b. Committee staff contact Okafor's office for the required Secretary certification. She certifies — the operation is within Tier 2 scope. The operation proceeds. The President and Okafor do not speak directly for eleven weeks. All communication passes through staff.
Month 8 President Hale considers opening §3.5 impeachment proceedings against Okafor. Legal counsel advises against: §3.5 applies to Presidential Officers and would require Articles of Impeachment specifying actual constitutional violations. Policy disagreement is not a §3.5 ground. An impeachment attempt on these facts would almost certainly fail in the House and would extend Okafor's constitutional protection throughout the proceedings. The President does not proceed.
Month 12 The twelve-month bar expires. The President issues a second Notice of Intended Removal for Secretary Okafor. The grounds are identical to the first Notice. The Senate Armed Services Committee convenes. The political dynamics have shifted. Three senators who voted for denial in Year 4 are no longer in office after the intervening National Election Day. The Committee recommends no action within the thirty-day window.
Day 31 Deemed Approved. The Senate does not vote within thirty days. On Day 31, removal is deemed approved by the constitutional default. Secretary Okafor's removal takes effect. She has served four years and thirty-one days. The President nominates a replacement that afternoon.
What This Scenario Tests

§2.1.i is not a veto over Presidential Officer selection. The President nominates; the Senate confirms. The Senate's role in removal is narrower — a thirty-day window, a simple majority required to deny, a twelve-month bar as the consequence of denial. The design does not prevent removal; it forces deliberation and attaches a cost to Senate interference with Presidential staffing decisions.

The cost in this scenario runs in both directions. The President pays twelve months of institutional friction — an officer he cannot remove who continues to testify, publish, and operate in ways he disagrees with. The Senate pays the political cost of public ownership of that friction: every Okafor action during the twelve months is a consequence of the Senate's denial vote, and every senator who voted for denial is associated with it. The twelve-month bar is both a price on the President and a clarifier for the Senate: denying removal is not a consequence-free action.

The Deemed Approved mechanism on the second Notice is the scenario's quiet conclusion. The Senate does not vote. Not because it would fail — the vote might have gone the same way again. The Senate does not vote because, in the changed political environment after National Election Day, it has decided not to spend the political capital a second denial would require. Inaction is approval. The President achieves through patience and a calendar what he could not achieve through a first Notice. The constitution's design holds: the President can remove Presidential Officers, but the Senate can make it take longer than he would like.
