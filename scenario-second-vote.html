Constitutional Amendment · §15.1.c · Path C Participation Floor

The 77 Percent

A Path C citizen amendment achieves 77.4% affirmative support and 62.1% participation. The 65% floor is not met. The amendment fails. No second vote is available — a failed Path C vote is final. The coalition files a new petition the following morning, attaching the NRS record of 77% as evidence of what the People, when they show up, want.

§15.1.c Path C — Citizen-Led §8.1.c JI Petition Screening §16.1 Electoral Commission §8.1.a LI Certification §9.1 NRS Immutability

The Digital Sovereignty Amendment proposes to add two sentences to §1.9: a floor on algorithmic transparency in government-administered systems, and a prohibition on government use of automated decision systems whose training data cannot be disclosed to affected parties. The petition gathered signatures over fourteen months, was screened by the JI under §8.1.c, and qualified for a national vote. The EC administers it on a Tuesday in late Year 9.

The Vote

Path C Vote — Final Result
Affirmative votes: 77.4%
Required affirmative: 70.0%
✓ Affirmative: EXCEEDED (+7.4 points)
Participation: 62.1% of eligible citizens
Required participation: 65.0%
✗ Participation: NOT MET (−2.9 points)
Amendment: FAILED

The EC certifies the result and publishes it to the NRS before midnight. The amendment has failed. §15.1.c requires both thresholds to be met. The participation floor is not a technicality — it is a substantive requirement that constitutional change represent genuine democratic engagement, not merely the mobilization of an energized fraction. 62.1% is genuine engagement. It is not sufficient engagement for constitutional change under Path C.

There is no second vote. The failed vote’s result is permanent and public.

§15.1.c — Path C · Certification
Both thresholds required; one threshold failed

The LI publishes a brief certification note. Path C requires 70% affirmative support with 65% citizen participation. The affirmative threshold was cleared by 7.4 percentage points. The participation threshold was missed by 2.9 percentage points. A vote that fails either threshold fails. No second vote is available. A new petition may be filed immediately under the same process, with this result’s NRS record available as public evidence of prior support.

The opposition issues a statement describing the result as the democratic process working correctly: the participation floor exists precisely to prevent constitutional change driven by a mobilized fraction rather than the genuine engagement of the broader electorate. 62% turned out. That is not 65%.

What the NRS Now Contains

The coalition’s amendment failed. But the NRS now contains something the coalition did not have before the vote: a permanent, verified, immutable record that 77.4% of the citizens who engaged with this question said yes. That record is public, freely accessible, and attached to the precise constitutional text the amendment proposed.

§9.1’s immutability provision means this record cannot be modified or suppressed. A future Legislature that wants to argue there is no public appetite for algorithmic transparency requirements now has to argue against a documented 77% affirmative. Future campaigns on adjacent issues can cite it. The failed vote is not a loss that disappears — it is evidence that persists.

NRS Entry — Path C Vote Certification — Year 9 Digital Sovereignty Amendment. Path C citizen-led vote. Affirmative: 77.4%. Participation: 62.1%. Affirmative threshold (70%): MET. Participation threshold (65%): NOT MET. Result: FAILED. No further vote available on this result. New petition available immediately under §15.1.c. This record is permanent and immutable.

The New Petition

The coalition files a new petition at 9:14 the following morning. They are entitled to. §15.1.c imposes no waiting period between a failed vote and a new petition. The amendment text is identical — the coalition considered revisions and decided against them. Their theory is that the problem was not the text but turnout in specific geographic areas with high affirmative rates and below-average participation. The NRS record gives them a precise map of where that problem lived.

The new petition requires the full process from the beginning: fourteen months of signature collection, JI eligibility screening, and EC administration. The coalition must reach 5% of eligible voters again. Nothing from the first petition carries over except the record and the organizing infrastructure.

The record is enough. The coalition’s second petition begins with a document that says, in effect: last time, 77% of the people who voted said yes. The question is not whether this amendment is a good idea. That question has a published answer on the NRS. The question is whether, this time, the people who already agree with it will show up.

Why the Floor Does Not Move

The coalition’s public statement after the failure includes a line that will recur in future debates about Path C: “If 77% is not enough, what is?” The answer is constitutional and precise: 70% affirmative is enough on substance. 65% participation is enough on engagement. 77% affirmative with 62% participation fails the second requirement, and the second requirement exists for a reason the coalition’s own result illustrates.

Path C bypasses the Legislature entirely — no elected body endorses this amendment before it reaches the People’s vote. That is its design and its legitimacy claim. But “the People” in a constitutional sense means something more than “the People who showed up on this particular Tuesday.” The participation floor is the constitutional expression of that distinction. Constitutional change should not be easier than the constitution requires it to be, even when the change is popular, even when the support is genuine, and even when the margin of failure is 2.9 points.

The coalition did not fail to build support. They failed to make their amendment matter enough to the people who already agreed with it. That is a different failure, and it is the one the participation floor was designed to catch. The 65% is not an obstacle to popular will. It is a definition of what popular will means for the purpose of changing the constitution.

Constitutional Position

The amendment failed. The NRS records the failure, the result, and the text. The coalition may petition immediately. The process begins again with the same requirements and the same consequences if those requirements are not met. What the constitution gives them is not a second attempt at the same ballot — it gives them the same democratic process, from the beginning, as many times as they are willing to run it.

The 77% is on the record. The 65% is still the floor.
