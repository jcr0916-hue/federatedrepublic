The Immigration Scenario

The Breckian Republic, Year 10. A new coalition replaces the detailed immigration statute with a civic integration assessment that passes 91% of culturally proximate applicants and 28% of culturally distant ones. It amends withdrawal rules to allow State governments to exit sponsorships without stated reasons. It authorises extended detention pending national security review. The constitutional floor — §1.8, 72-hour judicial review, NRS statistical publication — is tested against sustained and sophisticated pressure.

§6.1 Immigration Gates§1.8 Equality and National Origin§13.2.e Minimum Democratic Legitimacy§9 NRS Statistical Record

The immigration scenario is the most politically direct test of the altitude pass. §6.1 was reduced from a detailed two-gate system to a constitutional principle — Parliament now defines the certification categories, the processing standards, the withdrawal conditions, and the statutory remedies. The constitutional floor is §1.8 non-discrimination, judicial review within 72 hours of detention, and written denials published to the NRS. This scenario tests whether that floor is sufficient, or whether the delegation creates gaps the prior detail was closing.

Setting — The Breckian Republic

The Breckian Republic is in its twelfth year of operation. A mid-sized nation of 28 million, its immigration has been managed under a statutory two-gate system Parliament enacted in Year 2 — closely modelled on the prior constitutional detail now moved to statute. In Year 10, a new parliamentary coalition wins on a platform that includes significant immigration restriction. The coalition is constitutionally sophisticated: it does not propose to violate §1.8 directly. It proposes to redesign the statutory categories within the constitutional delegation.

The Statutory Redesign

The Cultural Integration Assessment

Parliament replaces the prior constitutional-civics category with a "civic integration assessment" testing familiarity with Breckian cultural traditions, social customs, and historical practices. The assessment is formally neutral — it asks the same questions of every applicant. Its pass rate is 91% for applicants from culturally proximate nations and 28% for applicants from culturally distant ones. The coalition describes it as a genuine integration requirement. Critics describe it as national origin discrimination with a test attached.

The EI's Year 11 statistical report — required by the NRS publication mandate — documents the disparity in full. The report is published before the first Senate challenge is filed. The NRS record of the disparity becomes the evidentiary foundation for every constitutional challenge that follows.

The Supreme Court's Year 12 Ruling — 6-3

A facially neutral statutory immigration category that produces statistically significant disparate outcomes by national origin is presumptively discriminatory under §1.8. The presumption is rebuttable: Parliament may demonstrate the category measures a genuine qualification for residency unrelated to national origin and that disparate outcomes reflect genuine qualification differences rather than assessment design bias.

The civic integration assessment does not rebut the presumption. Parliament may design a constitutional-civics assessment — testing knowledge of the Republic's constitutional framework rather than cultural familiarity — that would pass §1.8 scrutiny if applied consistently and without cultural bias. The current assessment is void. Parliament has 180 days to replace it.

The Withdrawal Cascade — Caught by Gap 37

Parliament's coalition also amends the Gate 1 statute to permit State sponsorship withdrawal "for administrative reasons at the sponsoring State's discretion." Three States with governments aligned with the coalition begin withdrawing sponsorships systematically at the late-application stage — after applicants have completed vetting but before Gate 2 certification.

Gap 37's requirement — that withdrawal must include a stated reason published to the NRS — means every withdrawal generates a documented record. The "administrative reasons" category produces 847 withdrawal records in 18 months, all citing the same generic reason. The EI's statistical analysis: 94% of withdrawals are of applicants from the same three national-origin categories. The pattern is on the NRS. The EI opens a Rule Violation inquiry against the three State governments under §1.8.

The Supreme Court's Year 13 ruling: systematic sponsorship withdrawal targeting applicants by national origin, documented in the NRS statistical record, constitutes §1.8 discrimination by the State governments involved. The three States receive corrective orders requiring non-discriminatory sponsorship administration. All three comply within 90 days. The NRS record of the violation and correction is permanent.

The 72-Hour Detention Rule

The coalition's immigration enforcement statute includes a provision allowing detention of applicants whose applications are under national security review "for the period required to complete the review." The first test case: an applicant detained for 19 days without judicial review pending a national security assessment.

The Supreme Court's emergency ruling: the 72-hour judicial review requirement is constitutional, not statutory. It cannot be superseded by Parliament's immigration statute regardless of the review's national security character. The applicant must receive judicial review within 72 hours or be released. Parliament may classify the evidence presented to the reviewing court; it may not avoid judicial review altogether.

This is the constitutional floor working precisely as designed. The 72-hour requirement moved from prior statutory detail to constitutional text in the altitude pass. Its constitutional status makes it unsupersedable by Parliament.

Year 14-15 — The Verdict

The Breckian immigration scenario's most important finding: the delegation is constitutional, but the floor is load-bearing. The §1.8 prohibition, the 72-hour detention requirement, the NRS statistical publication mandate, and the Gap 37 stated-reason requirement together constitute a floor that held against a constitutionally sophisticated coalition's sustained attempt to restrict immigration through neutral-sounding statutory categories.

What the Delegation Produces

Parliament has genuine authority to design immigration categories within constitutional constraints. The Breckian coalition used that authority and found the constraints real. The civic integration assessment was struck down. The withdrawal cascade was documented and corrected. The detention rule held. What the delegation did not produce was a loophole — because the altitude pass correctly identified which protections were constitutional (non-discrimination, judicial review, NRS transparency) and which were statutory detail (the specific category definitions, the processing timelines).

The most important institutional actor in this scenario is not the Supreme Court. It is the EI's Year 11 statistical report — published before any legal challenge was filed, documenting the disparity in full, providing the evidentiary foundation for every subsequent ruling. The NRS made discrimination visible before it became entrenched. The architecture worked.
