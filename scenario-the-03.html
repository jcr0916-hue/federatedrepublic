Presidential Election · §7.2 · RCV Runoff · State Plurality

The Split

Year 8. Three candidates. No one wins both a national RCV majority and a State plurality. The constitution is precise about what happens next. The Republic holds its first presidential runoff.

§7.2 Presidential Election System §7.2.b State Plurality Requirement §13.4 Electoral Supermajority Threshold §4.3.a Emergency Panel §10.1 National Record System §11.3 NVS Properties

By 11:00 PM on National Election Day, Elections Panel Chair Adaeze Nwofor had been on her feet for nineteen hours. The National Voting System had processed 218 million ballots. Every precinct-level count was already on the NRS — publicly visible, verifiable by anyone. That was the point.

Three candidates had cleared the opening rounds of ranked-choice tabulation. Senator Marcus Webb held 38.4% of first-preference votes — the highest single-candidate share in the Republic's eight-year history. Governor Dana Holt held 35.8%. Secretary Rosa Esteban, running independently, held 25.8%.

As Esteban's voters were eliminated and their second preferences transferred, the final national RCV result produced no majority. With 5.2% of ballots exhausted — voters who had ranked only Esteban — neither Webb nor Holt crossed 50% of active ballots cast. Under §7.2, a national RCV majority was required to win outright. No majority existed.

§7.2 — The Two Requirements
No candidate satisfied both conditions. The runoff was mandatory.

§7.2 establishes two concurrent requirements for an outright presidential win: a national RCV majority, and a State plurality — winning more first-preference votes than any other candidate in a majority of the Republic's States. A candidate who reaches 60% of active ballots nationally has the State plurality requirement waived under §13.4. Webb had 48.3% after transfers. Holt had 46.5%. Neither had won outright.

The State plurality counts were certified by the Elections Panel by 1:00 AM. Webb had won the State plurality — more first-preference votes in a majority of States. But he had not won the national RCV majority. The two requirements were split between the candidates: Webb won the State condition, but neither had the national condition. No one had both. Under §7.2, a runoff between the top two national first-preference vote-getters was required.

The Legal Challenge

Webb's campaign filed a petition with the Supreme Court by 9:00 AM the following morning. The argument was narrow: Webb had won the State plurality requirement. Under the constitution's two-concurrent-requirements structure, he had satisfied one of the two conditions while his opponent had satisfied neither. Should the candidate who satisfied one condition not receive some constitutional weight over the candidate who satisfied none?

The Court's Emergency Panel took the petition under the 48-hour mechanism. The ruling came 33 hours after filing.

"The petitioner asks this Court to find that satisfying the State plurality requirement, without a national RCV majority, constitutes a partial claim to the presidency that should be weighed in this Court's disposition. We decline this interpretation. §7.2 establishes two concurrent requirements. Neither partial satisfaction nor sole satisfaction of one requirement is the constitutional standard. A candidate who satisfies neither requirement does not lose to a candidate who satisfies one — both trigger the runoff. The runoff provision exists precisely for this: when no candidate has satisfied both conditions, the Republic asks a cleaner question. §4.3.b — Judicial Scope: courts decide constitutional questions, not policy questions. Whether the two-requirement structure is wise policy is not this Court's concern."

Holt's campaign published a single sentence to the NRS within the hour: "We'll see everyone in 21 days."

The Runoff

Under §7.2, the runoff was a head-to-head national RCV contest between the two candidates with the highest national first-preference totals. No State plurality requirement applied. The question was simpler: between these two people, which one does the Republic prefer?

Webb's campaign ran on the argument that the system had produced a perverse result — a candidate who had won more States, in a constitutional system that explicitly valued State plurality, was being handed a runoff by an exhausted-ballot technicality. The architecture was working against its own design intent.

Holt's campaign ran on something harder to articulate. She had competed everywhere by building coalitions across the Republic's geographic diversity. Webb had maximized first-preference votes in concentrated areas. The constitution's two-requirement structure was not a technicality — it was a statement that national breadth and national popularity were both worth measuring. The runoff wasn't a malfunction. It was the system identifying that no one had yet demonstrated both.

Secretary Esteban didn't formally endorse. On Day 12 she published a short statement: "I ran because I believed in governing across division. I'll leave my voters to make their own choice. I've made mine privately." Her campaign chair appeared at a Holt rally on Day 15. No one was surprised.

Turnout was 81.3% — higher than the initial election. Esteban's voters broke for Holt by roughly three to one. Holt won the runoff 53.4% to 46.6%.

§7.2 — Certification
The runoff resolved the split cleanly.

Chair Nwofor certified the result at 4:47 AM and published to the NRS. Under §7.2.b, the certification was final. No challenge was filed. Webb called Holt at 5:12 AM. His concession was published to the NRS at 5:38 AM. Holt's transition team published its first NRS filing eight minutes later.

What This Scenario Tests

§7.2's two-requirement structure is not primarily about preventing any particular candidate from winning. It is about what a presidential election is for. A candidate can win the national popular vote decisively while losing a majority of States — that candidate has broad but concentrated support. A candidate can win a majority of States while falling short nationally — that candidate has distributed but thin support. The Republic's design says both matter, and that a candidate who has demonstrated both deserves the presidency without a runoff.

The exhausted ballot — 5.2% of voters who ranked only Esteban — is why the runoff was triggered here rather than Webb winning on RCV majority. Those voters chose not to express a preference between the remaining two. The constitution doesn't override that choice. It routes the election to a direct question instead: without the three-way complexity, who does the Republic prefer?

Dana Holt is President because 53.4% of Republic citizens, given a binary choice, chose her. The path to that answer was longer than usual. The result was not less legitimate.
