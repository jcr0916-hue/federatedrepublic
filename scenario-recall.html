Legislature · Constructive Vote · Civic Consul Removal · §2.6

The Recall

Year 4. The Assembly initiates a constructive no-confidence vote against the Civic Consul. The replacement candidate is named. The constitutional clock runs. What a constructive vote actually requires.

§2.6 Constructive Vote of No Confidence§2.5 The Civic Consul — Domestic Executive Domain§3.1 Assembly Composition

The constructive vote of no confidence is one of the most precisely designed mechanisms in the constitution. It does not simply remove the Civic Consul — it simultaneously installs a replacement. The Assembly cannot vote out a Civic Consul without voting in a successor. This is not a technical formality. It is a constitutional requirement designed to prevent the kind of parliamentary crisis where a government falls and nothing is ready to take its place.

Year 4, Month 8. Civic Consul Theodora Walsh's coalition had held the Assembly by a margin of two seats since formation. Two defections — one over the healthcare funding legislation, one over a foreign policy decision the defecting member argued exceeded the Civic Consul's domestic authority — left the coalition with a formal minority. Walsh continued to govern, as she was entitled to do: a Civic Consul may govern without active Assembly confidence until a successful no-confidence vote removes them. But the informal negotiations between the disaffected factions and the opposition had been progressing for three weeks.

The Motion — §2.6

§2.6 — Constructive Vote Requirements
The vote simultaneously removes the sitting Civic Consul and installs a named replacement. Both must happen in one resolution.

Opposition Leader Sven Akintola published the motion at 10:00 a.m. on a Thursday. The motion named him as the proposed replacement Civic Consul. It cited the two legislative failures and the foreign policy controversy as grounds. Under §2.6, the motion required an absolute majority of full Assembly membership — not a majority of those present and voting, but a majority of every seated member regardless of attendance. With 84 Assembly members, the threshold was 43.

The constitutional clock under §2.6: after a formal no-confidence motion is filed, the Assembly must vote within a defined statutory period. Civic Consul Walsh had the option to call a confidence vote herself before the opposition motion could proceed — a manoeuvre that would have reset the clock and potentially forced the opposition to show their numbers before they were ready. She chose not to.

The debate lasted three days. Walsh governed normally throughout — attending Council of Ministers sessions, signing legislation, meeting with the Legat Consul on foreign affairs matters within the Legat Consul's domain. A Civic Consul whose government is under active confidence challenge does not suspend operations. The constitution does not recognise a caretaker status between the filing of a no-confidence motion and its resolution.

The Vote — §2.6

The Assembly voted on Day 4 of the formal period. The result was 43 in favour of the motion, 41 opposed. The motion passed by the exact required margin. Civic Consul Walsh's government ended at the moment the vote was certified by the Assembly Speaker. Akintola took the Civic Consul oath within 72 hours. His ministers was announced within 48 hours. The transition was seamless.

§2.6 — What the Constructive Vote Achieves
No gap in government. No caretaker period. No constitutional uncertainty about who governs.

Walsh's government fell, and Akintola's began, in the same constitutional moment. There was no interim, no acting Civic Consul, no constitutional question about who held Civic Consul authority for the hours between certification and oath. The constructive vote design ensures this: the Assembly, in voting out one Civic Consul, votes in another. The motion that removes Walsh is the motion that installs Akintola.

The 8-year consecutive limit under §2.6 applied to Akintola from his first day. His period of service — whether it lasted months or years — would count toward both the 8-year consecutive limit and the 12-year total limit on Civic Consul service. A Civic Consul who reaches the Assembly through a constructive vote serves under the same constitutional constraints as one who was the initial post-election formation choice.

What This Scenario Tests

The constructive no-confidence vote tests the accountability mechanism that keeps the Civic Consul answerable to the Assembly without producing constitutional instability. The requirement to name a replacement simultaneously is the design feature that distinguishes this from a pure no-confidence mechanism. The Assembly cannot simply express dissatisfaction — it must be prepared to govern differently. A majority that can vote out a Civic Consul must also be a majority that can agree on a successor.

The 8-year consecutive and 12-year total limits both begin counting from the first day of Civic Consul service regardless of how that service began. Akintola's term, whether it produces a four-year government or a four-month government, counts toward his limits. A Civic Consul who reaches the office through a constructive vote and then wins two elections and serves the full period has no more constitutional tenure than the limit allows.
