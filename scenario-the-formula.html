The Formula

Year 8. The EM's annual fiscal equalization review certifies that Evantine Territory and Norrath Territory lack the fiscal capacity to fulfill their §12.1 social state obligations — 23% and 14% below threshold, respectively. The Legislature has 90 days to respond. The Legislature responds. The formula works on paper. One year later, Evantine still cannot deliver universal healthcare coverage to all residents. What the constitution requires — and where it runs out.

§12.8 Fiscal Equalization §12.3 Taxing Power §12.1.a Monetary Authority §12.1 Social State §9.1.a Legislative Monitor §12.5 National Endowment

The EM's Year 8 fiscal equalization report found two Territories below the §12.1 threshold. Evantine's fiscal gap was 23%: its revenue base, combined with existing federal transfers, left it unable to fund universal healthcare coverage and social assistance at the constitutionally required adequacy level simultaneously. Norrath's gap was 14%: adequate healthcare coverage but a social assistance floor below the dignity standard the MA had certified as the constitutional minimum.

§12.8 establishes the Legislature's obligation and the EM's enforcement role. The Legislature must establish, by statute, a mechanism ensuring every State and Territory has fiscal capacity sufficient to fulfill §12.1 obligations. The EM certifies annually whether the mechanism is working. A finding of failure triggers a 90-day response obligation. The Legislature had responded to prior EM equalization findings by establishing the Federal Territorial Assistance Transfer — a formula-based mechanism calibrating federal transfers to each Territory's fiscal gap relative to the MA-certified national threshold.

Year 8's finding was the first time the FTAT formula itself had been identified as insufficient, not just underfunded. The EM found the formula was correctly applied — the transfers went out on schedule, at the formula amounts — but the formula's baseline calculation underweighted healthcare delivery costs in geographically sparse Territories. Evantine and Norrath were both large, sparse, and expensive to serve. The formula hadn't been built for them.

§12.8 — What the 90-Day Response Obligation Requires
The Legislature must respond within 90 days. "Respond" means address the finding — not acknowledge it.

The 90-day obligation is an action requirement, not a notice requirement. A resolution "acknowledging the EM's findings" would not satisfy it. The Legislature must enact a measure that addresses the identified fiscal gap — either by increasing transfers, revising the formula, or establishing a supplemental mechanism. The constitution does not specify the form; that is left to legislative discretion. What the constitution requires is that the response be adequate to close the gap the EM certified.

The Legislature passed the Territorial Healthcare Supplement Act within 67 days. The THSA provided direct federal appropriation for healthcare delivery costs in qualifying Territories — not formula-based transfers, but direct operational funding for the service itself. The MA reviewed the THSA and certified that, if fully implemented and disbursed, the funding was sufficient to close Evantine's 23% gap and Norrath's 14% gap. The EM published the certification to the NRS. The 90-day clock stopped.

§12.1.a — The Monetary Authority's Role
The MA certifies the formula's adequacy. The EM certifies whether States are actually meeting the standard. These are different functions.

The MA certified the THSA's funding as sufficient — that is its function: does the money add up? The EM's function is different: are the §12.1 obligations actually being met on the ground? These are different questions. The MA looks at the formula. The EM looks at the outcome. A formula can be certified adequate and still produce inadequate outcomes if implementation fails, administration is deficient, or the model assumptions don't match ground conditions.

One year after the THSA took effect, Evantine reported that universal healthcare coverage had not been achieved. Rural delivery costs had exceeded the THSA's per-capita model. The EM reopened its assessment. The MA reviewed Evantine's cost data and found the THSA's per-capita model had underestimated delivery costs by 18%. The formula was adequate in structure; the numbers were wrong. Two different bodies found two different problems, in sequence, using their distinct constitutional mandates.

§12.8 — Year 9: Second EM Finding, Second 90-Day Clock
A second EM certification of failure reopens the 90-day obligation. The Legislature must respond again.

The EM's Year 9 finding certified that Evantine remained below the §12.1 threshold despite the THSA. Norrath had cleared its gap — the THSA's per-capita model was accurate for Norrath's geography. Evantine was still short. A new 90-day clock opened. The Legislature amended the THSA to adjust the per-capita model based on the MA's revised cost data, adding a geographic dispersion coefficient that weighted delivery costs by population density and road network adequacy.

The MA certified the amended formula. Year 10's EM finding: Evantine at 3% below threshold — within the MA's certified rounding tolerance. Year 11's finding: Evantine above threshold for the first time. The obligation was satisfied. The EM published a clean certification to the NRS. The FTAT formula was subsequently amended by statute to incorporate the geographic dispersion coefficient for all future calculations.

§12.5 — The National Endowment Backstop
During the gap period, §12.1 obligations were maintained regardless. The Endowment did not activate — but the obligation did not pause.

Throughout Years 8-11, Evantine's §12.1 obligations to its residents did not pause. The constitution is explicit: emergency reduction of social state access is constitutionally prohibited. The Republic's obligation to ensure those rights were met ran continuously — through the formula failure, through the legislative response, through the implementation gap. The National Endowment under §12.5 is reserved for Severe Revenue Contraction as certified by the MA; a formula deficiency does not trigger it. The operative mechanism is the §12.8 response obligation and the Legislature's correction of the formula.

In practice, this meant Evantine operated at a deficit during the gap years, covering the shortfall through its Territory budget and federal emergency grants that were not formula-based. The EM's findings documented the gap; the NRS recorded it; the THSA closed it. The social state protections continued uninterrupted for residents throughout. The constitutional obligation ran from Republic to resident, not from Republic to Territory.

Constitutional Finding

§12.8 establishes an obligation and a clock but not a formula. The constitution requires the Legislature to ensure adequate fiscal capacity; it does not tell the Legislature how. The MA certifies adequacy; it does not enforce delivery. The EM certifies outcomes; it does not correct formulas. These functions are separated by design — the same independence structure that insulates the monitors from political pressure also means no single body holds the full chain from obligation to outcome.

The gap between a certified-adequate formula and actual adequate outcomes is the structural vulnerability this scenario exposes. The THSA passed the MA's sufficiency test and still failed in practice, because the test measured the formula against a model and the model was wrong. The constitution's response to this is iterative: find, respond, certify, find again. There is no single corrective moment — just a repeated obligation that continues until the EM stops finding failures.

The most important constitutional fact in this scenario is the one that never required action: Evantine's residents retained their §12.1 rights throughout. The formula failed. The Legislature corrected it. The gap was never a suspension of rights — it was a funding adequacy failure that produced a documented legislative obligation, satisfied over three audit cycles. The constitutional structure treats the adequacy gap as a legislative problem to solve, not a rights deprivation to adjudicate.
