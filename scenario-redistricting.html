House Apportionment · §3.1.a Webster Method · §3.4 Redistricting

The Census and the Count

Year 12 redistricting. Three states and a territory. Four apportionment methods produce nearly identical results — except for one small state where they sharply disagree.

§3.1.a — Webster Method §3.1.b — Census Authority §3.4 — Redistricting §3.4.a — Drawing Criteria §3.5 — House Composition §15.2 — Statehood Audit

Setting: Year 12  ·  Jurisdictions: Cascadia, Meridian, Augusta, Territory of Lakeshore  ·  Republic population: 362,000,000

The Year 12 Census

The Legislative Inspectorate completed the Year 12 population census in Month 8 and published the full results to the NRS within the constitutional 30-day window. Republic-wide population: 362,000,000 citizens, up from 341 million at the founding census.

The census triggered automatic reapportionment under §3.1.a. The Webster method applied immediately: divide each State's population by 750,000, round to the nearest integer, with a floor of one seat per State. Total House seats: 483. The LI published certified seat allocations within 30 days of the census completion, as constitutionally required.

Four jurisdictions drew particular attention from the LI's redistricting analysts. Three were States with different apportionment characteristics. One was a Territory participating in the process — holding one at-large House seat under §3.1.a, but watching from outside the Senate chamber, which remains reserved for States.

The Webster Method · §3.1.a

Webster apportionment uses the arithmetic mean as its rounding threshold: a State with quota 1.499 gets 1 seat; a State with quota 1.500 gets 2. The method is mathematically neutral — it neither systematically advantages large States (as Jefferson does) nor small ones (as Huntington-Hill does). The Republic adopted it specifically because it minimizes total representation error across the full State population.

The Four Jurisdictions

State of Cascadia
Population: 8,235,000  ·  Quota: 10.980  ·  Webster seats: 11

A large coastal state combining a major technology and finance hub with extensive agricultural interior and mountain regions. The Year 12 census showed continued urban growth on the coast and moderate decline in the rural interior — a pattern that complicated the State redistricting process's district-drawing work more than the seat count itself.

Quota of 10.980 rounds cleanly to 11 under all methods. No apportionment controversy here.

State of Meridian
Population: 11,310,000  ·  Quota: 15.080  ·  Webster seats: 15

A large plains and mountain state with population concentrated in several mid-sized cities and dispersed across an enormous agricultural territory. Meridian's challenge is geographic: 15 districts averaging 754,000 people across a state with vast low-density regions means some districts are geographically enormous while meeting the population criterion exactly.

Quota of 15.080 rounds to 15 under all methods. Hamilton's largest-remainder would also give Meridian 15 — the 0.08 remainder is too small to win an additional seat in competition with other States.

State of Augusta ← the interesting case
Population: 1,080,000  ·  Quota: 1.440  ·  Webster seats: 1  ·  H-H seats: 2

A small northeastern state with a single urban center and extensive rural territory. Augusta's quota of 1.440 sits in a narrow band where apportionment methods sharply disagree — above the Huntington-Hill threshold of √2 ≈ 1.414, but below the Webster threshold of 1.500.

This is the scenario's constitutional hinge point. Which method you use changes Augusta's representation by 100%.

Territory of Lakeshore
Population: 492,000  ·  Status: Territory  ·  House seats: 1 (at-large, full voting member)

A midwestern territory in the §15.2 Statehood Audit process. Under §3.1.a, Territories receive House seats by the same Webster method as States — Lakeshore's quota of 0.656 rounds to 1 under the minimum floor. Lakeshore holds one at-large House seat and full voting rights in the chamber. Its citizens are represented. What statehood adds is not representation but Senate seats — and the full constitutional standing of Statehood.

The Augusta Problem in Detail

Augusta's quota of 1.440 sits in a 0.086-wide band between the Huntington-Hill threshold (√(1×2) = 1.4142) and the Webster threshold (1.500). In this band, H-H gives 2 seats and Webster gives 1.

The Rounding Thresholds Compared

Webster (major fractions): Round to nearest integer. Threshold between 1 and 2 seats = 1.500. Augusta's 1.440 < 1.500 → 1 seat.

Huntington-Hill (geometric mean): Compare quota to geometric mean of n and n+1. Threshold = √(1×2) = 1.4142. Augusta's 1.440 > 1.4142 → 2 seats.

Hamilton (largest remainder): Floor all quotas, then distribute remaining seats by remainder size. Augusta's remainder of 0.440 is typically outcompeted by larger States' remainders → 1 seat in most realistic scenarios.

Jefferson (d'Hondt): Always floor. 1.440 floors to 1. → 1 seat. Jefferson systematically favors large States by discarding their large fractional remainders along with small States' smaller ones.

The policy question H-H versus Webster raises is genuine: which is better, a method that slightly tilts toward small-State representation (H-H) or one that is mathematically neutral between State sizes (Webster)? Augusta's extra H-H seat comes at the expense of a large State somewhere in the Republic. That is not a drafting accident — it is the known, intentional behavior of the Huntington-Hill method. The Republic chose Webster precisely to avoid this tilt.

Internal Redistricting

Seat allocation is only the first step. Once the LI certifies seat counts, the State redistricting process in each State convenes to draw district boundaries. The §3.4.a criteria hierarchy applies: population equality first, compactness and contiguity second, communities of interest third, existing political boundaries fourth. No partisan registration data, voting history, incumbent residence, or racial composition as primary consideration may be used.

Ideal district population: 748,636. The Commission drew its map around a single geographic tension: two-thirds of Cascadia's population lives within 60 miles of the coast; the remaining third is spread across a land area four times as large. Geographic compactness and equal population cannot both be fully optimized simultaneously.

Meridian — 15 Districts

Ideal district population: 754,000. Meridian presents the inverse of Cascadia's problem: instead of coastal concentration, population is spread across a grid of mid-sized cities and vast agricultural land with no dominant urban center. The Commission drew three urban-anchored districts around the three largest cities, then divided the remainder by watershed and agricultural community.

No boundary challenges were filed. The Commission's prohibited-inputs compliance was straightforward — Meridian's population distribution correlates weakly with partisan geography, making the criterion-order approach natural rather than strained.

Augusta — 1 Seat (At-Large)

Augusta's single seat requires no district drawing. The entire State is one constituency. The RBC was not convened. There is no redistricting to conduct, no boundary to draw, and no gerrymandering possible. Every one of Augusta's 1,080,000 citizens is in the same district: Augusta.

This is the cleanest constitutional outcome in apportionment. A single-seat State elects its one representative in a statewide vote. No community is carved out. No boundary is drawn to advantage any population over another. The Webster method's assignment of 1 seat produces this result; the H-H method's assignment of 2 seats would require Augusta to be split into two districts of ~540,000 each — raising the full range of redistricting politics the constitution's criteria hierarchy is designed to manage.

The Design Implication

Augusta's one-seat result under Webster is not a consolation prize for a small State — it is a clean constitutional outcome. Single-seat States are immune to internal redistricting conflicts. H-H's extra seat would force Augusta to confront exactly the boundary-drawing politics that larger States must manage. Webster's neutral arithmetic spares Augusta that problem.

Territory of Lakeshore — 0 Seats

The Territory of Lakeshore held its own census in parallel with the Republic's LI census. Result: 492,000 citizens, up from 411,000 at the founding. The LI accepted the territorial census as the baseline for Statehood Audit purposes.

Lakeshore holds one at-large House seat. Its 492,000 citizens have direct representation in the chamber that originates all legislation — full voting rights, no second-class status. The constitutional distinction between Territory and State is not representation; it is the Senate. Territories send no senators. Lakeshore's one House member participates in every vote, every committee, every confidence motion. What Lakeshore lacks is the two Senate seats that would give it an equal voice in the checking chamber. §15.2

The Year 12 census triggered Lakeshore's formal §15.2 Statehood Audit eligibility — the Territory now has the population and economic baseline to begin the process. The LI published a preliminary audit opening notice to the NRS in Month 11. The Territory's legislature voted 34–6 to begin audit proceedings.

If the audit succeeds and statehood is granted, Lakeshore's 492,000 citizens receive their first House seat under the Webster minimum. Their quota of 0.656 is below the 0.750 divisor threshold, but the constitutional floor guarantees them one seat regardless. The projection-to-actual gap for Lakeshore under all four methods is identical: one seat the moment they become a State.

International Context

The Republic's 750,000-per-seat constituency size places it in a distinct category among western democracies — larger constituencies than any major European legislature, essentially identical to the United States.

Two observations stand out. First, the Republic and the United States are the only major western democracies with constituencies above 700,000 — every European and Commonwealth democracy operates at 100,000–200,000 per seat. Second, Webster (major fractions) and Sainte-Laguë are mathematically identical methods with different names: Sainte-Laguë is Webster applied to party seat allocation in proportional systems. Germany, New Zealand, and several Scandinavian countries use the same mathematical principle as the Republic, adapted for party-list rather than State-by-State apportionment.

The US uses Huntington-Hill, not Webster — a deliberate choice that slightly benefits small States. The Republic's choice of Webster rather than H-H is a genuine departure from the US model, reflecting the constitutional preference for mathematical neutrality over small-State tilt.

LI Director's Census Report — Year 12

The Year 12 apportionment produced no contested allocations for 481 of the Republic's 483 House seats. Two States — one large, one small — received queries from neighboring States about the redistricting process. Both were resolved within the 60-day challenge window. The Webster method functioned as designed: no State received a seat allocation that any alternative major method would consider materially unfair.

The Territory of Lakeshore's statehood audit opening is noted. If the audit completes successfully, the Year 22 census will be the first at which Lakeshore casts a House ballot. The LI records this as a pending constitutional event.

What This Scenario Tests

§3.1.a — Webster Method

The Webster method's arithmetic-mean rounding is mathematically neutral between large and small States. The Augusta result — 1 seat under Webster vs 2 under H-H — is not an error or an injustice; it is the method behaving as designed. Augusta's quota of 1.440 is closer to 1 than to 2 under standard arithmetic rounding. The method gives Augusta the seat count nearest to its proportional share.

Webster vs Huntington-Hill

H-H uses geometric-mean rounding, which rounds up at a lower threshold than Webster (1.414 vs 1.500 for the 1→2 seat boundary). This systematically advantages small States at the margin — they need less population to earn an extra seat. The Republic's constitutional choice of Webster over H-H reflects a deliberate preference for neutral apportionment over the small-State tilt built into H-H. The US made the opposite choice in 1941 and has used H-H ever since.

Jefferson/D'Hondt

Jefferson always floors quotas, giving large States' large fractional remainders the same treatment as small States' small remainders: they're discarded. This systematically advantages large States, which accumulate more seats from their population. Cascadia would get only 10 seats under Jefferson — losing the 0.98 remainder that makes its Webster result 11. Jefferson was used in the early United States until its bias was recognized; D'Hondt is its proportional-list equivalent, still used in many European party allocation systems where its large-party bias is sometimes considered a feature for government stability.

§3.4.a — Redistricting Criteria

Cascadia's redistricting illustrates the tension built into the criteria hierarchy. Population equality is first; it produces the four geographically enormous eastern plateau districts. Compactness is second; it can be substantially optimized within those population-equal boundaries. Prohibited inputs — partisan data, voting history, incumbent residence — are absent from the process entirely. The Commission cannot optimize for political outcomes even if it wanted to, because the data that would enable that optimization is constitutionally excluded from the process.

§15.2 — The Statehood Incentive

Lakeshore's 492,000 citizens have one House member. Their neighbors in Augusta (1,080,000 citizens) also have one — Augusta's larger population earns the same seat count because its quota of 1.440 still rounds to 1 under Webster. The two jurisdictions have equal House representation despite significantly different populations. The constitutional gap between Territory and State is not the House seat — it is the two Senate seats that give States a voice in the checking chamber. Lakeshore's legislature voted 34–6 to begin the statehood audit. The incentive is real, if different than it used to be.

750,000 Per Seat vs European Norms

The Republic's constituency size (750,000) is seven times larger than Germany's, six times larger than the UK's, and more than twice Australia's. This is a deliberate choice for a manageable legislature — 483 seats is a functional deliberative body; 3,400 seats (European ratio) is not. The tradeoff is reduced local representational density: each Republic member represents a constituency the size of a European country's full legislature. Whether that tradeoff is worth it is a question the constitution answers implicitly by setting the divisor at 750,000 and giving the Legislature authority to adjust it by statute.
