Electoral Districts · §3.4 · Redistricting Challenge

The Map

The State of Veldun's redistricting commission draws a map that passes LI compliance review. A parliamentary minority of exactly 1/3 challenges it at the Supreme Court within the 60-day window. The constitutional standard: could this map have been drawn by a neutral actor applying the criteria correctly? The Court finds it could not. Orders specific districts redrawn. Cannot draw them itself.

§3.4.a Criteria Hierarchy §3.4.b Prohibited Inputs §3.4.c Drawing Authority — States §3.4.e LI Compliance Review §3.4.f Court Adjudication §3.4.g Continuity

The State of Veldun has eleven federal House districts. The post-census redistricting cycle produces a map from the State's independent redistricting commission — established by State statute, structurally independent of the State legislature and executive, with published methodology as required under §3.4.c. The LI reviews the map for compliance with the criteria hierarchy and prohibited inputs and certifies it as compliant. The 60-day public review period opens.

On day forty-one of the review period, 54 of the 162 members of the Veldun state legislature — exactly one-third — file a petition challenging the map at the Supreme Court. The petition is accompanied by a statistical analysis prepared by a team of computational geographers.

The Challenge

§3.4.f permits the Court to consider whether "a challenger demonstrates through clear statistical evidence that the map could not have been drawn by a neutral actor applying the criteria correctly." The statistical analysis does exactly this. It runs a simulation of 500,000 randomly generated compliant maps — maps satisfying equal population, contiguity, geographic integrity, and community cohesion in the criteria hierarchy order — and compares them to the Veldun commission's map. The central finding: on a measure of community cohesion, the Veldun map is at the 98th percentile of all simulated maps, but only for one specific type of community. Three of Veldun's eleven districts split existing municipal boundaries in ways that appear systematically in the simulation only when maps are generated using a community cohesion criterion applied differently to different community types.

The commission's published methodology does not document the basis for these three boundary choices. The methodology documentation covers the data sources and the criteria hierarchy application — but for the three contested districts, the discretionary choice notes read only: "geographic integrity preserved, community cohesion applied." The simulation shows this documentation is consistent with multiple possible approaches, one of which produces the statistical outcome found in the Veldun map and most of which do not.

§3.4.f — The Neutral Actor Standard
Not whether a better map exists. Whether a neutral actor could have drawn this one.

The Court receives briefs from the commission defending the map, from the State government supporting the commission, and from the minority challengers. The LI files an amicus brief noting that its compliance review is criteria-based — it reviews whether the map satisfies the criteria hierarchy and excludes prohibited inputs, not whether the discretionary choices within compliance are neutral.

The Supreme Court rules 5-2. The majority opinion is precise about what it finds and what it does not find. It does not find that the commission used prohibited inputs. It does not find intentional manipulation. It finds that three districts contain boundary choices that, taken together, produce a statistical outcome inconsistent with neutral criteria application and inadequately documented in the commission's published methodology. A neutral actor applying the criteria correctly could not be shown to have produced these three boundaries without additional documentation that the commission has not provided. That is the constitutional standard. The map fails it for three districts.

The Remedy

§3.4.f grants the Court authority to "order the State commission or the LI to redraw specific districts" but explicitly prohibits the Court from drawing districts itself. The majority opinion orders the Veldun redistricting commission to redraw districts 4, 7, and 9 within a 45-day period, with full methodology documentation for every discretionary choice. The order specifies the minimum documentation required. It does not specify what the redrawn districts should look like.

The commission redraws the three districts. The new boundaries are different — not dramatically, but verifiably so. The revised methodology documentation now specifies the exact data sources and decision criteria for every boundary choice in the three contested districts. The LI certifies the revised map as compliant. No further challenge is filed within the remaining review period. The map takes effect.

§3.4.g — The Continuity Question
What happens to scheduled elections during a map challenge?

The 60-day review period closed before the Court's ruling, but elections are not scheduled during a compliance challenge period. §3.4.g states: a valid district map remains in effect until the next census cycle. The prior cycle's map remains operative until the new map is certified as valid. The Supreme Court's order, the commission's redraw, and the LI's certification complete before National Election Day. The revised map governs the election.

If the timeline had not permitted pre-election resolution, §3.4.g would have left the prior map in force for that election cycle, with the revised map applying at the next cycle. The constitution does not allow a judicially-disputed map to govern an election — it defaults to the last valid map. Elections run on known, certified ground.

What This Scenario Tests

§3.4's redistricting framework is the constitution's answer to gerrymandering — not by designing "fair" districts (a contested concept) but by removing the tools parties use to engineer outcomes. Prohibited inputs (§3.4.b) eliminate partisan registration data, voting history, incumbent addresses, and racial composition as primary consideration. The independent commission requirement eliminates legislative control over the process. The statistical evidence standard in §3.4.f provides a judicial backstop for maps that passed compliance review but cannot be explained by neutral criteria application.

The scenario tests each layer. The commission passed LI review — the map satisfied all criteria and contained no prohibited inputs. The challenge succeeded not because the map was non-compliant but because three boundary choices could not be documented as neutrally derived. The Court's remedy — redraw specific districts, cannot draw them itself — preserves the commission's role as the drawing authority while removing the specific boundaries that failed the neutral actor test.

The 1/3 minority standing provision is the scenario's quiet design point. Any 1/3 of a state legislature can trigger the Court's review. This is deliberately low: it prevents the majority from protecting its own map from challenge. A party that drew or benefits from the map controls the state legislature majority; the minority standing rule means they cannot veto a challenge by controlling the petition process. The 1/3 threshold is low enough to be accessible, high enough to require genuine legislative minority coordination rather than individual litigation.
