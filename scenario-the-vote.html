Legislative Process · §2.7 · Suspensive Veto

The Vote

The Legislature passes a bill. The Civic Consul returns it within 10 business days. Tabled for one month — bypass-proof. The Senate tries for 2/3. Falls short. The bill must pass both chambers again. A fiscal notice goes out on the same day. The Assembly Speaker recalls. What each instrument does, what each silence means, and how the mechanisms interact.

§2.7 Suspensive Veto §3.1 The Legislature §2.5 The Civic Consul §9.1 The Executive Monitor

The Situation

The Assembly and Senate have passed the Neldrath Infrastructure Investment Act — a major fiscal bill allocating funds for territorial infrastructure across three states. The bill passed the Assembly 274–226 and cleared the Senate 61–39. It now goes to the Civic Consul and the Legat Consul concurrently. Both executives receive the bill from the date of final passage.

The CC, Ivar Sonn, has reservations. The bill allocates significantly more to Neldrath than the EM's equalization formula would produce for equivalent need. Sonn believes the Legislature has been politically generous to a swing-state coalition rather than following the constitutional fiscal framework. He is considering his options.

The Objection

Day 8 of 10 · Written objection
The CC exercises the suspensive veto by returning the bill within 10 business days of final passage with a written statement of objection specifying constitutional, fiscal, or operational grounds, published to the NRS.

Sonn returns the bill on Day 8 with a three-page objection published to the NRS. His grounds: the allocation mechanism bypasses the EM's §12.8 equalization formula without statutory authority to do so; the bill creates a fiscal precedent for political rather than formula-based territorial investment; and the total appropriation would cause or materially worsen a structural deficit in Year 14.

The third ground — structural deficit — is the same concern Sonn had flagged in his fiscal notice on Day 2. The fiscal notice and the suspensive veto are independent instruments; both are now on the NRS record. The suspensive veto proceeds to Senate consideration; the fiscal notice requires no Senate action and the Assembly Speaker has already declined to recall.

The bill is tabled from the date of return. It cannot be enacted, amended, or withdrawn during the tabling period.

The Bypass That Isn't

On Day 11, the Progressive Coalition — which holds 198 Assembly seats and authored the bill — moves a constructive no-confidence motion against Sonn, naming a preferred replacement. The motion attracts 241 votes. Short of the 300 required under §2.6. It fails.

Even if it had succeeded, it would not have helped. The tabling attaches to the bill, not the officer. A new CC installed on Day 11 inherits a tabled bill that cannot be enacted until the Senate acts or the month expires. The constitution anticipated this: the bypass-proof mechanism runs regardless of any change in the office of Civic Consul.

The Senate Override Attempt

Days 1–30 of tabling period · Senate consideration
The Senate may override the suspensive veto by 2/3 supermajority during the one-month tabling period.

The Senate has 100 members. Override requires 67 votes. The bill passed the Senate 61–39 on first passage — six votes short of what override requires. Senate leadership immediately begins working the six-vote gap.

Day 19: Three senators who voted against the bill announce they will support override on the grounds that the CC's fiscal objection, while legally valid, overstates the structural deficit risk. The gap is now three votes.

Day 24: The Progressive Coalition offers Neldrath senators a supplemental fiscal commitment in the next budget cycle in exchange for their override support. Two Neldrath senators confirm yes. Gap: one vote.

Day 29: The final Neldrath senator reviews the EM's preliminary assessment of Sonn's deficit projection. The EM, responding to a request from the Senate Fiscal Committee, indicates Sonn's structural deficit projection may overstate the risk by a factor of 1.4 to 1.8 — the bill is unlikely to cause a structural deficit under current projections. The senator announces no vote on override. Final Senate tally: 66–34. The override fails by one vote.

After the Month — Re-Passage

Day 30: The tabling period expires without override. Under §2.7, the bill is not dead — it returns to the Legislature and must pass both chambers again by ordinary majority. The suspensive veto has been exhausted: if the bill passes again, Sonn receives it back but cannot use the suspensive veto a second time.

The Progressive Coalition tables the bill for re-passage immediately. The political dynamics have shifted slightly — the EM's preliminary finding that Sonn overstated the deficit risk has been public for eleven days. Three Assembly members who voted against the original bill now vote yes. The bill passes the Assembly 277–223. In the Senate, the same 61 senators who voted yes the first time vote yes again. The bill returns to CC Sonn.

Sonn's options on return: sign the bill, issue a fiscal notice, or allow enactment by operation of law at 10 days. He cannot use the suspensive veto. The fiscal notice he issued on Day 2 is already on the NRS. He signs on Day 5.

The Fiscal Notice

Day 2 of the review window · Concurrent with suspensive veto
The Civic Consul may issue a fiscal notice — a written NRS statement that a bill would cause or materially worsen a structural deficit — at any point within the 10-business-day review window. Both instruments may be used on the same bill.

On Day 2 — the same day Sonn begins drafting his suspensive veto objection — he also issues a fiscal notice, published to the NRS: the bill's allocation mechanism, if replicated across future budget cycles, would produce a structural deficit within three fiscal years. The fiscal notice does not delay the bill or require a Legislative response. It does entitle the Assembly Speaker to recall the bill for reconsideration within the remaining review window.

The Assembly Speaker, facing pressure from the Progressive Coalition who authored the bill, declines to recall. The fiscal notice sits on the NRS. On Day 8, the suspensive veto return follows separately, triggering the one-month tabling. The fiscal notice and the suspensive veto are now both on the public record — one informational, one procedural.

When the bill eventually passes re-passage and returns to Sonn, the fiscal notice is already there. Any future Legislature that revisits this allocation mechanism will read that notice first. The CC's fiscal concern does not disappear when he signs. It is permanent.

What This Scenario Tests

The suspensive veto is not a blocking mechanism. It is a constitutionally bounded delay: the Senate may override by 2/3 and the bill enacts without CC assent; or, failing override, the bill returns to the Legislature and must pass both chambers again before returning to the CC. The CC cannot permanently block legislation — only force the recommitment. A CC who objects to every bill exhausts the mechanism: once the suspensive veto is used on a bill, the CC may not use it on that bill again, including where the bill lapses and is reintroduced in a subsequent session.

The bypass-proof design is the scenario's central test. The tabling attaches to the bill. A CC cannot be removed during the tabling period to install a more compliant officer and immediately enact the tabled bill. The constitution anticipated the most obvious circumvention and closed it by design rather than by trust.

The fiscal notice ran alongside the suspensive veto as the scenario's quieter instrument. It requires no override — it creates one right (the Assembly Speaker may recall) and if the Speaker declines, it simply exists permanently on the NRS. The Speaker declined on Day 3. Seven words: "The Speaker will not be recalling this bill." The fiscal notice outlasted that decision. Any future Legislature revisiting the Neldrath allocation formula will read Sonn's documented structural deficit projection first. The CC's informational power does not disappear when the CC signs or the bill passes re-passage. It is permanent.
