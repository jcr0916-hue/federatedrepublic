Paper No. 24

The Citizen's Reserve

On the referendum, the initiative, and the citizen-led amendment — the constitutional toolkit citizens hold directly, without passing through the representative filter, and why holding it changes what representative institutions can do.

The most important feature of a direct democracy mechanism may not be how often it is used. It may be the fact that it exists. A legislature that knows citizens can repeal its legislation by referendum operates in a different political environment than one that does not. A government that knows citizens can initiate legislation directly, bypassing parliamentary action, faces a different accountability structure than one insulated from direct challenge. The toolkit's existence shapes what representative institutions do, whether or not the toolkit is ever invoked.

This document is emphatically not a direct democracy. Citizens do not vote on legislation as a matter of course. The House passes laws, the Senate ratifies treaties, the executives administer and veto — the daily work of governance flows through representative institutions. Direct democracy mechanisms in this constitution are not an alternative to representative governance. They are a reserve: a set of constitutional tools that citizens hold and that representative institutions must govern in the knowledge that citizens hold them.

The Optional Referendum

The first tool is the optional referendum.§11.1 Within 90 days of a law's enactment, citizens may collect signatures from 5% of registered voters. If the threshold is met and verified by the LI, the law is suspended and placed before the electorate. A simple majority of votes cast repeals it. The Legislature may not re-enact the repealed law in the same session; if it wishes to address the same policy area, it must do so through new legislation subject to the same referendum right.

Five percent of registered voters is not a small number. In a national electorate of any size, it represents millions of signatures gathered in 90 days — a significant organisational and financial undertaking. The threshold is set high enough to filter out frivolous challenges while remaining achievable by a genuinely motivated civic coalition. It separates serious popular opposition from the ordinary discontent that follows any contested legislation.

The 90-day window is as important as the threshold. It is long enough to allow organisation but short enough to prevent the referendum mechanism from becoming a perpetual weapon against any legislation that attracts opposition. A law that survives the 90-day window has survived the most immediate window of popular challenge — it is still subject to repeal through subsequent legislative action, through judicial review, and through constitutional amendment, but the specific referendum right lapses. The legislature can plan around a defined period of vulnerability, which is a more workable design than open-ended referendum exposure.

The optional referendum is a corrective, not a veto. It operates after the legislature has acted — it cannot block legislation before passage. A legislature that wants to insulate a law from referendum challenge must pass it with enough support and legitimacy that 5% of the electorate cannot be organised to oppose it within 90 days. That is a reasonable discipline for legislation that affects large populations.

The Citizen Initiative

The citizen initiative§11.2 works differently from the referendum — it is an affirmative tool rather than a corrective one. Where 10% of registered voters sign a petition proposing specific legislation, the Legislature is required to vote on the proposal within 90 days. If the Legislature fails to act, or if it votes down the proposal, the initiative proceeds directly to a national referendum, where it passes on simple majority with a minimum 50% turnout requirement.

The initiative's design reflects a specific theory of how representative failure happens. Legislatures sometimes fail to act not because the relevant majority does not exist, but because the political costs of action — the lobbying opposition, the coalition management, the difficulty of scheduling controversial votes — exceed the political benefits of responding to popular demand. The initiative bypasses this calculation. It forces the question: the Legislature must either pass the legislation, or the citizenry will vote on it directly.

The turnout requirement for initiative referenda is more demanding than for optional referenda. An optional referendum that repeals existing legislation needs only a majority of votes cast. A citizen initiative that creates new law requires both a majority and a minimum participation threshold. The distinction reflects the difference between repeal and enactment: undoing something requires less democratic weight than installing something new. A law created by citizen initiative with 50% turnout and majority support has demonstrated both breadth and depth of public backing.

The Legislature's ability to pass the initiative legislation itself — before it goes to referendum — is a feature, not a limitation. It gives the Legislature an opportunity to respond to demonstrated popular support without requiring citizens to go through the referendum stage. A Legislature that passes an initiative's legislation is doing what representative institutions are supposed to do: responding to the people's expressed preferences. The citizen initiative is most successful when it produces legislative action rather than a referendum — because legislative action means the representative system has functioned correctly, prompted by the knowledge that citizens held the alternative.

The Citizen-Led Amendment

The most powerful direct democracy tool in this document is Path C: the citizen-led constitutional amendment.§15.1.c Where citizens collect signatures from 15% of registered voters, a proposed constitutional amendment is placed before the electorate in a national referendum. Passage requires 70% of votes cast with a minimum 65% turnout.

These thresholds are high. Deliberately so. A constitutional amendment changes the document that governs all other laws — it requires more than legislative majority support, more than a referendum majority, more than any ordinary democratic expression. Path C's thresholds are the highest in the document, higher than either of the parliamentary amendment paths, because they must substitute for the deliberative processes that parliamentary amendment provides. Parliamentary amendment requires extended debate, supermajority approval in both chambers, and in some paths State ratification. Path C replaces all of that with direct democratic will — which means the democratic will expressed must be extraordinarily broad and deep.

Path C is the mechanism through which citizens can amend the constitution entirely over the objection of the legislative and executive institutions — without a parliamentary supermajority, without executive involvement, without State chamber approval. This is a significant power. It is calibrated so that it cannot be exercised by a simple majority, cannot be exercised without substantial civic engagement, and cannot produce amendments that hollow out the document's core protections — because those protections are themselves subject to the most demanding amendment threshold available, which applies regardless of which path is used.

The citizen-led amendment path matters most as a backstop against institutional entrenchment. A legislature and executive that have aligned to prevent constitutional reform can be bypassed — not easily, but constitutionally and without crisis. The path exists. Citizens who can demonstrate 15% support for a proposed amendment and then 70% support with 65% turnout for ratification have demonstrated the kind of democratic consensus that a constitutional order should respond to.

The Reserve Theory

These three tools together constitute what this essay calls the citizen's reserve — the set of constitutional powers that citizens hold outside the representative system, available to invoke against representative decisions they find unacceptable. The reserve theory is not that direct democracy is better than representative democracy. It is that the existence of the reserve changes what representative democracy produces.

Consider how each mechanism operates on the political environment around it. The optional referendum means that a legislature passing contested legislation must consider whether it will face a 5% petition within 90 days. The initiative means that a legislature ignoring demonstrated popular demand must consider whether that demand will generate 10% signatures and proceed to referendum without it. The citizen-led amendment means that a legislature blocking constitutional reform must consider whether citizens will organise the 15% petition to take the question directly to the people.

None of these mechanisms needs to be invoked for these considerations to operate. A legislature that passes legislation without considering referendum vulnerability has not understood its constitutional environment. A government that ignores popular demand without considering the initiative path is operating as though the path does not exist. The reserve changes the calculus of representative governance whether or not the reserve is activated.

Switzerland provides the best documented evidence of this dynamic. The Swiss system of popular initiatives and referenda has produced not a country governed by direct democracy but a legislature that legislates with careful attention to what the population will accept at referendum — because the referendum is always available. The Swiss legislature passes pre-emptive legislation on topics where popular initiative pressure exists, makes concessions in legislative design to forestall petition campaigns, and maintains a policy posture calibrated to a citizenry that retains direct democratic tools. The reserve shapes the governance even when it is not used.

What the Reserve Cannot Do

The citizen's reserve is not unlimited. Three constraints are worth being explicit about.

First, the reserve cannot override constitutional rights. No referendum outcome — including a citizen-led amendment referendum — can suspend the non-derogable rights in §1.26, including the individual sovereignty floor, the prohibition on torture and cruel treatment, and the equality guarantee. A 70% referendum majority with 65% turnout cannot make cruel punishment constitutional. The reserve is a democratic tool; it operates within the constitutional order, not above it.

Second, the reserve requires organisation. Five percent, 10%, and 15% of registered voters are achievable thresholds, but they require sustained civic effort. The reserve is not available to any individual or small group that objects to legislation. It is available to coalitions that can demonstrate broad, organised opposition or support. This is a feature: constitutional mechanisms that can be triggered by narrow interested parties are not democratic tools — they are tactical weapons. The thresholds ensure the reserve is genuinely popular in the sense that matters constitutionally.

Third, the reserve operates on a timeline. Referendum rights expire. Initiative processes have defined windows. Path C requires sustained effort over a longer period. A government that passes legislation quickly and then acts to address the most urgent popular concerns during the 90-day referendum window may be able to reduce the organised opposition that a petition campaign requires. This is not a failure of the design — it is the design working as intended. Governments should have the opportunity to respond to public reaction. The question is whether a response is genuine or purely tactical, and on that question the NRS record of what the government does in the 90-day window will be the evidence future citizens examine.

Democracy as Architecture

Paper No. 1 describes this document as a bet on citizens — on the assumption that people who can see what their government is doing will hold it accountable for what they see. The citizen's reserve is the constitutional operationalisation of that bet in the domain of democratic governance. It assumes that citizens, given the tools to act collectively, will use or credibly threaten to use those tools in ways that improve the quality of representative governance.

That assumption can fail. Citizens may not organise even when they have the tools. Representative institutions may pass legislation they know citizens dislike because they believe the 90-day window will pass without a petition campaign. The initiative path may be used strategically rather than sincerely. Path C may prove so demanding that it is never invoked even against genuinely unacceptable constitutional entrenchment.

These failures are possible. They are not, however, arguments against the reserve's existence. A constitutional system that denies citizens direct democratic tools because those tools might be used imperfectly has made a very different bet: that representative institutions, unsupervised by the reserve, will produce better governance than representative institutions that know the reserve exists. The evidence from constitutional systems across two centuries does not strongly support that bet. The evidence from systems with robust direct democracy mechanisms suggests that holding the reserve, even imperfectly used, produces representative institutions that govern with more attention to popular preferences than those that do not face it. The reserve is the constitution's acknowledgment that representation, however well designed, is not the same as democracy — and that the difference matters enough to address directly.
