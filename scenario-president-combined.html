Presidential Election · §7.2.b · The Combined Score

The Combined Score

Three candidates. No national RCV majority. The combined score — 65% national first-preference share, 35% fraction of regions won — decides. The candidate with more total votes loses. The candidate with broader geographic support wins. The constitutional challenge reaches the Supreme Court within fourteen days.

§7.2.a National RCV Vote §7.2.b The Combined Score §7.2.c Election Regions §7.2.e Certification and Challenge §7.2.f Run-off Threshold §16.1 Electoral Commission

The Republic's sixth presidential election produces three credible candidates for the first time. Samir Osei, the incumbent Prime Minister, campaigns on domestic economic continuity. Elin Vasquez, a former Secretary for Foreign Affairs, campaigns on the Republic's international obligations and a more assertive foreign policy. Tamara Holt, a regional coalition leader, draws significant first-preference support in the industrial States of the north and west before being eliminated in the RCV count.

When the NVS completes its final RCV elimination round, no candidate has achieved a national majority. Holt's transferred votes split roughly 52-48 toward Vasquez. The first-preference shares are: Osei 46.1%, Vasquez 41.8%, Holt 12.1% eliminated. After full RCV rounds: Osei holds 46.1%, Vasquez 41.8% — the remaining 12.1% did not rank a second preference and exhaust. No majority. The combined score activates.

The Regions

The Republic comprises ten presidential election regions, defined by the Legislature under §7.2.c. Osei's vote is concentrated: he wins the four most populous regions — including the two that together contain 38% of all eligible voters — by decisive margins. Vasquez wins the remaining six regions, mostly by narrower margins, with particularly strong performance in the geographic periphery and mid-sized State clusters.

The Combined Score Calculation — §7.2.b
Formula: 0.65 × national first-pref share + 0.35 × fraction of regions won
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
Samir Osei: 0.65 × 0.461 + 0.35 × (4/10) = 0.2997 + 0.1400 = 0.4397
Elin Vasquez: 0.65 × 0.418 + 0.35 × (6/10) = 0.2717 + 0.2100 = 0.4817
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
Margin: 0.0420 (4.2 percentage points) — run-off threshold is 0.5%
Elin Vasquez wins the presidency — 4.2% margin, no run-off

The Electoral Commission certifies the result and publishes it to the NRS. Osei received 4.3 percentage points more of the national vote. Vasquez won six of ten regions. The combined score puts Vasquez ahead by 4.2 points — a comfortable margin, well clear of the 0.5% run-off threshold.

The Challenge

Under §7.2.e, any certification challenge must reach the Supreme Court within fourteen days of publication. On day eleven, a coalition of Osei's supporters files a challenge. The legal argument is direct: the combined score produces an outcome that inverts the popular will. The candidate who received more votes from more citizens loses to the candidate who received fewer votes. In what sense is this democratic?

The Supreme Court accepts the challenge and schedules oral argument for day seventeen of the fourteen-day window — which means it must rule by day twenty-eight from certification. The Court rules 6-1 against the challenge.

§7.2.b — The Court's Reasoning
What "democratic" means in the combined score design

The majority opinion addresses the central argument directly. The challenger assumes that "more votes" is the uniquely legitimate measure of democratic authority. The constitution does not make that assumption. The combined score measures two things simultaneously: how many citizens chose a candidate first, and how broadly distributed across the Republic's geographic and political space that support is. A president who wins by piling up enormous margins in the four most populous regions while losing six of ten regions does not have a mandate that reaches the whole Republic. A president who wins six of ten regions — including the regions that would otherwise feel permanently shut out of presidential outcomes — has demonstrated something the combined score was designed to capture: actual reach.

The dissent argues the opposite: six low-population regions contain fewer citizens than two high-population regions. The regional breadth component systematically overweights sparse geographic space relative to dense human settlement. This is not a distortion in the combined score design — it is the design. Whether that design is wise is a constitutional question answered at ratification, not by this Court reviewing a certified result.

President-elect Vasquez is inaugurated. Osei concedes eleven days after certification, before the Court rules. His statement is published to the NRS: "The combined score is the law. I received more votes and I lost. That is what the constitution produces and what I accepted when I ran under it. I do not agree with the design. But I am not the constitution."

What This Scenario Tests

§7.2.b is the most politically contested mechanism in the constitution — more so than the emergency powers, more so than the removal tracks — because it is the one provision most likely to produce a result that looks wrong on its face. The candidate with more votes loses. In any election system where that is possible, the loser's supporters will call it undemocratic.

The scenario tests whether the combined score can sustain a constitutional challenge on exactly those grounds. The Court's answer — it can — turns on the design's stated purpose. The combined score exists to prevent a specific failure mode: a president who wins decisively among heavily concentrated population centers while losing broad swaths of the Republic. Osei's result is precisely that failure mode. He won four regions containing the Republic's densest populations by large margins and lost six regions by smaller ones. The combined score weighted his national margin against his regional reach and found him wanting on the second measure.

The scenario also tests §7.2.f's run-off threshold. A 4.2% combined score margin is not close — no run-off. A scenario where the margin was 0.3% would produce a head-to-head national RCV contest between the top two candidates, which might reverse the result entirely. That edge case remains unwritten.
