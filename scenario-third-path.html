Constitutional Amendment · §15.1 · Ratification Mechanism · State vs Popular

The Ratification Choice

Year 11. A constitutional amendment achieves 2/3 of both chambers simultaneously. The proposing coalition must now specify the ratification mechanism — State Ratification or Popular Ratification — in the same legislative act. The choice is not procedural. It is itself a constitutional statement about where the amendment's legitimacy comes from.

§15.1 Amendment Paths §15.2 No Executive Veto §8.1.a Legislative Inspectorate §15.5 Amendment Consistency §11.4 Electoral Supermajority Threshold

The amendment proposed to restructure the Senate's confirmation threshold for Supreme Court justices — lowering it from 2/3 to a simple majority, while adding a mandatory 90-day deliberation period before any confirmation vote. The case for change was that 2/3 confirmation had produced three prolonged vacancies in eleven years, each destabilizing the Court during the vacancy period. The case against was that lowering the threshold made the SC more responsive to transient political majorities.

The legislative vote was not close. 71% of both chambers voted in favour — a comfortable 2/3 margin with room to spare. No executive signature was required; no executive veto applied. Every member's vote was published to the NRS within 24 hours of the session's close.

What remained was the ratification mechanism. §15.1 requires the proposing body to specify it simultaneously with the legislative vote. The coalition had 24 hours to finalize the choice before the vote record closed. The deliberation over those 24 hours was, in many respects, more consequential than the vote itself.

§15.1 — The Ratification Choice
State Ratification or Popular Ratification. The choice is part of the amendment.

State Ratification requires 2/3 of States to ratify within the period defined by statute. Popular Ratification requires a national referendum achieving 2/3 affirmative support with at least 55% citizen participation within 180 days. Both paths reach the same constitutional destination. The journey is the statement.

The amendment's proponents argued for Popular Ratification. Lowering the SC confirmation threshold was a question about the relationship between the Court and democratic majorities — and the People should answer it directly. Opponents argued for State Ratification. The Senate is a State institution; a change to confirmation thresholds should be ratified by the States whose representation it governs.

The Consistency Review — §15.5

While the coalition deliberated, the LI published its mandatory consistency analysis within 24 hours of the proposal's formal introduction. §15.5 requires the LI to assess whether a proposed amendment is logically consistent with the existing constitutional structure. The LI's finding is advisory — it does not delay or block — but it is a permanent NRS record that the ratification debate must reckon with.

The LI's analysis flagged one tension: the amendment lowered the confirmation threshold but did not address the EI's domain-assessment function over Presidential vetoes of confirmation-adjacent legislation. The LI noted — but did not resolve — whether the lower threshold changed the calculus of when such vetoes could be considered constitutionally grounded. The finding was published. The debate continued.

The Vote: Popular Ratification

The coalition chose Popular Ratification. The rationale was published to the NRS alongside the amendment text: this amendment concerns the relationship between the Supreme Court and democratic accountability. Its legitimacy should derive from the same source. The People should ratify a change that affects how the People's institutional relationship with the Court is mediated.

The referendum was held 160 days later. Turnout was 61% — above the 55% participation floor. The result was 67.4% in favour — above the 2/3 affirmative threshold. The amendment was certified by the Elections Panel and published to the NRS as the 168th provision of the Constitution.

What This Scenario Tests

The ratification mechanism is not a formality. It is a constitutional choice about the source of democratic legitimacy for a change to the constitutional order. State Ratification draws legitimacy from the federal compact — the States that constitute the Republic agreeing to alter its fundamental law. Popular Ratification draws legitimacy from direct democratic consent — the People ratifying a change in their own name.

The choice is constrained by the nature of the amendment. An amendment restructuring the federal relationship between States and the national government carries a different legitimacy claim than one affecting the individual rights of citizens or the accountability of national institutions. The §15.1 design makes the proposing body articulate that legitimacy claim explicitly — in the same act as the legislative vote, on the public NRS record, before ratification begins.

The LI's consistency analysis is also tested here. It is not a veto and it is not binding. But a published finding of constitutional tension — even an unresolved one — becomes part of the ratification debate's documentary record. Future courts, interpreting the amendment, will have access to the LI's contemporaneous assessment of what tensions the amendment did or did not resolve. That is the Transparency Principle operating in its most subtle form.
