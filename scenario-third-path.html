Constitutional Amendment · §15.1 · Path A vs Path B

The Third Path

Year 11. A constitutional amendment achieves 2/3 of both chambers. The proposers must choose: Path A requires 2/3 of States. Path B requires a popular referendum. They choose differently than expected.

§15.1 Two Amendment Paths §15.2 No Executive Veto §8.1.a The Legislative Inspectorate §15.5 Amendment Consistency and Supremacy

The Social State Adequacy Amendment proposed to constitutionalize the MA's adequacy certification function — elevating the statutory mechanism into the constitution itself. Its sponsors argued this would prevent any future government from abolishing or weakening the MA's certification mandate through ordinary statute. By Year 11, it had the support of 71 of the 100 senators and 302 of the 450 House members — both exceeding the 2/3 threshold. The amendment was formally proposed.

Under §15.1, the proposers had to specify which path applies at the time of proposal. Path A: 2/3 of States. Path B: 2/3 popular referendum with 55% participation. Both were available. The choice was not constitutional — it was political and strategic.

The Path Choice

The amendment's lead sponsor, Senator Adaeze Mensah, announced Path B: a national popular referendum. The decision surprised commentators who had assumed a 2/3 legislative majority would choose the State ratification path — Path A requires 2/3 of States, which the amendment's polling suggested was achievable.

Why Path B When Path A Was Available
Path A ratifies in state legislatures. Path B ratifies in a popular referendum. The same constitutional outcome, different democratic source.

Senator Mensah's explanation was published as part of the official proposal record: "This amendment is about the social state's adequacy guarantee to citizens — the constitutional commitment that the MA will certify whether it is genuinely meeting its obligations. The appropriate body to ratify a citizens' rights guarantee is citizens. State legislatures are the appropriate body to ratify structural changes to government architecture. This amendment is about rights. It goes to Path B."

The distinction she drew — rights amendments by popular ratification, structural amendments by State ratification — was not constitutionally required. §15.1 makes both paths available for any amendment. But the distinction was constitutionally meaningful: a rights amendment ratified by citizens carries a different democratic source than one ratified by state legislatures, even if both meet the 2/3 threshold.

The Referendum — Four Months Later

The referendum appeared on the next federal electoral period ballot. The LI published a consistency analysis within 30 days of the proposal, noting three existing provisions the amendment would interact with and one it would functionally supersede. The analysis was advisory but permanent.

The referendum achieved 68.4% affirmative support with 61% participation — both exceeding the constitutional minimums of 2/3 affirmative and 55% participation. The Elections Panel certified the result. The LI certified the consistency analysis. The amendment took effect immediately on NRS publication. No executive signature was required or sought.

§15.2 — No Executive Veto
Neither the President nor the PM has any role in the constitutional amendment process. An amendment takes effect immediately upon certification and NRS publication.

Both executives acknowledged the amendment's passage in brief statements. PM Asante expressed support; President Calloway expressed concern about constitutionalizing what she characterised as a statutory mechanism better left flexible. Neither had any formal role. The amendment took effect at the moment the Elections Panel's certification appeared on the NRS. The executives' views were noted and irrelevant.

What This Scenario Tests

§15.1's two-path design makes a constitutional choice available that most systems do not offer: the same amendment can be ratified by state governments or by citizens directly, and the proposers choose which democratic source legitimates the change. The Social State Adequacy Amendment chose citizen ratification for a citizen-rights guarantee. A future structural amendment might choose State ratification for a change to government architecture. The path choice is constitutionally unrestricted but democratically meaningful.

The scenario also tests the amendment process's executive exclusion. A President who opposed the amendment had no procedural vehicle to delay or block it. The amendment's democratic legitimacy came from 71 senators, 302 House members, 68.4% of voters, and 61% participation — not from executive assent. The constitution is amended by democratic majorities, not by governmental agreement.
