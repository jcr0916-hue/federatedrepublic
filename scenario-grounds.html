Scenario · §9.14.a · Monitors & Accountability · Judicial Track Removal

Grounds

Year 13. The LM Monitor General has systematically failed to audit a specific category of legislative mandate compliance — creating a documented blind spot that benefited a long-dominant Assembly coalition. The Standard Track's legislative initiation threshold is demonstrably unreachable. The EM and JM jointly petition the SC under §9.14.a: the Judicial Track. The SC has 30 days to determine whether grounds exist. The Senate has 60 days after that to vote. 3/5 is required for permanent removal.

§9.14.a Judicial Track Removal §9.14 Standard Track Removal §9.1 Monitor Mandate and Non-Dependency §9.5 Monitor General Removal §9.6 Monitor Access Obstruction §10.1 National Record System

The Reformist Accord had controlled the Assembly for six years — not by majority, but by coalition. Forty-three seats, reliably, from three parties whose policy positions overlapped just enough to sustain working government. The coalition had survived two constructive no-confidence attempts, one Senate confrontation over judicial appointments, and the kinds of ordinary legislative attrition that erode every long-running coalition. It had survived because its members shared one commitment more durable than any policy position: none of them wanted an LM audit finding that would expose the fiscal mechanics of the social state expansion they had spent six years building.

LM Monitor General Sael Pren had been in the position since Year 1. She was thorough on electoral finance. She was thorough on constitutional compliance. On fiscal mandate compliance — specifically, the Legislature's obligation under §12.2 to audit and certify the social state funding baseline annually — her published findings had been consistently vague. Not wrong, exactly. The findings satisfied the formal requirements. They cited the right provisions. They produced numbers. But the audit methodology that produced those numbers was, in the judgment of anyone who looked closely, designed to avoid finding what it would have found if applied rigorously.

The JM had looked closely. Its annual audit of Monitor operations included a methodology review. In Year 11, the JM published a finding — advisory, as all Monitor findings are — noting that the LM's social state compliance audit appeared to apply a non-standard interpretation of the statutory compliance baseline that resulted in underreporting of the gap between actual social state funding and the constitutionally required minimum. The finding was published to the NRS. The LM Monitor General published a rebuttal. The LM's methodology was not changed.

In Year 12, the JM published the same finding, more specifically. In Year 13, the EM joined. The EM's annual audit of constitutional officers included an assessment of dereliction — "failure to perform a constitutionally mandated function within the required period where that failure was not caused by circumstances genuinely beyond the officer's control." The EM found dereliction: the LM's social state compliance audits had systematically failed to apply the statutory methodology in a way that would have produced accurate findings. Three consecutive years of findings. Political disagreement with a constitutional requirement was not a defense under §9.1.b.

Why Not the Standard Track?

§9.14's Standard Track requires a 1/3 initiation from either chamber. In a 200-seat Assembly with 43 Reformist Accord members, the coalition could not block initiation on its own — 1/3 of 200 is 66 seats, and the coalition held 43. But removal after initiation required a 2/3 concurrent vote of both chambers: 134 Assembly seats and 41 Senate seats, simultaneously.

The EM and JM ran the numbers. The Reformist Accord coalition held 43 Assembly seats. Preventing 2/3 removal required blocking 134 seats. With 43 votes against removal, you needed 67 more — and there were 157 non-Accord Assembly seats. The coalition would need to peel away 24 non-Accord members to block removal. That was achievable, based on observed voting patterns in three prior controversial votes where the coalition had successfully traded procedural support for policy concessions.

More specifically: the EM's dereliction finding documented that the LM's audit methodology had the effect of shielding specific social state programs that 31 Assembly members outside the Reformist Accord had publicly defended. Those 31 members had an incentive to block a removal that would replace the LM with someone who might audit those programs rigorously. The math worked out — narrowly, but plausibly — in favor of the coalition.

The Judicial Track exists for exactly this situation. §9.14.a: "available where the Standard Track's legislative threshold appears unreachable due to demonstrated political gridlock." The question was whether the EM and JM could demonstrate the gridlock sufficiently to access the Track.

Standard Track — §9.14
Initiation: 1/3 of either chamber, or 2 Monitors jointly
SC review: 14 days, constitutional category only
If grounds confirmed: duty suspension + 2/3 both chambers
Vote timing: no election proximity restriction
Judicial Track — §9.14.a
Initiation: 2 Monitors jointly assess and petition SC
SC review: 30 days, sufficiency of cause
If cause found: suspension + 3/5 Senate only
Vote timing: 60-day Senate window; ballot if Senate fails

The Petition

The EM and JM filed jointly with the SC at Month 2 of Year 13. Their petition ran 47 pages, appended with six years of LM audit records, the JM's Year 11 and Year 12 advisory findings, and a comparative analysis of the LM's social state compliance methodology against the statutory methodology it was required to apply. The petition argued two things: first, that the grounds for removal were factually established; and second, that the Standard Track's legislative threshold was unreachable due to demonstrated political gridlock, citing specific vote records and the documented financial interests of the 31 non-Accord members whose votes would be needed to reach 2/3.

The LM Monitor General responded within 72 hours. Her response was also substantive: the methodology used in the social state compliance audits was within the range of reasonable interpretations of the statutory baseline; reasonable methodological disagreement was not dereliction; and the characterization of 31 Assembly members as having "financial interests" in her methodology was speculative and unsubstantiated.

The SC took the case immediately under its expedited jurisdiction. Under §9.14.a, it had 30 days to determine whether the grounds constituted sufficient cause for suspension. The SC's role was explicitly limited: it did not adjudicate factual merits, only whether the grounds as stated — if true — constituted a sufficient basis for the removal process to proceed.

SC Ruling — §9.14.a Grounds Determination — Day 27

The Court's role under §9.14.a is to determine whether the grounds asserted by the petitioning Monitors, taken as stated, constitute sufficient cause for suspension pending Senate review. The Court does not resolve factual disputes about the underlying conduct. Three years of documented advisory findings by two independent Monitors, asserting a systematic and non-incidental failure to apply the statutory audit methodology, constitute grounds of the kind that §9.14.a was designed to address. The grounds are sufficient.

On the Judicial Track's availability: §9.14.a requires that the Standard Track's legislative threshold "appear unreachable due to demonstrated political gridlock." The petition documents a pattern of observed voting behavior by specific Assembly members whose policy interests align with the audit methodology at issue. This is sufficient to demonstrate the appearance of unreachability. The Court does not reach the question of whether those members would in fact have blocked removal — the constitutional standard is appearance of unreachability, not certainty of failure.

Grounds confirmed. The LM Monitor General is suspended from duty immediately. The acting mechanism under §9.1.d activates. The Senate has 60 days to hold a removal vote; 3/5 of full seated membership is required for permanent removal.

The Senate

The 60-day Senate window was not a foregone conclusion. The Reformist Accord's coalition held influence in the Senate too — their 43 Assembly seats translated to roughly 14 reliable Senate votes out of 61. 3/5 of 61 was 36.6, rounded up to 37. The coalition needed to hold 25 senators against removal. They had 14 reliable. They needed 11 more.

The Senate Judiciary Committee held three days of public hearings. LM Monitor General Sael Pren appeared and testified at length. Her testimony covered the audit methodology in detail — the specific statutory interpretation that the JM had characterized as non-standard, the policy rationale for that interpretation, and her position that she had acted within the range of constitutionally permissible discretion. She was methodical and undefensive. She also invoked her right, under the removal framework, to have the public proceeding conducted without interruption for the first three hours of her testimony — a provision intended to prevent the Senate from using the removal hearing as a political forum at the officer's expense.

The EM and JM jointly presented their case: three audit cycles, six years of documented divergence, a statutory methodology that did not admit of the interpretation the LM had applied. They submitted the same evidence that had been in the SC petition. They were brief and specific.

The Senate voted on Day 44 of the 60-day window. The vote was 39-22 in favor of permanent removal. 39 exceeded the 37-vote threshold. The LM Monitor General was permanently removed.

§9.1.d — Acting Mechanism
The LM deputy serves through the remainder of the term.

The acting LM Monitor General was the LM's most senior deputy, designated under §9.1.d's acting mechanism from the moment of suspension. She served for the 44 days of the Senate process and continued after permanent removal until the Monitor General selection process produced a confirmed replacement through the standard pool-and-confirmation mechanism.

Her first independent act as acting Monitor General was to publish the Year 13 social state compliance audit using the statutory methodology. The finding was materially different from the prior three years' findings. The compliance gap it identified — the difference between actual social state funding and the constitutionally required minimum — was 4.1% of the required baseline. The prior year's audit, using the LM Monitor General's interpretation, had found a gap of 0.3%.

The Legislature had 18 months to address the compliance gap before the next audit cycle. That was a different proceeding. But the Year 13 finding — the one the removed LM had systematically avoided publishing — was now on the NRS permanently.

What This Scenario Tests

The Judicial Track exists because the Standard Track has a structural vulnerability: if the officer being removed has sufficient political protection in the Legislature, the removal process can be blocked by the same actors whose interests the removal threatens. This is not a bug in the Standard Track — it's an intentional design that prioritizes democratic accountability over swift removal. But it creates a gap when the officer's dereliction is specifically designed to benefit those who can block the removal.

The §9.14.a Judicial Track closes that gap by removing the initiation power from the Legislature entirely. When two Monitors jointly assess grounds and petition the SC, the Legislature's blocking power disappears from the initiation stage. The Senate still votes on permanent removal — but at 3/5 rather than 2/3, and with the officer already suspended. The dynamics of a vote on permanent removal of a suspended officer are different from a vote on initiating a process against an active one.

The SC's role is deliberately narrow: grounds determination, not factual adjudication. The court does not resolve whether the LM Monitor General's conduct was actually dereliction — only whether the grounds, as stated, are sufficient to warrant the process proceeding. This keeps the SC out of the fact-finding role that should belong to the Senate. The Senate hears the full record. The Senate votes. The SC only determines whether the process should begin.

The "demonstrated political gridlock" standard is the hardest part of this provision to apply. The SC held that observed voting patterns and documented financial alignment with the methodology at issue constitute sufficient demonstration. This is a lower bar than proving that the votes would have failed — it requires showing that the appearance of unreachability is real and documented. Future petitioners will cite this ruling. The bar is not zero, but it is not impossibly high.
