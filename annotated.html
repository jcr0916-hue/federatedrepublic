Article I — The Individual Sovereignty Floor

§1.1 through §1.27 · Rights, equality, and the absolute floor

Article I is a floor, not a ceiling. Rights enumerated here are minimums. Every power granted to government is a ceiling — what the Constitution does not grant, government does not have.

§1.1Individual Sovereignty Floor

Every person holds an individual sovereignty that government authority may limit only as this Constitution permits — and may never eliminate. No official, institution, or emergency suspends this floor.

§1.2Prohibition of Torture and Cruel Treatment

Absolute prohibition on torture, cruel treatment, and degrading punishment. No exception exists — not for national security, not for emergency, not for military operations. One of six permanently unamendable provisions under §15.3.a.

§1.3Prohibition of Slavery and Forced Labor

Slavery, forced labor, and involuntary servitude are prohibited in all forms. The only carve-out is jury service as defined in §10.6.a. Also permanently unamendable.

§1.8Equality and Non-Discrimination

Every person is equal before the law. No person may be discriminated against on the basis of the enumerated characteristics. Distinctions based on race, ethnicity, religion, sex, sexual orientation, gender identity, national origin, or disability are presumptively unconstitutional, requiring a compelling government purpose achievable by no less discriminatory means. Distinctions based on age or economic status require only rational connection to a legitimate purpose — the means-testing carve-out in this section is the primary application of this lower standard

§1.9.aPhysical Sovereignty

Every person has the absolute right to refuse any medical procedure or biological sampling. No mandate is permissible under any declared emergency under any declared emergency unless the individual provides uncoerced, revocable written consent.

§1.4.aCensorship by Proxy

No government agent may coerce, incentivize, or direct private entities to suppress protected speech.

§1.4.bSeparation of Church and State

No religion may be established by law, and public funds directed to religious organizations must comply with the equality requirements of §1.8. The separation of church and state is structural, not merely preferential.

§1.9.bData Ownership

Individuals own their personal data, likeness, and biometric information, likeness, and biometric information. Data collected by any government actor is subject to strict necessity limits — it may not be retained beyond the purpose for which it was collected, shared with third parties without...

§1.9.cEncryption Protection

Cryptographic keys are private property. No court or agency may compel their disclosure. Absolute rule, consistent with §1.16's self-incrimination prohibition — closes the digital search-and-seizure loophole present in most existing constitutions.

§1.10.aHome and Digital Privacy

'Home' extends to all physical residences and all digital spaces secured by private cryptographic keys. Access requires a warrant specifying the precise data target or physical space, verified on the NRS before execution. General warrants — broad or unspecified authorizations — are absolutely prohibited.

§1.10.bEminent Domain

Seizure of private property requires proof of absolute public necessity, payment of 150% of fair market value, and NRS publication. Private-to-private transfer via eminent domain via eminent domain — using government condemnation to benefit another private party — is constitutionally prohibited.

§1.11.aRight to Bear Arms

The right to keep and bear arms for personal defense and hunting must not be infringed. The provision is deliberately narrow in scope — personal defense and hunting — which bounds the right without eliminating it.

§1.11.bState Licensing Latitude

States may establish licensing and safety training requirements as a condition of carrying or possessing firearms. States may regulate the exercise of the right but may not use regulation as a prohibition by another name.

§1.11.cNon-Prohibition Principle

No State requirement may function as an effective prohibition on keeping or bearing arms. A requirement is an effective prohibition if it imposes costs, delays, or conditions that make lawful ownership practically inaccessible to ordinary citizens. The burden of proof lies with the State to demonstrate proportionality.

§1.13.aFirst Appearance

Any person taken into custody must appear before a judicial officer within 72 hoursMeasured from the moment of physical custody, not from formal arrest. Clock does not pause for weekends or public holidays. of arrest. At the First Appearance, the judge must determine whether custody is lawful and whether conditions of release are appropriate.

§1.13.bHabeas Corpus Response Deadline

A court receiving a habeas corpus petition must rule within 48 hours of filing. No procedural rule may extend this deadline. A court that fails to rule within 48 hours is deemed to have found the detention unlawful is deemed to have found the detention unlawful — the detained person must be released or brought before a judicial officer immediately a judicial officer immediately.

§1.13.cHabeas Corpus

Any person detained by the State has the absolute right to challenge the lawfulness of their confinement before a judge at any time. The right cannot be suspended by emergency declaration by emergency declaration — it appears in §1.25.b's list of absolutely non-derogable rights.

§1.14.aRight to Counsel

Any person accused of a crime or subject to detention has the right to retain and consult with a lawyer before questioning. If they cannot afford counsel, the Republic provides it. The right attaches from the moment of accusation or detention, not from formal charging.

§1.14.bCaseload Guarantee

Parliament must establish maximum caseload limits for publicly funded defense — ensuring that the right to counsel means genuine preparation, not nominal presence. Public defenders must be resourced equivalently to prosecutorial offices handling comparable cases.

§1.14.cRight to Be Informed of Charges

Any person detained or accused must be informed promptly, in plain language they understand, of the specific grounds for their detention and the charges being considered. Detention without meaningful explanation of its basis is unconstitutional from its first moment.

§1.14.dRight to Full Disclosure

Any accused person has the right to full disclosure of all State evidence — including evidence the prosecution does not intend to use — before trial. Any withholding of exculpatory evidence is a constitutional violation, not a procedural irregularity.

§1.15.aSpeedy Trial Principle

Every accused person has the right to trial without unreasonable delay. Delay caused by the State is not a neutral fact — prolonged pre-trial detention is itself a punishment imposed without verdict. What constitutes unreasonable delay is defined by Parliament in statute, subject to judicial review.

§1.15.bBail Proportionality

Pre-trial detention may only be ordered where the court makes a specific, individualized finding that the accused poses a genuine flight risk or documented danger to an identifiable person. Financial inability to post bail does not justify detention — bail conditions must be proportionate to what the accused can actually pay to what the accused can actually pay.

§1.15.cProtection from Trial Penalties

The right to trial may not be penalized. A defendant who exercises their right to trial may not receive a harsher sentence than they would have received by entering a guilty plea for the same offense. Sentencing 'trial penalties' are constitutionally prohibited.

§1.15.dSource Code Right

Any accused person has the right to inspect, challenge, and rebut any algorithm, automated system, statistical model, or raw dataset used as evidence or to generate a score influencing their case. Technology-opaque evidence against which no defense can be mounted is constitutionally inadmissible.

§1.16Right to Silence

No person may be compelled to testify against themselves or to produce self-incriminating evidence. The right of silence is complete — silence may not be commented upon or used to draw adverse inferences or used to draw adverse inferences. Consistent with §1.9.c, this extends to cryptographic keys.

§1.17Right to a Public Trial

Every accused person has the right to a public trial before a judge and, in criminal matters, a jury of peers. Secret or closed proceedings are constitutionally prohibited except for the minimal portion where a specific witness's safety requires it — and even then the verdict must be public. Also permanently unamendable.

§1.18.aNo Retroactive Criminal Laws

No person may be convicted of an act that was not defined as a criminal offense at the time it was committed. No sentence may exceed the maximum available at the time of the offense. The prohibition on retroactive criminal law is absolute and permanently unamendable.

§1.18.bDouble Jeopardy

No person may be tried or punished more than once for the same offense arising from the same factual events. Acquittal is final. This applies across all levels of the Republic's court system — a State court acquittal cannot be retried in a federal court on the same facts.

§1.19Right to Democratic Participation

No government action may make voting materially more difficult for any eligible citizen. The right to stand for election is constitutionally coextensive with the right to vote.

§1.20Right to Marry and Found a Family

The right to marry or form a legally recognized partnership belongs to any two consenting adults regardless of sex, gender, or sexual orientation. The Republic must recognize such partnerships for all legal purposes. Parliament and States may not create hierarchies of partnership recognition based on the characteristics of the parties.

§1.21Rights of Children

Every child has the right to protection from exploitation, abuse, and neglect. In all State actions affecting minors, the welfare and best interests of the child are the primary consideration — not merely one factor to be weighed. The protection of children is the one area where the Individual Sovereignty Floor explicitly sets 'the primary' rather than 'a' standard.

§1.22Environmental Accountability

Article I's environmental provisions establish two constitutional obligations: Government Non-Harm (§1.22.a) prevents authorities from knowingly exposing communities to environmental hazards without disclosure, consultation, and mitigation; Environmental Stewardship (§1.22.b) creates an enforceable duty to maintain ecological integrity. Together they make environmental protection a constitutional obligation with Inspectorate reporting requirements, not merely a policy preference.

§1.22.aGovernment Non-Harm

No government authority may knowingly expose any community of Inhabitants to environmental hazards without full disclosure, meaningful consultation, and proportionate mitigation. The Government Non-Harm principle makes environmental poisoning of communities a a constitutional violation, not merely a policy failure.

§1.22.bEnvironmental Stewardship

The long-term stewardship of the Republic's natural environment is a constitutional obligation of the federal government — not aspirational language. It creates an enforceable duty to maintain ecological integrity that the Inspectorates are mandated to report on annually.

§1.23Federal Education Assistance

Three interlocking education provisions: §1.23.a requires the Republic to fund education assistance so no child's opportunity depends on their territory's wealth. §1.23.b requires public education to equip students to meet the Constitutional Logic Test standard — education for citizenship is a constitutional obligation. §1.23.c preserves parental choice: the Republic sets the floor; it may not mandate the method.

§1.23.aFederal Education Assistance

The Republic must provide adequate education assistance to States and Territories so that no child's educational opportunity is determined primarily by the wealth of their territory. Federal assistance must address the gap between what a jurisdiction can fund locally and what the constitutional literacy standard requires.

§1.23.bConstitutional Literacy

The Republic's constitutional literacy standard for citizenship purposes is the CLT, defined in §5.4. Public education must equip students to meet that standard — education for citizenship is a constitutional obligation, not a civic nicety.

§1.23.cParental Right

Parents retain the right to educate children through alternative means — homeschooling, faith-based schools, alternative institutions — provided those alternatives meet the constitutional literacy standard. The Republic may set the floor; it may not mandate the method.

§1.24Housing Non-Exclusion

Article I's housing provisions create constitutional barriers to exclusion and a constitutional obligation to provide. The Non-Exclusion Principle (§1.24.a) prohibits exclusionary zoning and any regulatory mechanism designed to keep certain populations out. The BNF Housing Obligation (§1.24.b) anchors housing as a Basic Needs Floor category — persons who cannot independently secure housing are constitutionally entitled to it through the BNF system.

§1.24.aNon-Exclusion Principle

No government authority — federal, State, or local — may use regulatory or land-use powers to exclude any person from access to housing on the basis of any characteristic enumerated in §1.8. Exclusionary zoning designed to keep certain populations out of a community is constitutionally prohibited.

§1.24.bBNF Housing Obligation

The Republic's obligation to ensure housing access for persons who cannot independently secure it is part of the Basic Needs Floor under §10.1. This provision anchors housing as a BNF category and makes housing exclusion an enforceable constitutional violation.

§1.25.aEmergency Derogation — Declaration Requirements

The Republic may temporarily limit rights in this Article through a Declaration issued jointly by the President and Prime Minister stating the specific emergency.

§1.25.bNon-Derogable Rights

These five rights are absolute under any circumstances: the prohibition on torture (§1.2); slavery (§1.3); habeas corpus (§1.13); the right to a public trial (§1.17); and no retroactive punishment (§1.18).

§1.25.cInspectorate Continuity

The three Inspectorates continue to operate with full constitutional authority throughout any emergency — they cannot be suspended or have their mandate reduced by emergency declaration. Accountability infrastructure is precisely most important when emergency powers are active.

§1.25.dAutomatic Expiry and Judicial Review

Emergency measures lapse automatically at the expiry of the Declaration. Judicial review of any emergency measure is available to any citizen at any time, and courts may rule during the emergency that a specific measure is unconstitutional.

§1.25.eNo Permanent Emergency

No emergency measure may be made permanent by parliamentary act. Every emergency measure lapses automatically at the expiry of the Declaration unless re-enacted through the ordinary legislative process. Parliament may not extend an emergency measure by re-declaration within 60 days of its expiry, or at all unless a 2/3 majority certifies continued necessity.

§1.26Associated Community Residents
§1.27Non-Refoulement

Absolute prohibition on returning any person to persecution, torture, or death based on the specified grounds. Extends to territorial waters and wherever Republic authorities exercise effective custody. Unamendable.

Article II — The Dual Executive

§2.1 through §2.9 · Dual executive — authority, accountability, and succession

The Republic has two executives with constitutionally separated domains. The President holds defense and diplomacy. The Prime Minister holds domestic affairs. Neither may claim the other's domain.

§2.1The President — Term and Transfer

The President serves a single non-renewable six-year term. No action by the outgoing President is required — transfer of executive authority is automatic at the constitutionally fixed moment.

§2.1.aTier 1 — Immediate Defensive Response

Tier 1 covers immediate response to active, imminent, or ongoing attacks. The President must notify Parliament within must notify Parliament within 6 hours; the Senate Armed Services Committee must authorize within 72 hours for the operation to continue beyond immediate necessity. All Tier 1 actions are published to the NRS in real time.

§2.1.a–cMilitary Authority — Three Tiers

Presidential military authority is divided into three tiers. Tier 1 is routine; Tier 2 requires parliamentary notification; Tier 3 requires a full parliamentary Declaration of Defense.

§2.1.bTier 2 — Senate Armed Services Authorization

Offensive military operations or defensive deployments transitioning beyond immediate necessity require Senate Armed Services Committee authorization — Tier 2. The SASC must vote within 72 hours of notification. This democratic pause before offensive action is a deliberate design choice.

§2.1.cTier 3 — Parliamentary Declaration of Defense

All military operations beyond 30 days require a Parliamentary Declaration of Defense — a 2/3 concurrent majority of both chambers. Once 30 days pass, Parliament must affirmatively authorize continuation. No executive can use Tier 1 or Tier 2 authority to avoid Tier 3 by claiming continuity of an earlier operation.

§2.1.ePrivacy Firewall

Any order for domestic surveillance or intelligence activity directed at Republic citizens requires a judicial warrant. The Privacy Firewall separates national security authority from domestic intelligence operations — the President may not direct surveillance of domestic targets without independent judicial authorization.

§2.1.fPresidential Veto — Scope

The President may veto bills that raise a genuine constitutional concern within the presidential domain of defense and diplomacy. A veto is not a general legislative override — it must be grounded in a specific articulable constitutional justification, not policy disagreement. A void veto is simply void.

§2.1.f.iJudicial Review and Override

The Supreme Court reviews any veto challenge within 48 hours. A void veto permits the bill to proceed to enactment. A veto found to be in bad faith — issued as obstruction rather than genuine constitutional concern — may constitute a Rule Violation under §2.7.

§2.1.gPresidential Clemency

The President may grant clemency for individuals convicted of offenses within the presidential domain. The Executive Inspectorate must publish a factual background report before any clemency is granted, making the basis permanently public. No self-clemency; no clemency for Rule Violations.

§2.2The Prime Minister — Domestic Executive Domain

The PM is the domestic executive — responsible for all internal governance. Neither executive may direct, countermand, or assume the functions of the other's domain.

§2.2.aThe Record System Mandate

The PM must record all executive orders, contracts, and expenditures on the NRS within defined timeframes. Failure to record is itself a constitutional violation — makes the NRS mandate self-enforcing through the Rule Violation mechanism through the Rule Violation mechanism.

§2.2.bDuty of Refusal

Civil servants must refuse and report any order that lacks PM fund certification or would require unlawful action. The duty of refusal gives civil servants a constitutional shield — they cannot be disciplined for refusing an unlawful order, and the refusal is itself constitutionally protected action.

§2.2.cPM Maximum Consecutive Service

No person may serve as Prime Minister for more than 8 consecutive years. On the date a PM takes office, the Executive Inspectorate records the expiry date on the National Record System.

§2.2.dCivil Service Integrity

The PM has constitutional responsibility for civil service integrity — merit-based appointment, politically neutral service, and protection for civil servants who refuse unlawful orders. Using hiring, firing, or assignment to reward or punish political loyalty is a Rule Violation.

§2.2.eDomestic Emergency Authority

The PM may declare a domestic state of emergency in response to natural disaster, industrial accident, or public health crisis — not political crisis. Parliament must ratify within 30 days; the declaration lapses at 30 days without ratification. The 60-day re-declaration bar prevents continuous emergency through rolling declarations.

§2.3.aSelection

The House selects the PM by absolute majority — a majority of all seated members, not just those present. A PM who cannot command a genuine working majority cannot be appointed, preventing minority government through strategic abstention.

§2.3.bConstructive Vote of No Confidence

The House may remove the PM through a constructive vote of no confidence — a vote that simultaneously designates a successor. Government continues without gap or dissolution.

§2.3.cCaretaker Protocol

When the PM office is vacant, the Senior Domestic Affairs Council Officer serves as Caretaker PM — a constitutionally defined holding pattern that maintains essential domestic governance. The Caretaker may not make policy decisions beyond routine administration.

§2.4.aSuspensive Veto

The PM may return any bill to the House with a written Statement of Domestic Concern — a suspensive veto that forces reconsideration but cannot permanently block. The House may re-pass the bill by simple majority after considering the PM's objections. The PM's veto is deliberative, not decisive.

§2.4.a–bSuspensive Veto and Fiscal Objection

The Prime Minister may return any bill to the House with a written Statement of Objection specifying the grounds — constitutional, fiscal, operational, or policy. The Statement is recorded permanently on the National Record System.

§2.6Presidential Succession

If the President dies, is permanently incapacitated, resigns, or is removed, the constitutional succession line is: Senate Speaker → Senate Deputy Speaker → most senior Armed Services Committee member. The PM is deliberately not in the presidential succession line — preventing the domestic executive from gaining presidential authority through the death or incapacity of the external executive.

§2.6.bIncapacity Certification

The PM has a potential political interest in certifying presidential incapacity. This provision acknowledges the conflict and requires the PM to recuse from incapacity certification where a conflict exists, with the process moving to the Supreme Court.

§2.6.cSuccession-Based Presidential Term

A succession-based President must stand for election at the next regular National Election Day or in a special election called within 90 days. Succession is an emergency holding mechanism, not a full term grant — democratic authorization must follow.

§2.7Constitutional Violations and Rule Violations

A Rule Violation is a Supreme Court finding that an official has violated a specific constitutional provision. Consequences are automatic — not subject to the official's agreement or Parliament's confirmation. The framework distinguishes minor violations (triggering the corrective order track under §2.7.c) from major violations (triggering suspension and Senate trial). Good-faith procedural errors are handled proportionately; deliberate circumvention is not.

§2.7.cMinor Violations — Corrective Order Track

A violation is minor — triggering the corrective order track — when it is procedural, involves no evidence of deliberate circumvention, and rights were not materially prejudiced. Good-faith officials who made constitutional errors may correct them without losing office.

§2.8Emergency Governance Protocol

The Emergency Governance Protocol addresses simultaneous loss of both executives and other catastrophic continuity scenarios where the normal succession mechanisms under §2.6 cannot function. It is the constitutional backstop for the Republic's most extreme continuity scenarios — covering attack on the Seat of Government, the complete exhaustion of the presidential succession line, and simultaneous incapacity of both executives.

§2.9.cSelf-Clemency Absolute Prohibition

No elected or appointed official of the Republic may grant clemency to themselves, to a co-defendant in any matter involving their own conduct, or to any person whose conviction arose from their own acts while in office. Self-clemency is absolutely prohibited.

Article III — The Parliament

§3.1 through §3.6 · House, Senate, and the budget process
§3.1.aHouse Membership Floor

The House has a constitutionally guaranteed minimum membership floor below which no statute may reduce representation.

§3.1.bCensus Authority

The Legislative Inspectorate — not the Executive — conducts the census for House seat allocation. This prevents the executive branch from influencing seat allocation by controlling the population count.

§3.1.cSingle-Subject Rule

Every bill must address a single, clearly defined subject identified in its title. Where a bill contains a non-conforming provision, any citizen or parliamentary minority of one-third may petition the Supreme Court during the Legislative Review Period. The Court severs the non-conforming provision; the remainder returns to both chambers for a revote at the original passage threshold. A severed provision may not be reintroduced in the same legislative session.

§3.1.dBill Exposure

Every bill must be published in full on the NRS for a minimum public exposure period before it may be voted on. Secret legislation — drafted and passed the same day — is constitutionally prohibited. Every piece of legislation is publicly reviewable before it becomes law.

§3.3.aParliamentary Mandate Enforcement

If Parliament fails to enact a constitutionally mandated statute within the specified timeframe — or within two budget cycles where no timeframe is specified — the matter automatically becomes eligible for an Optional Referendum under §11.1 without requiring...

§3.3.dRecord System Entry

The presiding officer of each chamber certifies and records each enacted law on the NRS with a timestamp. Discrepancies between the certified NRS record and any other document resolve in favor of the NRS. The record is what is law.

§3.5Impeachment

Inquiry requires simple majority; Articles of Impeachment require absolute majority; Senate conviction requires a 2/3 supermajority of seated senators.

§3.5.aImpeachment Inquiry

Any 1/3 minority of the House may open a formal impeachment inquiry — a deliberately low threshold ensuring opposition parties can trigger accountability proceedings without majority cooperation. The inquiry opens investigation; Articles of Impeachment require a full House simple majority.

§3.5.bArticles of Impeachment

The full House votes on Articles of Impeachment by simple majority of the full membership — not those present. Impeachment requires genuine majority support, not the ability to time a vote when opponents are absent.

§3.6.cAnti-Weaponization Threshold

Any expulsion vote taking effect within 30 days of a scheduled vote on a bill the expelled member was known to oppose is presumptively retaliatory. This prevents parliamentary majorities from using expulsion to silence dissent before critical votes.

§3.6.dConsequences and Restoration

An expelled member may not stand for federal legislative election for two years. The consequence is proportionate — expulsion does not permanently bar democratic participation, but creates a meaningful cooling period.

§3.2The Senate — States as Constitutional Actors

The Senate — States as Constitutional Actors.

§3.2.cSenate Must Act on Every House Bill

The Senate must take up every House-passed bill within a defined period — indefinite Senate delay used as a de facto veto is prohibited. If the Senate fails to act within the period, the bill is deemed confirmed and passes to the executive. Senate opposition must be expressed, not implied through silence.

§3.2.eTreaty Ratification

All international treaties require a 2/3 Senate vote. Treaties bind all States — the supermajority requirement ensures that no treaty imposes obligations without the consent of a meaningful cross-section of State representation.

§3.2.fImpeachment Trials

Impeachment trial conviction requires a 2/3 supermajority of seated senators — not those present. An absentee strategy to reduce the threshold through non-attendance is constitutionally closed. Removal is a constitutional act of last resort; the standard is deliberately high.

§3.2.gSurvivor Quorum — Catastrophic Casualty Events

Where Senate membership is reduced by 50% or more in a single catastrophic event, survivors may constitute a quorum and exercise full Senate authority. A single devastating attack cannot eliminate the legislative function of government.

§3.4Electoral Districts

Electoral districts for the House of the Republic are drawn through an independent commission process governed by a strict criteria hierarchy: equal population first, then geographic contiguity, then community integrity, then competitive balance. Partisan data — voting history, registration, incumbents' home addresses — is constitutionally prohibited as an input. The Legislative Inspectorate reviews every map for compliance; any citizen may challenge a non-compliant map before the Supreme Court.

§3.4.aCriteria Hierarchy

Districts are drawn applying criteria in order of priority: equal population first, then geographic contiguity, then community integrity, then competitive balance. A district that satisfies lower criteria while sacrificing equal population is constitutionally invalid. The ordering makes priorities explicit rather than leaving them to judicial inference.

§3.4.bProhibited Inputs

Prohibited inputs to the district-drawing process: partisan registration data, voting history by party, incumbents' home addresses, demographic projections beyond census data, and any data the LI certifies as a proxy for prohibited inputs. 'Districts whose shape requires mathematical rather than geographic explanation fail the geographic contiguity criterion' — one of the clearest lines in the document.

Article IV — The Judicial Architecture

§4.1 through §4.4 · The Supreme Court
§4.2.a–cJudicial Election Process

Justices are elected through a Qualified Jurist Pool process — an independent panel publishes assessments; citizens elect by ranked-choice vote.

§4.2.dEmergency Panel — Scope Challenges

When either executive claims the other has exceeded their domain, the Emergency Panel — drawn from the Supreme Court — resolves the dispute within 48 hours.

§4.2.eVacancy Operating Rules

When a Supreme Court seat is vacant, the remaining justices continue to function. For a tied decision during a vacancy, the majority position is affirmed without precedential weight — the case is decided but the law is not settled until a full Court rules. This prevents strategic vacancy creation to paralyze constitutional resolution.

§4.4.aJudicial Judgments

A final judgment of any State or Territory court is entitled to recognition and enforcement throughout the Republic. States may not selectively ignore each other's court orders — a custody order from one State cannot be relitigated from scratch in another from scratch in another.

§4.4.bNRS Authentication

A judgment published to the NRS by the issuing court is presumptively authentic — no further authentication is needed for enforcement in another jurisdiction. The NRS eliminates the certification and verification bureaucracy that makes interstate judgment enforcement slow and expensive.

Only the House may create or abolish inferior courts by statute. This prevents the executive from restructuring the judiciary to achieve favorable outcomes — court-packing or court-elimination requires the full democratic process of legislation.

§4.1.bAppointment of Inferior Courts

The PM nominates lower court judges; the Senate confirms by a supermajority Parliament defines by statute. Neither the House nor the President controls federal lower court appointments — nomination by the domestic executive with Senate confirmation connects the judiciary to democratic accountability at multiple points.

The Supreme Court hears only concrete cases — actual disputes involving genuine harm. Advisory opinions and pre-enforcement challenges are not within its jurisdiction.

Article V — Citizenship, Residency, and National Identity

§5.1 through §5.5 · Citizenship, residency, and the Constitutional Logic Test
§5.1.aInherent Citizenship
§5.1.a.iStatelessness Prevention

A child born on Republic territory to parents holding neither Citizen nor Legal Resident status is an Inhabitant from birth, with full Article I protections and BNF access. Where no country recognizes the child as a citizen, the Republic provides a determination process consistent with §6.4's protection principles — protection first, determination second. A child determined to hold no other citizenship is eligible for Legal Residency from birth, with all Inhabitant years counting toward the §5.1.c residency requirement.

§5.1.bComing of Age

Children of citizens are Inhabitants at birth with full Article I protections. Full citizenship rights — including the right to vote — activate at 18. The distinction between Inhabitant and Citizen status for citizen children prevents citizenship from being used to exempt children from local law.

§5.1.cNaturalization

A Legal Resident may earn citizenship after five continuous years of residency, passing the Constitutional Logic Test, and demonstrating a working knowledge of the Republic's official language. The CLT tests understanding of the Republic's constitutional structure of the Republic's constitutional structure — not cultural assimilation.

§5.1.dNational Service Path

A Legal Resident may earn citizenship through honorable completion of military or national service — a service path that runs parallel to the residency path. Years of service are a legitimate basis for constitutional membership.

The CLT assesses knowledge of the Republic's structure, powers, limits, and rights — not cultural trivia or political views. Required for naturalization citizenship paths.

§5.1.eCitizenship Irrevocability

Citizenship may not be revoked as punishment or for any reason once granted by birth or naturalization. The Republic may not manufacture statelessness as a punitive tool. See also §5.1.a.i, which ensures children born on Republic territory to undocumented parents cannot be left stateless by inaction.

§5.2Legal Resident Rights

Legal Residents hold a Residency Credential allowing them to work, enter contracts, own property, access BNF programs, and participate in local elections where Parliament authorizes. They hold all Article I rights as Inhabitants. What they lack that citizens hold: federal voting rights and the right to stand for federal office.

§5.3Custodial Care of Citizen Children

Where a parent or guardian who is the primary caregiver of a citizen child is removed from the Republic, the Republic accepts responsibility for that citizen child — housing, care, and connection to remaining family. The Republic does not separate citizen children from their sole caregivers from their sole caregivers without providing for them.

§5.4The Constitutional Logic Test
§5.4.aSenate Authority over the CLT Standard

The Senate establishes and maintains the CLT content and passing threshold. Raising the standard requires a simple Senate majority; lowering it requires a 2/3 supermajority. This asymmetric threshold protects the citizenship pathway from debasement through political pressure.

§5.5.aAutomatic Citizenship Grant

All individuals holding voting rights on Day Zero of ratification are automatically granted full citizenship without testing or application. The Day Zero Grandfather Clause prevents the new Republic from immediately disenfranchising its existing population by requiring existing voters to re-qualify under new standards.

A child born to at least one parent who holds Citizen or Legal Resident status at the time of birth is automatically a citizen. Citizenship follows lineage, not paperwork — no registration or application is needed. The automatic grant closes the administrative gap that leaves children in legal limbo when parents fail to register births.

Article VI — Immigration and Residency

§6.1 through §6.4 · Immigration gates, enforcement, and asylum protection
§6.1.a–bThe Dual Gate

Legal residency requires passing both gates. Neither gate alone is sufficient — both must be satisfied. The dual-gate architecture prevents both federal overreach and State capture.

§6.1.cPresidential Role

Once an applicant clears both gates, the President must ministerially issue residency credentials. The word 'ministerially' is deliberate — the President has no discretion to refuse or delay what the law requires. Presidential interference with credential issuance constitutes a Rule Violation.

§6.2Enforcement and Removal

Only the Federal government may remove individuals from the Republic — States may not conduct deportations. Removal occurs only for violations of laws Parliament has defined.

§6.3Application Appeals

Gate 2 denials are subject to judicial challenge on three grounds: the denial relied on factually inaccurate information; the denial applied a standard not authorized by law; or the denial violated Article I. The national security track routes in-camera to a security-cleared judge. The three-grounds limitation prevents judicial review from becoming a general appeal of executive discretion.

§6.4Asylum and Protection

Seven sub-provisions governing the asylum and protection framework: asylum seekers as BNF Inhabitants from filing date; right to determination before removal; the Asylum Court as a constitutionally independent judicial body under Article IV — not an executive tribunal. Non-penalization for irregular entry; unexecutable removal orders; security and criminal protection cases; Territory remote proceedings. New in v118: §6.4.h establishes a federal legal guardian for unaccompanied minors within 24 hours of contact.

§6.4.aAsylum Seekers as Inhabitants

Any person who files an asylum claim under §1.27 is an Inhabitant of the Republic from the date of filing — immediately entitled to BNF coverage and all Article I protections. Protection-first: the determination process does not happen in a rights-free zone.

§6.4.bRight to Determination and Protection During Proceedings
§6.4.cAsylum Court

The Asylum Court is a federal judicial institution constitutionally independent of both executive branches. Its judges hold full tenure protections. Parliament may not establish any asylum determination body as a subdivision or instrumentality of either executive branch — administrative asylum tribunals dependent on the executive are constitutionally prohibited.

§6.4.dNon-Penalization for Irregular Entry

The manner or means of entry into the Republic — including irregular or unauthorized entry — does not affect the right to claim asylum or have that claim determined. No penalty arising solely from irregular entry may be imposed on a person whose asylum claim is pending or has been granted.

§6.4.fSecurity and Criminal Protection Cases

Where the Asylum Court grants §1.27 protection to a person who poses a documented security threat, removal remains constitutionally barred — but indefinite unreviewed detention is not constitutionally available. Parliament must establish a framework with regular judicial review. 'Indefinite unreviewed detention is not a constitutionally available outcome' — one of the clearest lines in the document.

§6.4.hUnaccompanied Minors

An unaccompanied minor arriving in the Republic or presenting to Republic authorities is an Inhabitant from the moment of contact and holds full §1.27 protection. A federal legal guardian must be appointed within 24 hours. No unaccompanied minor may be detained in a facility not specifically designated and resourced for children.

No person whose asylum claim is pending before the Asylum Court may be removed until all proceedings are concluded and removal is finally authorized. The commencement of removal proceedings does not extinguish a pending asylum claim. Protection during proceedings is constitutionally guaranteed, not discretionary.

Article VII — Elections and the National Vote

§7.1 through §7.3 · The combined-score presidential election
§7.2.aNational Ranked-Choice Vote

Presidential elections use ranked-choice voting nationally. Votes transfer until one candidate achieves a national majority.

§7.2.bThe Combined Score

Where no candidate achieves a national majority, the winner has the highest combined score — national vote share plus State-weighted electoral count.

§7.2.cPresidential Election Regions

The Republic is divided into presidential election regions to provide the geographic breadth component of the combined score. At least three regions are required; no more than one per ten States once the Republic exceeds thirty States. Parliament draws regions; the LI reviews for compliance.

§7.2.dTerritory Presidential Voting

Territory citizens hold the full §1.19 right to vote in presidential elections. Their ballots count in the national popular vote and the combined score calculation.

§7.2.eElection Certification

The Electoral Commission certifies the final presidential election result to the NRS within a period Parliament defines by statute. Certification is final and may be challenged only before the Supreme Court within 14 days. No transfer of presidential authority may be delayed beyond the constitutionally fixed moment.

§7.2.fRun-off

If two candidates are within 0.5% of each other on the combined score, a head-to-head national RCV runoff is conducted between those two candidates. The runoff closes the edge-case gap where the combined score is mathematically indeterminate.

§7.3.aNo Executive Interference

The President may not declare, postpone, or influence the scheduling of elections — including declaring emergencies that have the effect of postponing them. §1.25.e's prohibition on emergency-based election postponement backs this up: even a genuine emergency cannot defer a scheduled election.

§7.3.bNational Voting System

The Electoral Commission administers the National Voting System through which all federal elections and referenda are conducted. The NVS is constitutionally required to maintain ballot secrecy, public auditability, and resilience — properties defined in Article XVI creating a cross-constitutional guarantee.

§7.3.cParticipation Protection

No State may take any action that reduces NVS access, functionality, or availability within its territory. State-level voter suppression — long lines, ID requirements that function as bars, polling place closures that reduce access — is constitutionally prohibited to the extent it reduces access for eligible citizens.

Article VIII — The Inspectorates

§8.1 through §8.11 · Three independent oversight bodies

The three Inspectorates audit different branches. None has enforcement power. All publish findings permanently to the NRS. Their power is the power of complete, public disclosure.

§8.1Three Inspectorates

Three permanent, constitutionally established Inspectorates — Legislative (§8.1.a), Executive (§8.1.b), and Judicial (§8.1.c) — each audit a different branch of government. None has enforcement power: their mandate is information, not action. All findings are published permanently to the NRS. Their power is the power of complete, mandatory public disclosure — accountability through transparency rather than compulsion.

§8.1.aThe Legislative Inspectorate (LI)

The Legislative Inspectorate audits the House and Senate for fiscal accuracy, constitutional compliance of enacted legislation, redistricting compliance, and BNF provision rates at the State level. The LI is not selected by the legislative branch it audits — its IG is nominated by the Supreme Court and confirmed by the Senate.

§8.1.bThe Executive Inspectorate (EI)

The Executive Inspectorate audits both executive offices and the Monetary Authority for constitutional compliance and rule violation evidence. It publishes factual background reports on every clemency petition before either executive acts. Nominated by the House — not by either executive it audits.

§8.1.cThe Judicial Inspectorate (JI)

The Judicial Inspectorate audits the court system for compliance with judicial scope and independence standards, maintains the Qualified Jurist Pool, and audits the Electoral Commission. Its IG is selected by lottery from the QJP — removing human discretion from the selection of the body that watches the judiciary.

§8.3.aLegislative IG

The Legislative IG is nominated by the Supreme Court and confirmed by the Senate with a 2/3 supermajority. Nomination by the judiciary — itself independent of Parliament — gives the LI's leadership genuine independence from the institution it audits.

§8.3.bExecutive IG

The Executive IG is nominated by the House and confirmed by the Senate. Neither executive nominates or confirms the body that audits them. The deemed confirmed provision prevents either chamber from blocking an IG appointment through indefinite inaction.

§8.5.aFor-Cause Removal

For-cause IG removal requires a 2/3 supermajority of both chambers simultaneously. Grounds: documented dereliction, incapacity, undisclosed conflict of interest, or criminal conviction. The standard is deliberately high — Inspectorate independence depends on removal being genuinely difficult.

§8.5.bNo-Cause Removal

No-cause removal requires a 3/4 vote of both chambers simultaneously — the extraordinary supermajority signals that no-cause removal is a constitutional last resort, not a political convenience. The mandatory 90-day delay between vote and removal allows judicial challenge of politically motivated removals.

§8.6.aReports

Each Inspectorate publishes its findings as formal Reports to the NRS — permanent, unalterable, publicly accessible from publication. Reports cover every audit, investigation, and non-pursuit decision. Inspectorate work is the property of the public, not the institution being audited.

§8.6.bNo Classification

Inspectorate Reports are permanently exempt from classification. Even where an investigation touches classified matters, the finding itself must still be published. Classification may not be used to suppress accountability findings.

§8.6.cNo Binding Force

Inspectorate findings carry no binding legal force on their own — they are published to the NRS and create political accountability, not legal commands.

§8.6.dNon-Pursuit Disclosure

Every decision not to open an investigation following a valid trigger must be published to the NRS with the reasoning. Non-pursuit disclosure closes the suppression-by-inaction pathway — an Inspectorate cannot simply decline to investigate a problem and remain silent about the decision.

§8.6.eMinority Reports

Any Inspectorate staff member may submit a Minority Report directly to the NRS — bypassing their IG — if they believe a finding was suppressed or mischaracterized. The protection is absolute for good-faith professional disagreement about findings.

§8.9.aPowers

The Inspectorate Oversight Committee may review internal investigation files, methodologies, and non-pursuit decisions. It can request written justification for any decision and recommend IG removal. The IOC cannot direct the content of any Inspectorate report — it may scrutinize process, not dictate findings.

§8.9.bLimits

The IOC may not alter, suppress, delay, or direct the content of any Inspectorate report or interfere with ongoing investigations. The separation between 'parliamentary oversight of the oversight body' and 'parliamentary control of the oversight body' is constitutionally maintained.

§8.11Eighth-Year Assessment

Every eight years, the three Inspectorates jointly publish a comprehensive assessment of the constitutional order — the constitutional system auditing itself.

Article IX — The National Record System

§9.1 through §9.4 · NRS architecture, co-equal records, classification, and intelligence sharing
§9.1NRS Properties

The National Record System is the Republic's single source of constitutional truth — every constitutionally required publication finds its permanent, unalterable record here.

§9.1.aImmutability

All NRS entries are permanent — they may never be deleted or altered after confirmation. Superseded records are annotated with the superseding record; the original remains. The immutability is the technical foundation for the Republic's accountability architecture: politicians cannot disappear inconvenient records.

§9.1.bDistributed Custody

The NRS is maintained by a network of independent, constitutionally mandated nodes — not a single central server under government control. Each Inspectorate and each State operates at least one independent node. No single point of failure, no single point of control, no single point of government capture.

§9.1.cPublic Accessibility

All NRS entries must be readable by any Inhabitant at no cost and without registration. The accessibility requirement is independently mandated from the operational requirement — a system that technically operates but is practically inaccessible fails both. The NRS is the Republic's public square for government accountability.

§9.1.eTechnology Neutrality

No specific technology, protocol, or cryptographic standard is named in this Constitution. The NRS must have defined properties — immutability, distribution, public accessibility, cryptographic signing — and technology must meet those properties. This technology neutrality ensures the constitution does not become obsolete as technology evolves.

The RSA Director is nominated by the Prime Minister from a Candidate Register maintained under the same mechanism as §10.4.a — the Executive Inspectorate publishes a factual assessment of each candidate's professional background and relevant expertise to the...

§9.1.gNRS Self-Authentication

All records published to the National Record System pursuant to a constitutional mandate are self-authenticating in any constitutional or legal proceeding. No additional foundation laying, certification, apostille, or authentication process may be required...

§9.2Node Operators

Each Inspectorate and each State must operate at least one independent node. Parliament may charter additional nodes from civil society, universities, or international organizations. Independence — meaning free from centralized manipulation — is a constitutional property, not a technical preference.

§9.4.aClassification Authority
§9.4.bClassification Duration

Standard classification expires 10 years from creation without renewal — and renewal must be separately justified. Automatic declassification prevents the indefinite accumulation of secret government records that characterizes most existing classification systems.

§9.4.cClassification Registry

The existence of every classified document must be registered on the NRS — even if its contents remain classified. Citizens know that classified information exists even if they cannot access it. No secret program can exist without its existence being publicly acknowledged.

§9.4.dAutomatic Declassification

Upon expiration of classification without renewal, all affected documents transfer automatically to the NRS with full public accessibility. No action is required — declassification is the automatic outcome of inaction. Secrecy requires active maintenance, not transparency.

§9.4.eImproper Classification

Classification used to conceal a Rule Violation, an illegal order, or embarrassing but non-sensitive information constitutes a Rule Violation in itself. Improper classification is not an administrative error — it is a constitutional offense — it is a constitutional offense.

Only the President may classify national defense and diplomatic operational information. Only the Prime Minister may classify domestic information with genuine national security implications.

Article X — Public Finance and Ethics

§10.1 through §10.6 · The BNF, Monetary Authority, and public ethics
§10.1The Basic Needs Floor

A constitutionally guaranteed minimum of provision — housing, healthcare, food security, education, and social protection — indexed to the Republic's actual economic capacity.

§10.1.aAnti-Evasion Principle

The BNF guarantees actual provision, not a formula satisfied on paper. The anti-evasion principle prohibits reclassification, formula manipulation, and creative accounting. The MA's certification detects structural evasion.

§10.1.dSevere Contraction Protocol — Activation

When the MA certifies that current gross revenue has fallen 25% below baseline in any fiscal year, the Severe Contraction Protocol activates automatically. Activation is not a political decision — it is a certification finding. The protocol allows the BNF floor to contract proportionally to actual economic capacity.

§10.1.eSevere Contraction Protocol — Effects

During a certified Severe Contraction, the BNF floor may be reduced proportionally — but never below a Severe Contraction minimum defined in statute. Even in crisis, equality of reduced provision is constitutionally mandated — the contraction cannot be used to differentially reduce provision for particular demographic groups.

§10.1.fSevere Contraction Protocol — Parliamentary Review

The Legislative Inspectorate reviews every Severe Contraction certification within 45 days and publishes a compliance report. Parliament must hold a debate within 30 days of the LI report. If Parliament finds the contraction is being used to target specific groups or evade the anti-evasion principle, it may override by 2/3 vote.

§10.1.gFederal Supplementation Obligation
§10.4.aMA Director — Candidate Register
§10.2Taxation Authority

Taxation authority originates exclusively in the House — no tax bill may originate in the Senate. The Fiscal Objection mechanism (§2.4.b) gives the PM a specialized check on tax legislation distinct from the ordinary suspensive veto. Within those constraints, Parliament has broad authority to structure taxation as it sees fit — the constitution establishes the process, not the rates.

§10.2.aTax Origination and Fiscal Objection

Tax bills must originate in the House of the Republic. The PM may issue a Fiscal Objection — separate from the ordinary suspensive veto — on grounds that a bill creates fiscal obligations the Republic cannot meet without BNF evasion or unauthorized deficit. A Fiscal Objection returns the bill with a written fiscal impact statement and requires the House to address the identified concern before re-passage.

§10.2.bAnnual Budget and Continuation Spending

Parliament must pass an annual budget. Where Parliament fails to pass a budget before the start of the fiscal year, a Continuation Spending Order automatically takes effect — maintaining prior-year spending levels at the BNF-adjusted rate for a period Parliament defines by statute. The Continuation Order prevents budget disagreements from becoming constitutional crises: the Republic continues to function while Parliament resolves its differences.

§10.3Deficit Controls

The Republic may not spend beyond projected revenue in any fiscal year without a 3/4 vote of both chambers simultaneously — the same extraordinary supermajority required for no-cause IG removal. This is a deliberate constitutional barrier to deficit spending. The BNF and Severe Contraction spending are explicitly exempted from this calculation — constitutional obligations that precede fiscal limits.

§10.3.aInherited Deficit Transition Threshold

During any fiscal year in which the Republic's debt-to-GNR ratio exceeds the Inherited Deficit Threshold the MA certifies, Parliament may run a controlled deficit by 2/3 vote rather than 3/4, provided the ratio is declining. This transitional provision prevents the new Republic from being immediately paralyzed by fiscal obligations inherited from its predecessor government.

§10.3.bBNF and Contraction Exemptions

The Basic Needs Floor and Severe Contraction supplementation obligations are exempted from deficit calculations during their respective activation periods. Constitutional fiscal obligations take precedence over constitutional fiscal limits — the hierarchy is explicit rather than left to judicial interpretation.

The PM maintains a running Candidate Register of qualified MA Director candidates — pre-vetted individuals who can take office immediately if a vacancy occurs.

§10.4.bMA Independence

The Monetary Authority operates with constitutional independence from both executives. Neither may direct its certifications, modify its findings, or remove its Director except through the constitutional process.

§10.4.cTransparency

The MA must publish all decisions, models, and revenue assessments to the NRS in real time — including the models it uses to calculate GNR. The transparency mandate makes the MA's methodology auditable by anyone, closing the black-box fiscal assessment pathway.

§10.4.dMethodology Integrity

The MA's calculation methodology must use a single, consistent definition of GNR for all constitutional formula applications. Parliament cannot define GNR differently for BNF calculations than for deficit calculations than for supplementation triggers. Formula manipulation through definitional fragmentation is constitutionally prohibited.

§10.5.aOfficial Benefits

The Republic provides standardized housing for officials in session — no luxury differential, no official residences that become perks of office. Official time and official resources are for official purposes.

§10.5.bFinancial Disclosure

Every elected and appointed official must file a complete public financial disclosure within 30 days of taking office and annually thereafter. Disclosures are published to the NRS within 7 days of filing. Failure to file on time, material omission, or knowing misstatement is a Rule Violation, material omission, or knowing misstatement is a Rule Violation.

§10.5.cIntegrity Bans

Officials are subject to a post-service lobbying ban of not less than five years. Parliament may extend the ban but may not reduce it below five years. The minimum is constitutionally entrenched to prevent future parliaments from eroding the revolving-door restriction under lobbying pressure.

§10.5.dAnti-Ego Provision

No public works, buildings, programs, or infrastructure may be named after any living official or any person who held public office within a recent period defined by House statute. Exception: officials who die in service to the Republic may be honored by a...

§10.5.hOath of Office Requirement

Every official of the Federated Republic — elected, appointed, or career — must swear or affirm the constitutional oath before exercising any official function. The oath is a constitutional requirement, not a ceremony: an official who has not taken the oath holds no lawful authority.

§10.6.aJury Duty

Citizens must serve on juries when called, compensated at the national median daily wage. Jury service is the one form of compelled service the constitution permits — §1.3's prohibition on forced labor explicitly carves it out. Compensation prevents jury service from being an economic hardship concentrated on those who cannot afford to refuse.

Article XI — Direct Democracy

§11.1 through §11.2 · Citizen petition and subsidiarity
§11.1Optional Referendum

Citizens may petition for an Optional Referendum on any enacted statute. Parliament may repeal before the referendum; if not, the result is binding.

§11.1.aCitizen Petition Right

Citizens may pause the implementation of any newly enacted law by submitting verified signatures of 5% of eligible voters within 90 days of enactment. The 5% threshold requires genuine popular mobilization. The pause goes into effect immediately upon certification, preventing the law from being entrenched during the referendum period.

§11.1.bEligibility Screen

Before certification, the Judicial Inspectorate screens the petition for eligibility — ensuring the law is referendum-eligible, that the signatures are genuine, and that the petition was filed within the window. The screening protects against procedural abuse without making certification a substantive barrier.

§11.1.cReferendum Thresholds and Finality

A referendum requires 50% participation to be valid — the participation floor ensures the result represents genuine democratic engagement, not a mobilized minority. A simple majority affirmative vote overturns the law. Parliament may not re-enact substantially identical legislation within two years of a referendum result.

§11.2.aSubsidiarity Principle

Government makes decisions at the lowest level of governance capable of effectively addressing the matter. The subsidiarity principle is justiciable.

§11.2.bLocal Government

Local governments — cities, counties, municipalities, and other subdivisions — receive constitutional recognition as the primary points of contact between citizens and government. They are entitled to reasonable fiscal capacity to carry out their constitutional functions. States may not starve local governments to achieve de facto state centralization.

Article XII — National Defense

§12.1 through §12.5 · Civilian command, military authority, and accountability
§12.2Parliamentary Declaration of Defense

Military authorization operates on three tiers. Tier 1 (immediate defensive response) requires no prior approval but must be notified within 6 hours and authorized by the SASC within 72. Tier 2 (Senate Armed Services Committee) covers offensive operations and transitional deployments. Tier 3 (Parliamentary Declaration of Defense, 2/3 of both chambers) covers all operations beyond 30 days. No executive can skip a tier by claiming the operational context of a lower one.

§12.2.aParliamentary Declaration of Defense

A Parliamentary Declaration of Defense requires a 2/3 concurrent majority of both chambers simultaneously — one of the highest thresholds in the document. The concurrent requirement means the House and Senate must vote on the same Declaration; sequential approval where one chamber can walk back the other's commitment is not available.

§12.2.bFive-Year Maximum

A Parliamentary Declaration of Defense may be renewed, but after 50 years of continuous Declaration (ten 5-year periods), no further renewal is permitted without a new strategic authorization. Each renewal requires a full Declaration vote — it is not automatic. Forever-war is constitutionally impossible.

§12.2.dSenate Armed Services Committee

The Senate Armed Services Committee exercises Tier 2 military authorization. SASC authorization must happen within 72 hours of presidential notification. When the presidential succession line is active, the SASC Chairman is the first civilian authority to whom military commanders report.

§12.2.eMilitary Authority During Presidential Succession Vacuum

When the presidential succession line is fully exhausted, the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs may continue operations already underway and issue orders strictly necessary to protect Republic forces from active, immediate attack — return fire and immediate defensive maneuvering only. No initiation, repositioning, or escalation. All orders published to the NRS within one hour. Maximum 14 days.

§12.1Authorized Purposes for Military Force

The Republic authorizes military force for three and only three purposes: defense of the Republic's territory from armed attack, protection of Republic citizens abroad facing imminent threat, and fulfillment of ratified mutual defense treaty obligations. Nation-building, resource extraction, ideological projection, and preemptive offensive war are not constitutionally authorized. A military deployment for any other purpose is unconstitutional regardless of any executive or parliamentary authorization.

§12.4Military Accountability

Extended occupations — deployments lasting more than 10 years without Senate-approved renewal — are unconstitutional and must terminate within 90 days. Inhabitants of any territory under Republic military occupation for more than one year may petition to join the Republic through the §13.6 voluntary incorporation process. The Republic may not use the prospect of Territory status as an inducement to consent to or extend a military occupation.

§12.5.aTreaty Emergency Certification

When a ratified mutual defense treaty is triggered by an attack on a treaty partner, the PM may issue a Treaty Emergency Certification within 48 hours. The PM's certification initiates the parliamentary authorization process; it does not authorize military action by itself.

§12.5.cNo Blank Check

The Treaty Emergency Certification covers operational costs for the initial response and triggers parliamentary authorization for any extended engagement. Treaties cannot be blank checks for extended military commitment for extended military commitment — each sustained engagement requires parliamentary authorization regardless of what the treaty says.

Article XIII — National Expansion and State Devolution

§13.1 through §13.4 · Territory, statehood, and the devolution architecture
§13.4Voluntary Devolution

A State may choose to become a Territory through a voluntary devolution referendum requiring a two-thirds vote of residents with a minimum 10-year Citizen Voting Credential. The constitutional pathway runs inward as well as outward — the threshold is two-thirds, consistent with other significant constitutional decisions throughout the document.

§13.4.bFiscal Transparency Requirement

Before a voluntary devolution vote, the MA must publish a Fiscal Incidence Study showing what the State currently pays and receives from the federal system, and what it would pay and receive as a Territory. Citizens vote on the basis of accurate information about the economic consequences.

§13.4.cNet-Contributor Transparency

Where the Fiscal Incidence Study finds the State is a net contributor to the federal system, that finding must appear in plain language on the ballot itself. Citizens choosing voluntary devolution must be explicitly informed if they are choosing to give up net federal fiscal benefits.

§13.5Associated Community Relationships

Associated Community status — the constitutional framework governing compact relationships with sovereign entities — is governed exclusively by Article XX. Article XIII governs inward relationships (Territories and States); Article XX governs outward relationships (sovereign nations in compact). The two frameworks are deliberately separate.

§13.6Voluntary Incorporation

The inbound pathway the document previously lacked — a sovereign entity voluntarily joining the Republic's constitutional framework as a Territory. Requires 2/3 legislature, confirmatory referendum, JI assessment, and 2/3 parliamentary acceptance.

§13.2The Statehood Audit

Territories become States by passing the Statehood Audit — a comprehensive assessment by the three Inspectorates covering fiscal integrity, legal synchronization with the Individual Sovereignty Floor, democratic legitimacy, institutional capacity, and NRS connectivity. The audit is not a political vote; it is a constitutional compliance assessment that produces a factual finding, not a judgment call. Statehood is earned by meeting constitutional standards, not granted by political favor.

§13.2.cThe Provisional Membership Period

Upon passing the Statehood Audit, a territory enters a Provisional Membership Period during which it sends non-voting observers to both chambers. House seats are allocated by population; Senate seats activate at full Statehood. The Provisional Membership Period creates a structured transition rather than an immediate jump to full participation.

§13.2.eMinimum Democratic Legitimacy

Three functional requirements every State must maintain: electoral accountability, judicial independence, and public law governance. Both EC technical certification and LI political assessment are required.

§13.2.fTerritorial Minimum Institutional Floor

Every Territory must maintain: (1) a governance council with defined membership and published authority; (2) resident access to federal judicial review of executive action; and (3) direct enforcement of Article I rights for all Territorial residents by...

§13.2.gConflict-Affected State Provisional Status

Protects States in genuine crisis without sheltering States whose governance created the crisis. Joint Inspectorate assessment, five-year maximum, and default joint administration close exploitable pathways.

§13.3Mandatory Devolution

Mandatory State Devolution is the constitutional consequence of sustained audit failure — three consecutive years of failing the Statehood Audit trigger a Senate Motion of Devolution. The Early Warning system (§13.3.b) provides two years of remediation before Devolution is considered. The 3/4 Senate supermajority requirement for Devolution ensures it cannot be used as a political weapon. The path back to full Statehood is always available.

§13.3.bEarly Warning — Years 1 and 2

Upon a first consecutive audit failure, the LI publishes a State Remediation Plan — a specific, actionable set of requirements for restoring compliance. The State has 24 months to implement before the next audit. Early Warning is the document's anti-failure mechanism: most States that enter it never reach Devolution because the plan is effective.

§13.3.cDevolution Vote — Year 3

Upon the third consecutive audit failure, the Senate votes on a Motion of Devolution requiring a 3/4 supermajority. The high threshold means Mandatory Devolution requires near-consensus in the States' chamber — it cannot be used as a political weapon against a State by a partisan majority.

§13.3.dEffect of Mandatory Devolution

A devolved State loses its Senate seats on the date the Devolution Motion passes. It retains House seats proportional to population. Federal law continues to apply. The State government continues as territorial administration under federal oversight. All effects are published to the NRS the moment the Motion passes.

Article XIV — The Indigenous Nation Compact

§14.1 through §14.5 · Indigenous sovereignty and rights
§14.5.dPre-Existing Sovereignty Pathway

An indigenous nation whose sovereignty is constitutionally recognized under Article XIV may petition for Associated Community status under Article XX without completing the independence process. The pathway acknowledges that recognized sovereign nations need not gain formal independence they already hold in constitutional terms — their sovereignty exists independently of Republic recognition.

§14.3.aFree, Prior, and Informed Consent

The Republic may not take any action affecting indigenous lands or sovereignty without free, prior, and informed consent from the affected indigenous nation.

§14.3.bUnamendable Rights Override

The FPIC requirement does not apply to enforcement of the six permanently unamendable provisions within indigenous jurisdictions. These six rights can be enforced without indigenous consent — they represent the floor below which even negotiated sovereignty cannot go. The Republic can enforce freedom from torture and non-refoulement regardless of any compact.

§14.3.cEmergency Rights Writ
§14.1Acknowledgment of Prior Sovereignty
§14.4.aEminent Domain Prohibition

The Republic may not acquire indigenous lands by eminent domain or any other mechanism without the voluntary consent of the indigenous nation, expressed through a free democratic act following full disclosure of purpose, scope, and compensation. No national security exception, no emergency exception. Indigenous land acquisition requires genuine consent, period.

§14.4.bConservation Trust

The Republic may not designate indigenous lands as National Trust or conservation lands without the indigenous nation's consent. Conservation designation cannot be used to effectively dispossess an indigenous nation of management authority while leaving nominal ownership intact.

§14.5.aArticle I Rights

Every individual member of an indigenous nation is an Inhabitant of the Republic and holds the full protections of Article I, enforceable directly by federal courts regardless of the nation's internal governance structure. Indigenous nations hold collective sovereignty; their members hold individual constitutional rights. The two coexist.

§14.5.bDual Citizenship

Members of indigenous nations may hold both indigenous national citizenship and Republic citizenship. Dual citizenship acknowledges that constitutional membership in the Republic and membership in an indigenous nation are not mutually exclusive — they are parallel, complementary forms of belonging.

The Republic acknowledges that indigenous nations held sovereign authority over their ancestral lands before the formation of the Republic — this acknowledgment is not a grant of sovereignty but a recognition of sovereignty that already exists. Sovereignty recognized cannot be revoked by the recognizing party.

§14.2Treaty Supremacy and Continuity

All treaties entered into by predecessor governments with indigenous nations are binding obligations of the Republic. Treaty rights cannot be diminished by legislation — they must be honored, renegotiated with consent, or formally superseded through the FPIC framework.

Any individual within an indigenous jurisdiction alleging an Article I violation may petition the Supreme Court for an emergency Rights Writ — overriding compact provisions to protect the individual.

Article XV — Amending the Constitution

§15.1 through §15.6 · Amendment paths, independence framework, and the unamendable floor
§15.1Two Amendment Paths

Three amendment paths, each calibrated to the nature and significance of the change. §15.1.a Parliamentary alone (3/4 of both chambers — for technical corrections); §15.1.b Parliamentary with popular confirmation (2/3 of both chambers plus 55% national vote / 55% participation — for significant changes); §15.1.c Citizen-Led (70% national vote / 65% participation — for when citizens wish to lead change Parliament will not). The State Convention path was replaced with a national popular vote option.

§15.1.aParliamentary Path

Ratification by a three-quarters supermajority of the full seated membership of both chambers simultaneously. Individual voting records published to the NRS within 24 hours. Appropriate for technical corrections, gap-filling, and amendments with broad cross-party consensus.

§15.1.bParliamentary and Popular Path

A two-thirds supermajority of both chambers, followed by a national popular vote achieving 55% affirmative support with 55% participation. Parliament leads; the people confirm. The popular vote must be held within 180 days of the parliamentary vote. For significant constitutional changes where democratic mandate beyond Parliament matters.

§15.1.cCitizen-Led Path

A national vote achieving 70% affirmative support with 65% participation. The highest threshold — for constitutional changes the people wish to make when Parliament will not act. The Electoral Commission certifies the result; any person with standing may challenge it before the Supreme Court within 30 days.

§15.3.aPermanently Unamendable Provisions

Six provisions are permanently unamendable: the prohibitions on torture (§1.2), slavery (§1.3), and non-refoulement (§1.27); habeas corpus (§1.13); the right to a public trial (§1.17); and the prohibition on retroactive punishment (§1.18). These may not be altered, qualified, or removed by any amendment process by any amendment process.

§15.4.aTerritorial Integrity

The territorial integrity of the Republic is not subject to unilateral dissolution. A State may not simply declare independence — the pathway requires: an advisory referendum, then a negotiated Framework Agreement, then formal independence under §15.5. The structured process prevents both secessionist capture and federal suppression of genuine independence movements.

§15.4.bAdvisory Referenda

The National Trust.

Design Rationale
What "materially more difficult" means

The US Constitution does not contain an explicit right to vote in its original seven articles — suffrage protection was added through the Fifteenth, Nineteenth, Twenty-Fourth, and Twenty-Sixth Amendments over 150 years, each addressing a specific exclusion rather than stating the right affirmatively. This document states it directly in Article I: every citizen has the right to vote. The standard — "materially more difficult" — is deliberately imprecise, a standard for courts to apply to facts rather than a bright line that can be gamed.

Historical examples that qualify: poll taxes, costly ID requirements, polling location consolidation that dramatically increases travel distance, registration barriers designed to exclude. The "materially" threshold is set below "significantly" — the provision protects against cumulative burden as well as single dramatic acts.

§1.19 · Article I
Design Rationale
Why candidacy tracks the vote right

Most constitutional rights instruments bundle the right to vote with the right to stand for election — separating them creates a gap that has been historically exploited. Requirements that allow someone to vote but prohibit them from running — wealth requirements, property qualifications, prohibitive signature thresholds — are a form of partial disenfranchisement.

The "unless this Constitution specifies otherwise" carve-out covers legitimate restrictions: age requirements, residency requirements, the prohibition on multiple simultaneous offices. These are narrowly specified elsewhere.

§1.19 · Article I
Design Rationale
Why the Constitution addresses emergencies explicitly

Constitutions that don't address emergencies don't prevent emergency powers — they ensure those powers are improvised. When a real emergency arrives and the constitution has nothing to say, governments act, courts improvise, and the improvisation tends to normalize permanently.

The §1.25 framework is specifically designed to protect rights during emergencies. The scope limitation mechanism — a Declaration can only limit the rights it specifically names — proved more significant in practice than the non-derogable list, because most emergency overreach involves assuming powers the Declaration didn't explicitly claim.

§1.25.a · Article I
Design Rationale
Why the Declaration requires both executives

§1.25 is not a disaster response mechanism — it is a rights-suspension mechanism. The President can deploy resources under Tier 1 authority. The PM can direct domestic emergency management under §2.2.e. Neither needs §1.25 to respond to a crisis — only to temporarily limit a constitutional right.

A Prime Minister who won't co-sign is telling you something important: a senior elected official with democratic legitimacy looks at the same situation and doesn't see a genuine emergency requiring rights suspension. That friction is valuable. The history of democratic backsliding almost always begins with one person declaring an emergency and acting on it alone.

§1.25.a · Article I
Design Rationale
Why these five rights are non-derogable

The five non-derogable rights were chosen because their violation is what distinguishes a government from a tyranny, regardless of emergency circumstances. A government that tortures, enslaves, disappears people without judicial review, tries them in secret, or punishes them retroactively has become something other than a constitutional republic — regardless of what crisis it claims justifies these acts.

The five were expanded from an original three (torture, slavery, habeas corpus) in the final pre-ratification revision to include public trial and retroactive laws. The logic: if these rights cannot be suspended during an emergency, they cannot be amended away during peacetime. Both the emergency list and the amendment list are aligned.

§1.25.b · §15.3.a
Design Rationale
The anti-normalization provision

Temporary emergency measures have a structural tendency to survive their emergencies. The mechanism is well-documented: the measure is enacted under crisis conditions; the crisis recedes; the measure remains because it is institutionally embedded; it is eventually made permanent by ordinary statute. "Temporary" becomes the permanent baseline.

§1.25.e interrupts this mechanism. A measure Parliament wants to keep must be re-enacted through the full legislative process as ordinary statute — deliberate, public, and subject to all normal constitutional constraints. It cannot slide from emergency authorization to permanent law without passing through that filter.

§1.25.e · Article I
Design Rationale
Why a single six-year term

Article II of the US Constitution, as modified by the Twenty-Second Amendment, allows a President to serve two four-year terms — a design that retains democratic accountability through re-election while limiting total tenure. This document makes a different choice: a single non-renewable six-year term removes the re-election incentive entirely from presidential decision-making. Presidents who cannot run again have no electoral constituency to please, no donor base to maintain, no party apparatus to accommodate. The six-year length reflects the longer timeframe of the defense and diplomatic domain the President governs.

§2.1 · Article II
Design Rationale
Why the transfer is automatic

Constitutional transfers of power should not depend on the goodwill of the outgoing official. The NRS records the moment of transfer; credentials activate at noon on the prescribed date whether the outgoing President cooperates or not; no ceremonial act is required. The most dangerous moment in a democracy is not a contested election but a contested transfer — when the outgoing official retains physical control while legal authority has already shifted.

§2.1 · Article II
Design Rationale
Why Tier 1 requires no authorization

Tier 1 covers immediate defensive response — where delay costs lives and the strategic situation is unambiguous. The President acts; the Senate Armed Services Committee reviews within 7 days; the NRS records everything. The design deliberately limits Tier 1 to genuinely defensive actions. Offensive operations, extended deployments, and actions beyond the immediate defensive moment require Tier 2 authorization — even if they begin within hours of a Tier 1 event.

§2.1.a · Article II
Design Rationale
Why eight years maximum

The eight-year maximum protects against the institutional capture that extended tenure enables. A PM who has governed for a decade has appointed a significant fraction of the senior civil service, built relationships across every domestic domain, and developed informal influence that is difficult to audit. The reset provision allows former PMs to return, but requires a genuine interruption that disrupts accumulated informal influence networks.

§2.2.c · Article II
Design Rationale
Why the constructive no-confidence requires a named successor

In a standard Westminster no-confidence vote — as used in the UK and many parliamentary systems — a majority need only agree that the current government should fall. The constructive no-confidence, drawn from Germany's Basic Law Article 67, requires that the same majority simultaneously elect a named successor. The second requirement is dramatically harder to satisfy — it prevents governments being brought down without a viable alternative, converting the removal mechanism into a transfer mechanism.

§2.3.b · Article II
Design Rationale
Why the House size is constitutionally floored

The US House has had 435 members since 1929 — while the population has tripled. Each member now represents three times as many constituents. The Republic's constitution prevents this by mandating that the seat-to-citizen ratio cannot fall below 1 per 750,000. Parliament can move toward 1 per 500,000 — a more representative chamber — but it requires a published justification. The hard floor prevents political majorities from diluting their own accountability.

§3.1.a · Article III
Design Rationale
Why one bill, one subject

Omnibus legislation — bills bundling many unrelated subjects into a single vote — is one of the primary mechanisms through which unpopular provisions become law. A member who opposes a specific provision cannot vote against it without voting against everything bundled with it. The single-subject rule forces every provision to survive on its own merits. The LI reviews every bill before the Senate vote — a bill violating the rule is returned before enactment, not challenged after.

§3.1.c · Article III
Design Rationale
Why 2/3 for conviction

The 2/3 threshold requires genuine bipartisan consensus — not mere majority will. An official who can be removed by a simple Senate majority is vulnerable whenever the opposing party holds a Senate majority, which transforms impeachment from a constitutional remedy into a political tool. The suspension-not-removal approach during trial prevents the reputational damage of an impeachment charge from becoming de facto removal without the conviction threshold being met.

§3.5.c · Article III
Design Rationale
Why the Court is elected from a qualified pool

The design combines two accountability mechanisms: democratic accountability of election and the qualification filter of the Jurist Pool. Neither alone is sufficient. A purely elected court is vulnerable to unqualified justices winning on name recognition. A purely appointed court is vulnerable to political capture by whoever controls appointments. The combination ensures the public chooses from constitutionally qualified options, not an open field.

§4.2.a–c · Article IV
Design Rationale
Why the Emergency Panel exists

The dual executive's domain separation is constitutionally defined, but constitutional definitions require interpretation in specific cases — immigration crosses both domains, pandemics cross both domains, cyberattacks from foreign state actors cross both domains. The Emergency Panel provides fast constitutional resolution without requiring a full Court proceeding. The 48-hour timeline converts a potential crisis into a manageable constitutional question.

§4.2.d · Article IV
Design Rationale
Why the Court rules only on concrete cases

Advisory opinions — rulings on hypothetical or future legislation — politicize the court by making it a participant in legislative drafting. They produce rulings without the factual record a real case generates, and create precedent without the adversarial process that tests reasoning. The concrete-case requirement keeps the Court in its constitutional role rather than converting it into an advisory committee.

§4.3.a · Article IV
Design Rationale
Why citizenship cannot be revoked

Statelessness — having no citizenship anywhere — is one of the most severe conditions a government can impose. A stateless person has no government to whom they can appeal for protection, no passport, no recognized legal identity in any jurisdiction. The Republic closes this as an instrument of political punishment. A citizen who commits the most serious crimes faces the full force of criminal law — but is not made legally invisible.

§5.1.e · Article V
Design Rationale
Why the Senate owns the CLT standard

Citizenship determines who votes, which determines the composition of the House, which determines State representation. The States, through the Senate, have a direct constitutional interest in the citizenship knowledge threshold. A body with no accountability to States setting the standard for joining those States would be constitutionally misaligned.

The asymmetric threshold — simple majority to raise, 2/3 supermajority to lower — protects the standard from political erosion. A Senate majority can make the test harder when civic knowledge demands warrant it. No Senate majority can make it easier without broad consensus that doing so is constitutionally appropriate. The status quo maintenance provision closes the deadlock manipulation pathway: a Senate that cannot agree on a change does not force one by inaction.

§5.4.a · Article V
Design Rationale
The CLT Administrator and the Candidate Register

The CLT Administrator applies the same Candidate Register mechanism as the Monetary Authority Director and the RSA Director. The PM maintains a running pool of assessed candidates; the EI publishes factual assessments before any nomination; the Senate confirms. The mechanism is identical because the design problem is identical: an administrator whose independence matters is precisely the administrator a politically motivated PM might want to capture.

The 5-year non-renewable term removes the re-appointment incentive. The Senate confirmation gives the States a voice in who administers the test that determines citizenship. The LI's annual audit ensures the administration is consistent and the results are published permanently to the NRS.

§5.4.b · Article V
Design Rationale
Why the National Trust is constitutional rather than statutory

A statutory National Trust can be dissolved whenever political will shifts. A constitutional National Trust cannot. The natural environment held in common — federal lands, navigable waters, ecologically significant areas — is not a resource to be liquidated by political majorities. It is held in trust for citizens who have not yet been born. That inter-generational obligation belongs in the constitution, not in statute.

The reclassification threshold — national referendum plus parliamentary supermajority — means no government can permanently impair Trust lands without asking the people directly. The stewardship obligation creates an enforceable constitutional standard: the LI certifies annually whether Trust management meets it. The National Trust Administrator, operating under the same Candidate Register protections as other independent administrators, cannot be directed to certify convenient findings rather than accurate ones.

§17.4 · Article XVII
Design Rationale
Why immigration requires two gates

The dual gate distributes immigration authority between the level closest to the impact (States, which absorb new residents into their communities and labor markets) and the level responsible for national security and legal consistency (the federal government). State sponsorship without federal certification would allow States to circumvent national security requirements. Federal certification without State sponsorship would centralize decisions at the federal level, removing local community voice.

§6.1.a–b · Article VI
Design Rationale
Why ranked-choice for presidential elections

Plurality voting produces winners who may be opposed by more than half the voters, incentivizes strategic rather than sincere voting, and structurally advantages two-party dominance by punishing voters who support third candidates. Ranked-choice eliminates this dilemma: citizens vote their actual preferences. If their first choice is eliminated, their vote transfers to their second. The winner emerges with genuine majority support from the electorate's actual preference ordering.

§7.2.a · Article VII
Design Rationale
The 65/35 combined score

The combined score addresses two design tensions that appear in other electoral systems. The US Electoral College, established under Article II Section 1 and the Twelfth Amendment, allocates electors by state regardless of popular vote margin — producing geographic breadth but occasionally disconnecting the winner from the national popular preference. A pure national popular vote, by contrast, can produce winners who maximise support in densely populated regions while building no breadth of coalition across the republic. The 65/35 split weights national popular mandate more heavily — the national vote is the primary legitimacy source — while giving regional breadth enough weight to require genuinely broad coalitions.

§7.2.b · Article VII
Design Rationale
Why geographic breadth has constitutional value

A President governing a geographically diverse continental republic needs a mandate reflecting that diversity. A candidate who wins by maximizing support in coastal metropolitan areas while ignoring inland, rural, and mountain regions has not demonstrated the breadth of support that governing those regions requires. The combined score ensures this demonstration is a constitutional requirement, not an aspiration. It is an anti-capture mechanism — preventing the presidency from becoming the instrument of whichever concentrated populations can produce the largest raw vote totals.

§7.2.b–c · Article VII
Design Rationale
Why the Inspectorates have no enforcement power

An Inspectorate with enforcement power is an Inspectorate that governs. The moment an oversight body can compel action, it has become a fourth branch of government — unelected, with coercive authority, accountable to no democratic constituency. The information-only mandate is not a limitation — it is the design. The Inspectorates' power is the power of complete, permanent, public disclosure. A government that consistently acts in ways the Inspectorates document will face political consequences from an informed citizenry. That is stronger than enforcement authority — because it cannot be negotiated, settled, or suppressed.

§8.6.c · Article VIII
Design Rationale
The Eighth-Year Assessment

The eight-year cycle coincides with approximately two presidential terms — long enough to assess structural patterns rather than individual decisions, short enough to catch systematic problems before they become irreversible. The joint nature ensures that cross-institutional patterns are visible, not just siloed findings. The Assessment has no binding force by design. It is an institutional record — a constitutional mirror held up to the Republic itself.

§8.11 · Article VIII
Design Rationale
Why the NRS is the most important provision in the document

Most constitutional systems — including the US system under FOIA and the Administrative Procedure Act — address transparency through statute rather than constitutional structure, meaning transparency requirements can be narrowed by ordinary legislation. This document constitutionalizes the transparency infrastructure itself: the NRS is not a statute that can be amended away. Every accountability mechanism in the document depends on it — the Inspectorates' findings are only as credible as the records they examine, and the citizen's right to petition is only as meaningful as their access to the information they need to petition about.

§9.1 · Article IX
Design Rationale
The Candidate Register system

The Register solves an appointment problem that credential requirements alone cannot: constitutional bodies whose independence matters most are precisely the bodies where political actors have the strongest incentive to appoint loyalists. The Register forces a different dynamic. The EI publishes a public record of every person on the PM's list before any nomination. The PM's list is public. A PM who maintains a list of clearly unqualified candidates faces political exposure before a single nomination is made. The accountability is prospective, not retrospective.

§9.3 · §10.4.a
Design Rationale
Why the BNF is constitutionalized

Most constitutional democracies, including the United States, leave social spending to statutory determination — Congress or Parliament sets spending levels annually, and those levels change with political will. This document takes a different approach: a constitutionally guaranteed minimum of 5% of gross national revenue for basic needs — housing, nutrition, healthcare, and education — that cannot be reduced below the floor without constitutional amendment. The floor was chosen as a constitutionally defensible minimum: low enough to sustain even in a contracting economy, high enough to represent a meaningful commitment that the social compact will be honoured regardless of the political moment.

§10.1 · Article X
Design Rationale
The anti-evasion principle

Without this principle, a determined government could satisfy the BNF formula while delivering nothing. Redefine "gross national revenue" to exclude certain revenue streams. Redefine "housing provision" to exclude the most expensive cases. Apply the percentage to a narrower definition of the covered population. Each individual redefinition might survive legal challenge; their cumulative effect is to hollow out the BNF while nominally complying with the 5% formula. The MA's certification function exists specifically to detect this kind of structural evasion.

§10.1.a · Article X
Design Rationale
The MA Candidate Register

The MA Director certifies every number the constitution's fiscal commitments rest on. A director who produces convenient numbers rather than accurate ones can constitutionally hollow out the fiscal framework without anyone formally violating anything. The Register addresses this by making the PM's candidate pool public before nomination. A PM who maintains a pool of clearly unqualified candidates faces political accountability before a single nomination is made — the accountability is prospective, not retrospective.

§10.4.a · Article X
Design Rationale
Why the MA is fully independent

Fiscal policy is the primary mechanism through which governments erode constitutional commitments without formally violating them. If Parliament can redefine GNR, it can redefine the BNF out of existence while leaving the 5% formula intact on paper. If the executive can direct the MA, it can certify convenient numbers. The removal requirement — 2/3 supermajority plus a documented EI finding of dereliction — means a government that disagrees with the MA's numbers cannot remove the Director for that disagreement alone.

§10.4.b · Article X
Design Rationale
The Optional Referendum

The Optional Referendum gives citizens a constitutional check on parliamentary inaction as well as action. A Parliament that fails to enact a constitutionally mandated statute cannot simply ignore the obligation indefinitely. The referendum eligibility mechanism converts the mandate from an aspirational requirement into an enforceable one. The name "Optional" refers to the fact that the referendum is optional for citizens to trigger — it doesn't happen automatically. Citizens decide whether to activate the mechanism.

§11.1 · Article XI
Design Rationale
Subsidiarity — governing at the right level

Subsidiarity means decisions are made at the most local level capable of making them effectively. The federal government is not the default level of governance — it is the level of last resort, appropriate only for matters genuinely requiring national coordination. This addresses a recurring pattern in federal systems: the gradual expansion of federal authority into every corner of civic life, producing a government responsible for everything and accountable clearly for nothing.

§11.2.a · Article XI
Design Rationale
Why defense declarations expire

Open-ended authorizations for military force produce perpetual wars authorized by majorities that no longer exist, for purposes that have long since changed. A Declaration of Defense that doesn't expire requires continuous democratic reauthorization to remain legitimate. The escalating threshold for successive renewals — each renewal requires a higher majority than the previous — is the key design feature. A war that requires increasingly broad political consensus to continue faces genuine democratic scrutiny at each renewal rather than sliding forward on institutional momentum.

§12.2.b · Article XII
Design Rationale
Why voluntary devolution exists

The standard Statehood framework is designed for the average State. Some States have characteristics — small population, high per-capita income, strong net-contributor fiscal position — that make Territory status a better fit. The 60% threshold, the Fiscal Incidence Study, and the ballot transparency requirements ensure the decision is genuinely informed. A State that voluntarily devolves has made that choice with full information and a supermajority mandate.

§13.4 · Article XIII
Design Rationale
Free, Prior, and Informed Consent

FPIC is an internationally recognized standard developed through decades of international law and treaty practice. The Republic's version is among the strongest in any democratic constitution: consent withheld is final. The "free" requirement means no coercion or financial pressure. "Prior" means before the action is taken, not after. "Informed" means the nation has access to all relevant information about the proposed action's effects. All three are required; none substitutes for the others.

§14.3.a · Article XIV
Design Rationale
The Emergency Rights Writ

The Writ was restored to the constitution in its final revision after nearly being deleted as "too detailed for a founding document." The threshold consent question — the Court resolves whether FPIC covered the alleged action before ruling on the Writ — is the provision's most important feature. It prevents the Writ from becoming a general override of indigenous jurisdiction while ensuring individuals have a specific, time-limited enforcement pathway when their Article I rights are genuinely violated.

§14.3.c · Article XIV
Design Rationale
Why five rights are beyond amendment

The 2043 challenge confirmed: the amendment process physically does not reach permanently unamendable provisions. Parliament cannot propose such an amendment — it simply has no jurisdiction. The five were expanded from an original three (torture, slavery, habeas corpus) to include the right to a public trial and the prohibition on retroactive laws. The logic: if these rights cannot be suspended during an emergency, they cannot be amended away during peacetime. Both lists are aligned.

§15.3.a · §1.25.b
Design Rationale
Why the EC is elected, not appointed

An appointed Electoral Commission depends on the integrity of the appointing authority. An elected Electoral Commission has a democratic mandate independent of any other institution. The body that administers every election in the Republic must itself be the product of an election — the source of its authority must match the nature of its function. The qualification requirements ensure the election produces administrators rather than politicians.

§16.1–16.2 · Article XVI
Design Rationale
Why only natural persons can contribute

Political contribution rights attach to democratic participation. Democratic participation is a citizen right. Corporations are not citizens — they have no vote, no obligation of military service, no exposure to criminal punishment as a person. The extension of citizen political rights to non-citizen entities is a legal fiction the Republic constitutionally refuses. The US Supreme Court has interpreted the First Amendment as protecting corporate political expenditure as a form of protected speech — a position this document's framers examined and declined to adopt. The Republic's constitution closes the question explicitly: only natural persons may contribute, regardless of how campaign expenditure is characterised under speech doctrine.

§18.1.b · Article XVIII
Design Rationale
Aggregate attribution — closing the shell company gap

Without aggregate attribution, the natural persons-only requirement is circumventable through shell companies, subsidiaries, trusts, and intermediary entities. A billionaire who cannot personally contribute more than the statutory maximum can create many entities that each contribute the maximum — unless attribution rules attribute all those contributions to the controlling person. The attribution standard is designed to be technology-neutral and structure-neutral: however complex the intermediary structure, contributions of the network count against the controlling natural person's limit.

§18.3.c · Article XVIII
Design Rationale
Why the Republic assumes predecessor obligations

A constitutional transition that repudiates prior government obligations would destroy the Republic's international creditworthiness on day one. Foreign creditors who cannot rely on successor governments to honor debt obligations will not lend. The assumption of social insurance and veterans' obligations is both a moral commitment and a practical one: citizens who organized their lives around these programs deserve continuity. The BNF-takes-precedence provision ensures assumption of prior obligations cannot justify reducing basic needs below the constitutional floor.

§19.6 · Article XIX
Design Rationale
The transition bootstrapping problem

The Candidate Register system requires an incumbent PM who has been building a list over time. The first PM has no list. The EI has had no time to review anyone. But the MA needs to be constituted quickly — the inherited debt certification and GNR assessment are needed before the first budget cycle. The joint Inspectorate panel bootstraps this by building initial Registers during Phase 2, before the first PM takes office. The diaspora recognition — citizens abroad are domestic expertise for this purpose — ensures qualified candidates are available regardless of what prior institutional arrangements looked like.

§19.8 · Article XIX
Design Rationale
The Individual Sovereignty Floor — why it opens the document

Most constitutions open with an assertion of governmental authority — the state declares itself, establishes its institutions, and then grants rights to citizens. This document inverts that sequence. The first operative provision establishes that every person within the Republic's jurisdiction holds individual sovereignty as a prior fact — not a grant from government but a condition government must respect. The Republic's authority flows from citizens; it does not precede them.

The floor is absolute and non-negotiable. No emergency suspends it. No amendment may remove it — it is embedded in the structure of the unamendable provisions. A government that could eliminate individual sovereignty would not be amending this constitution. It would be replacing it.

§1.1 · Article I
Design Rationale
Why violations scale — the two-track accountability system

A Rule Violation architecture with only one outcome — suspension or removal — produces two bad results. Minor procedural errors become existential political crises, creating incentives to challenge every finding and litigate every violation to avoid the one outcome. Or the severity threshold is set so high that only the most egregious conduct triggers accountability, leaving systematic minor violations unaddressed.

The corrective order track solves this. Minor violations — procedural, incidental, no deliberate circumvention — are addressed through correction rather than removal. The official acknowledges the finding, corrects the conduct, and the record is closed. Major violations trigger suspension. The EI monitors both tracks. And critically, the pattern of minor violations can itself escalate to major — three corrective orders that individually would each close become, together, evidence of systematic disregard that the Court must assess.

§2.7 · Article II
Design Rationale
The Fiscal Incidence Study — informed democratic choice

Voluntary devolution is an irreversible constitutional choice for everyone in the State, including the roughly 35% who vote no. The document's commitment to honest governance runs directly into this decision. A State population voting on voluntary devolution without knowing whether they net contribute to or receive from federal redistribution is making a consequential choice on incomplete information.

The Fiscal Incidence Study requirement makes that information mandatory. The MA publishes the assessment. The net-contributor or net-receiver finding must appear on the ballot. Citizens who would gain substantially from devolution know it explicitly. Citizens who would lose federal distributions know that too. The design bets that accurate information produces better democratic outcomes than strategic information management — and requires that bet to be honored at the ballot.

§13.4.b · Article XIII
Design Rationale
Why two amendment paths rather than one

A single amendment path has a single vulnerability. The State Convention Path is vulnerable to State legislatures that block popular amendments for institutional reasons. The Direct Democracy Path is vulnerable to temporary popular movements that lack genuine geographic breadth.

The two paths protect against each other's failure mode. If State legislatures are captured by interests opposing a genuinely popular amendment, the Direct Democracy Path remains. If a concentrated popular majority seeks constitutional change that most of the Republic's geographic communities oppose, the State Convention Path requires that breadth. Neither path alone provides both protections. Together, they create a redundancy that makes constitutional change appropriately difficult without making it practically impossible.

§15.1 · Article XV
Design Rationale
Why Non-Refoulement belongs in Article I, not Article VI

The placement of §1.27 in Article I rather than Article VI (Immigration) is the most important structural decision in the asylum framework. Non-refoulement is not an immigration rule — it is a rights guarantee. It says: the Republic's prohibitions on torture and persecution do not stop at its borders; they follow wherever Republic authority reaches.

Placing it in Article I alongside §1.2 (prohibition on torture) and §1.3 (prohibition on slavery) makes the constitutional logic explicit. These three provisions together establish that the Republic cannot inflict, facilitate, or enable a specific class of extreme harms — and cannot outsource those harms to another government by sending someone to face them. The maritime jurisdiction extension closes the loophole every government that runs offshore processing schemes exploits: effective custody by Republic authorities, anywhere in the world, engages §1.27's prohibition.

The unamendable designation reflects the same logic. You cannot amend the prohibition on torture. You cannot amend the prohibition on slavery. Non-refoulement belongs in the same category — not because international law requires it (though it does) but because the Republic's own constitutional character requires it.

§1.27 · Article I
Design Rationale
Why the Asylum Court must sit under Article IV, not the executive

The US Immigration Court system's fundamental flaw is that it sits within the Department of Justice — the same executive branch that prosecutes removal cases. The result is a tribunal that looks like a court but functions as administrative processing. The presiding officers are executive employees. Their decisions can be appealed by the same attorney general who employs them. This is not judicial independence; it is judicial aesthetics applied to executive authority.

§6.4.c constitutionally requires the Asylum Court to sit within the Article IV judicial architecture. Parliament creates the court — Parliament specifies its procedures, composition, and integration with the existing court system — but Parliament cannot create it as an executive body. The constitutional floor is structural independence: judges with tenure protections, subject to Supreme Court appellate review, insulated from direction by either executive.

The domain split in asylum is clean: the President provides intelligence inputs about country conditions and individual security concerns. The PM implements tribunal decisions and manages support logistics. The Asylum Court decides. Neither executive decides whether someone gets asylum. This is what an asylum system looks like when it is actually designed to work.

§6.4.c · Article VI
Design Rationale
§13.6 — the gap the Supreme Court found

The document always had an independence pathway — how States and Territories move from internal to sovereignty. What it lacked was the inverse: a mechanism for a sovereign entity to voluntarily join the Republic's constitutional framework as a Territory. The document could send entities outward but had no structured process for bringing them inward.

The gap was identified in the 30-Year American Republic scenario when Guam — having achieved independence through §15.4-15.5 and spent a decade as an Associated Community — petitioned for Territory status. The Supreme Court correctly identified the gap rather than improvising a pathway by judicial interpretation: that is a constitutional amendment question, not a judicial one. The amendment process produced §13.6.

The design mirrors the independence process's democratic threshold logic in reverse: a high entry bar (2/3 legislature plus confirmatory referendum) reflecting the permanence of incorporation, JI pre-acceptance assessment to screen systematic Article I violators, and parliamentary acceptance with a mandatory public explanation if declined. The Republic cannot compel or incentivise initiation — voluntary incorporation must be an unencumbered democratic choice of the petitioning entity's own governance.

§13.6 · Article XIII

Bodily Autonomy as Constitutional Foundation

The right to refuse any medical procedure or biological sampling — without exception for declared emergencies — is one of the strongest individual sovereignty provisions in the document. It closes the emergency mandate pathway that governments have historically used to override individual medical decisions during public health crises. Combined with §1.9.c (encryption) and §1.10.a (home and digital privacy), this forms a three-part physical and digital sovereignty cluster that the document treats as foundational rather than contingent.

Data Ownership and the Behavioral Profile Prohibition

The prohibition on using government-collected data to build behavioral profiles of individuals is more specific and more significant than it might appear. It closes the surveillance capitalism pathway through government: agencies cannot collect data for a legitimate purpose and then use the incidentally collected data to model citizen behavior, predict political views, or create social credit-equivalent systems. The necessity limit (data may not be retained beyond the purpose for which it was collected) is the constitutional version of data minimization — a principle most digital rights frameworks have only achieved at the statutory level.

The Warrant Standard — Precision and NRS Verification

The warrant requirement for home and digital searches includes two features absent from most existing warrant frameworks: the requirement that the warrant specify the data target or physical space with precision (not merely identify a person or premises broadly), and the requirement that the warrant be verified on the NRS before execution — creating a public record of every search warrant issued. This closes the secret warrant pathway and makes the volume and targeting of state surveillance a matter of permanent public record, auditable by anyone.

Self-Incrimination and the Digital Extension

The right of silence is complete in this Constitution — silence may not be commented upon, treated as probative of guilt, or used to draw adverse inferences. The explicit extension to cryptographic keys in §1.9.c is constitutionally significant: it resolves the question that has divided legal systems worldwide about whether compelling a password or decryption key is compelled testimony against oneself. The Republic's answer is that it is — cryptographic keys are private property and compelling their disclosure is both a search (§1.10.a) and compelled self-incrimination (§1.16).

The Trial Penalty Prohibition

The prohibition on trial penalties — heavier sentences for exercising the right to trial than for entering a guilty plea — addresses one of the most significant structural distortions in most existing criminal justice systems. Where plea bargaining effectively prices the right to trial at the sentencing differential, defendants routinely plead guilty to offenses they might contest because the cost of losing at trial is prohibitively high. This provision makes that pricing mechanism constitutionally unavailable. It does not prohibit plea agreements; it prohibits using the sentencing differential as leverage to price the right to trial out of practical reach.

The Public Trial Requirement and Its Scope

The permanent unamendable right to a public trial covers both the proceeding and the verdict. A judge may close a minimal portion of a hearing where a specific witness's safety requires it — but even then, the verdict must be public. This is stronger than most existing public trial protections, which allow extensive in-camera proceedings and sealed proceedings. The combination of permanent unamendability and verdict-mandatory publicity makes secret conviction in the Republic constitutionally impossible.

Retroactivity and the Prohibition on Punishing What Was Legal

The prohibition on retroactive criminal laws and excessive sentences covers both criminalization (you cannot be convicted of something legal when you did it) and punishment (your sentence cannot exceed what was the maximum at the time of the offense). The double jeopardy protection in §1.18.b adds a cross-jurisdictional dimension unusual in federal systems: State court acquittal cannot be retried in federal court on the same facts. Both provisions are permanently unamendable — they appear on the same list as the torture prohibition and non-refoulement.

Electoral Competition as a Constitutional Value

The right to stand as a candidate is constitutionally coextensive with the right to vote — both are grounded in §1.19. This has implications for ballot access law that most existing constitutional frameworks leave open: Parliament cannot impose ballot access requirements so burdensome as to functionally limit competition to established parties, because doing so would abridge the candidacy right guaranteed here. Combined with the Electoral Finance provisions of Article XVIII (no corporate money, public funding available), this creates a constitutional framework that takes electoral competition seriously as a structural right rather than a formal one.

Marriage as a Universal Right — Structure and Effect

The right to marry or form a legally recognized partnership is extended to any two consenting adults regardless of sex, gender, or sexual orientation — and the Republic must recognize such partnerships for all legal purposes. The second sentence is as significant as the first: recognition for all legal purposes means inheritance, taxation, immigration sponsorship, medical decision-making, and every other domain where partnership status creates legal consequences. Parliament and States may not create hierarchies of recognition — a partnership that qualifies under this provision qualifies for everything.

Environmental Accountability as Enforceable Obligation

The Government Non-Harm principle makes environmental poisoning of communities a constitutional violation with the same legal character as any other Article I violation — subject to federal court enforcement and Inspectorate reporting. This is categorically different from most environmental rights provisions in existing constitutions, which are framed as state objectives or directive principles rather than justiciable individual rights. A community that can demonstrate knowing exposure to environmental hazards without the required disclosure, consultation, and mitigation has a constitutional claim, not merely a political grievance. The Environmental Stewardship provision (§1.22.b) adds a prospective obligation: ecological integrity is not merely a right to not be harmed, but an active government duty.

The Dual-Gate Architecture — Why Both Gates Matter

The dual-gate system's power lies precisely in its division: States sponsor (Gate 1) but cannot override federal standards; the federal government certifies (Gate 2) but cannot override State decisions not to sponsor. This creates a genuine federal immigration partnership rather than either pure federal control or pure state control. The practical result is that immigration into the Republic reflects both federal policy (who meets the constitutional standards) and State policy (which States want which kinds of residents). States that actively use Gate 1 can shape their demographic and economic composition in ways no existing US state can. States that barely use it defer more entirely to federal standards.

The Presidency and the 6-Hour Clock

The 6-hour notification requirement for Tier 1 military operations creates one of the few constitutional provisions that runs on a real-time clock. Every Tier 1 defensive action must be notified to Parliament within 6 hours — not 24 hours, not 3 days. This is a constitutionally embedded transparency obligation that applies even to genuine immediate defensive responses. The requirement cannot be waived for operational security: notification of the action is mandatory even if the specifics of the action remain classifiable. Combined with real-time NRS publication of all Tier 1 actions, this makes the Republic's military decision-making in genuine emergencies among the most transparently governed in any constitutional democracy.

The 48-Hour Habeas Rule — Closing the Processing Delay Loophole

The 48-hour ruling deadline on habeas petitions closes a specific gap present in most habeas frameworks: the ability to delay judicial review through procedural overload, calendar management, or administrative classification of detention as something other than judicial custody. A court that receives a habeas petition has 48 hours, full stop. If it fails to rule, the person is deemed unlawfully detained and must be released. This makes detention review self-executing: government inaction in the face of a habeas petition produces the same result as a court finding in the petitioner's favor.

FPIC — What Consent Actually Means

The most significant design choice in the FPIC framework is the anti-gaming provision: "a process that produces objection from the indigenous nation does not produce consent, regardless of the process's duration or the government's good faith." This forecloses the most common circumvention pathway in indigenous consultation frameworks worldwide — running an extended, good-faith-appearing consultation process until the indigenous party is exhausted or isolated, then characterizing continued disagreement as unreasonableness rather than ongoing objection. In the Republic's framework, the indigenous nation's objection ends the process. The government's remaining options are to modify the proposal, find an alternative, or accept that the action will not proceed.

Gate 1 Sponsorship as State Labor Policy

The ability to sponsor Legal Residents through Gate 1 gives states a policy lever with no equivalent in any existing federal system. States can actively shape who arrives — targeting sectors with documented labor shortages, geographic areas with specific needs, or skill profiles that serve the state's economic strategy. The federal government sets the floor standards that every sponsored resident must meet; states decide whether to sponsor at all, and on what terms above the federal floor. Varentis uses quarterly sector-specific targeting. Kailos uses sector partnership agreements with port and technology employers. Arveld does not use it substantially at all. These are genuine policy choices with genuine economic consequences — and the differences between states in Gate 1 posture will compound over decades.

Equal State Representation and Its Tensions

Two Senate seats per state is the Republic's version of the counter-majoritarian principle: States matter as States, not merely as population containers. Varentis (2.1 million people in 340 km²) and Arveld (3.1 million people across a vast landlocked territory) each hold two Senate seats. This is working exactly as intended — the Senate is the chamber where State interests as States register, regardless of population weight. The House is where population weight registers. The tension this creates is a feature of the constitutional design, not a bug: it forces federal legislation to achieve both popular majority support (House) and cross-State support (Senate), making it harder to enact federal law that benefits large population centers at the expense of smaller states.
