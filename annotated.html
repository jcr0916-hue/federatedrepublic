Article I — The Individual Sovereignty Floor

§1.1 through §1.27 · Rights, equality, and the absolute floor

Article I is a floor, not a ceiling. Rights enumerated here are minimums. Every power granted to government is a ceiling — what the Constitution does not grant, government does not have.

§1.0¶Right of Access to Courts

Self-executing right: every person may seek legal remedy for any rights violation. Court access cannot be made meaninglessly expensive through procedural costs. If the Legislature fails to fund legal aid, courts appoint counsel at public expense until a statutory framework exists — the self-executing default ensures the right is never purely nominal.

§1.1¶Individual Sovereignty Floor

Every person holds an individual sovereignty that government authority may limit only as this Constitution permits — and may never eliminate. No official, institution, or emergency suspends this floor.

§1.2¶Prohibition of Torture and Cruel Treatment

Absolute prohibition on torture, cruel treatment, and degrading punishment. No exception exists — not for national security, not for emergency, not for military operations. One of six permanently unamendable provisions under §15.3.a.

§1.3¶Prohibition of Slavery and Forced Labor

Slavery, forced labor, debt bondage, and compelled service are prohibited absolutely and in all forms. This provision is permanently unamendable (§15.3.a) — no future majority can restore any form of servitude through constitutional amendment, even a unanimous one. The unamendable status reflects the drafters' conclusion that some prohibitions define the minimum conditions of human dignity that democratic government exists to protect. A democracy that permits slavery has abolished the premise on which democratic legitimacy rests. The prohibition extends to all persons within the Republic's jurisdiction, not merely to citizens.

§1.4¶Freedom of Expression

No branch, agency, official, or instrumentality of the Republic or any State may abridge the freedom of speech, press, or assembly in any medium — physical or digital. §1.4.a prohibits censorship by proxy (government coercion of private entities to suppress speech); §1.4.b establishes separation of church and state and prohibits religious establishment by law.

§1.4.a¶Censorship by Proxy

The most operationally significant speech protection. Governments that cannot censor directly regularly achieve the same result by threatening, incentivizing, or coercing private platforms and institutions to suppress speech on their behalf. This provision closes that route: a government agent who pressures a private entity to remove speech commits the same constitutional violation as direct censorship — the proxy does not launder the act. The provision applies equally to digital and physical media. It also prohibits viewpoint-based conditions on government benefits, contracts, or licensing.

§1.4.b¶Separation of Church and State

No religion may be established by law, and public funds directed to religious organizations must comply with the equality requirements of §1.8. The separation of church and state is structural, not merely preferential.

§1.5¶Freedom of Thought, Conscience, and Religion

Every person has the right to hold, practice, change, or decline any belief and to manifest it through worship, observance, practice, and teaching. No person may be compelled to participate in any religious observance contrary to their beliefs. Free exercise does not authorize harm to others and creates no exemption from laws protecting other enumerated rights.

§1.6¶Freedom of Association

Every person has the right to freely associate with others for any lawful purpose — including political parties, trade unions, civic organizations, and professional associations. The State may not compel membership as a condition of employment or civic participation, nor prohibit any association solely on ideological grounds unless it advocates violent overthrow of this Constitution or the denial of enumerated rights.

§1.7¶Right to Petition

Every citizen has the right to petition any branch of government on any matter of public or personal concern. All petitions must receive a formal response within a Legislature-defined period. The subject and status of petitions are published to the National Record System; petitioner identity is withheld where the petitioner requests anonymity. The right includes the right to file formal complaints against government officials and agencies.

§1.8¶Equality and Non-Discrimination

Every person is equal before the law. No person may be discriminated against on the basis of the enumerated characteristics. Distinctions based on race, ethnicity, religion, sex, sexual orientation, gender identity, national origin, or disability are presumptively unconstitutional, requiring a compelling government purpose achievable by no less discriminatory means. Distinctions based on age or economic status require only rational connection to a legitimate purpose — the means-testing carve-out in this section is the primary application of this lower standard

§1.9¶Bodily Autonomy and Digital Privacy

Three protections: §1.9.a — absolute right to refuse any medical procedure or biological sampling; no mandate is permissible under any emergency without uncoerced revocable written consent. §1.9.b — individuals own their personal data, likeness, and biometric information; government data collection is subject to strict necessity limits and may not build behavioral profiles. §1.9.c — cryptographic keys are private property; no court or agency may compel their disclosure.

§1.9.a¶Physical Sovereignty

Every person has the absolute right to refuse any medical procedure or biological sampling. No mandate is permissible under any declared emergency under any declared emergency unless the individual provides uncoerced, revocable written consent.

§1.9.b¶Data Ownership

§1.9.c¶Encryption Protection

Cryptographic keys are private property. No court or agency may compel their disclosure. Absolute rule, consistent with §1.16's self-incrimination prohibition — closes the digital search-and-seizure loophole present in most existing constitutions.

§1.10¶Inviolability of the Home and Property Rights

§1.10.a extends "home" to all physical residences and digital spaces secured by private cryptographic keys; State access requires a precise warrant recorded on the NRS before execution; general warrants are absolutely prohibited. §1.10.b governs eminent domain: requires proof of absolute public necessity, 150% fair market value payment, NRS recording, and prohibits private-to-private transfer.

§1.10.a¶Home and Digital Privacy

'Home' extends to all physical residences and all digital spaces secured by private cryptographic keys. Access requires a warrant specifying the precise data target or physical space, verified on the NRS before execution. General warrants — broad or unspecified authorizations — are absolutely prohibited.

§1.10.b¶Eminent Domain

Seizure of private property requires proof of absolute public necessity, payment of 150% of fair market value, and NRS publication. Private-to-private transfer via eminent domain via eminent domain — using government condemnation to benefit another private party — is constitutionally prohibited.

§1.11¶Self-Defense and the Right to Bear Arms

The right to keep and bear arms for personal defense and hunting must not be infringed. States may establish proportionate licensing requirements (§1.11.b) but may not impose requirements that function as effective prohibitions on lawful ownership (§1.11.c). The burden of proof lies with the State to demonstrate any challenged requirement is proportionate, non-discriminatory, and genuinely serves a safety purpose.

§1.11.a¶Right to Bear Arms

The right to keep and bear arms for personal defense and hunting must not be infringed. The provision is deliberately narrow in scope — personal defense and hunting — which bounds the right without eliminating it.

§1.11.b¶State Licensing Latitude

States may establish licensing and safety training requirements as a condition of carrying or possessing firearms. States may regulate the exercise of the right but may not use regulation as a prohibition by another name.

§1.11.c¶Non-Prohibition Principle

No State requirement may function as an effective prohibition on keeping or bearing arms. A requirement is an effective prohibition if it imposes costs, delays, or conditions that make lawful ownership practically inaccessible to ordinary citizens. The burden of proof lies with the State to demonstrate proportionality.

§1.12¶Freedom of Movement and Labor

All Inhabitants have the right to travel and reside anywhere within the Republic. State professional licensing requirements may not create barriers to labor mobility; licenses issued under State law are recognized throughout the Republic. Every person has the right to form and join trade unions and bargain collectively; the right to strike as a collective work stoppage is protected, subject to reasonable regulation of essential services.

§1.13.a¶First Appearance

Any person taken into custody must appear before a judicial officer within 72 hoursMeasured from the moment of physical custody, not from formal arrest. Clock does not pause for weekends or public holidays. of arrest. At the First Appearance, the judge must determine whether custody is lawful and whether conditions of release are appropriate.

§1.13.b¶Habeas Corpus Response Deadline

A court receiving a habeas corpus petition must rule within 48 hours of filing. No procedural rule may extend this deadline. A court that fails to rule within 48 hours is deemed to have found the detention unlawful is deemed to have found the detention unlawful — the detained person must be released or brought before a judicial officer immediately a judicial officer immediately.

§1.13.c¶Habeas Corpus

Any person detained by the State has the absolute right to challenge the lawfulness of their confinement before a judge at any time. The right cannot be suspended by emergency declaration by emergency declaration — it appears in §1.25.b's list of absolutely non-derogable rights.

§1.14.a¶Right to Counsel

Any person accused of a crime or subject to detention has the right to retain and consult with a lawyer before questioning. If they cannot afford counsel, the Republic provides it. The right attaches from the moment of accusation or detention, not from formal charging.

§1.14.b¶Caseload Guarantee

the Legislature must establish maximum caseload limits for publicly funded defense — ensuring that the right to counsel means genuine preparation, not nominal presence. Public defenders must be resourced equivalently to prosecutorial offices handling comparable cases.

§1.14.c¶Right to Be Informed of Charges

Any person detained or accused must be informed promptly, in plain language they understand, of the specific grounds for their detention and the charges being considered. Detention without meaningful explanation of its basis is unconstitutional from its first moment.

§1.14.d¶Right to Full Disclosure

Any accused person has the right to full disclosure of all State evidence — including evidence the prosecution does not intend to use — before trial. Any withholding of exculpatory evidence is a constitutional violation, not a procedural irregularity.

§1.15.a¶Speedy Trial Principle

Every accused person has the right to trial without unreasonable delay. Delay caused by the State is not a neutral fact — prolonged pre-trial detention is itself a punishment imposed without verdict. What constitutes unreasonable delay is defined by the Legislature in statute, subject to judicial review.

§1.15.b¶Bail Proportionality

Pre-trial detention may only be ordered where the court makes a specific, individualized finding that the accused poses a genuine flight risk or documented danger to an identifiable person. Financial inability to post bail does not justify detention — bail conditions must be proportionate to what the accused can actually pay to what the accused can actually pay.

§1.15.c¶Protection from Trial Penalties

The right to trial may not be penalized. A defendant who exercises their right to trial may not receive a harsher sentence than they would have received by entering a guilty plea for the same offense. Sentencing 'trial penalties' are constitutionally prohibited.

§1.15.d¶Source Code Right

Any accused person has the right to inspect, challenge, and rebut any algorithm, automated system, statistical model, or raw dataset used as evidence or to generate a score influencing their case. Technology-opaque evidence against which no defense can be mounted is constitutionally inadmissible.

§1.16¶Right to Silence

No person may be compelled to testify against themselves or to produce self-incriminating evidence. The right of silence is complete — silence may not be commented upon or used to draw adverse inferences or used to draw adverse inferences. Consistent with §1.9.c, this extends to cryptographic keys.

§1.17¶Right to a Public Trial

Every accused person has the right to a public trial before a judge and, in criminal matters, a jury of peers. Secret or closed proceedings are constitutionally prohibited except for the minimal portion where a specific witness's safety requires it — and even then the verdict must be public. Also permanently unamendable.

§1.18.a¶No Retroactive Criminal Laws

No person may be convicted of an act that was not defined as a criminal offense at the time it was committed. No sentence may exceed the maximum available at the time of the offense. The prohibition on retroactive criminal law is absolute and permanently unamendable.

§1.18.b¶Double Jeopardy

No person may be tried or punished more than once for the same offense arising from the same factual events. Acquittal is final. This applies across all levels of the Republic's court system — a State court acquittal cannot be retried in a federal court on the same facts.

§1.19¶Right to Democratic Participation

No government action may make voting materially more difficult for any eligible citizen. This covers not only formal disenfranchisement but practical barriers: polling place closures, identification requirements that function as wealth or document-access tests, registration obstacles designed to suppress participation, and reductions in early or mail voting access. The provision is self-executing: a citizen facing a material barrier may bring a constitutional claim directly in court. The right to democratic participation is the meta-right on which all other constitutional rights depend — a citizenry unable to vote cannot protect any of the other rights this Article guarantees.

§1.20¶Right to Marry and Found a Family

The right to marry or form a legally recognized partnership belongs to any two consenting adults regardless of sex, gender, or sexual orientation. The Republic must recognize such partnerships for all legal purposes. The Legislature and States may not create hierarchies of partnership recognition based on the characteristics of the parties.

§1.21¶Rights of Children

Every child has the right to protection from exploitation, abuse, and neglect. In all State actions affecting minors, the welfare and best interests of the child are the primary consideration — not merely one factor to be weighed. The protection of children is the one area where the Individual Sovereignty Floor explicitly sets 'the primary' rather than 'a' standard.

§1.22¶Environmental Accountability

Article I's environmental provisions establish two constitutional obligations: Government Non-Harm (§1.22.a) prevents authorities from knowingly exposing communities to environmental hazards without disclosure, consultation, and mitigation; Environmental Stewardship (§1.22.b) creates an enforceable duty to maintain ecological integrity. Together they make environmental protection a constitutional obligation with Inspectorate reporting requirements, not merely a policy preference.

§1.22.a¶Government Non-Harm

No government authority may knowingly expose any community of Inhabitants to environmental hazards without full disclosure, meaningful consultation, and proportionate mitigation. The Government Non-Harm principle makes environmental poisoning of communities a a constitutional violation, not merely a policy failure.

§1.22.b¶Environmental Stewardship

The long-term stewardship of the Republic's natural environment is a constitutional obligation of the federal government — not aspirational language. It creates an enforceable duty to maintain ecological integrity that the Inspectorates are mandated to report on annually.

§1.23¶Federal Education Assistance

Three interlocking education provisions: §1.23.a requires the Republic to fund education assistance so no child's opportunity depends on their territory's wealth. §1.23.b requires public education to equip students to meet the Constitutional Logic Test standard — education for citizenship is a constitutional obligation. §1.23.c preserves parental choice: the Republic sets the floor; it may not mandate the method.

§1.23.a¶Federal Education Assistance

The Republic must provide adequate education assistance to States and Territories so that no child's educational opportunity is determined primarily by the wealth of their territory. Federal assistance must address the gap between what a jurisdiction can fund locally and what the constitutional literacy standard requires.

§1.23.b¶Constitutional Literacy

The Republic's constitutional literacy standard for citizenship purposes is the CLT, defined in §5.4. Public education must equip students to meet that standard — education for citizenship is a constitutional obligation, not a civic nicety.

§1.23.c¶Parental Right

Parents retain the right to educate children through alternative means — homeschooling, faith-based schools, alternative institutions — provided those alternatives meet the constitutional literacy standard. The Republic may set the floor; it may not mandate the method.

§1.24¶Housing Non-Exclusion

Article I's housing provisions create constitutional barriers to exclusion and a constitutional obligation to provide. The Non-Exclusion Principle (§1.24.a) prohibits exclusionary zoning and any regulatory mechanism designed to keep certain populations out. The BNF Housing Obligation (§1.24.b) anchors housing as a Basic Needs Floor category — persons who cannot independently secure housing are constitutionally entitled to it through the BNF system.

§1.24.a¶Non-Exclusion Principle

No government authority — federal, State, or local — may use regulatory or land-use powers to exclude any person from access to housing on the basis of any characteristic enumerated in §1.8. Exclusionary zoning designed to keep certain populations out of a community is constitutionally prohibited.

§1.24.b¶BNF Housing Obligation

The Republic's obligation to ensure housing access for persons who cannot independently secure it is part of the Basic Needs Floor under §10.1. This provision anchors housing as a BNF category and makes housing exclusion an enforceable constitutional violation.

§1.25.a¶Emergency Derogation — Declaration Requirements

Emergency derogation of Article I rights requires a precisely structured declaration specifying: the nature of the emergency, which rights are limited, the geographic scope, the duration, and the review mechanism. The specificity requirement prevents open-ended emergency licenses — the government must commit in advance to exactly what it is doing, for how long, and where. Blanket declarations are constitutionally invalid. The declaration is published to the NRS immediately: an undisclosed emergency is not a constitutionally recognized emergency. Automatic expiry (§1.25.d) without renewal ensures that even a properly declared emergency cannot become a permanent parallel constitutional order through inaction or extension by the same actor who declared it.

§1.25.b¶Non-Derogable Rights

These five rights are absolute under any circumstances: the prohibition on torture (§1.2); slavery (§1.3); habeas corpus (§1.13); the right to a public trial (§1.17); and no retroactive punishment (§1.18).

§1.25.c¶Inspectorate Continuity

The three Inspectorates continue to operate with full constitutional authority throughout any emergency — they cannot be suspended or have their mandate reduced by emergency declaration. Accountability infrastructure is precisely most important when emergency powers are active.

§1.25.d¶Automatic Expiry and Judicial Review

Emergency measures lapse automatically at the expiry of the Declaration. Judicial review of any emergency measure is available to any citizen at any time, and courts may rule during the emergency that a specific measure is unconstitutional.

§1.25.e¶No Permanent Emergency

No emergency measure may be made permanent by legislative act. Every emergency measure lapses automatically at the expiry of the Declaration unless re-enacted through the ordinary legislative process. The Legislature may not extend an emergency measure by re-declaration within 60 days of its expiry, or at all unless a 2/3 majority certifies continued necessity.

§1.26¶Associated Community Residents

Every person residing within any Associated Community operating under a compact with the Republic holds the full protections of Article I, enforceable directly by federal courts regardless of the Associated Community's internal governance structure. No compact provision may limit or qualify any Article I right for any resident of Associated Community territory.

Article II — The Dual Executive

§2.1 through §2.10 · Dual executive — authority, accountability, and succession

The Republic has two executives with constitutionally separated domains. The President holds defense and diplomacy. The Prime Minister holds domestic affairs. Neither may claim the other's domain.

§2.1¶The President — Term and Transfer

The President serves a single non-renewable six-year term — long enough to develop genuine foreign policy and defense continuity, not renewable enough to entrench. The non-renewability is absolute and self-executing: no action by the outgoing President or any other actor can extend the term. The President's domain is defense and foreign affairs; the PM's is domestic governance. This separation means the President cannot use military emergency to expand domestic authority, and the PM cannot use fiscal policy to constrain defense operations without Senate authorization. Each executive is supreme within their domain and constitutionally blocked from the other's.

§2.1.a¶Tier 1 — Immediate Defensive Response

Tier 1 covers immediate response to active, imminent, or ongoing attacks. The President must notify the Legislature within must notify the Legislature within 6 hours; the Senate Armed Services Committee must authorize within 72 hours for the operation to continue beyond immediate necessity. All Tier 1 actions are published to the NRS in real time.

§2.1.a–c¶Military Authority — Three Tiers

The Tier framework converts the open-ended question of presidential military authority into a structured decision tree with different authorization requirements at each level. Tier 1 (immediate defensive response) is presidential discretion with automatic 96-hour funding. Tier 2 (extended or offensive operations) requires Senate Armed Services Committee authorization within 96 hours. Tier 3 (legislative declaration) requires both chambers. The escalating authorization requirement reflects escalating gravity of commitment. Crucially, the fund-certification mechanism (§2.1.d) makes the Tiers real: uncertified spending is void and pay freezes follow — the civilian PM's domestic authority enforces military subordination through resource flows.

§2.1.b¶Tier 2 — Senate Armed Services Authorization

Offensive military operations or defensive deployments transitioning beyond immediate necessity require Senate Armed Services Committee authorization — Tier 2. The SASC must vote within 72 hours of notification. This democratic pause before offensive action is a deliberate design choice.

§2.1.c¶Tier 3 — Legislative Declaration of Defense

All military operations beyond 30 days require a Legislative Declaration of Defense — a 2/3 concurrent majority of both chambers. Once 30 days pass, the Legislature must affirmatively authorize continuation. No executive can use Tier 1 or Tier 2 authority to avoid Tier 3 by claiming continuity of an earlier operation.

§2.1.d¶Funding Lock

The President controls military operations but cannot spend domestic funds. All military expenditures require PM Certification of Funds. Tier 1 defensive operations trigger automatic 96-hour Emergency Fund Certification. This separates the command of force from the appropriation of money — no President can sustain a war without PM cooperation.

§2.1.e¶Privacy Firewall

Any order for domestic surveillance or intelligence activity directed at Republic citizens requires a judicial warrant. The Privacy Firewall separates national security authority from domestic intelligence operations — the President may not direct surveillance of domestic targets without independent judicial authorization.

§2.1.f¶Presidential Veto — Scope

The President may veto bills that raise a genuine constitutional concern within the presidential domain of defense and diplomacy. A veto is not a general legislative override — it must be grounded in a specific articulable constitutional justification, not policy disagreement. A void veto is simply void.

§2.1.f.i¶Judicial Review and Override

The Supreme Court reviews any veto challenge within 48 hours. A void veto permits the bill to proceed to enactment. A veto found to be in bad faith — issued as obstruction rather than genuine constitutional concern — may constitute a constitutional prohibition under Article VIII.

§2.1.g¶Presidential Clemency

The President may grant clemency for individuals convicted of offenses within the presidential domain. The Executive Inspectorate must publish a factual background report before any clemency is granted, making the basis permanently public. No self-clemency; no clemency for constitutional violations.

§2.2¶The Prime Minister — Domestic Executive Domain

The PM is responsible for domestic governance: legislation, civil service, domestic courts, public services, internal security, and the domestic economy. Separate from the President's national and foreign domain. The PM answers to the House through the constructive no-confidence mechanism. Six sub-provisions cover the PM's specific domestic authorities.

§2.1.h¶Dual Executive Conflict Resolution During Active Operations

When a single event implicates both executive domains simultaneously (e.g., a foreign attack on domestic infrastructure), both executives act within their respective domains concurrently. Neither may claim the whole event to displace the other. Dispute resolution goes to the Supreme Court on an emergency basis.

§2.1.i¶Presidential Officer Appointments and Removal

Complete lifecycle for senior Presidential domain officers (Director of Intelligence, Secretary for Foreign Affairs, Secretary for Defense). Appointment: President nominates, Senate confirms by simple majority, Deemed Confirmed if Senate fails to act. All appointments published to NRS within 24 hours. Removal: the President's removal authority is unilateral — a written Notice of Removal is published to the NRS and takes effect immediately upon publication. The Senate's role in appointments does not extend to tenure protection. Officers under active §3.5 proceedings may not be removed by the President, preventing the removal power from being used to obstruct a legislative accountability process. Amended v146: removed Senate denial mechanism and one-year bar.

§2.2.a¶The Record System Mandate

The PM must record all executive orders, contracts, and expenditures on the NRS within defined timeframes. Failure to record is itself a constitutional violation — makes the NRS mandate self-enforcing through the Inspectorate reporting mechanism through the Inspectorate reporting mechanism.

§2.2.b¶Duty of Refusal

Civil servants must refuse and report any order that lacks PM fund certification or would require unlawful action. The duty of refusal gives civil servants a constitutional shield — they cannot be disciplined for refusing an unlawful order, and the refusal is itself constitutionally protected action.

§2.2.c¶PM Maximum Consecutive Service

No person may serve as PM for more than 8 consecutive years. On the date a PM takes office, the Executive Inspectorate records the expiry date on the National Record System — publicly and immediately, so the term limit is visible from day one and cannot later be disputed. The 8-year limit prevents the entrenchment of a single PM across multiple electoral cycles while allowing enough continuity for a two-term government to pursue a coherent policy program. After the cooling-off period, the former PM is eligible again — the limit is consecutive service, not lifetime service, reflecting that democratic recycling of leadership is healthier than permanent disqualification of capable former officeholders.

§2.2.d¶Civil Service Integrity

The PM has constitutional responsibility for civil service integrity — merit-based appointment, politically neutral service, and protection for civil servants who refuse unlawful orders. Using hiring, firing, or assignment to reward or punish political loyalty is a constitutional violation.

§2.2.e¶Domestic Emergency Authority

The PM may declare a domestic state of emergency in response to natural disaster, industrial accident, or public health crisis — not political crisis. The Legislature must ratify within 30 days; the declaration lapses at 30 days without ratification. The 60-day re-declaration bar prevents continuous emergency through rolling declarations.

§2.2.f¶PM Clemency

PM grants clemency for domestic criminal law offenses. Requires EI factual background report before granting. Subject to §2.9.d cross-chamber review: House has 45 days to disapprove; silence is deemed approval; disapproval voids the grant with a one-year bar on resubmission. The PM's clemency authority is parallel to but separate from the President's domain.

§2.2.g¶Ministerial Appointments

The PM appoints and removes ministers within the Domestic Affairs Council. All ministerial appointments and removals are published to the NRS within 24 hours. Ministers exercise only authority delegated within the PM's constitutional domain — no minister may act outside that domain regardless of how the delegation is framed. All ministerial orders are subject to the same NRS recording requirement as PM orders; unrecorded ministerial orders are void. Civil servants hold the same duty of refusal with respect to ministerial orders as they do with PM orders. Ministers serve at the PM's pleasure; removal does not affect accountability for prior conduct.

§2.2.h¶Administrative Acting Officer Framework

Consolidates the escalating acting officer removal procedure that applies to all PM-nominated Senate-confirmed administrative positions. (1) PM removes freely within 90 days of acting assumption. (2) After 90 days, removal requires a simple Senate majority. (3) After 180 days, removal requires the full For-Cause threshold for that position. (4) Senate confirmation at any time converts acting status to permanent appointment with the full term beginning from confirmation. Applies to: CLT Administrator (§5.4.b), RSA Director (§9.3), National Trust Administrator (§17.4.g), and MA Board members (§10.4.a). The PM may not use acting status to indefinitely circumvent Senate confirmation — the duty to nominate and pursue confirmation is a continuous constitutional obligation.

§2.3.a¶Selection

The House selects the PM by absolute majority — a majority of all seated members, not just those present. A PM who cannot command a genuine working majority cannot be appointed, preventing minority government through strategic abstention.

§2.3.b¶Constructive Vote of No Confidence

The House can only remove the PM by simultaneously electing a successor — it cannot vote the PM out and leave a vacuum. This constructive requirement forces the House to demonstrate that its dissatisfaction is accompanied by a positive governing alternative. A parliamentary majority that can only oppose is not constitutionally empowered to govern. The requirement prevents the most common failure mode of parliamentary systems: a legislature that destroys without building, collapsing governments without bearing responsibility for what follows. The House majority that can sustain a PM is the governing majority; the opposition majority that cannot name a successor must remain in opposition.

§2.3.c¶Caretaker Protocol

Two scenarios: (1) PM vacancy mid-term — caretaker PM from existing Cabinet serves until House elects a replacement; (2) PM formation failure after an election — the outgoing PM stays on as caretaker for the statutory formation period. Neither scenario suspends the House or produces a constitutional gap.

§2.4.a¶Suspensive Veto

PM returns a bill with a written Statement of Objection — suspending it for one full legislative session. The House overrides by absolute majority at the next session; failure to vote within that session lapses the bill. May not be used on the annual budget, constitutionally mandated bills, or §2.8 emergency measures. One use per bill. Not a true veto: the Legislature always has the final word.

§2.4.b¶Fiscal Objection

PM issues a Fiscal Objection on grounds of fiscal solvency or constitutional fiscal compliance — requires a written Fiscal Impact Statement and an independent MA assessment, both on the NRS. House overrides by simple majority, formally accepting the stated fiscal risk. One tool per bill: a Fiscal Objection and a suspensive veto may not both be applied to the same bill.

§2.4.c¶Objection Limits

The suspensive veto is a deliberation interval — it forces the Legislature to recommit to legislation, not a mechanism for converting PM policy preferences into law. Any objection without the required written statement is void. The PM's objection power is procedurally constrained to prevent abuse as a stalling mechanism.

§2.4.a–b¶Suspensive Veto and Fiscal Objection

The Prime Minister may return any bill to the House with a written Statement of Objection specifying the grounds — constitutional, fiscal, operational, or policy. The Statement is recorded permanently on the National Record System.

§2.5.a¶Emergency Legislative Continuity

If the House cannot convene during the presidential review period due to a declared catastrophic emergency, the Senate may exercise the House's response functions on an emergency basis for that specific review period only. Constitutional deadlines do not lapse because of physical inability to convene.

§2.6¶Presidential Succession

If the President dies, is permanently incapacitated, resigns, or is removed, the constitutional succession line is: Senate Speaker → Senate Deputy Speaker → most senior Armed Services Committee member. The PM is deliberately not in the presidential succession line — preventing the domestic executive from gaining presidential authority through the death or incapacity of the external executive.

§2.6.a¶Presidential Succession Line

Succession order: (1) Senate Speaker, (2) Senate Deputy Speaker, (3) most senior Senator. The succession line does not include the PM — maintaining the separation of executive domains. The sitting SC is also excluded from succession (§4.2), preventing judicial self-selection into executive power.

§2.6.b¶Incapacity Certification

The PM has a potential political interest in certifying presidential incapacity. This provision acknowledges the conflict and requires the PM to recuse from incapacity certification where a conflict exists, with the process moving to the Supreme Court.

§2.6.c¶Succession-Based Presidential Term

A succession-based President must stand for election at the next regular National Election Day or in a special election called within 90 days. Succession is an emergency holding mechanism, not a full term grant — democratic authorization must follow.

The Emergency Governance Protocol addresses simultaneous loss of both executives and other catastrophic continuity scenarios where the normal succession mechanisms under §2.6 cannot function. §2.9.b¶Legislative Amnesty

Broad amnesty — remission of criminal liability for a defined group — requires democratic authorization by simple majority statute. Neither executive may grant group amnesty unilaterally. Mass clemency decisions affecting many people warrant legislative, not executive, authorization.

§2.9.c¶Self-Clemency Absolute Prohibition

No elected or appointed official of the Republic may grant clemency to themselves, to a co-defendant in any matter involving their own conduct, or to any person whose conviction arose from their own acts while in office. Self-clemency is absolutely prohibited.

§2.9.d¶Cross-Chamber Clemency Review

Cross-chamber clemency review added in v137. Senate reviews PM grants; House reviews Presidential grants. Executive files written explanatory report within 7 days of any grant. EI reviews for factual accuracy and publishes to NRS within 14 days. Reviewing chamber has 45 days to disapprove by simple majority; no action = deemed approved. Disapproval = grant void + one-year resubmission bar.

§2.10¶Presidential Removal — Two Political Tracks

Two political removal paths independent of the Article VIII Inspectorate reporting and political accountability. These address situations where the President has not committed a technical constitutional violation but has lost democratic confidence. Both require either cross-institutional consensus or direct citizen participation.

§2.10.a¶Legislative Removal

2/3 of full House membership to initiate — a Statement of Grounds specifying the basis for removal accompanies the vote and defines the subject of the Senate trial. 2/3 of full Senate membership to convict and remove. Both thresholds are of full membership, not those present. Trial proceeds under §3.5.c mechanics. A Statement of Grounds is required — a general no-confidence vote without specific stated grounds is insufficient.

§2.10.b¶Senate Referral to Popular Recall

Where a simple majority of the full Senate votes to refer the President to popular recall, a national recall vote is held. Removal requires a majority of all eligible voters — not just those who participate. If fewer than 50% of eligible voters vote for removal, the President survives regardless of the participation rate. Only one Senate referral per presidential term under §2.10.c.

§2.10.c¶One Referral Per Term

No second Senate referral to popular recall within the same presidential term. One-shot rule prevents a hostile Senate from using repeated recall elections as attrition. After a failed recall, the President serves out their term with the NRS record intact.

Article III — The Legislature

§3.1 through §3.6 · House, Senate, and the legislative process · Includes recall and vacancy provisions
§3.1.a¶House Membership Floor

The minimum membership floor prevents the House from legislating away its own representation. Without a floor, a Legislature could reduce its own size to concentrate power among a small membership while remaining nominally constitutional. The floor is tied to population through the census (§3.1.b), ensuring that proportional representation scales with the population it serves. States with declining populations are protected against effective disenfranchisement through House shrinkage. The floor is a constitutional minimum; the Legislature may set a higher membership by statute.

§3.1.b¶Census Authority

The Legislative Inspectorate — not the Executive — conducts the census for House seat allocation. This prevents the executive branch from influencing seat allocation by controlling the population count.

§3.1.c¶Single-Subject Rule

Every bill must address a single, clearly defined subject identified in its title. Where a bill contains a non-conforming provision, any citizen or legislative minority of one-third may petition the Supreme Court during the Legislative Review Period. The Court severs the non-conforming provision; the remainder returns to both chambers for a revote at the original passage threshold. A severed provision may not be reintroduced in the same legislative session.

§3.1.d¶Bill Exposure

Every bill must be published in full on the NRS for a minimum public exposure period before it may be voted on. Secret legislation — drafted and passed the same day — is constitutionally prohibited. Every piece of legislation is publicly reviewable before it becomes law.

§3.1.e¶Caretaker Activation and PM Formation

Failure to seat a PM within the statutory period activates the Caretaker Protocol (§2.3.c). The House continues in session. LI publishes weekly accountability reports naming each member's participation record. Updated in v145: removed stale election bar reference from the old dissolution model removed in v138.

§3.1.f¶House Term Limits

No House member may serve more than six consecutive 2-year terms (12 consecutive years). After six consecutive terms, one full House electoral cycle cooling-off is required before re-standing. The cooling-off period runs from the sixth term's expiry regardless of how it ended. After cooling-off, the member is again eligible with no cumulative lifetime restriction. A partial term from a mid-term vacancy or special election counts as a full consecutive term. This operative-text limit mirrors the Senate's §3.2.a limit — previously House term limits existed only in non-operative Schedule A and were therefore unenforceable.

§3.3.a¶Legislative Mandate Enforcement

If the Legislature fails to enact a constitutionally mandated statute within the specified timeframe — or within two budget cycles where no timeframe is specified — the matter automatically becomes eligible for an Optional Referendum under §11.1 without requiring...

§3.3.c¶Presidential Veto Override

A Presidential Veto requires a 2/3 vote in both chambers simultaneously to override. The simultaneous requirement means both chambers must agree — a Senate that agrees with the President can block an override even if the House disagrees.

§3.3.b¶Standard Passage

Bills pass by simple majority in both chambers, subject to the Legislative Review Period (§2.5). Simple majority for ordinary legislation places the accountability burden on the reviewing period and executive objection mechanisms.

§3.3.d¶Record System Entry

The presiding officer of each chamber certifies and records each enacted law on the NRS with a timestamp. Discrepancies between the certified NRS record and any other document resolve in favor of the NRS. The record is what is law.

§3.4.g¶Continuity

A valid district map remains in effect until the next constitutionally required redrawing. Census-triggered redraws follow census publication. Courts may order interim remedial maps for constitutional violations, but only if a compliant map cannot be put in place before the next scheduled election.

§3.5¶Impeachment

This track applies to executive and judicial officers only — legislators are governed by §3.6. Inquiry: 1/3 House minority. Articles: simple majority of full House membership. Senate trial and conviction: 2/3 of full Senate membership. Separate from the §2.10 presidential removal track (which requires 2/3 House to initiate) and the Article VIII Inspectorate reporting and political accountability (which triggers automatically on Supreme Court finding).

§3.5.a¶Impeachment Inquiry

Any 1/3 minority of the House may open a formal impeachment inquiry — a deliberately low threshold ensuring opposition parties can trigger accountability proceedings without majority cooperation. The inquiry opens investigation; Articles of Impeachment require a full House simple majority.

§3.5.b¶Articles of Impeachment

The Senate may refer the President to popular recall by 2/3 of full seated membership. This triggers the §2.10.b popular recall process. The Senate referral cannot be combined with a §3.5.a impeachment proceeding; the two tracks are mutually exclusive for a sitting President.

§3.5.c¶Senate Trial

SC's most senior justice presides — longest-serving, age as tiebreaker — unless that justice is the subject, in which case the Senate Speaker presides. Trial is public, recorded on NRS. Conviction requires 2/3 of full Senate membership — not just those present. On conviction: automatic removal, possible disqualification from future office.

§3.5.d¶Executive Continuity During Impeachment

Presidential impeachment does not suspend operations — succession line activates immediately for defense and diplomatic functions when the House passes Articles, but the President retains authority pending Senate trial outcome. Prevents impeachment from being used as an operational shutdown weapon.

§3.6.a¶House Expulsion

2/3 of full House membership, preceded by a published ethics committee investigation with specific findings published to NRS before any vote. The full-membership threshold — not just those present — makes expulsion genuinely difficult and resists use as a partisan weapon.

§3.6.b¶Senate Expulsion

2/3 of full Senate membership, subject to the same published investigation and NRS publication requirements as House expulsion. The Senate's expulsion power applies to its own members only — parallel to but structurally separate from the House process.

§3.6.c¶Anti-Weaponization Threshold

Any expulsion vote taking effect within 30 days of a scheduled vote on a bill the expelled member was known to oppose is presumptively retaliatory. This prevents parliamentary majorities from using expulsion to silence dissent before critical votes.

§3.6.d¶Consequences and Restoration

An expelled member may not stand for federal legislative election for two years. The consequence is proportionate — expulsion does not permanently bar democratic participation, but creates a meaningful cooling period.

§3.6.e¶Recall — States and Territories

States and Territories may establish recall mechanisms for both their Senators and House members by their own statute. State recall and chamber expulsion operate independently — whichever takes effect first governs. The federal government may not restrict States from exercising this authority.

§3.6.f¶Vacancy Filling — States and Territories

When a House or Senate seat becomes vacant mid-term, the relevant State or Territory determines the replacement process by its own statute — special election, appointment, party nomination, or other mechanism. The Legislature sets a maximum vacancy period by statute: no State may leave a seat indefinitely vacant. All supermajority thresholds are calculated against full constitutional membership, not reduced seated membership.

§3.2¶The Senate — States as Constitutional Actors

The Senate gives States equal voice at the federal level regardless of population — three senators per State ensures that no coalition of populous States can dominate the chamber alone. This deliberate counter-majoritarian design is the Senate's constitutional function: the House represents people proportionally; the Senate represents political units equally. No bill becomes law without both chambers. The Senate's additional functions — treaty ratification, Supreme Court candidacy within the open-candidacy judicial selection process, acting-officer confirmation thresholds — reflect its role as the deliberative check on House-majority action and as the federal chamber through which States exercise their constitutionally guaranteed voice.

§3.2.b¶Senate Speaker Role Limit

No Senator may serve as Speaker for more than 3 consecutive sessions (6 years). After 3 consecutive sessions, a mandatory 2-session cooling-off applies before standing for Speaker again. This limit is on the Speaker role only — the Senator retains their seat and continues representing their State throughout.

§3.2.a¶Senator Terms and Limits

6-year terms, maximum 2 consecutive terms (12 years), then mandatory 6-year cooling-off. Partial terms following mid-term appointments count as full consecutive terms. Structural rotation prevents the entrenchment of indefinite incumbency.

§3.2.c¶Senate Must Act on Every House Bill

The Senate must take up every House-passed bill within a defined period — indefinite Senate delay used as a de facto veto is prohibited. If the Senate fails to act within the period, the bill is deemed confirmed and passes to the executive. Senate opposition must be expressed, not implied through silence.

§3.2.d¶Judicial Confirmation

Senate confirms inferior court judicial appointments. PM nominees confirmed by supermajority in the 55%-67% range (Legislature sets exact threshold within this constitutional band). Deemed Confirmed if Senate fails to vote within the statutory period. SC justices are elected nationally under §4.2, not confirmed.

§3.2.e¶Treaty Ratification

All international treaties require a 2/3 Senate vote. Treaties bind all States — the supermajority requirement ensures that no treaty imposes obligations without the consent of a meaningful cross-section of State representation.

§3.2.e.i¶Treaty Domestic Application

Ratified treaties are directly applicable as federal law in domestic courts from the date of ratification — unless the treaty requires implementing legislation or its provisions are insufficiently definite for direct judicial application. The Legislature must pass a treaty implementation framework within two years of Phase 4 activation; the framework may designate specific treaties or categories as requiring implementing legislation. In the absence of such a designation, courts apply treaties directly. A Legislature determination that a treaty requires implementing legislation is not subject to presidential veto — it governs the domestic legal order, not the President's domain.

§3.2.e.ii¶Treaty Withdrawal

The President may initiate withdrawal from any ratified treaty by publishing a notice of withdrawal to the NRS — specifying the treaty, the effective date (minimum six months from publication), and the grounds. The President simultaneously notifies treaty partners through diplomatic channels. Three withdrawal tracks: General treaties — the Senate may block by simple majority within six months; if the Senate does not vote, withdrawal proceeds automatically. Mutual defense treaties — the Senate must affirmatively confirm withdrawal by simple majority within six months or the treaty remains in force; withdrawal may not be initiated during active Tier 2 or Tier 3 operations under the treaty. Indigenous treaties — no withdrawal, abrogation, or modification by any Republic authority; Article XIV governs. All notices, votes, and withdrawal dates are permanent NRS entries.

§3.2.f¶Impeachment Trials

Impeachment trial conviction requires a 2/3 supermajority of seated senators — not those present. An absentee strategy to reduce the threshold through non-attendance is constitutionally closed. Removal is a constitutional act of last resort; the standard is deliberately high.

§3.2.g¶Survivor Quorum — Catastrophic Casualty Events

Where Senate membership is reduced by 50% or more in a single catastrophic event, survivors may constitute a quorum and exercise full Senate authority. A single devastating attack cannot eliminate the legislative function of government.

§3.2.h¶Senate Speaker Vacancy

When the Senate Speaker role becomes vacant — through death, resignation, expulsion, or recall — the Deputy Speaker assumes the succession role immediately. The Senate must elect a new Speaker within 72 hours. Given the Speaker is first in the presidential line of succession, the 72-hour ceiling prevents any gap in succession authority. If the Senate cannot convene within 72 hours due to a catastrophic event, the Deputy Speaker continues until a new Speaker is elected.

§3.4¶Electoral Districts

Electoral districts for the House of the Republic are drawn through an independent commission process governed by a strict criteria hierarchy: equal population first, then geographic contiguity, then community integrity, then competitive balance. Partisan data — voting history, registration, incumbents' home addresses — is constitutionally prohibited as an input. The Legislative Inspectorate reviews every map for compliance; any citizen may challenge a non-compliant map before the Supreme Court.

§3.4.a¶Criteria Hierarchy

Districts are drawn applying criteria in order of priority: equal population first, then geographic contiguity, then community integrity, then competitive balance. A district that satisfies lower criteria while sacrificing equal population is constitutionally invalid. The ordering makes priorities explicit rather than leaving them to judicial inference.

§3.4.b¶Prohibited Inputs

The district-drawing process may not use: partisan registration data, voting history, incumbent or candidate residential addresses, or racial/ethnic composition as a primary consideration. Use of prohibited inputs by any participant is reported to the NRS by the LI. This list removes the specific data inputs parties use to engineer electoral outcomes.

§3.4.c¶Drawing Authority — States

Each State draws its own federal House district maps through an independent redistricting commission — structurally independent of both State legislature and State executive. The LI reviews for compliance but cannot redraw for policy preferences. A map that "could not have been drawn by a neutral actor" is rejected.

§3.4.d¶Drawing Authority — Federal Territories

For Territories (no State legislature), the LI draws federal House districts directly, applying the same criteria and prohibitions. Territorial residents may petition the LI for public hearings.

§3.4.e¶National Compliance Review

LI reviews every State-drawn map for criteria hierarchy compliance. Review is strictly criteria-based — LI cannot substitute its policy judgment for a State's neutral map. The LI may require a State to redraw a non-compliant map; it may not redraw it itself.

§3.4.f¶Court Adjudication

Any citizen, State government, or parliamentary minority of 1/3 may challenge a proposed district map by petitioning the SC within the 60-day review period after LI compliance certification. The Court reviews whether criteria were correctly applied — not whether a different map would have been better.

Article IV — The Judicial Architecture

§4.1 through §4.4 · The Supreme Court
§4.2¶Open-Candidacy Judicial Selection

Redesigned in v141, replacing the Triad. Any citizen on the SC Candidate Registry (§8.4.e) may register. Judicial Selection Board of five lottery-selected inferior court judges publishes advisory assessment. Citizens elect by RCV. No political nominations. Sitting SC removed from succession.

§4.2.a¶Candidate Registration

Any SC Registry member files disclosure record with EI. EI reviews factual accuracy within 14 days, publishes to NRS. Material inaccuracy or failed Registry criteria removes candidate. All others proceed to JI certification and election.

§4.2.b¶Judicial Selection Board

Five inferior court judges by lottery, 10+ years on bench. Advisory assessment published 60 days before election. No gatekeeping authority — cannot remove candidates, no endorsement. Published to NRS at no cost.

§4.2.c¶Judicial Inspectorate Certification

JI confirms Registry status only. Certification refusals published to NRS. Disputes go to Appellate Courts — not the sitting Supreme Court, removing the self-selection dynamic of the former Triad.

§4.2.d¶Public Election

All certified candidates on national RCV ballot on National Election Day, or special election within 90 days for vacancies. Electoral Commission administers and certifies to NRS.

§4.2.e¶Mid-Term Vacancies

Same open-candidacy process, adjusted to the 180-day window: registration day 30, EI review day 60, Board assessment day 90, JI certification day 120, election day 180. The longer window — compared to the previous 90 days — is possible because the Temporary Associate Justice under §4.2.g maintains the Court at full capacity throughout. JI may extend by up to 30 days for extraordinary circumstances. Replacement serves remainder of the original 12-year term only. Amended v147.

§4.2.f¶Emergency Panel for Tier 1 Scope Challenges

Four justices by rotation each two-year cycle. Rules within 48 hours on Tier 1 scope challenges. Binding unless full Court overrules within 72 hours.

§4.2.g¶Vacancy Operating Rules

Automatic Temporary Associate Justice: the most senior Appellate Court judge by continuous time served (age as tiebreaker) is automatically designated within 5 days of the vacancy — no nomination, confirmation, or legislative action required. The TAJ holds a full vote; is excluded from any case arising from a ruling they authored or joined below; may not stand as a candidate in the election to fill the permanent seat; and returns to the Appellate bench without loss of seniority when the replacement is seated. Equal-division backstop retained for recusal edge cases. New v147.

§4.2.h¶Judicial Recusal

Legislature establishes recusal framework by statute. Self-executing default: recusal where impartiality could reasonably be questioned. Petition determined by remaining justices.

§4.3.a¶Concrete Cases Only

The SC may declare any statute, executive order, treaty provision, or administrative regulation unconstitutional. Declaration renders the provision void. Individual provisions may be struck without voiding the entire statute. A declaration is permanent unless the constitution is amended to accommodate the struck provision.

§4.3.b¶Organizational Standing

An organization may bring a constitutional claim on behalf of its members where: at least one member would individually have standing, the claim relates to the organization's purpose, and individual participation isn't required. Extends constitutional claims beyond well-resourced individual litigants.

§4.3.c¶Ripeness and Mootness

Courts may not rule on cases not yet ripe (no concrete harm) or moot (no practical relief possible). Voluntary cessation doctrine: government cannot moot a case by ceasing challenged conduct while retaining the ability to resume it.

§4.1.a¶Establishment

Only the House may create courts of the Republic by statute. Courts created by statute carry the full constitutional authority of the judicial branch. Administrative tribunals established by executive order are subject to Article I review by any court to which their decisions are appealed.

§4.4.a¶Judicial Judgments

A final judgment of any State or Territory court is entitled to recognition and enforcement throughout the Republic. States may not selectively ignore each other's court orders — a custody order from one State cannot be relitigated from scratch in another from scratch in another.

§4.4.b¶NRS Authentication

Judgments and court orders authenticated through the NRS process are self-authenticating in any court or government proceeding throughout the Republic. A party presenting an NRS-authenticated judgment need not independently prove its authenticity or the jurisdiction of the issuing court.

§4.4.c¶Public Acts and Official Records

Official acts and public records of any State or Territory are recognized throughout the Republic. A government document authenticated through the NRS is self-authenticating in any court or proceeding. Eliminates the administrative burden of re-authenticating documents across State/Territory lines.

§4.4.d¶Status Orders — Special Protections

Orders establishing personal legal status — marriage, parenthood, name, gender, disability, guardianship — follow a person throughout the Republic regardless of whether the granting State recognizes the same status class today. A legally established status cannot be invalidated by crossing a border.

§4.4.e¶Limits — No Obligation Beyond Republic Borders

Recognition obligations apply within the Republic only. A foreign government is not required to recognize Republic judicial decisions; reciprocity is a matter of treaty or diplomatic arrangement.

§4.4.f¶Legislative Implementation Mandates

Legislature must enact implementation statutes for full-faith-and-credit recognition within the first budget cycle after Phase 4. Implementation statutes cannot create exceptions violating §1.8 equality.

§4.1.b¶Appointment of Inferior Courts

The PM nominates lower court judges; the Senate confirms by a supermajority the Legislature defines by statute. If the PM fails to nominate within 90 days of a vacancy, the Senate may nominate a candidate. Inaction triggers Deemed Confirmed. No vacancy may remain unfilled beyond 180 days. The Legislature-defined confirmation period cannot push past this ceiling.

§4.1.c¶Removal of Inferior Court Judges — Three Tracks

SC justice removal. The House passes a Statement of Grounds — a written document specifying the conduct, decisions, or failures constituting the basis for removal, published to the NRS simultaneously with the House vote — by 2/3 of full seated membership. The Senate then conducts a trial under §3.5.c mechanics, limited strictly to the stated grounds. Conviction requires 2/3 of full seated Senate. Both thresholds are of full seated membership, not merely those present. No popular recall for SC justices. Inferior court judges are removed through §3.5 impeachment, which applies to them in full.

§4.1.d¶Judicial Tenure Protection

Inferior court judges serve during good behavior once commissioned — not on fixed terms. The Legislature may establish a maximum service age or maximum total service period by statute, but the period may not be less than 15 years from first commissioning, and fixed terms shorter than 15 years are prohibited. This ensures the §3.5 impeachment process is the operative accountability mechanism for inferior court judges. Without tenure protection, a Legislature could set very short judicial terms and make judges removable through non-reappointment — bypassing the constitutional removal process entirely. The 15-year floor closes that vulnerability.

Article V — Citizenship, Residency, and National Identity

§5.1 through §5.5 · Citizenship, residency, and the Constitutional Logic Test
§5.1.a¶Inherent Citizenship

A child is automatically a citizen at birth if at least one parent is a Citizen or Legal Resident at the time of birth. Statelessness prevention clause: where a child would otherwise be stateless, citizenship is granted regardless of parental status. No citizenship may be stripped from a natural-born citizen.

§5.1.a.i¶Statelessness Prevention

A child born on Republic territory to parents holding neither Citizen nor Legal Resident status is an Inhabitant from birth, with full Article I protections and BNF access. Where no country recognizes the child as a citizen, the Republic provides a determination process consistent with §6.4's protection principles — protection first, determination second. A child determined to hold no other citizenship is eligible for Legal Residency from birth, with all Inhabitant years counting toward the §5.1.c residency requirement.

§5.1.b¶Coming of Age

Children of citizens are Inhabitants at birth with full Article I protections. Full citizenship rights — including the right to vote — activate at 18. The distinction between Inhabitant and Citizen status for citizen children prevents citizenship from being used to exempt children from local law.

§5.1.c¶Naturalization

A Legal Resident may earn citizenship after five continuous years of residency, passing the Constitutional Logic Test, and demonstrating a working knowledge of the Republic's official language. The CLT tests understanding of the Republic's constitutional structure of the Republic's constitutional structure — not cultural assimilation.

§5.1.d¶National Service Path

A Legal Resident may earn citizenship through honorable completion of military or national service — a service path that runs parallel to the residency path. Years of service are a legitimate basis for constitutional membership.

§5.1.e¶Citizenship Irrevocability

Citizenship may not be revoked as punishment or for any reason once granted by birth or naturalization. The Republic may not manufacture statelessness as a punitive tool. See also §5.1.a.i, which ensures children born on Republic territory to undocumented parents cannot be left stateless by inaction.

§5.1.f¶Derivative Status

A child born before a parent acquired Legal Residency or Citizenship, who accompanies that parent to the Republic within a Legislature-defined window, receives derivative status. Prevents a parent's pre-Republic legal status from permanently cutting off their child's path to citizenship.

§5.1.g¶Accelerated Naturalization — Long-Term Pre-Ratification Residents

Any person who resided continuously in the Republic's territory for 5+ years immediately before Day Zero but did not hold predecessor voting rights — including persons disenfranchised by laws subsequently nullified under §19.2 — may apply for citizenship through an accelerated process the Legislature must establish within one year of Phase 4. The process must waive the CLT requirement and complete within 90 days. This closes the gap created by §5.5.a's Grandfather Clause, which grants Day Zero citizenship to prior voters but leaves long-term residents who were denied voting rights by the predecessor government on the standard naturalization path — an inequity the accelerated process corrects.

§5.2¶Legal Resident Rights

Legal Residents hold a Residency Credential allowing them to work, enter contracts, own property, access BNF programs, and participate in local elections where the Legislature authorizes. They hold all Article I rights as Inhabitants. What they lack that citizens hold: federal voting rights and the right to stand for federal office.

§5.3¶Custodial Care of Citizen Children

Where a parent or guardian who is the primary caregiver of a citizen child is removed from the Republic, the Republic accepts responsibility for that citizen child — housing, care, and connection to remaining family. The Republic does not separate citizen children from their sole caregivers from their sole caregivers without providing for them.

§5.4¶The Constitutional Logic Test

The CLT is required for earned citizenship through naturalization (§5.1.c) and national service (§5.1.d). It tests knowledge of constitutional principles and civic structure — not political opinions or cultural loyalty. The Senate deadlock rule (§5.4.c) ensures the existing test remains in force if the Legislature cannot agree on a new standard.

§5.4.a¶Senate Authority over the CLT Standard

The CLT tests functional understanding of the constitution's structure: rights, institutional relationships, amendment processes, and foundational principles. It does not test political opinion, language beyond functional communication, cultural knowledge, or religious practice. Linguistic accessibility is constitutionally required.

§5.4.b¶CLT Administrator

The Constitutional Logic Test is administered by a CLT Administrator nominated by the PM from a Candidate Register, confirmed by the Senate. Structural independence from partisan influence: the CLT process cannot be used to create barriers that track political outcomes.

§5.4.c¶Status Quo Maintenance

Senate deadlock on CLT standard changes keeps the existing version in force. Deadlock cannot be weaponized to eliminate the CLT or make it impossibly difficult — the existing test remains operative.

§5.5.a¶Automatic Citizenship Grant

All individuals holding voting rights on Day Zero of ratification are automatically granted full citizenship without testing or application. The Day Zero Grandfather Clause prevents the new Republic from immediately disenfranchising its existing population by requiring existing voters to re-qualify under new standards.

Article VI — Immigration and Residency

§6.1 through §6.4 · Immigration gates, enforcement, and asylum protection
§6.1.a–b¶The Dual Gate

Legal residency requires passing both gates. Gate 1 is the federal substantive assessment — national standards that no State can circumvent. Gate 2 is State acceptance — no State can be forced to absorb unlimited immigration obligations without its participation. The dual-gate architecture prevents two failure modes simultaneously: race-to-the-bottom competition among States offering easy Gate 2 acceptance, and federal imposition of immigration outcomes that States must bear without agreement. The Presidential role (§6.1.c) is to set Gate 1 criteria by executive order within House-enacted parameters — giving the executive flexibility to respond to changing conditions without requiring constant legislative action.

§6.1.b¶Gate 2 — Federal Certification

Federal certification across four statutory categories: identity verification, criminal history, national security assessment, self-sufficiency or support guarantee. Cannot impose ideological screening or nationality-based quotas. Any denial must specify the precise ground in writing. National security denials published in redacted form protecting sources.

§6.1.c¶Presidential Role

Once an applicant clears both gates, the President must ministerially issue residency credentials. The word 'ministerially' is deliberate — the President has no discretion to refuse or delay what the law requires. Presidential interference with credential issuance is constitutionally prohibited.

§6.1.d¶Federal Sponsorship Category

The Legislature may establish a Federal Sponsorship Category where designated federal agencies serve as Gate 1 sponsors for applicants whose skills directly serve a defined federal interest — research programs, national infrastructure, national security-adjacent technical roles. The agency assumes all State Gate 1 responsibilities including notification obligations. Crucially: the Federal Sponsorship Category does not bypass Gate 2 — all four federal certification categories still apply. State Gate 1 authority is entirely unaffected; the federal category operates alongside, not instead of, State sponsorship.

§6.2¶Enforcement and Removal

Only the federal government may remove individuals from the Republic — States may not conduct deportations independently. This prevents States from developing parallel immigration enforcement regimes that could be used to target populations the State government dislikes regardless of federal law. Removal occurs only for violations of laws the Legislature has defined — executive detention without statutory basis is not removal, it is unlawful detention subject to habeas corpus. The federal monopoly on removal also ensures that immigration enforcement standards are uniform across States rather than varying by State political composition.

§6.3¶Gate 2 Appeal Rights

Gate 2 denials are judicially challengeable on three grounds: the denial relied on a criterion outside the four certified categories; the denial was discriminatory contrary to §1.8; or the stated ground is factually unsupported by the evidence used. Gate 1 denials — a State's decision not to sponsor — are not subject to federal judicial review. The asymmetry is deliberate: federal certification is a federal act subject to federal accountability; State sponsorship is a State prerogative, reviewable only where §1.8 discrimination is alleged.

§6.3.a¶Judicial Challenge

An applicant may challenge a Gate 2 denial on three grounds: criterion outside the four certified categories; discriminatory application; or procedurally deficient process. Three-ground limitation channels challenges rather than eliminating them.

§6.3.b¶National Security Denials — In-Camera Review

Where Gate 2 is denied on national security grounds, a reviewing court may examine the classified assessment in camera and determine whether it constitutes a credible, specific, and documented concern — without publishing classified content. The in-camera review is outcome-determinative: either the security concern meets the constitutional standard and the denial stands, or it does not and recertification is ordered. This closes the "security claim as absolute veto" pathway while protecting genuine intelligence sources and methods.

§6.4¶Asylum and Protection

Seven sub-provisions governing the asylum and protection framework: asylum seekers as BNF Inhabitants from filing date; right to determination before removal; the Asylum Court as a constitutionally independent judicial body under Article IV — not an executive tribunal. Non-penalization for irregular entry; unexecutable removal orders; security and criminal protection cases; Territory remote proceedings. §6.4.h establishes a federal legal guardian for unaccompanied minors within 24 hours of contact.

§6.4.a¶Asylum Seekers as Inhabitants

Any person who files an asylum claim under §1.27 is an Inhabitant of the Republic from the date of filing — immediately entitled to BNF coverage and all Article I protections. Protection-first: the determination process does not happen in a rights-free zone.

§6.4.b¶Right to Determination and Protection During Proceedings

Asylum proceedings must provide: a qualified adjudicator with decision authority, access to an interpreter, the right to present evidence and witnesses, a written decision specifying grounds, and a right of appeal. Standards apply regardless of the applicant's point of entry into the Republic.

§6.4.c¶Asylum Court

The Asylum Court is a federal judicial institution constitutionally independent of both executive branches. Its judges hold full tenure protections. The Legislature may not establish any asylum determination body as a subdivision or instrumentality of either executive branch — administrative asylum tribunals dependent on the executive are constitutionally prohibited.

§6.4.d¶Non-Penalization for Irregular Entry

The manner or means of entry into the Republic — including irregular or unauthorized entry — does not affect the right to claim asylum or have that claim determined. No penalty arising solely from irregular entry may be imposed on a person whose asylum claim is pending or has been granted.

§6.4.e¶Unexecutable Removal Orders

Legislature must define a minimum status, material conditions, and periodic judicial review for persons subject to final removal orders that cannot be executed — because the destination country won't accept them, or execution would violate §1.27. The constitution does not allow indefinite stateless detention.

§6.4.f¶Security and Criminal Protection Cases

Where the Asylum Court grants §1.27 protection to a person who poses a documented security threat, removal remains constitutionally barred — but indefinite unreviewed detention is not constitutionally available. The Legislature must establish a framework with regular judicial review. 'Indefinite unreviewed detention is not a constitutionally available outcome' — one of the clearest lines in the document.

§6.4.g¶Territory Accessibility

The asylum proceedings statute must ensure asylum is accessible to persons in Territories and geographically remote areas — not just major population centers. The right to seek asylum applies regardless of where in the Republic a person arrives.

§6.4.h¶Unaccompanied Minors

An unaccompanied minor arriving in the Republic or presenting to Republic authorities is an Inhabitant from the moment of contact and holds full §1.27 protection. A federal legal guardian must be appointed within 24 hours. No unaccompanied minor may be detained in a facility not specifically designated and resourced for children.

Article VII — Elections and the National Vote

§7.1 through §7.3 · The combined-score presidential election
§7.1¶Standard Elections — Ranked Choice

All federal elections on National Election Day, every two years. All elections for House, Senate, SC, and Electoral Commission seats use Ranked Choice Voting — majority required, not plurality. No federal elections outside National Election Day except special elections for vacancies. This concentrates democratic attention and ensures all federal officers have genuine majority mandates.

§7.2.a¶National Ranked-Choice Vote

Presidential elections use RCV nationally, preventing the geographic distortions of district-based winner-take-all systems. Votes transfer until one candidate holds a majority of active ballots nationwide. The national RCV tends to produce presidents who are the genuine second or third choice of a larger electorate — a broader mandate than first-preference plurality. This mechanism interacts with the regional combined score (§7.2.b–c): a candidate must achieve both a national majority and broad geographic distribution, preventing a president elected entirely by densely populated regional concentrations from claiming a national mandate.

§7.2.b¶The Combined Score

Where no candidate achieves a national RCV majority, the combined score mechanism favors the candidate with the broadest geographic distribution of support. A candidate who wins large margins in a few populous regions but has near-zero support elsewhere represents a fundamentally different governing coalition than a candidate with narrower leads distributed across all regions. The combined score is the constitution's structural requirement that the President represent the full polity — not merely its most concentrated population centers. This mechanism prevents the national RCV from being won by geographic concentration alone.

§7.2.c¶Presidential Election Regions

The Republic is divided into presidential election regions to provide the geographic breadth component of the combined score. At least three regions are required; no more than one per ten States once the Republic exceeds thirty States. The Legislature draws regions; the LI reviews for compliance.

§7.2.d¶Territory Presidential Voting

Territory citizens hold the full §1.19 right to vote in presidential elections. Their votes count in the national first-preference total — the 65% component of the combined score — but Territories are not assigned to presidential election regions and do not contribute to the 35% regional breadth component. The regional breadth measure reflects the geographic distribution of the Republic's State structure. Territory citizens participate fully in the national democratic mandate; they do not give Territories a footprint in the regional component that States earn through the Statehood process.

§7.2.e¶Election Certification

The Electoral Commission certifies the final presidential election result to the NRS within a period the Legislature defines by statute. Certification is final and may be challenged only before the Supreme Court within 14 days. No transfer of presidential authority may be delayed beyond the constitutionally fixed moment.

§7.2.f¶Run-off

If two candidates are within 0.5% of each other on the combined score, a head-to-head national RCV runoff is conducted between those two candidates. The runoff closes the edge-case gap where the combined score is mathematically indeterminate.

§7.3.a¶No Executive Interference

The President may not declare, postpone, or influence the scheduling of elections — including declaring emergencies that have the effect of postponing them. §1.25.e's prohibition on emergency-based election postponement backs this up: even a genuine emergency cannot defer a scheduled election.

§7.3.b¶National Voting System

The Electoral Commission administers the National Voting System through which all federal elections and referenda are conducted. The NVS is constitutionally required to maintain ballot secrecy, public auditability, and resilience — properties defined in Article XVI creating a cross-constitutional guarantee.

§7.3.c¶Participation Protection

No State may take any action that reduces NVS access, functionality, or availability within its territory. State-level voter suppression — long lines, ID requirements that function as bars, polling place closures that reduce access — is constitutionally prohibited to the extent it reduces access for eligible citizens.

Article VIII — The Inspectorates

§8.1 through §8.11 · Three independent oversight bodies

The three Inspectorates audit different branches. None has enforcement power. All publish findings permanently to the NRS. Their power is the power of complete, public disclosure.

§8.1¶Three Inspectorates

Three permanent, constitutionally established Inspectorates — Legislative (§8.1.a), Executive (§8.1.b), and Judicial (§8.1.c) — each audit a different branch of government. None has enforcement power: their mandate is information, not action. All findings are published permanently to the NRS. Their power is the power of complete, mandatory public disclosure — accountability through transparency rather than compulsion.

§8.1.a¶The Legislative Inspectorate (LI)

The Legislative Inspectorate audits the House and Senate for fiscal accuracy, constitutional compliance of enacted legislation, redistricting compliance, and BNF provision rates at the State level. The LI is not selected by the legislative branch it audits — its IG is nominated by the Supreme Court and confirmed by the Senate.

§8.1.b¶The Executive Inspectorate (EI)

The Executive Inspectorate audits both executive offices and the Monetary Authority for constitutional compliance and rule violation evidence. For appointed positions, the EI reviews candidate disclosure records submitted by the nominating actor — it does not generate independent assessments — and publishes the record with its recommendation. It publishes factual background reports on every clemency petition. Nominated by the House — not by either executive it audits.

§8.1.c¶The Judicial Inspectorate (JI)

The Judicial Inspectorate audits the court system for compliance with judicial scope and independence standards, maintains the Supreme Court Candidate Registry under §8.4.e (candidates submit registration records; the JI reviews for accuracy and applies the §8.4.e eligibility criteria), and audits the Electoral Commission. Its IG is selected by lottery from the Registry — removing human discretion from the selection of the body that watches the judiciary.

§8.2¶Selection and Independence

Each IG is selected through a model that excludes the watched branch from controlling its own oversight: Legislature (SC nominates LI), Executive (House nominates EI), Judiciary (lottery from qualified pool). No branch oversees itself — the watched-branch principle applied to the Inspectorate selection mechanism.

§8.3.c¶Term and Staggering

Single non-renewable 10-year terms. No two IG terms may begin within 18 months of each other. Staggering prevents simultaneous vacancy across all three Inspectorates. Simultaneous vacancy would leave all three Inspectorates leaderless at once.

§8.3.a¶Legislative IG

The Legislative IG is nominated by the Supreme Court and confirmed by the Senate with a 2/3 supermajority. Nomination by the judiciary — itself independent of the Legislature — gives the LI's leadership genuine independence from the institution it audits.

§8.3.b¶Executive IG

The EI is nominated by the House and confirmed by the Senate. Crossed nomination: the watched branch (the executive) does not select its own auditor — the House nominates, reflecting the House's role as the legislature most directly exposed to executive conduct. Single 10-year non-renewable term.

§8.4.a¶Judicial IG Candidate Pool

A standing pool of qualified candidates maintained by the JI and published on the NRS. The Pool's criteria reflect the research and reporting nature of the role — not judicial credentials as such. Constitutional minimums: no elected or appointed government position within the prior 10 years; no financial interest in any entity regulated by the Republic; no criminal conviction for conduct committed while holding government office. Legislature may add to these minimums but not reduce them. The Pool is the lottery's source — no Pool, no legitimate draw.

§8.4.b¶The Draw

When a Judicial IG term ends, the outgoing IG conducts a public, verifiable random draw from the Pool, witnessed by the other two IGs and three citizens selected by jury duty protocols. Result published to NRS immediately. Transparency makes manipulation detectable.

§8.4.c¶Term and Pool Depletion

Judicial IG serves a single non-renewable 10-year term. If the Pool contains fewer than three candidates when a draw is required, the outgoing IG serves an extension of up to 6 months while the Pool is reconstituted. 6 months is the hard cap.

§8.4.d¶Inspectorate Staff Continuity

Inspectorate staff retain their constitutional mandate to continue all mandatory publication and audit functions during any IG vacancy, including vacancy caused by death. Staff actions during a vacancy are subject to full review upon appointment of a successor IG. In catastrophic simultaneous vacancy events, the Senate appoints temporary IGs within 14 days under survivor quorum rules.

§8.4.e¶Supreme Court Candidate Registry

New in v142. Maintained by JI separately from Judicial IG Candidate Pool. Exclusively for SC candidacy under §4.2. Criteria: 15+ years legal practice OR 10+ years as a judge — judicial service does not disqualify. No current executive or legislative position. No financial conflicts affecting SC impartiality. No criminal conviction in office. Registry inclusion does not expire. JI publishes annually to NRS.

§8.5.a¶For-Cause Removal

For-Cause IG removal requires a 2/3 supermajority of both chambers simultaneously. Before the Legislature may vote, the State Designee Panel — a rotating body of five State-appointed representatives drawn by lot — publishes a binary verification finding on whether stated grounds are substantiated. Grounds: documented dereliction, incapacity, undisclosed conflict of interest, or criminal conviction. The standard is deliberately high — Inspectorate independence depends on removal being genuinely difficult.

§8.5.b¶No-Cause Removal

No-cause removal requires a 3/4 vote of both chambers simultaneously — the extraordinary supermajority signals that no-cause removal is a constitutional last resort, not a political convenience. The mandatory 90-day delay between vote and removal allows judicial challenge of politically motivated removals.

§8.5.c¶Judicial IG — For-Cause Only

The Judicial IG may only be removed via the For-Cause track. No-Cause removal is unavailable for the Judicial IG. A legislature that disagrees with a JIG audit result cannot remove the JIG without documented cause.

§8.6.a¶Reports

Each Inspectorate publishes its findings as formal Reports to the NRS — permanent, unalterable, publicly accessible from publication. Reports cover every audit, investigation, and non-pursuit decision. Inspectorate work is the property of the public, not the institution being audited.

§8.6.b¶No Classification

Inspectorate Reports are permanently exempt from classification. Even where an investigation touches classified matters, the finding itself must still be published. Classification may not be used to suppress accountability findings.

§8.6.c¶No Binding Force

Inspectorate findings carry no binding legal force on their own — they are published statements of observed fact. This design is intentional. An institution that can both discover violations and punish them concentrates power in ways that corrupt both functions. The EI finds and publishes; courts determine constitutional violations; the Senate decides removal; citizens decide elections. Each consequence flows from a different actor using the finding as evidence, never from the finding itself. Factual permanence on the NRS without coercive force is the architecture — and it is strong precisely because it cannot be short-circuited by a single institutional actor.

§8.6.d¶Non-Pursuit Disclosure

Every decision not to open an investigation following a valid trigger must be published to the NRS with the reasoning. Non-pursuit disclosure closes the suppression-by-inaction pathway — an Inspectorate cannot simply decline to investigate a problem and remain silent about the decision.

§8.6.e¶Minority Reports

Any Inspectorate staff member may submit a Minority Report directly to the NRS — bypassing their IG — if they believe a finding was suppressed or mischaracterized. The protection is absolute for good-faith professional disagreement about findings.

§8.6.f¶Inspectorate Funding Guarantee

Cross-reference: see §8.10 for the constitutional minimum funding guarantee. Inspectorate funding cannot be reduced below prior year without a 2/3 vote of both chambers.

§8.6.g¶Citizen Discrepancy Petition

Any citizen who identifies a factual discrepancy in a candidate disclosure record or Registry registration record published to the NRS may petition their elected House member or Senator in writing. No fee, threshold, or filing requirement may be imposed. The elected representative may raise the matter in the confirmation process, request a formal investigation, or publish a statement to the NRS. The representative's response — including a decision not to act — is their own constitutional judgment, subject to their accountability to their constituents.

Any elected or appointed official may formally request an investigation into conduct within their domain by filing a written request with the relevant Inspectorate. The request and the Inspectorate's response — open or decline — are both published to the NRS. Officials can initiate oversight as well as be subject to it.

Any official may request investigation of conduct in their domain. The Inspectorate must either open a formal investigation or publish written reasons for declining within a Legislature-defined period. Asymmetric accountability: officials can ask for scrutiny but cannot prevent it.

§8.9¶Inspectorate Oversight — State Designee Panel

A standing pool of State and Territory-appointed representatives — each State and Territory nominates one designee — that constitutes into working panels when needed. The Panel's sole constitutional functions: (1) verifying For-Cause grounds for IG removal (§8.9.a), and (2) gatekeeping determinations for referendum eligibility, EC candidates, and SC Candidate Registry. The State Designee Panel's sole constitutional function is For-Cause IG removal verification. Upon receipt of a For-Cause removal motion, the Panel convenes, reviews the stated grounds, and determines whether they meet the constitutional standard. Narrow mandate prevents scope creep.

§8.9.b¶Appeals

An IG who disputes a Panel substantiation finding may petition the Supreme Court within 14 days. The SC reviews only whether the Panel applied the correct legal standard — not whether the underlying conduct warranted removal. Narrow review scope prevents the SC from substituting its judgment for the Panel's factual determinations.

§8.9.d¶Gatekeeping Appeal

Any party aggrieved by a JI gatekeeping determination — referendum ineligibility, EC candidate disqualification, or SC candidate Registry exclusion — may petition the Appellate Courts within 14 days. These disputes do not go to the sitting Supreme Court, removing the self-selection dynamic.

§8.10¶Inspectorate Minimum Funding Guarantee

Each Inspectorate is guaranteed adequate annual funding to perform its constitutional mandate fully and independently. Legislature may not reduce any Inspectorate's funding below the constitutionally required minimum. Prevents defunding as a form of retaliation — an Inspectorate that produces inconvenient reports cannot be fiscally starved.

§8.11¶Eighth-Year Assessment

The eight-year joint assessment is the constitution's built-in self-evaluation mechanism — the document asking whether it is working as designed. Every eight years, the three Inspectorates jointly assess constitutional performance across all domains: Are rights enforced? Is the Inspectorate reporting mechanism functioning or being abused? Are fiscal obligations met? Is military subordination complete? Is NRS coverage comprehensive? The assessment creates a permanent longitudinal record allowing comparison across periods. No previous constitution has built a scheduled self-assessment into its text. The mechanism operationalizes the drafters' epistemic humility: the document cannot know in advance whether it works; it builds in the machinery to find out, permanently and publicly.

Article IX — The National Record System

§9.1 through §9.5 · NRS architecture, co-equal records, classification, and intelligence sharing
§9.1¶NRS Properties

The NRS is not a government archive — it is the medium in which government action becomes constitutionally real. Unrecorded PM orders are void. Unregistered classifications are illegal. Unrecorded judicial proceedings cannot be sealed. Three properties are constitutionally non-negotiable: immutability (entries survive without deletion or alteration), distributed custody (no single entity controls a majority of nodes), and public accessibility (any Inhabitant may read the NRS at no cost). The China Scenario stress-tested all three: building a §9.1.b compliant node network against a prior infrastructure designed to prevent transparency required four years and the provisional framework of §19.3.f — revealing a transition gap the document now explicitly addresses.

§9.1.a¶Immutability

All NRS entries are permanent — they may never be deleted or altered after confirmation. Superseded records are annotated with the superseding record; the original remains. The immutability is the technical foundation for the Republic's accountability architecture: politicians cannot disappear inconvenient records.

§9.1.b¶Distributed Custody

The NRS is maintained by a network of independent, constitutionally mandated nodes — not a single central server under government control. Each Inspectorate and each State operates at least one independent node. No single point of failure, no single point of control, no single point of government capture.

§9.1.c¶Public Accessibility

Once recorded, an NRS entry cannot be altered, overwritten, or deleted by any person, government body, or court order. Corrections are made by publishing a new entry that explicitly supersedes the prior one — the original remains visible. Immutability is the foundation of the NRS's legal authority.

§9.1.d¶Cryptographic Signing

Every entry is digitally signed by the issuing official or body. Any alteration is mathematically detectable. The signing standard is defined by statute and may be updated as technology evolves, provided backward compatibility of existing signatures is maintained. No official can deny having signed an entry — cryptographic non-repudiation.

§9.1.e¶Technology Neutrality

The NRS operates across a minimum number of geographically distributed nodes defined by statute, with no single point of failure. No government body may operate a majority of nodes. Network consensus is required for any new entry. Prevents any government from controlling the record by controlling the infrastructure.

§9.1.f¶Migration Overlap Protocol

Technical standard migrations must run old and new systems in parallel with identical entries for a minimum statute-defined overlap period before the old system is retired. Any divergence must be immediately reported to the LI and the migration suspended. Prevents a migration from being used to selectively lose inconvenient records.

§9.3¶RSA Director

The RSA Director is nominated by the Prime Minister from a Candidate Register maintained under the same mechanism as §10.4.a — the Executive Inspectorate publishes a factual assessment of each candidate's professional background and relevant expertise to the...

§9.1.g¶NRS Self-Authentication

All records published to the National Record System pursuant to a constitutional mandate are self-authenticating in any constitutional or legal proceeding. No additional foundation laying, certification, apostille, or authentication process may be required...

§9.2¶Node Operators

Each Inspectorate and each State must operate at least one independent node. The Legislature may charter additional nodes from civil society, universities, or international organizations. Independence — meaning free from centralized manipulation — is a constitutional property, not a technical preference.

§9.4¶Classification Authority

During Emergency Mode, the NRS accepts no new entries but remains fully readable. Entries queued during Emergency Mode are published in chronological order once quorum is restored. No entries may be selectively delayed or reordered during queue-clearing. Emergency Mode is a constitutional status the LI monitors and reports on the NRS itself.

§9.5.a¶Classification Authority

Only the President may classify national defense and diplomatic operational information. Only the PM may classify domestic information with genuine national security implications. Neither executive may delegate classification authority below their respective Council level. No other official or body may classify — any purported classification by an unauthorized party is void. A President who classifies information specifically to prevent the PM from performing domestic constitutional functions commits a constitutional violation regardless of whether the underlying classification is otherwise valid.

§9.5.b¶Classification Duration

Standard classification expires 10 years from creation without renewal — and renewal must be separately justified. Automatic declassification prevents the indefinite accumulation of secret government records that characterizes most existing classification systems.

§9.5.c¶Classification Registry

The existence of every classified document must be registered on the NRS — even if its contents remain classified. Citizens know that classified information exists even if they cannot access it. No secret program can exist without its existence being publicly acknowledged.

§9.5.d¶Automatic Declassification

Upon expiration of classification without renewal, all affected documents transfer automatically to the NRS with full public accessibility. No action is required — declassification is the automatic outcome of inaction. Secrecy requires active maintenance, not transparency.

§9.5.e¶Improper Classification

Classification used to conceal a constitutional violation, an illegal order, or embarrassing but non-sensitive information is constitutionally prohibited in itself. Improper classification is not an administrative error — it is a constitutional offense — it is a constitutional offense.

Article X — Public Finance and Ethics

§10.1 through §10.6 · The BNF, Monetary Authority, and public ethics
§10.1¶The Basic Needs Floor

The BNF is funded through the GNR formula rather than discretionary appropriation — the Legislature cannot defund it through budget decisions alone. The MA Director certifies annually whether the floor has been met, creating independent verification. The right is self-executing: in the absence of the implementing statute, the constitutional floor operates directly in court. The Severe Contraction Protocol (§10.1.d–g) addresses hard cases: when GNR falls severely, the floor reduces proportionally while federal supplementation obligations activate for States that fall short. The BNF design reflects the principle that social rights requiring legislative implementation need constitutional defaults — otherwise the right exists in text but not in practice.

§10.1.a¶Anti-Evasion Principle

The BNF guarantees actual provision, not a formula satisfied on paper. The anti-evasion principle prohibits reclassification, formula manipulation, and creative accounting. The MA's certification detects structural evasion.

§10.1.d¶Severe Contraction Protocol — Activation

When the MA certifies that current gross revenue has fallen 25% below baseline in any fiscal year, the Severe Contraction Protocol activates automatically. Activation is not a political decision — it is a certification finding. The protocol allows the BNF floor to contract proportionally to actual economic capacity.

§10.1.e¶Severe Contraction Protocol — Effects

During a certified Severe Contraction, the BNF floor may be reduced proportionally — but never below a Severe Contraction minimum defined in statute. Even in crisis, equality of reduced provision is constitutionally mandated — the contraction cannot be used to differentially reduce provision for particular demographic groups.

§10.1.f¶Severe Contraction Protocol — Parliamentary Review

The Legislative Inspectorate reviews every Severe Contraction certification within 45 days and publishes a compliance report. The Legislature must hold a debate within 30 days of the LI report. If the Legislature finds the contraction is being used to target specific groups or evade the anti-evasion principle, it may override by 2/3 vote.

§10.1.g¶Federal Supplementation Obligation

The Legislature must by statute define the administrative infrastructure for BNF delivery within the first budget cycle after Phase 4 activation. Implementation statutes may specify delivery mechanisms and eligibility verification — but may not set the BNF below the constitutionally certified floor or exclude any Inhabitant category.

§10.4.a¶MA Director — Elected Office

The MA Director is elected by national RCV on National Election Day to a single non-renewable 7-year term, staggered from the board cycle. The board comprises five members nominated by the PM and confirmed by the Senate to staggered 5-year terms. The Director holds a veto over any board decision; the board may override by unanimous vote. Neither Director nor board members may hold any other government position.

§10.2¶Taxation Authority

Taxation authority originates exclusively in the House — no tax bill may originate in the Senate. The Fiscal Objection mechanism (§2.4.b) gives the PM a specialized check on tax legislation distinct from the ordinary suspensive veto. Within those constraints, the Legislature has broad authority to structure taxation as it sees fit — the constitution establishes the process, not the rates.

§10.2.a¶Tax Origination and Fiscal Objection

Legislative Pay Withholding During Budget Failure. Added in v138. Legislator pay withheld simultaneously with government employee pay when the 90-day continuation period expires and discretionary spending suspends. Parallel treatment, no special exemption for legislators. Withheld pay escrowed — released within 14 days of budget enactment. MA publishes daily running count of days withheld for both legislators and employees on NRS.

§10.2.b¶Annual Budget and Continuation Spending

the Legislature must pass an annual budget. Where the Legislature fails to pass a budget before the start of the fiscal year, a Continuation Spending Order automatically takes effect — maintaining prior-year spending levels at the BNF-adjusted rate for a period the Legislature defines by statute. The Continuation Order prevents budget disagreements from becoming constitutional crises: the Republic continues to function while the Legislature resolves its differences.

§10.3¶Deficit Controls

The Republic may not spend beyond projected revenue in any fiscal year without a 3/4 vote of both chambers simultaneously — the same extraordinary supermajority required for no-cause IG removal. This is a deliberate constitutional barrier to deficit spending. The BNF and Severe Contraction spending are explicitly exempted from this calculation — constitutional obligations that precede fiscal limits.

§10.3.a¶Inherited Deficit Transition Threshold

During any fiscal year in which the Republic's debt-to-GNR ratio exceeds the Inherited Deficit Threshold the MA certifies, the Legislature may run a controlled deficit by 2/3 vote rather than 3/4, provided the ratio is declining. This transitional provision prevents the new Republic from being immediately paralyzed by fiscal obligations inherited from its predecessor government.

§10.3.b¶BNF and Contraction Exemptions

The Basic Needs Floor and Severe Contraction supplementation obligations are exempted from deficit calculations during their respective activation periods. Constitutional fiscal obligations take precedence over constitutional fiscal limits — the hierarchy is explicit rather than left to judicial interpretation.

§10.4.b¶MA Independence

The Monetary Authority operates with constitutional independence from both executives. Neither may direct its certifications, modify its findings, or remove its Director except through the constitutional process.

§10.4.c¶Transparency

The MA must publish all decisions, models, and revenue assessments to the NRS in real time — including the models it uses to calculate GNR. The transparency mandate makes the MA's methodology auditable by anyone, closing the black-box fiscal assessment pathway.

§10.4.d¶Methodology Integrity

The MA must use a unified, internationally recognised national income accounting standard applied consistently year to year. Methodology changes require advance publication and public comment. The Legislature may not mandate a methodology that would produce a predetermined GNR figure. Any statute targeting a specific GNR outcome through methodology specification violates this provision.

§10.4.e¶Uncertifiable Inherited Fiscal Position

Where the joint Inspectorate panel certifies during transition that the inherited fiscal position cannot be determined accurately due to predecessor government concealment or record destruction, the Legislature may by supermajority establish a reasonable estimate. The estimated position becomes the constitutional baseline for §10.3.a deficit calculations.

§10.5.a¶Official Benefits

Standardized housing in session, no luxury stipends. All officials use the same healthcare, retirement, and public infrastructure as the citizens they serve. The extended equality standard: elected and appointed officials receive the same benefits and are subject to the same limitations as a civil service employee in a comparable role. Departure from this standard is permitted only where an official's constitutional responsibilities create a demonstrable operational need — presidential security, dedicated transport for secure communications — and only to the minimum extent that need requires. The principle covers all perquisites without listing them: travel, accommodation, equipment, staff ratios. The cooks eat what they expect their staff and customers to eat. The EI annual review and NRS transparency are the enforcement mechanisms.

§10.5.b¶Financial Disclosure

Every elected and appointed official must file a complete public financial disclosure within 30 days of taking office and annually thereafter. Disclosures are published to the NRS within 7 days of filing. Failure to file on time, material omission, or knowing misstatement is a constitutional violation, material omission, or knowing misstatement is a constitutional violation.

§10.5.c¶Integrity Bans

Officials are subject to a post-service lobbying ban of not less than five years. The Legislature may extend the ban but may not reduce it below five years. The minimum is constitutionally entrenched to prevent future parliaments from eroding the revolving-door restriction under lobbying pressure.

§10.5.d¶Anti-Ego Provision

No public works, buildings, programmes, infrastructure, or government institutions may be named after any living official. Applies to all levels of government. The prohibition is permanent during the official's lifetime. Posthumous naming is not restricted. The LI reports violations to the NRS.

§10.5.e¶Official Records Belong to the Republic

All documents, communications, records, and data created by any official in their official capacity belong to the Republic. Officials may not retain, destroy, or selectively disclose official records. Deletion or destruction of official records is constitutionally prohibited and reportable to the relevant Inspectorate.

§10.5.f¶Official Gifts

Gifts received in an official capacity belong to the Republic. Officials must declare any gift within a Legislature-defined period. Gifts above a statutory threshold are published to the NRS. The provision prevents gift-giving from becoming a mechanism for hidden compensation or influence.

§10.5.g¶Official Intellectual Property

Works created by officials using government resources, government time, government information, or access derived from official position belong to the Republic. Officials may not privately profit from intellectual work product created in their official capacity. Prevents officials from using public resources to create privately-owned intellectual property.

§10.5.h¶Oath of Office Requirement

Every official of the Federated Republic — elected, appointed, or career — must swear or affirm the constitutional oath before exercising any official function. The oath is a constitutional requirement, not a ceremony: an official who has not taken the oath holds no lawful authority.

§10.6.a¶Jury Duty

Citizens must serve on juries when called. Compensation is set at the national median daily income rate — ensuring jury service is not financially punishing. Unexcused refusal is a constitutional violation. The Legislature defines valid excuses by statute. No exemption may be granted based on occupation, social status, or government employment.

§10.6.b¶Defense Readiness and Veterans Obligations

The Republic relies on a volunteer military. Emergency mobilization is governed by Article XII. The Republic acknowledges a special obligation to persons who served in military or national service — distinct from the Basic Needs Floor. Veterans obligations are constitutional commitments that survive any government transition.

Article XI — Direct Democracy

§11.1 through §11.2 · Citizen petition and subsidiarity
§10.7¶Compensation of Public Officials

No government official may set their own compensation or their direct supervisors' compensation. All official compensation is set by House statute. Compensation rates must be published to the NRS. Any increase in official compensation takes effect only at the start of the next electoral cycle — the officials who vote for a pay increase cannot personally receive it until after the next election.

§11.1¶Optional Referendum

Citizens may force a referendum on any enacted statute, but not immediately or easily. The petition must meet a signature threshold; the Legislature has a response window to amend or repeal voluntarily before the referendum proceeds. The 50% participation threshold (§11.1.c) adds a second filter: a referendum majority among a small turnout cannot overturn legislation passed by a representative majority. This friction is intentional — it prevents well-organized minorities from using the referendum mechanism to serially attack legislative decisions they dislike. The mechanism empowers genuine popular majorities; it does not empower motivated minorities with superior mobilization capacity.

§11.1.a¶Citizen Petition Right

Citizens may pause the implementation of any newly enacted law by submitting verified signatures of 5% of eligible voters within 90 days of enactment. The 5% threshold requires genuine popular mobilization. The pause goes into effect immediately upon certification, preventing the law from being entrenched during the referendum period.

§11.1.b¶Eligibility Screen

Before certification, the Judicial Inspectorate screens the petition for eligibility — ensuring the law is referendum-eligible, that the signatures are genuine, and that the petition was filed within the window. The screening protects against procedural abuse without making certification a substantive barrier.

§11.1.c¶Referendum Thresholds and Finality

A referendum requires 50% participation to be valid — the participation floor ensures the result represents genuine democratic engagement, not a mobilized minority. A simple majority affirmative vote overturns the law. The Legislature may not re-enact substantially identical legislation within two years of a referendum result.

§11.2.a¶Subsidiarity Principle

Subsidiarity is the organizing principle of the federal-state relationship: governance at the lowest level capable of effectively addressing the problem, unless a specific constitutional provision requires otherwise. When the constitution is silent on whether a function belongs to federal or State government, subsidiarity answers: State, unless federal action is constitutionally necessary. This limits federal creep without requiring an exhaustive enumerated list of State powers — the default runs toward States, and the constitution specifies exceptions, not the other way around.

§11.2.b¶Local Government

Local governments — cities, counties, municipalities, and other subdivisions — receive constitutional recognition as the primary points of contact between citizens and government. They are entitled to reasonable fiscal capacity to carry out their constitutional functions. States may not starve local governments to achieve de facto state centralization.

Article XII — National Defense

§12.1 through §12.5 · Civilian command, military authority, and accountability
§12.2¶Legislative Declaration of Defense

Consolidated in v143 from former §12.2 and §12.6. Three provisions: §12.2.a Declaration requirements (2/3 both chambers, 180-day renewal, 5-year max, 90-day cessation). §12.2.b Aggression prohibition — §12.1 purposes exhaustive. §12.2.c Offensive operations require Tier 2 or Tier 3; President cannot act offensively on Tier 1 alone. §12.6.e preemptive bypass removed. Process detail moved to statute.

§12.2.a¶Legislative Declaration of Defense

A Legislative Declaration of Defense requires a 2/3 concurrent majority of both chambers simultaneously — one of the highest thresholds in the document. The concurrent requirement means the House and Senate must vote on the same Declaration; sequential approval where one chamber can walk back the other's commitment is not available.

§12.2.b¶Five-Year Maximum

The Republic may not initiate armed conflict against any sovereign nation or recognised government for conquest, regime change, resource acquisition, or territorial expansion. Tier 1 and Tier 2 operations are defensive or treaty-obligated by definition. Any operation outside the Tier definitions requires a full Article XII war declaration.

§12.2.c¶Successive Declaration Standard

A new Legislative Declaration of Defense issued within 12 months of a mandatory cessation must be based on a materially different threat — a distinct adversary, different geographic theater, or qualitatively different form of attack. Not a resumption of the same conflict under a different pretext. The Legislative Inspectorate reviews any such Declaration within 15 days and publishes a factual finding on whether the stated threat meets this standard. This closes the "perpetual war via rolling Declarations" pathway: a government cannot simply reissue the same Declaration in succession to circumvent the 30-day re-authorization and 5-year maximum.

§12.3¶Intelligence Firewall

All domestic intelligence operations against Republic citizens are permanently prohibited — the single clearest line in Article XII. Any order for domestic surveillance of Republic citizens is an automatic constitutional violation for the President and Director of Intelligence under §2.1.e, triggering immediate suspension and Senate trial within 7 days. The Firewall cannot be suspended by emergency declaration, treaty obligation, or legislative act. The President's intelligence domain ends at the Republic's own citizens. Foreign intelligence collection, counterintelligence against non-citizens, and physical border security remain within the presidential domain.

§12.2.d¶Senate Armed Services Committee

The Senate Armed Services Committee exercises Tier 2 military authorization. SASC authorization must happen within 72 hours of presidential notification. When the presidential succession line is active, the SASC Chairman is the first civilian authority to whom military commanders report.

§12.2.e¶Military Authority During Presidential Succession Vacuum

When the presidential succession line is fully exhausted, the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs may continue operations already underway and issue orders strictly necessary to protect Republic forces from active, immediate attack — return fire and immediate defensive maneuvering only. No initiation, repositioning, or escalation. All orders published to the NRS within one hour. Maximum 14 days.

§12.1¶Authorized Purposes for Military Force

The Republic authorizes military force for three and only three purposes: defense of the Republic's territory from armed attack, protection of Republic citizens abroad facing imminent threat, and fulfillment of ratified mutual defense treaty obligations. Nation-building, resource extraction, ideological projection, and preemptive offensive war are not constitutionally authorized. A military deployment for any other purpose is unconstitutional regardless of any executive or parliamentary authorization.

§12.4¶Military Accountability

Extended occupations — deployments lasting more than 10 years without Senate-approved renewal — are unconstitutional and must terminate within 90 days. Inhabitants of any territory under Republic military occupation for more than one year may petition to join the Republic through the §13.6 voluntary incorporation process. The Republic may not use the prospect of Territory status as an inducement to consent to or extend a military occupation.

§12.5.a¶Treaty Emergency Certification

When a ratified mutual defense treaty is triggered by an attack on a treaty partner, the PM may issue a Treaty Emergency Certification within 48 hours. The PM's certification initiates the parliamentary authorization process; it does not authorize military action by itself.

§12.5.c¶No Blank Check

The Treaty Emergency Certification covers operational costs for the initial response and triggers parliamentary authorization for any extended engagement. Treaties cannot be blank checks for extended military commitment for extended military commitment — each sustained engagement requires parliamentary authorization regardless of what the treaty says.

Article XIII — National Expansion and State Devolution

§13.1 through §13.4 · Territory, statehood, and the devolution architecture
§13.3.e¶Constitutional Challenge to Devolution

Within 30 days of a Senate Devolution vote, the affected State may petition the SC for expedited review on a single narrow ground: whether the audit failures met the constitutional standard. The Court reviews facts and legal standard — it cannot substitute its governance judgment for the LI's assessment. The narrow review scope preserves the audit's constitutional function.

§13.4¶Voluntary Devolution

A State may choose to become a Territory through a voluntary devolution referendum requiring a two-thirds vote of residents with a minimum 10-year Citizen Voting Credential. The constitutional pathway runs inward as well as outward — the threshold is two-thirds, consistent with other significant constitutional decisions throughout the document.

§13.4.a¶Voluntary Devolution Vote

A State may voluntarily return to Territory status by a 2/3 vote of residents who have held a Citizen Voting Credential for minimum 10 years. The 10-year residency requirement ensures the decision reflects a settled community preference — not a temporary political majority that includes recent arrivals. The path back to Statehood is always available; there is no permanent devolution.

§13.4.b¶Fiscal Transparency Requirement

Before a voluntary devolution vote, the MA must publish a Fiscal Incidence Study showing what the State currently pays and receives from the federal system, and what it would pay and receive as a Territory. Citizens vote on the basis of accurate information about the economic consequences.

§13.4.c¶Net-Contributor Transparency

The voluntary devolution vote is conducted through the NVS. Only residents who have held a Citizen Voting Credential for the minimum 10-year period may vote — ensuring the decision reflects a settled community preference. Simple majority of eligible long-term residents required. The vote must be preceded by a published framework agreement specifying transition terms.

§13.4.d¶Effect of Voluntary Devolution

Voluntary devolution produces: Senate seats vacated; House seats retained proportionally; BNF distributions continue; tax treatment adjusts per statute. The State carries its entire legal history. Neither the devolution nor the subsequent Audit cycle erases prior legal commitments or obligations.

§13.4.e¶Negotiated Devolution Pathway

A State may request a formal negotiated devolution agreement before voting — defining transitional arrangements, any federal commitments, and the relationship between prior State obligations and future Territory status. The negotiated agreement must be published to the NRS before the devolution vote. Citizens vote on a defined arrangement, not an abstract status change.

§13.6¶Voluntary Incorporation

The inbound pathway the document previously lacked — a sovereign entity voluntarily joining the Republic's constitutional framework as a Territory. Requires 2/3 legislature, confirmatory referendum, JI assessment, and 2/3 parliamentary acceptance.

§13.1¶Territorial Compact

Territories hold all Article I rights. Senate votes by simple majority on Territory petitions; Article I protections are immediate upon petition regardless of vote outcome. Tax and BNF treatment differ by how Territory status was acquired: new territories (entering voluntarily) are BNF-exempt until Statehood; devolution territories retain BNF distributions. The constitutional principle: rights are not contingent on Statehood.

§13.2¶The Statehood Audit

A Territory seeking Statehood must pass a comprehensive Statehood Audit conducted by the LI, covering: fiscal integrity (§13.2.a), NRS connectivity, Article I legal compliance (§13.2.b), institutional capacity, and Competency Baseline certification. The audit is annual. Passing three consecutive audits without failure qualifies a Territory for a Statehood petition.

§13.2.a¶Fiscal Integrity

NRS connectivity infrastructure and a projected revenue ratio demonstrating the Territory can sustain fiscal obligations without federal assistance beyond the BNF. The MA certifies the revenue ratio as part of the Statehood Audit. Fiscal sustainability is a necessary condition for Statehood — not punitive but reflecting that Statehood confers Senate seats with real governance obligations.

§13.2.b¶Legal Sync

All local statutes must be audited for compliance with Article I by the LI, with findings published to the NRS before the Provisional Membership Period may begin. The audit is a compliance review, not a political assessment — a Territory may have diverse statutory preferences, but none may violate the Individual Sovereignty Floor.

§13.2.c¶The Provisional Membership Period

Upon passing the Statehood Audit, a territory enters a Provisional Membership Period during which it sends non-voting observers to both chambers. House seats are allocated by population; Senate seats activate at full Statehood. The Provisional Membership Period creates a structured transition rather than an immediate jump to full participation.

§13.2.d¶Partial Territory Statehood

A defined geographic subdivision of an existing Territory may petition for Statehood independently of the rest of the Territory. Prevents a large Territory's failure from permanently blocking a compliant subdivision from achieving Statehood. Each petitioning subdivision completes its own Statehood Audit independently.

§13.2.e¶Minimum Democratic Legitimacy

Three functional requirements every State must maintain: electoral accountability, judicial independence, and public law governance. Both EC technical certification and LI political assessment are required.

§13.2.f¶Territorial Minimum Institutional Floor

Every Territory must maintain: (1) a governance council with defined membership and published authority; (2) resident access to federal judicial review of executive action; and (3) direct enforcement of Article I rights for all Territorial residents by...

§13.2.g¶Conflict-Affected State Provisional Status

Protects States in genuine crisis without sheltering States whose governance created the crisis. Joint Inspectorate assessment, five-year maximum, and default joint administration close exploitable pathways.

§13.3¶Mandatory Devolution

Mandatory State Devolution is the constitutional consequence of sustained audit failure — three consecutive years of failing the Statehood Audit trigger a Senate Motion of Devolution. The Early Warning system (§13.3.b) provides two years of remediation before Devolution is considered. The 3/4 Senate supermajority requirement for Devolution ensures it cannot be used as a political weapon. The path back to full Statehood is always available.

§13.3.a¶Devolution Triggers

A State enters Mandatory Devolution after three consecutive Statehood Audit failures. The consecutive rule matters: the countdown resets to zero if the State passes an audit in the interim. This prevents a State from being caught in permanent devolution proceedings by a single anomalous bad year.

§13.3.b¶Early Warning — Years 1 and 2

Upon a first consecutive audit failure, the LI publishes an Early Warning Report to the NRS, distributed to all eligible voters in the State. The State government and the Domestic Affairs Council must jointly publish a Remediation Plan within the statutory period; the LI reviews it and publishes a finding on its adequacy. Grant withholding activates automatically if the LI finds the Plan inadequate and the PM takes no action. Early Warning is the constitution's remedial mechanism: most States that receive it never reach Devolution.

§13.3.c¶Devolution Vote — Year 3

Upon the third consecutive audit failure, the Senate votes on a Motion of Devolution requiring a 3/4 supermajority. The high threshold means Mandatory Devolution requires near-consensus in the States' chamber — it cannot be used as a political weapon against a State by a partisan majority.

§13.3.d¶Effect of Mandatory Devolution

A devolved State loses its Senate seats on the date the Devolution Motion passes. It retains House seats proportional to population. Federal law continues to apply. The State government continues as territorial administration under federal oversight. All effects are published to the NRS the moment the Motion passes.

Article XIV — The Indigenous Nation Compact

§14.1 through §14.5 · Indigenous sovereignty and rights
§14.5.d¶Pre-Existing Sovereignty Pathway

An indigenous nation whose sovereignty is constitutionally recognized under Article XIV may petition for Associated Community status under Article XX without completing the independence process. The pathway acknowledges that recognized sovereign nations need not gain formal independence they already hold in constitutional terms — their sovereignty exists independently of Republic recognition.

§14.3.a¶Free, Prior, and Informed Consent

FPIC operationalizes the government-to-government relationship. "Free" means without coercion, inducement, or manipulation. "Prior" means before the decision — not post-hoc consultation as a procedural formality. "Informed" means full disclosure of scope, alternatives, and consequences. Consent must come from the indigenous nation's own governance processes, not individuals selected or approved by federal authorities. FPIC is a genuine prerequisite, not a courtesy: when the Uyghur Nation refused a water diversion project under its compact rights in the China Scenario, the project was not built. The only area where FPIC does not apply is enforcement of the unamendable Article I prohibitions — those apply universally within all jurisdictions without requiring consent.

§14.3.b¶Unamendable Rights Override

The FPIC requirement does not apply to enforcement of the six permanently unamendable provisions within indigenous jurisdictions. These six rights can be enforced without indigenous consent — they represent the floor below which even negotiated sovereignty cannot go. The Republic can enforce freedom from torture and non-refoulement regardless of any compact.

§14.3.c¶Emergency Rights Writ

Any individual within an indigenous jurisdiction who alleges an Article I violation may petition the SC for an Emergency Rights Writ. The Writ requires the indigenous nation to address the specific violation within a defined period or demonstrate it did not occur. The government-to-government relationship is maintained throughout.

§14.3.d¶Internal Enforcement Support

With the consent of the relevant indigenous nation, the Republic may provide technical or financial support for the development of internal rights enforcement mechanisms within the nation's own legal traditions. The operative word is consent — the Republic may not unilaterally impose an enforcement framework on an indigenous nation in the name of rights protection. The design authority remains with the nation; the Republic's role is supportive, not directive.

§14.3.e¶Inspectorate Jurisdiction Limit

The Inspectorates' audit jurisdiction does not extend to the internal governance of indigenous nations — with one exception: where a specific allegation of violation of a permanently unamendable provision is made against an individual member, the Supreme Court has jurisdiction on petition. The scope is narrow and individual: general governance audits of indigenous nations are constitutionally prohibited; targeted judicial proceedings for specific rights violations against specific persons are available. The Inspectorates observe the line.

§14.1¶Acknowledgment of Prior Sovereignty

The Republic acknowledges that indigenous nations held sovereign authority over their ancestral territories before the Republic's founding. This acknowledgment is not a land rights determination — it is a constitutional recognition of historical fact, forming the basis for the government-to-government relationship in §§14.2-14.5.

§14.4.a¶Eminent Domain Prohibition

The Republic may not acquire indigenous lands by eminent domain or any other mechanism without the voluntary consent of the indigenous nation, expressed through a free democratic act following full disclosure of purpose, scope, and compensation. No national security exception, no emergency exception. Indigenous land acquisition requires genuine consent, period.

§14.4.b¶Conservation Trust

The Republic may not designate indigenous lands as National Trust, conservation territory, or any federal use right without Free, Prior, and Informed Consent from the relevant indigenous nation. This prohibition applies regardless of the stated purpose of the designation.

§14.4.c¶National Record System

Indigenous nations may voluntarily register land boundaries and governance documents on the NRS. Registration is not compelled and does not alter land rights or governance authority. The NRS entry is a transparency and documentation tool — it does not constitute recognition of boundaries otherwise disputed.

§14.5.a¶Article I Rights

Every individual member of an indigenous nation is an Inhabitant of the Republic and holds the full protections of Article I, enforceable directly by federal courts regardless of the nation's internal governance structure. Indigenous nations hold collective sovereignty; their members hold individual constitutional rights. The two coexist.

§14.5.b¶Dual Citizenship

Members of indigenous nations may hold both indigenous national citizenship and Republic citizenship simultaneously. Neither citizenship extinguishes the other. No Republic statute may penalise dual citizenship in this context.

§14.5.c¶National Voting System — Voluntary Connection

Indigenous nations may voluntarily connect to the National Voting System for their members' participation in Republic-level elections. Connection is not compelled — the NVS infrastructure is available; its use is the nation's choice. The electoral infrastructure of indigenous nations for their own internal governance is a matter of indigenous sovereign authority and is entirely outside the Republic's constitutional mandate. The Republic may not condition any benefit, treaty obligation, or federal support on NVS connection for internal elections.

§14.2¶Treaty Supremacy and Continuity

All treaties entered into by predecessor governments with indigenous nations are binding obligations of the Republic. Treaty rights cannot be diminished by legislation — they must be honored, renegotiated with consent, or formally superseded through the FPIC framework.

Article XV — Amending the Constitution

§15.1 through §15.6 · Amendment paths, independence framework, and the unamendable floor
§14.6¶Indigenous Nation Status Elections

Indigenous nations hold the constitutional right to initiate a status change process at ratification and at any subsequent time through their own governance process. The right to initiate is continuous; the change takes effect only when the constitutional process for the relevant transition is complete. Four options: (a) Article XIV compact — the default, no action required; (b) Associated Community status under Article XX, available directly via §14.5.d; (c) Territorial integration via the two-step §14.5.d → §13.6 pathway; (d) Full sovereign independence — the Republic must negotiate a treaty within 24 months covering transit, trade, citizenship, and infrastructure; geographic position within Republic territory does not affect the right. The Republic may not prefer, discourage, or offer inducements toward any option. Return path after independence: a nation that completes independence under (d) may not re-elect Article XIV status — it must use §20 or §13.6 as any sovereign.

§15.1¶Two Amendment Paths

Three amendment paths, each calibrated to the nature and significance of the change. §15.1.a Parliamentary alone (3/4 of both chambers — for technical corrections); §15.1.b Parliamentary with popular confirmation (2/3 of both chambers plus 55% national vote / 55% participation — for significant changes); §15.1.c Citizen-Led (70% national vote / 65% participation — for when citizens wish to lead change the Legislature will not). The State Convention path was replaced with a national popular vote option.

§15.1.a¶Parliamentary Path

Ratification by a three-quarters supermajority of the full seated membership of both chambers simultaneously. Individual voting records published to the NRS within 24 hours. Appropriate for technical corrections, gap-filling, and amendments with broad cross-party consensus.

§15.1.b¶Parliamentary and Popular Path

A two-thirds supermajority of both chambers, followed by a national popular vote achieving 55% affirmative support with 55% participation. The Legislature leads; the people confirm. The popular vote must be held within 180 days of the legislative vote. For significant constitutional changes where democratic mandate beyond the Legislature matters.

§15.1.c¶Citizen-Led Path

A national vote achieving 70% affirmative with 65% participation — the highest threshold, for constitutional changes the people wish to make when the Legislature will not act. Near-miss provision: if participation falls between 60-65% and affirmative exceeds 75%, the EC automatically schedules a second vote within 12 months (no sooner than 90 days). The second vote uses the full 70%/65% standard — the threshold never moves. If the second vote also fails, the amendment fails with no further re-vote available on that text. No institution decides whether the re-vote is merited; the provision self-executes. Amended v149.

§15.3.a¶Permanently Unamendable Provisions

Six provisions are permanently unamendable: the prohibitions on torture (§1.2), slavery (§1.3), and non-refoulement (§1.27); habeas corpus (§1.13); the right to a public trial (§1.17); and the prohibition on retroactive punishment (§1.18). These may not be altered, qualified, or removed by any amendment process by any amendment process.

§15.4.a¶Territorial Integrity

The territorial integrity of the Republic is not subject to unilateral dissolution. Independence may only be recognized by a 70% affirmative national popular vote via the National Voting System with at least 65% participation — a national threshold, not the departing territory's vote alone. Advisory referenda (§15.4.b) may gauge sentiment but have no legal effect. The transition process following a successful vote is governed by §15.5.

§15.5.d¶Executive Authority During Transition

The President holds negotiating authority for the international dimensions of the framework agreement — treaty relationship, diplomatic recognition, foreign property. The PM holds authority over domestic transition matters — civil service continuity, existing contracts, BNF transition. The dual executive's domain structure applies to independence negotiations as it applies to ordinary governance.

§15.6¶Supremacy of This Constitution

This Constitution is the supreme law of the Federated Republic. All legislative acts, executive orders, treaties, administrative decisions, and government actions of any kind must be consistent with it. Any provision of law inconsistent with this Constitution is, to the extent of the inconsistency, without legal force or effect.

§15.6.b¶Federal-State Statutory Hierarchy

Federal statutes enacted within the Legislature's constitutional competence prevail over conflicting State statutes. Where federal and State statutes address the same subject without conflict — where a person can comply with both simultaneously — both operate concurrently and the State statute is not displaced. Courts apply the conflict test and the obstacle test before finding preemption; field preemption by implication requires clear evidence of legislative intent. The Legislature may expressly state whether a federal enactment occupies a field exclusively, permits concurrent State regulation, or sets a floor. This gives States the maximum space to legislate above the federal floor without creating unpredictable preemption from federal silence.

§15.2¶No Executive Veto Over Amendments

Neither the President nor the PM holds any veto, delay power, or objection authority over constitutional amendments. Amendments ratified through any §15.1 path take effect upon Inspectorate certification and NRS publication — executives have no role in the process.

§15.2.a¶No Executive Veto

Neither the President nor the PM holds veto authority over constitutional amendments. Amendments take immediate effect when the Inspectorates certify ratification and publish to the NRS. Constitutional change belongs to the People and Legislature — not executives. Distinct from ordinary legislation where both executives have response mechanisms.

§15.4.b¶Advisory Referenda

States may conduct advisory referenda on any political question, including independence — provided they are explicitly labeled non-binding and do not purport to have legal effect. Advisory results are political expression protected under §1.4 and §1.7. They do not constitute steps toward independence recognition under §15.4.a and cannot trigger legal consequences on their own.

§15.5¶Independence Transition Framework

The independence transition framework (§15.5.a through §15.5.e) governs the period between a successful §15.4 independence referendum and formal independence. Key provisions: the Framework Agreement must be negotiated before independence takes effect; a minimum 24-month and maximum 60-month transition period; the BNF continues through the transition; the Republic's President holds negotiating authority for international dimensions; simultaneous independence processes are sequenced to prevent institutional collapse.

§15.5.a¶Framework Agreement Requirement

Before any §15.4 independence referendum is held, the Republic and the proposed independent territory must negotiate and publish a Framework Agreement specifying: the transition timeline, treatment of existing obligations, citizenship options for residents, currency and fiscal arrangements, and the new entity's relationship to the Republic. Citizens vote on a defined arrangement, not an abstract status change.

§15.5.b¶Transition Period — 24 to 60 Months

Formal independence takes effect no sooner than 24 months and no later than 60 months after referendum certification. The transition period is governed by the Framework Agreement. Republic obligations to Territory residents continue during the transition as specified in the Agreement. The timeline cannot be accelerated below 24 months by any unilateral action.

§15.5.c¶Basic Needs Floor Transition

Despite §10.1 applying to Republic Inhabitants, the Republic's obligations to the departing territory during the transition period must be specified in the framework agreement. Independence does not end Republic obligations to former Territory residents immediately. The transition period is a period of defined ongoing obligation.

Article XVI — The National Voting System and Electoral Commission

§16.1 through §16.7 · Electoral Commission, the Citizen Voting Credential, and the NVS
§16.1–16.2¶Electoral Commission

The Electoral Commission is five members, all elected by citizens at staggered intervals. No member may have held elected office or party membership within ten years of appointment — the independence requirement is structural, not aspirational. The Commission administers all national elections, certifies candidate registers, and is the final authority on electoral integrity. Because it administers the elections of the very officials who might otherwise control it, its independence from all political branches is the condition of its legitimacy. Its own members are elected, giving it a democratic mandate that does not flow from the actors whose elections it oversees.

§16.2¶Electoral Commission Qualifications

Electoral Commission members must meet three independently verified requirements: at least 15 years of professional expertise in elections, technology, or public administration; no current or recent partisan affiliation; and passage of the Constitutional Logic Test. The qualifications are substantive — the EC requires genuine expertise.

§16.2.a¶Electoral Commission Removal

For-cause only — no no-cause removal. EC administers all elections; no-cause removal would be a vector for electoral manipulation. Process: JI documents finding → State Designee Panel substantiates → 14-day SC appeal window expires (or SC upholds on appeal) → member suspended from all EC duties. The 180-day election proximity restriction in §16.5.c applies to the removal vote, not to Panel-triggered suspension. On legislative acquittal, member returns to full duties immediately. 2/3 both chambers for removal. Vacancy: 90-day special election, full new 6-year term. Amended v148.

§16.3¶NVS Properties

Three constitutionally required properties of the National Voting System: ballot secrecy (technically impossible to link any vote to any voter), public auditability (aggregate results mathematically verifiable by anyone with NRS access), and resilience (capable of operating in degraded conditions without compromising either secrecy or auditability). These properties are independently required — neither can be sacrificed for the other.

§16.3.a¶Ballot Secrecy

No person or institution — including Electoral Commission members — may identify how any individual citizen voted. Ballot secrecy is constitutionally absolute. The NVS must make it technically impossible to link any vote to any voter, even in post-election audits.

§16.3.b¶Public Auditability

Every vote cast through the NVS must be individually verifiable by the voter who cast it, without revealing that voter's identity to any other party. The NVS must produce an audit trail that allows independent verification of aggregate results without individual identification. Both requirements must be simultaneously satisfied.

§16.3.c¶Resilience and Privacy

The NVS must operate in degraded conditions without compromising ballot secrecy or auditability. No information generated by the NVS — including voter participation records — may be shared with any government body, law enforcement, or foreign entity for any purpose other than administering the relevant election or referendum. Voter participation data is constitutionally protected even from the government.

§16.4.a¶The Citizen Voting Credential

The Citizen Voting Credential is issued and managed by the Electoral Commission. The system stores only cryptographic identifiers — not names, biometric data, or personal information — ensuring the link between a citizen and their credential cannot be used to...

§16.4.b¶Remote Voting

Legislature may by statute authorize authenticated remote voting through the NVS. Remote voting must meet the same cryptographic and auditability standards as in-person NVS voting. No remote voting system may reduce ballot secrecy or auditability below the in-person standard.

§16.4.c¶State Voting Method Flexibility

States may implement early voting periods, mail-in ballot options, and alternative participation methods, provided all methods connect to the NVS and meet its cryptographic and auditability standards. States may not reduce NVS access or capacity as a condition of offering alternative methods. Added v143.

§16.4.d¶Federal Ballot Information

Before each federal election window opens, the Electoral Commission delivers to every eligible citizen: the list of federal races on their ballot, the NVS access procedures, and the certification timeline. Delivery must reach every eligible citizen through at least two distinct channels. Added v143.

§16.4.e¶State Ballot Information on Request

At a state's request, EC may extend ballot information delivery to cover state and local races. State bears cost. EC cannot refuse except on operational capacity grounds. Entirely voluntary. New in v143.

§16.5.a¶Campaign Funding

Campaigns for Electoral Commission seats are publicly funded in full. No private money may be spent on EC campaigns. The EC administers elections for all other offices — it cannot itself be captured by the interests that it would regulate in other campaigns.

§16.5.b¶JI Oversight

The Judicial Inspectorate audits the Electoral Commission annually. The JI is the appropriate Inspectorate for EC oversight — it audits judicial independence, and electoral integrity has similar structural properties. JI audit findings on the EC are published to the NRS and presented to both chambers.

§16.5.c¶Removal

For-cause only, 2/3 both chambers simultaneously, preceded by State Designee Panel verification. 180-day election proximity rule: any removal vote within 180 days of a major national election is automatically suspended pending SC review within 48 hours. Critically: the proximity restriction applies to the removal vote only — Panel-triggered suspension of duties under §16.2.a is not subject to the proximity window. Amended v148.

§16.5.d¶Mid-Term Vacancy

Where any Electoral Commission seat becomes vacant mid-term, a special election is held within 90 days through the NVS. The replacement serves a full new non-renewable 6-year term from certification — not the remainder of the vacated term. The Commission operates under its active-membership threshold during the vacancy period.

§16.6¶Electoral Commission Technical Deadlock

Legislature must establish by statute a procedure for the EC to adopt temporary technical measures in imminent security threats or system failures, bypassing the normal 3/5 decision threshold. The emergency power is narrowly constrained — it cannot change electoral rules, timelines, or candidate eligibility. A system-integrity tool only.

§16.7¶Day Zero Voting System Transition

Legislature must establish by statute the transition from any predecessor voting infrastructure to the constitutionally required NVS. The statute must ensure continuity of democratic participation — no gap between the predecessor system going offline and the NVS coming online. The ratification vote itself was conducted on the NVS (§19.1).

Article XVII — Federal Property and the Seat of Government

§17.1 through §17.5 · Federal property, seat of government, and National Trust
§17.1¶The Use Doctrine

Federal government holds no territorial sovereignty within any State or Territory. All federal property rights are functional use rights — operational control over a designated footprint for the duration of the stated function. Use rights do not constitute ownership, do not transfer sovereignty from the host State, and do not survive the termination of the function that justified them. Every use right must be on the NRS.

§17.2¶Seat of Government

The Republic does not have a permanent capital city with territorial sovereignty. The Seat of Government is hosted by a consenting State — the host retaining sovereignty outside the specific federal footprint. The Republic exercises functional authority, not territorial sovereignty, within the footprint. This design prevents the accumulation of federal territorial power over a de facto national enclave that characterizes most existing federal capitals.

§17.2.a¶Host State Consent

The host State consents to hosting the Seat of Government by a majority vote of its legislature, recorded on the NRS. Consent is explicit, documented, and revocable. A State cannot be involuntarily assigned as the host.

§17.2.b¶Federal Control Within the Footprint

Within the specific registered footprint, the federal government has exclusive operational control. Host State may not tax federal operations, interfere with federal functions, or apply State law to federal employees acting in official capacity on federal grounds. The footprint is fixed — expansion requires a new Host State Agreement. Federal control is functional, not sovereign.

§17.2.c¶Resident Rights

No State may be involuntarily designated as the permanent seat of government. A State may withdraw consent to host the seat of government by a 2/3 vote of its legislature, subject to a statutory transition period during which the Republic must identify a new host or trigger the provisional host designation under §17.2.e.

§17.2.d¶Reversion on Relocation

If the seat of government relocates, the prior host State recovers full sovereignty over all previously designated grounds upon completion of the statutory transition period. Individual facilities remaining in active operational use may be retained as Operational Use Rights by mutual agreement. All improvements revert to the host State unless otherwise negotiated within the statutory window.

§17.2.e¶Provisional Seat — No Voluntary Host

If no State consents to host the seat within 24 months of ratification — or within 24 months of a host State withdrawing consent — the Legislature may by 2/3 vote designate any State as provisional host. The provisional designation holds until a State volunteers; the designated State may not be involuntarily permanent.

§17.3.a¶Registration

Every federal operational use right must be registered on the NRS with precise geographic boundaries, authorized purpose, and termination conditions. There are no unregistered federal land claims. Federal property rights are explicit, bounded, and auditable.

§17.3.b¶Termination and Reversion

When a federal function ends, the Operational Use Right terminates automatically. The federal government must publish notice of termination to the NRS within 30 days. The land and all improvements revert to the host State automatically unless the federal government and host State negotiate a retention agreement within the statutory settlement window.

§17.3.c¶Improvements — Negotiated Settlement

All improvements on federal Operational Use Rights default to reverting to the host State with the land. The federal government and host State may negotiate a different arrangement within the statutory reversion window. No agreement means automatic reversion at no cost. The burden is on the federal government to negotiate retention — not on the State to purchase back.

§17.3.d¶Disputes

Disputes over use right boundaries, termination, or improvement valuation go to the SC on an expedited track. Neither party may occupy or alter disputed land during pending resolution. The expedited track ensures federal operational continuity — resolution must happen fast enough to maintain the relevant federal function.

§17.3.e¶Independence Transition

Where a State achieves independence through §15.4 and §15.5, automatic reversion under §17.3.b does not immediately apply to Operational Use Rights within the departing territory. Use rights continue under the terms of the framework agreement and independence treaty negotiated under §15.5.a. This prevents independence from instantly stranding critical infrastructure with no transition arrangement.

§17.4¶The National Trust

The National Trust holds all federal land not conveyed by statute to States, indigenous compact territories, or private parties. Land disposal requires statute — the executive cannot convey or encumber public land unilaterally, preventing privatization by executive discretion. The National Trust Administrator is independently confirmed under the §2.2.h escalation framework, insulating land management from PM pressure. The Trust is structurally distinct from the Community Land Trust: the NTA manages federal holdings; the CLT Administrator manages affordable residential land held in trust for Inhabitants.

§17.4.a¶Trust Nature

National Trust lands are designated as permanent heritage, ecological, or public recreation resources. Trust designation requires a 2/3 vote of both chambers with host State consent. Trust lands are not subject to Operational Use Right termination. The stewardship obligation (§17.4.b) applies from the date of designation.

§17.4.b¶Stewardship Obligation

The federal government has an active, enforceable obligation to maintain ecological integrity and public accessibility of National Trust lands. EI monitors stewardship compliance annually, cross-referenced with §1.22 (Right to a Healthy Environment). Failure triggers a mandatory remediation plan reported to the NRS. The Trust carries active obligations — not passive title.

§17.4.c¶Co-Management

States and indigenous nations may enter co-management agreements with the Domestic Affairs Council for National Trust lands within their borders or ancestral territories. Co-management does not transfer Trust ownership or alter the constitutional Trust status. Indigenous nations' participation reflects the §14.3 government-to-government relationship for lands within their ancestral territories.

§17.4.d¶Reclassification Threshold

Land may be removed from National Trust designation only by a 2/3 vote of both chambers with host State consent, followed by SC review within 60 days. The SC reviews whether the removal complies with any applicable international obligations. Removal is permanent — land removed from Trust does not automatically revert to Trust status.

§17.4.e¶International Sites

National Trust lands recognized under international conservation agreements require a 2/3 Senate vote to withdraw from the relevant international obligation before the national removal process begins. The Republic cannot quietly delist internationally recognized sites without formal democratic action.

§17.4.f¶Admission of New Trust Lands

Land may be designated National Trust by a 2/3 vote of both chambers with host State consent (land in Territories: 2/3 vote alone). No executive action may designate Trust lands — designation is a legislative act. Prevents executive land grabs under Trust designation and executive stripping of Trust status.

§17.4.g¶National Trust Administrator

The National Trust is administered by a National Trust Administrator nominated by the PM from a Candidate Register. EI reviews the disclosure record for factual accuracy and publishes with recommendation before the confirming chamber votes. The NTA's independence from the political executive reflects the Trust's long-term stewardship mandate — management horizons that extend far beyond any electoral cycle.

§17.5¶Federal Property in Territories

Federal use rights in Territories follow the same framework as in States — Operational Use Rights (§17.3) and National Trust (§17.4) — without requiring host State legislative consent (Territories have no State legislature). The Territory's representative body and the LI are consulted. Same constitutional principles; consent mechanism adapts to the Territory's structure.

Article XVIII — Electoral Finance and Campaign Integrity

§18.1 through §18.3
§18.1.b¶Natural Persons Only

Only individual human beings who are citizens or legal residents may make financial contributions to campaigns. Corporations, associations, political parties, unions, and any non-human entity are excluded entirely. This complements the explicit rejection of money-as-speech (§18.1.a): organizations have no speech rights in the electoral context because they are not the citizens the electoral system exists to serve. The provision closes the primary route through which organizational wealth enters campaign finance — not through named donors but through entities whose beneficial ownership is diffuse or entirely non-citizen. The "natural persons only" rule makes the source of every campaign contribution traceable to an individual human being.

§18.1.c¶Voter-Donor Parity

Only a citizen eligible to vote in a particular election may make financial contributions to candidates or measures in that election. The voter-donor parity rule connects electoral giving to electoral eligibility: you may give money only to elections in which you may vote.

§18.1.d¶Full Disclosure

All financial transactions connected to any election must be disclosed publicly and in real time to the Campaign Finance Registry. Real-time disclosure means contributions are visible before the election, not only in post-election reports. Citizens can see who is funding whom before they vote.

§18.2.a¶Qualification Threshold

Candidates qualify for public funding by demonstrating sufficient popular support within a defined pre-campaign period — through petition signatures, small-donor counts, or a combination defined by statute. The threshold must be achievable by a credible candidate without existing financial backing, screening out frivolous candidacies without excluding serious ones.

§18.2.b¶Adequate Allocation

The public campaign allocation must be sufficient to allow a qualified candidate to conduct a meaningful campaign — reaching voters through the major communication channels available in the election cycle. Legislature reviews adequacy after each election and adjusts. A system providing nominal amounts insufficient to reach voters fails this standard.

§18.2.c¶Optional Participation

Candidates may decline public funding and campaign entirely on private contributions subject to the Voter-Donor Parity rule and statutory spending limits. Publicly funded candidates cannot coordinate with independent expenditure groups receiving private money. The opt-out preserves constitutional space while maintaining system integrity.

§18.2.d¶EC Administration

EC administers the public funding system: verifying qualification, distributing allocated funds, auditing compliance. EI provides annual audit findings to NRS. EC administration ensures the public funding system is run by the same independent body that administers the elections — not a government agency with political interests.

§18.3.a¶Separation from the Voting System

The Campaign Finance Registry is constitutionally separated from the National Voting System — different infrastructure, different administrators, different security protocols. Combining them would allow contribution history to be linked to voting behavior. The separation is a constitutional privacy protection.

§18.3.b¶Registry Contents

The Registry publicly records: contributor identity, amount, date, recipient, and the purpose of each expenditure. The level of detail is constitutionally mandated — partial disclosure systems that reveal totals but conceal sources do not satisfy the constitutional requirement.

§18.3.c¶Aggregate Attribution

Any organisation receiving more than 50% of its electoral spending funds from a single citizen must disclose the identity of that citizen as the functional donor in the Campaign Finance Registry. Pass-through structures that obscure individual donor identity are constitutionally prohibited.

§18.3.d¶Administration

EC administers the Campaign Finance Registry. EI audits annually and publishes findings as part of the Annual State of the Republic Report. All Registry data is publicly accessible on the NRS. The Registry is a transparency tool — enforcement is for the courts and Legislature acting on what the Registry reveals.

§18.1.a¶Financial Contributions as Electoral Acts

Financial contributions to electoral campaigns are not constitutionally protected speech under §1.4, and are not protected as associational activity under §1.7. The regulation of electoral finance is a matter of democratic integrity explicitly reserved from both speech and associational protection. One of the most direct rejections of Citizens United reasoning in the document.

Article XIX — Ratification and Transition

§19.1 through §19.9 · How the Republic comes into being
§19.1¶The Activation Threshold

Three subsections after v145 restructuring: activation threshold (§19.1.a), institutional accommodation for existing legal structures (§19.1.b), and multi-founder formation for sovereign nations pooling sovereignty (§19.1.c). Previously a single wall-of-text paragraph — restructuring makes §19.1 readable without changing any substantive content.

§19.1.a¶Activation Threshold

Activated upon: (1) interim EC certification that the NVS is operational and meets §16.3; and (2) a national ratification vote achieving 60% affirmative support with at least 50% participation. The NVS certification requirement ensures the system that conducts the ratification vote meets constitutional standards before the vote itself.

§19.1.b¶Institutional Accommodation

Where the ratifying polity maintains existing institutions overlapping with constitutional mechanisms, the Legislature must by statute within two years of Phase 4 define their relationship with the constitutional framework. Non-diminishment: accommodation statutes may not reduce legal standing of pre-existing governance structures below their pre-Phase 4 status. 2/3 of both chambers required to amend.

§19.1.c¶Multi-Founder Formation

Where multiple sovereign nations simultaneously pool sovereignty through a treaty of union, transition provisions adapt: joint Phase 0/1 certification, consolidated debt assumption, combined Day Zero NRS publication of all founding sovereigns' records, and proportional obligation assumption per treaty terms.

§19.2¶Day Zero

Day Zero is the date the ratification threshold is certified as achieved by the interim EC. Upon ratification: all laws conflicting with the Individual Sovereignty Floor are immediately null and void; all public assets transfer to the NRS; the 24-month Transition Window begins. The Republic exists the moment the EC certifies the threshold.

§19.3¶The Four-Phase Transition

24-month, four-phase process. Seven provisions: Phase 0 pre-ratification preparation (§19.3.a), Phase 1 emergency stabilisation Days 1-90 (§19.3.b), Phase 2 institutional construction Days 91-270 (§19.3.c), Phase 3 first elections Days 271-450 (§19.3.d), Phase 4 full operation (§19.3.e), provisional NRS authority (§19.3.f), and phase extension mechanism (§19.3.g, new v143).

§19.3.a¶Phase 0 — Pre-Ratification Preparation

Phase 0 is the pre-ratification preparation period. The interim Electoral Commission is constituted, the National Voting System is certified operational, and the joint Inspectorate panel is formed — all before any ratification vote occurs. The Republic's constitutional infrastructure must exist before it can be activated.

§19.3.b¶Phase 1 — Emergency Stabilization (Days 1–90)

Existing government officials serve as caretakers under §19.4. The joint Inspectorate panel assumes oversight. The NRS begins recording all government acts. Priority functions: Basic Needs Floor delivery continuity, public safety, and essential services. No new permanent legislation. This phase is explicitly temporary — its only mandate is stability while Phase 2 institutional construction begins.

§19.3.c¶Phase 2 — Institutional Construction (Days 91–270)

Phase 2 (Days 91–270) is Institutional Construction. Statehood Audits are conducted for all entering territories. Inspectorates are formally constituted. The Candidate Register for MA board members and the RSA Director is assembled. Electoral Commission is certified as operational. The National Voting System is fully deployed for citizen registration. Phase 2 ends at Day 270.

§19.3.d¶Phase 3 — First Elections (Days 271–450)

Phase 3 (Days 271-450) is First Elections. The first National Election Day. Supreme Court justices elected through the open-candidacy judicial selection process. Electoral Commission inaugural elections. Phase 3 ends when the first the Legislature is sworn in and the first executives take office — the moment at which the Republic's constitutional design becomes self-sustaining.

§19.3.e¶Phase 4 — Full Constitutional Operation

Phase 4 is Full Constitutional Operation — beginning the day the first Legislature convenes and the first executives take the constitutional oath. From Phase 4, all constitutional provisions are fully operative. The 18-month Transition Window concludes at the end of Phase 4.

§19.4¶Caretaker Status

All existing government officials serve as caretakers during the Transition Window — restricted to maintaining existing services only. No new permanent legislation. Transition supervised by joint Inspectorate panel: one representative per Inspectorate, appointed by their respective IGs. The panel dissolves automatically at the Transition Window's conclusion. Caretaker status prevents the predecessor government from entrenching itself during transition.

§19.5¶Pre-Constitutional Classified Documents

Legislature must establish a Classification Review Commission within 90 days of Phase 4 activation. Commission applies §9.5 criteria to predecessor classified material: legitimately classified material enters the §9.5 duration framework from Day Zero; improperly classified material (concealing violations or policy decisions) is immediately declassified and published to the NRS. Any person may challenge a Commission determination before the SC.

§19.6¶Pre-Existing Obligations

The Republic assumes the public debt obligations of its predecessor government as a constitutional matter, not a discretionary choice. This prevents the Republic from being born into sovereign default by simply denying predecessor debts — a move that would destabilize the fiscal foundations of the transition period. The assumption covers public debt, not private commercial obligations or claims arising from illegal predecessor-government conduct. The distinction matters: the Republic inherits the legitimate fiscal commitments of the predecessor state while preserving the right to pursue claims against individuals who misused predecessor authority.

§19.6.a¶Debt Assumption

The Republic assumes all legally recognized debts of the prior government. The Republic does not inherit a clean slate — it inherits what was borrowed. Selective repudiation of prior obligations is not available as a founding act.

§19.6.b¶Active Military Operations

Active military operations of the prior government continue under existing authorization until the §12.2 Tier framework can apply. The President, once inaugurated, must certify each active operation under the appropriate Tier within 14 days or begin orderly cessation. Operations not certified within 60 days of inauguration cease unless the Legislature grants a one-time extension. No indefinite continuation of predecessor-authorized military action.

§19.6.c¶Social Insurance Programs

Existing social insurance programs — retirement pensions, disability benefits, health coverage — of the prior government continue uninterrupted during the Transition Window. The Republic does not create a gap in social protection as part of its founding.

§19.6.d¶Veterans and National Service Obligations

The Republic inherits and honors all benefit obligations to persons who served in military or national service roles under the prior government. These are existing contractual commitments of the sovereign that survive transition as continuity-of-state. Legislature must pass a Veterans and National Service Benefits Act within the first budget cycle following Phase 4 activation.

§19.6.e¶Constitutional Claims Court

The immediate nullification of laws conflicting with the Individual Sovereignty Floor creates constitutional disputes that existing courts may not have capacity to absorb. The Legislature must by statute establish an interim Constitutional Claims Court within Phase 2, with jurisdiction over all Article I claims arising from Day Zero nullification.

§19.6.f¶Legal Continuity

The Federated Republic is the legal continuation of the prior government as a matter of international law. All international obligations, financial commitments, treaty relationships, and contractual obligations of the prior government as of Day Zero are assumed by the Republic as its successor sovereign. This ensures that the Republic is not born into sovereign default or diplomatic isolation.

§19.7.a¶Transition-Period Territorial Elections

Territories or subdivisions meeting the §13.2 Competency Baseline by Phase 2 end may petition to enter as State or Territory. Those failing the Baseline enter as Territory under §13.1 and may begin the Statehood Audit immediately. Any territory may choose Territory status by simple legislative vote regardless of whether it meets the Statehood Baseline.

§19.7.b¶Subdivision Elections

Prior government states or territories may voluntarily subdivide during the Transition Window by vote of their existing legislative body. New subdivisions are treated as separate petitioning entities for §19.7.a purposes. Legislature must establish by statute the subdivision recognition process.

§19.7.c¶Phase 2 Statehood Audit Failures

Where a state fails the Phase 2 Statehood Audit, it enters as a Territory under §13.1 rather than a State. Its population retains all Article I rights. Territory residents from failed-audit states participate in first House elections proportionally. Audit failure does not disenfranchise the population.

§19.7.d¶Pre-Existing Territories and the District

All prior government territories that have not achieved statehood by Day Zero enter as Territories from Phase 4 activation — without undergoing Phase 2 Statehood Audits, beginning the standard §13.2 Statehood Audit process after Phase 4. Any capital district or special administrative region that was neither a state nor standard territory is assigned Territory status and follows the §13.2 path.

§19.8¶Transition Candidate Registers

The joint Inspectorate panel assembles the initial Candidate Registers for all constitutionally established positions before Phase 3 elections can occur. Without populated registers, the elections cannot proceed — the Registers are a constitutional prerequisite for the democratic machinery to activate. The panel's role here is transitional: it performs the register-assembly and vetting function that the permanent Inspectorates will assume after Phase 4. Registers assembled by the panel carry full constitutional validity; the Legislature ratifies and continues them through the first budget cycle after Phase 4 activation.

§19.8.a¶Initial Register Assembly

During Phase 2, the joint Inspectorate panel assembles an initial Candidate Register for the MA board members and RSA Director. Panel receives disclosure records, reviews factual accuracy, applies constitutional eligibility criteria, and publishes with recommendations to NRS. May draw on prior government, international, and diaspora expertise where domestic expertise is unavailable or compromised.

§19.8.b¶Interim Functions and First Nomination

The joint Inspectorate panel performs interim MA fiscal certification functions from Phase 4 activation until the first MA Director takes office following the Phase 3 election. These interim certifications carry full constitutional weight. The first PM must nominate MA board members within 90 days of Phase 4 activation and an RSA Director within 180 days.

§19.9¶Transitional Accountability Mechanism

Where a ratifying polity's predecessor government has documented constitutional violations — systematic rights abuses, documented atrocities — the Republic may establish a Transitional Accountability Mechanism by statute. The TAM is time-limited, non-criminal (it cannot impose criminal sentences), and subject to NRS publication. It is the the constitutional answer to how a new Republic deals with the wrongs of what it replaced with the wrongs of what it replaced.

§19.9.a¶TAM Establishment and Scope

The Transitional Accountability Mechanism investigates, documents, and recommends remedies for serious violations committed by predecessor government entities during the period before ratification. Its jurisdiction is bounded to predecessor-era conduct — it does not adjudicate Republic-era acts, which fall to ordinary courts. Findings are published to the NRS and may recommend criminal referrals, institutional restructuring, or reparative measures, but carry no binding force. The TAM embodies the constitutional separation between information-generation and consequence-imposition: it finds and publishes; courts, the Legislature, and the PM decide whether to act on what it finds.

§19.9.b¶TAM Amendment and Successor Bar

The establishing statute and any amendments to it require the same 2/3 majority of both chambers as the original statute. No amendment may expand the Mechanism's subject matter scope beyond the scope defined in the establishing statute.
