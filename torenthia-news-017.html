The Other Negotiation

While the LC candidates debated the eastern lake, Legat Consul Rehn spent the last four months of his term running a second negotiation nobody covered. The Toren River treaty expires at the end of his term. What the next LC inherits depends on how much time he has left.

Petra Vend Political Correspondent · Verentum — Year 13, Month 1

There is a river on the northern border. It has been there since before the Republic. The Toren River originates in Caldris, runs through a stretch of jointly-administered territory whose status has been governed by bilateral treaty since Year 3, and supplies water to approximately 340,000 Republic citizens in the three northern states. The treaty governing the river's joint administration is due to expire at the end of Legat Consul Casimir Rehn's term. That date is now four months away. The candidates running to succeed him have given the Toren River a combined total of perhaps forty minutes of public attention since declaring their candidacies.

Rehn's foreign affairs team has been in Caldris four times in the past six months. The delegations have been quiet — no press advisories, no joint statements, a single NRS record of the third visit noting it occurred and nothing else. The fourth visit happened while RNN was running continuous coverage of Carrow's intelligence summary and Vael's forty-eight hour response window. This reporter became aware of it from a source in the Caldris economic affairs ministry. The LC's office did not confirm or deny.

"The Toren River will outlast everyone in this race. The question is whether we close this before I leave or hand it to someone who hasn't read the file."

The Toren River treaty is not glamorous. It governs flow allocation by season, maintenance responsibilities for the jointly-administered stretch, dispute resolution procedures, and the framework for a water infrastructure investment program that Caldris has been seeking for years and that the Republic has been declining to fund at the level Caldris considers adequate. The negotiation has been running since Year 9 — Rehn's team inherited a framework from his predecessor that had been allowed to drift without resolution. The current treaty's expiration date was always going to fall in the Year 13 transition period. Nobody designed it that way. It happened because no one closed it earlier.

What expiry without a new treaty looks like: the existing administration arrangements lapse; Caldris and the Republic revert to customary water law, which is disputed; the jointly-administered stretch enters a legal grey zone; and three northern states begin the process of finding alternative sources for approximately 18% of their water supply. This is not a crisis on the scale of Lake Varda. It is a slower, more institutional problem — the kind that builds for years before it becomes visible, and that becomes significantly harder to solve once it does.

One person in the LC field has spoken substantively about the Toren River in the past four months. Tobias Vael gave a speech about it in Varek in Month 12. He was widely criticized for talking about the wrong border during an eastern lake crisis. He was not wrong about the Toren River. He was wrong about the timing. Those are different errors, and the distinction matters as this race enters its final phase.

Seren Mak has not addressed the treaty. Her campaign operates on the argument that the Lake Varda situation is the defining foreign affairs test of this election, and that infrastructure capacity on the eastern shore is the domestic corollary. The Toren River sits outside that frame. Ines Carrow's candidacy declaration said she had read every Sunderland intelligence file from the past six years. Her record on Caldris is held in the classified foreign affairs archive, where this reporter cannot reach it. Pell Sandris has said nothing about the Toren River, but has said several times that the LC's job includes more than the current crisis — a claim that applies as precisely here as it does to anything.

Rehn's office, when asked directly about the state of the Caldris negotiation, provided one sentence: "The Legat Consul is working to complete the Toren River framework before the end of his term." It was not a confirmation that completion was likely. It was not a denial. It was the kind of sentence that experienced LC's offices release when they want to say something without saying anything. This reporter has seen that sentence before. It usually means it is close, and possibly not close enough.

The next Legat Consul takes office with an unresolved eastern lake crisis, a Sunderland intelligence picture that has changed what diplomatic instruments can accomplish, an EM fiscal review of Korda Territory that the Legislature has not yet responded to, and — depending on what Rehn manages in the next four months — either a closed Toren River treaty or an open one. The field has spent six months arguing about instincts and borders. The northern border has been running on its own calendar the entire time.

Petra Vend is The Torenthian's political correspondent, based in Verentum.
