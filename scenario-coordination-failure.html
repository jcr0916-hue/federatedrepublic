The Stalemate

Year 7. The Legat Consul concludes a mutual defense treaty requiring domestic infrastructure commitments. The Civic Consul refuses to authorize the spending on fiscal grounds. Both are acting within their constitutional domain. Neither will yield.

§2.14.a Coordination Failure §2.14 Council of Ministers §2.4 Consular Domain §3.7 Legislative Process §10.1 National Record System

The Meridian Mutual Defense Treaty committed the Republic to a fifteen-year program of joint infrastructure investment: hardened communication nodes, shared satellite ground stations, and a network of dual-use logistics facilities along the northern territorial boundary. The Legat Consul concluded the treaty in Year 6, with Senate ratification following in Year 7. The treaty's domestic implementation provisions — the infrastructure spending itself — required parliamentary appropriation.

The Civic Consul read the MA's preliminary cost assessment and refused to authorize the spending request. Her fiscal objection was specific: the treaty's Year 8–12 commitments would coincide with the Republic's structural deficit correction window, the period during which three consecutive Satisfactory MA certifications were required before any new major capital commitment could be added to the medium-term fiscal framework. The Civic Consul was not disputing the Legat Consul's authority to conclude the treaty. She was declining to authorize spending that her fiscal framework could not accommodate.

The Legat Consul's position was equally precise. The treaty was ratified. It was the law of the Republic. The domestic infrastructure commitments were treaty obligations, not discretionary spending. The Civic Consul's fiscal objection — however legitimate as fiscal policy — did not override a ratified treaty commitment. The Legat Consul was not asking the Civic Consul to violate her fiscal framework. He was stating that the Republic had a binding international obligation that the domestic fiscal framework would have to accommodate.

Both were right. Neither was wrong. The constitutional machinery had no obvious answer.

Why the Standard Mechanisms Failed

This was not a domain dispute. The Legat Consul's authority to conclude and implement mutual defense treaties is explicitly enumerated in §2.4. The Civic Consul's authority over domestic fiscal authorization is the core of her residual mandate. Neither executive was claiming authority over the other's domain. The SC's domain determination process under §2.14.a was unavailable — there was nothing to determine. Each executive was acting entirely within their own constitutional space. Their actions simply produced incompatible outcomes.

The Council of Ministers had been working on the problem for four months before the coordination failure declaration. The joint working group had identified three possible bridging mechanisms: a phased implementation schedule that deferred the most expensive commitments beyond the deficit correction window, a contingency appropriation that would activate automatically on MA certification of structural balance, and a treaty renegotiation to extend the implementation timeline. The Legat Consul found the first two inadequate to meet the treaty's Year 8 benchmarks. The Civic Consul found the third diplomatically unacceptable — renegotiating a ratified mutual defense treaty over domestic fiscal concerns would signal a constitutional unreliability she was not willing to create.

§2.14.a — Coordination Failure Declaration
Day 127

The Legat Consul published a coordination failure declaration to the NRS under §2.14.a. The declaration specified the incompatibility precisely: the treaty's binding Year 8 infrastructure benchmarks, the Civic Consul's documented fiscal authorization refusal, and the four-month Council of Ministers record demonstrating that the joint body had been unable to produce a resolution. The NRS timestamp made the declaration permanent from the moment of publication.

The declaration triggered the §2.14.a mechanism. The Legislature now had 30 days to resolve the deadlock by concurrent resolution — a determination that could bind both executives to its terms for the duration of the impasse without permanently altering either executive's domain.

The Legislature's 30-day window opened. The Civic Consul published a formal statement noting that the coordination failure declaration was the first in the Republic's history and that she was prepared to work with the Legislature on a concurrent resolution framework — but that she would not authorize spending commitments that violated the MA's structural balance parameters without legislative authorization that explicitly addressed the fiscal consequences.

The Concurrent Resolution

The Legislature had 30 days to produce a concurrent resolution that bound both executives to its terms for the duration of the impasse. The constitutional constraint on the resolution was significant: it could not alter permanent domain boundaries. It could resolve the specific impasse — prescribe how this particular incompatibility would be addressed — but it could not permanently limit the Legat Consul's treaty authority or the Civic Consul's fiscal authorization function.

The resolution that emerged after 26 days of joint Assembly-Senate drafting did three things. It authorized a contingency appropriation for the Meridian infrastructure commitments, structured as a separate fiscal instrument outside the standard medium-term framework, subject to MA certification of solvency at each disbursement trigger. It required the Legat Consul to transmit a revised implementation schedule to the Senate within 60 days, with Year 8 benchmarks adjusted to reflect the first-tranche authorization only. And it required the Civic Consul to present the overall fiscal impact to the Assembly within 90 days, with a proposed offset mechanism that the Assembly could approve or reject by ordinary majority.

§2.14.a — Concurrent Resolution Effect
Day 167

The concurrent resolution passed both chambers and was published to the NRS. Under §2.14.a, its terms bound both executives for the duration of the impasse — which the resolution itself defined as ending when the Civic Consul presented the fiscal offset mechanism to the Assembly and the Assembly acted on it. The impasse was not resolved by the resolution; it was governed by it, with a defined path to resolution.

The Legat Consul submitted the revised implementation schedule on Day 221. The Civic Consul presented the offset mechanism on Day 248. The Assembly approved it 289-211. The original fiscal authorization dispute was resolved through ordinary legislative process — which is exactly where the constitution intended it to land.

What This Scenario Tests

§2.14.a addresses a failure mode that the Council of Ministers and the domain determination rule cannot reach: two executives each acting within their constitutional authority whose actions are materially incompatible. The domain determination process under §2.14.a governs disputes about which executive has authority over a given action — it has nothing to say when neither executive's authority is in dispute, only their outcomes conflict. §2.14.a handles that remainder.

The Coordination Failure Protocol is not a solution. It is a structured path through an impasse that the constitutional machinery cannot otherwise resolve. The Legislature gets a defined role and a 30-day window. The Civic Consul's residual authority governs if the Legislature fails to act. Every step is on the NRS permanently — the declaration, the legislative response. The public can see, in real time, who declared the failure and what the Legislature did with it.

The Legat Consul's decision to declare a coordination failure rather than continue the Council of Ministers process indefinitely was a political calculation as much as a constitutional one. The NRS record of the declaration was permanent — future readers would see that the Legat Consul had concluded the Council of Ministers could not resolve the matter and had invoked the protocol. That is a reputational cost worth incurring only when the alternative is ongoing paralysis on a treaty commitment. The Civic Consul's willingness to work with the Legislature rather than simply maintain her fiscal position reflected the same calculation: the NRS record of the coordination failure was permanent, and a Civic Consul who was seen as blocking treaty implementation without providing a resolution path would bear the political consequences of that record.

The Meridian Treaty was ultimately implemented — late, restructured, and through a more laborious process than the Legat Consul had intended. The constitutional order held. The impasse was resolved not by one executive yielding to the other but by the Legislature performing the function that representative institutions exist to perform: resolving genuine political disagreements between constitutionally valid positions.
