The CoalitionThe Assembly has the votes to remove the Civic Consul but cannot agree on a successor — and under a constructive vote of no confidence, that is the same as having no votes at all.

The Twenty-Four HoursThe Council determines the Civic Consul cannot exercise authority. He publishes a declaration and is back in a day — because §2.16 makes restoration his own act. It works exactly once.
The Second RenewalYear 8. The Halvern Strait escort operation comes up for its second renewal — and §14.3 presents its bill: no renewal without a Senate-approved Transition and Reconstruction Plan. The constitution's answer to the forever war.
The VoteThe CC returns a bill within 10 business days. Tabled for one month — bypass-proof. The Senate tries for 2/3. Falls short by one vote. The bill lapses. What §2.7's suspensive veto requires at every step.
The ObjectionYear 5. The Civic Consul issues a §2.7 fiscal notice against the Universal Care Expansion Act. The Assembly Speaker declines to recall. The law takes effect. Seven years later, the MA finds the structural deficit the notice predicted.

Immigration & Rights

The ReturnA legal resident leaves the Republic to visit a friend in Europe. She is not allowed back in. The government cites national security. She cites her religion, her family name, and §1.6.
The UnremovableYear 5. A final removal order that cannot be executed: no country will take him, and the one that would violates §1.21. He is also a documented security concern. §6.3.a closes both easy exits.

Indigenous & Minority Rights

The Founding ChoiceThe Transition Window. The Corrin Nation must elect its status under §16.2 — Associated Community or Territorial Integration — as a federal officer makes an inducement the constitution forbids. A study in the founding choice and the no-inducements rule.
The DepartureYear 14. The Vaskeld Nation, an Associated Community since founding, invokes its unconditional §20.4 right to leave the Republic. The exit cannot be refused — but shared infrastructure, cross-border obligations, and twelve thousand dual citizens must still be untangled.

Rights & Emergency Architecture

The Second DeclarationYear 3. A regional emergency expires. 72 hours later, a new emergency issues — same geography, same rights, different label. The EM publishes its assessment. The SC rules.
The Automatic FloorYear 7. The budget deadline passes with no enacted budget. The interim automatic appropriation takes effect by constitutional operation. What continues. What stops.
The Finality ActYear 8. The Legislature strips judicial review from intelligence-based residency denials. A permanent resident with no forum petitions the SC directly under §4.5.
The Void ExceptionYear 18. A valid amendment — 2/3 supermajority, state ratification — contains a 90-day habeas carve-out. The SC applies the amendment and voids only the carve-out.
The OrderYear 9. A military officer refuses a rendition order under §2.8 — compliance would violate §1.1 and §6.2. Her superior initiates disciplinary proceedings. §2.8's fourth sentence: any penalty imposed for a lawful refusal is itself a constitutional breach.

States & Territories

The MapYear 2. A State's redistricting map fails the LM's compactness test. The LM publishes — that's all it can do. But publication gives standing. The Elections Panel is ready to draw an interim map if needed.
The Sponsoring StateYear 4. A State legislature passes an act purporting to revoke State sponsorship for 340 existing residents. Federal credentials are federal. The State's role ended when the credentials were issued.
The AuditYear 3. A Territory passes both Statehood Audit components. No political vote required — the pathway is automatic. What triggers, what's guaranteed, and what no political coalition can block.
The PetitionYear 7. A State initiates voluntary devolution — converting from State to Territory status. Two requirements: 60% referendum and Senate ratification. What the federal government can and cannot do about it.
The Sixty PercentYear 12. A geothermal reserve sits beneath National Trust land. The Legislature clears two-thirds. Then the citizenry votes.
The Third StrikeYear 13. Valdrath fails its Statehood Audit for the third consecutive year. No automatic devolution — citizens vote on two paths: a federal remediation commission or Provisional status. The constitution gives the decision to Valdrath's own voters.
The AdministratorYear 7. Haverford refuses NVS compliance for the third year running. The cure period expires. The Elections Panel announces it will administer the federal election in Haverford directly. Haverford petitions the SC. The election proceeds anyway.
The ReferendumYear 9. Carenthal's independence referendum passes: 61.3% in favor, 62.1% participation. The Elections Panel publishes to the NRS. The election is irrevocable from that moment. What §15.9 requires next — and what it absolutely prohibits.
The RestorationYear 16. Valdrath passes its second consecutive clean Statehood Audit while in Provisional status. Statehood restored automatically — no vote, no ratification, no executive action. The same audit mechanism that triggered mandatory devolution triggers restoration.
The FormulaYear 8. The EM certifies that Evantine and Norrath lack fiscal capacity for their §12.1 social state obligations. The Legislature responds. The formula is certified adequate. A year later, Halvern still cannot deliver universal healthcare. What the constitution requires — and where it runs out.
The Prior ClaimYear 9. Halvern refuses a federal infrastructure mandate. Its argument: it was sovereign before the Republic, never gave up that sovereignty, and should hold the same compact-level relationship as indigenous nations. The SC rules unanimously.
