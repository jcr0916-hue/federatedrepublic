The Case for Limits

On why the Republic uses term limits for its Prime Minister, its Supreme Court justices, and its inferior court judges — and why each limit has a different structure serving a different constitutional purpose.

The Republic's three term limit structures look related from a distance but are motivated by distinct constitutional concerns. The PM's dual limit — eight consecutive years, twelve total — is designed to prevent entrenchment of democratic authority. The SC's twelve-year non-renewable term is designed to produce judicial independence without permanent tenure. The inferior courts' fifteen-year non-renewable term is designed to extend the same independence logic to the institution where most of the Republic's constitutional adjudication actually happens. Each limit is a different answer to a different question about how long a person should hold a particular kind of power before the exercise of that power begins to distort the institution they serve.

The most useful way to see these limits clearly is to ask, in each case, what they are protecting against — and what they are sacrificing to provide that protection.

Office Limit structure Primary purpose
Prime Minister 8 years consecutive · 12 years total Prevent entrenchment of democratic authority
Supreme Court 12 years · non-renewable Independence from reappointment pressure
Inferior courts 15 years · non-renewable Independence at the institution of volume

The PM's Limits — Democratic Entrenchment

The PM's term structure begins from a democratic premise: the Prime Minister derives authority from the confidence of the House. Where a PM holds that confidence continuously for many years, they are not violating any constitutional norm — they are succeeding at the democratic test the constitution sets for them. So why limit their tenure at all?

The answer lies in the difference between democratic authority and democratic accountability. A PM who has governed for a decade has accumulated institutional relationships, informal influence, and governing networks that are genuinely hard for successors to displace. The party structures that would normally provide internal accountability grow around a long-serving PM rather than checking them. The opposition's capacity to offer a credible alternative atrophies when the alternative has never governed. These effects are not constitutional violations — they are political realities that gradually erode the conditions under which democratic accountability can function as designed.

The eight-year consecutive limit§2.3.b is a structural response to this erosion. It does not say the PM has failed. It says that eight consecutive years is long enough for the entrenchment dynamics to become real, and that the democratic system benefits from a mandatory transition before those dynamics become permanent. A PM who steps aside after eight years leaves behind a party that has had to think seriously about successorship, an opposition that has had to prepare itself, and an institutional landscape that has not fully calcified around a single governing style.

The twelve-year total cap addresses the gap the consecutive limit leaves open: a PM who serves eight years, steps aside, and returns. Nothing about a mandatory break resets the entrenchment clock. A leader who governed for eight years, spent four years as opposition, and returned with heightened public standing is, in some respects, more institutionally embedded than they were when they first stepped aside. The total cap says that twelve years of PM service — however distributed across non-consecutive periods — is the constitutional maximum. A return after eight years is permitted; a return that would bring total service above twelve years is not.

The sacrifice is real. A PM who reaches both limits may be the most capable governing leader in the Republic at the moment they are required to step aside. Constitutional term limits do not distinguish between the PM who fails after six years and the PM who succeeds across eight. That indifference to individual merit is a feature, not a failure: a limit that applied only to unpopular PMs would not be a limit at all. The cost of structural protection is that it applies regardless of whether the protected-against risk has actually materialised.

The SC's Twelve Years — Appointed Independence

The logic of judicial term limits runs through a different channel. Judges are not democratically accountable — deliberately not. A Supreme Court justice who faces no elections owes their tenure to no one once confirmed. The institution's legitimacy rests not on popular mandate but on the quality of its reasoning and the consistency of its application of written law. The threat to judicial independence is not entrenchment in the democratic sense but dependence: a judge who must secure reappointment will, over time and however subtly, adjust their reasoning to remain acceptable to whoever controls reappointment.

Lifetime tenure solves the reappointment problem completely. A justice who serves until death or voluntary retirement faces no reappointment pressure at any point. But lifetime tenure creates its own distortions. A justice appointed at forty-five may serve for thirty-five years — longer than most citizens' entire engagement with the constitutional system. The court's composition becomes, over decades, increasingly determined by the accident of which justices happened to die or retire during which government's tenure. Lifetime terms also create a specific incentive toward strategic retirement: a justice who cares about their institutional legacy will time their departure to coincide with a sympathetic government's period of control. This is not a corruption of the judicial function — it is a rational response to the incentives lifetime tenure creates.

The twelve-year non-renewable term§4.2.d eliminates both problems simultaneously. A non-renewable term means no reappointment pressure — the justice serves their twelve years knowing that the term ends regardless of their relationship with the political branches. A fixed term means no strategic retirement — there is no advantageous departure timing because the end date is set at the beginning. A twelve-year non-renewable term produces the cleanest independence structure available: appointment accountability at the moment of confirmation, then genuine freedom from political pressure for the entire term, then a fixed and predictable departure.

Twelve years is long enough to allow a justice to develop genuine constitutional expertise and institutional knowledge. It is short enough that the court's composition turns over regularly — three justices in each four-year Parliament, on the staggered class schedule§4.2.h — without any single government controlling a majority of the court's seats. The stagger is the mechanism that prevents the term limit from becoming a court-packing opportunity: even a PM serving the full eight-year consecutive limit cannot nominate a majority of the nine justices through ordinary turnover.

The Inferior Courts' Fifteen Years — The Institution of Volume

The Republic's inferior courts — district courts, appellate courts, and specialist courts established by statute — are the institution where the overwhelming majority of constitutional adjudication actually happens. The SC hears a small number of cases each year, selected for constitutional significance. The inferior courts process hundreds of thousands of cases annually, applying constitutional provisions to the specific facts of specific disputes. Most citizens who encounter the constitution's rights protections, property rules, or administrative procedures will encounter them through an inferior court judge, not an SC justice.

The fifteen-year non-renewable term§4.1.c extends the same independence logic that justifies the SC's twelve-year limit to the institution that does more of the constitutional work. A district court judge who has served for fifteen years under good-behavior conditions — free from reappointment pressure for the full term — has produced a body of constitutional reasoning that is genuinely independent. The institutional memory loss when that judge's term ends is real and significant; fifteen years of jurisdictional expertise does not transfer automatically to a successor. But that cost is accepted in exchange for the structural independence benefit, exactly as it is at the SC level.

The additional three years — fifteen rather than twelve — reflects the different character of inferior court work. SC justices interpret constitutional meaning at the highest level of abstraction; they need time to develop a sophisticated constitutional jurisprudence, but the cases they hear are chosen specifically for their constitutional novelty. Inferior court judges develop expertise in the application of settled constitutional principles to the specific jurisdictional and factual terrain of their court. A district court judge in a jurisdiction with complex indigenous compact obligations and cross-border digital infrastructure questions — to name two domains explicitly covered by the Republic's constitution — needs more development time before their jurisdictional expertise reaches its full value. Fifteen years rather than twelve acknowledges that difference without abandoning the principle.

Why Not Elected Judges

The term limit logic only makes sense in the context of appointment. Elected officials do not need term limits to achieve independence — they achieve accountability through elections instead. The question of how long an elected official should serve is answered, in normal democratic theory, by the electorate: they serve until voters decide otherwise. Term limits for elected officials are a second-order intervention on democracy, justified only by specific documented failures of electoral accountability to prevent entrenchment (executive power in presidential systems being the canonical case).

The PM's term limit is therefore a different kind of provision from the judicial term limits, even though it uses similar language. The PM's limit is a correction for a failure mode of democratic accountability — the entrenchment effect described above. The judicial term limits are not corrections for a democratic failure; they are substitutes for democratic accountability in an institution that is deliberately designed to operate without it. The PM's limit says: democracy needs help preventing this specific pathology. The judicial limits say: these institutions cannot use democracy as their accountability mechanism, so here is what replaces it.

This distinction explains why other constitutional officers — the President, House members, senators — do not have term limits in this constitution. The President is directly elected with a national mandate; electoral accountability is the mechanism, and the four-year term with no renewal provision§7.2 provides sufficient structural constraint without a separate total cap. House members and senators face regular election cycles that are themselves the accountability mechanism. The offices that do have term limits — the PM, SC justices, inferior court judges — are offices where either democratic accountability fails without structural assistance (the PM) or where democratic accountability is deliberately absent by design (the judiciary).

The Shared Sacrifice

All three term limit structures share one cost: they are indifferent to merit. A fifteen-year district court judge who has become the foremost expert in the Republic on indigenous compact obligations must step down when the term ends, regardless of how valuable their continued service would be. A PM who has governed with genuine distinction for eight years must leave, regardless of whether there is a successor of equivalent calibre prepared to take over. An SC justice who has developed into the court's most sophisticated constitutional reasoner serves twelve years and leaves, regardless of the moment's constitutional needs.

This indifference is not a flaw in the design. It is the design. A term limit that applied only when the officer's quality had declined would not solve the problems that term limits are designed to solve. Reappointment pressure affects good judges as well as mediocre ones. Entrenchment builds around capable PMs as readily as around inadequate ones — more readily, in fact, because capable long-serving PMs earn the institutional deference that makes entrenchment possible. The structural benefits of term limits are only available if the limits apply unconditionally.

What the constitution offers in exchange for that unconditional application is a different kind of continuity: institutional continuity rather than individual continuity. The SC's staggered classes mean the court never entirely turns over. The PM's term limits create a cycle of succession that forces institutions to develop leadership depth rather than depending on a single long-serving incumbent. The inferior courts' regular turnover creates jurisdictions that are defined by their caseload and constitutional mandate rather than by the personality and jurisprudence of a particular judge. These are not consolations for lost individual continuity. They are the constitutional argument for why institutional continuity is the more valuable thing.
