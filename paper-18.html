Paper No. 18

The Open Field

On the pool nomination system — why elections select for the wrong qualities in appointed technical roles, how making the full field of qualified candidates publicly visible before any nomination renders the second face of executive power accountable, and why the SC registry is the design's flagship test.

The nomination of a Supreme Court justice, a central bank governor, or a director of intelligence is one of the most consequential acts an executive can perform. And yet in almost every democratic system that uses executive appointment, the public sees only the last step of the process. They see the nominee. They do not see the field. They cannot see who was considered and passed over, what the range of qualified candidates looked like, or whether the nominee was the best available choice or simply the most politically convenient one.

This is what constitutional scholars sometimes call the second face of power: not the visible act of choosing, but the invisible act of shaping the choice set. The most effective exercise of executive influence often happens before any nomination is made — in the cultivation of networks, the narrowing of who is considered plausible, the quiet elimination of candidates who might prove inconvenient. None of this is visible to the public, and none of it is subject to democratic scrutiny. The pool nomination system addresses this directly.

Why Not Election

Before explaining how the pool system works, it is worth explaining what it replaces — and why. Earlier drafts of this constitution elected the Supreme Court justices by national ranked-choice vote. The argument for direct election was compelling: democratic legitimacy flowing directly from citizens rather than from the political branches the Court is meant to constrain.

The argument against proved stronger. A national SC election campaign produces political figures. Voters evaluating judicial candidates must assess qualifications most voters cannot evaluate — constitutional precedent, federal jurisdiction doctrine, evidence law. What voters can assess is whether the candidate seems to share their values and sympathies. The result, reliably, is that judicial elections select for ideological alignment rather than legal quality. State supreme court elections in jurisdictions that have tried them demonstrate this consistently: they attract campaign money, produce more ideologically extreme justices, and over time corrode the institutional legitimacy the election was supposed to provide.

The problem is general, not specific to courts. Consider what an election for a cryptographic infrastructure administrator would actually select for: communication skills, name recognition, the ability to build a coalition of supporters who trust you with a role most of them cannot technically evaluate. None of these qualities determine whether a distributed record system's cryptographic standards will hold under a coordinated attack in Year 9. Where the gap between what voters can assess and what the role requires is wide, elections select for the wrong things. The pool mechanism is the alternative.

The Mechanism

Every appointed constitutional position operates from a publicly maintained pool of qualified candidates.§8.13 Any citizen who meets the qualifications may apply. The Elections Panel maintains the pool as a public record — receiving applications, storing records, and providing access through the NRS. The appropriate Inspectorate certifies each applicant's eligibility against the stated criteria and publishes that certification to the NRS alongside the applicant's record.

The pool is not a shortlist. It is not curated by the executive. Every qualified applicant who is certified goes in. The Elections Panel may not exclude a certified applicant — the independence of the registry function from the nominating function is constitutionally explicit.§8.14 Eligibility criteria are strictly objective: years of practice or service, office status, and financial interests as defined by the constitution or statute. No administrator may apply criteria relating to legal philosophy, judicial temperament, ideological orientation, or political association. Any exclusion on such grounds is constitutionally void.

The result is a public document, on the NRS, accessible to every citizen, that shows exactly who the executive could choose from. When a vacancy arises, the executive nominates from the pool. The Senate confirms. Nothing in the traditional appointment process changes — the executive retains full discretion over whom to nominate from among certified candidates, and the Senate retains full discretion over whether to confirm. The pool adds no new bureaucratic step, no new veto point, no new filter on executive judgment. What it adds is visibility.

What Visibility Does

The importance of making the pool public operates through several distinct mechanisms. The first is direct accountability. A President who nominates a Director of Intelligence from the pool must do so against a background of public knowledge about who else was available. If the pool contains ten certified candidates with stronger operational records and the President chooses the eleventh, citizens and senators can ask why. They cannot be told there were no better options, because the options are on the NRS.

The second is structural. The existence of the pool changes the information environment for Senate confirmation hearings. A senator questioning a nominee no longer works only with information the executive chooses to provide. They can look at the pool, assess the relative qualifications of the field, and ask pointed questions grounded in that knowledge.

The third is pre-emptive. Because the pool is permanent and public, it changes the incentives around who applies and who is discouraged. In a system without a pool, informal gatekeeping happens through networks — certain candidates never make it into the consideration set because they lack the right connections. The public pool breaks that mechanism. If you are qualified and certified, you are in the pool. The executive cannot remove you from the choice set without making a nomination — a public, accountable action — rather than simply not considering you.

The SC Registry as Flagship Test

The Supreme Court Candidate Registry§8.4.e is the pool system's most consequential application. Any citizen with at least fifteen years of legal practice or ten years of judicial service may apply. The JI certifies eligibility and publishes the complete record. The EI reviews financial disclosure. The Registry is public, permanent, and freely accessible on the NRS. Every person qualified to be nominated is on it.

The PM must nominate from the Registry, but the Registry belongs to no one. A PM who nominates a personal ally with weak credentials is nominating someone whose credential gap is published on the NRS before the Senate sees the nomination. The Registry makes patronage nominations visible rather than concealing them. The SC Candidate Registry§4.2 provides an expert assessment layer: five senior judges chosen by lot publish advisory assessments of registered candidates covering judicial record, legal reasoning, and professional independence. The Board cannot restrict nominations; the PM may nominate any certified candidate regardless of the Board's assessment. But the assessment is published and transmitted to every Senator before any confirmation vote. A PM who nominates against an adverse Board assessment has made that choice publicly.

Senate confirmation requires 2/3 of full seated membership — the equivalent function that elections were supposed to serve. At that threshold, the PM cannot appoint ideological allies unless the governing coalition commands an extraordinary Senate supermajority. The structural incentive points toward nominees who command cross-party support — which in practice means nominees closer to the qualified center than the partisan edge.

The Second Face, Made Visible

The phrase "the second face of power" describes power exercised not through visible decisions but through control of what decisions get made — the ability to keep certain options off the agenda, to ensure certain candidates are never seriously considered, to shape the choice set before any choosing begins. It is power that is almost impossible to contest because it is almost impossible to see.

The pool nomination system is a direct constitutional response to this problem in the specific domain of executive appointment. It does not eliminate the second face of power — no mechanism could do that entirely. But it makes one of its most significant manifestations visible. The choice set is public. The executive's choice from it is public. The gap between the two is public. Citizens who can see the field can ask better questions. Senators who confirm against a background of public knowledge are performing a richer constitutional function. Future nominees who know their application becomes a public record have different incentives than nominees who exist only in private networks.

The pool nomination system changes nothing about who nominates, who confirms, or who serves. It changes only one thing: whether the public can see. And in a constitutional system designed around the principle that power exercised in public is power that can be questioned, that is not a small thing.
