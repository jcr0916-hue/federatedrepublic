Sandris on the Formula Deferral: "I Said This in Month 12"

Three months after the Monetary Authority's Korda remedy plan, and three weeks after the Assembly deferred the structural fix it called for, Pell Sandris has issued his first direct statement on the matter. It is not about the plan. It is about what happened to it.

Petra Vend Political Correspondent · Verentum Year 13, Month 5

Pell Sandris did not respond to the Monetary Authority's remedy plan when it was published in Month 2. He did not respond when Mak endorsed all three of its recommendations within hours, or when the Legislature's ninety-day window began to close, or when RNN asked his campaign directly, in Month 3, whether he had a position. His campaign's answer at the time was one word: reviewing.

He has a position now. It arrived three weeks after the Assembly passed the minimum transfer and declined to take up the formula review — the outcome Sandris had, without naming it specifically, predicted five months earlier.

"The formula review didn't fail. It was never scheduled. Those are different problems, and only one of them has a fix."

"I read the remedy plan when it came out," Sandris said, at a Revenue and Infrastructure Committee briefing in Aldenmere that was not organized as a campaign event and drew one reporter besides this one. "I didn't respond immediately because the plan was correct and I had nothing to add to it. What I've been waiting to see is whether the Legislature would treat the structural recommendation with the same seriousness as the emergency one. It didn't. That's not a surprise. That's the pattern."

The Pattern

Sandris's argument, first made in this paper in Month 12 of Year 12, is that the Republic's constitutional institutions are designed to coordinate and that the Legat Consul race has been conducted as though coordination is someone else's problem. The Assembly's Month 4 vote is, in his telling, exhibit two. The MA identified a design gap — no displacement modifier in the equalization formula. The Legislature funded the symptom and left the diagnosis on the table.

"Nobody voted against the formula review," he said. "It just wasn't on the floor when the emergency transfer was. That's the whole story. The urgent thing crowded out the important thing, and everyone went home having done something, which felt like enough."

Asked why he waited three months to say so, Sandris did not offer a strategic explanation. "I wanted to see what actually happened, not what I expected to happen. I could have predicted this in Month 2. I'd rather be right about what occurred than right in advance about what I assumed would."

What Comes Next

Sandris's proposal, released alongside his statement, is narrow: a standing rule requiring any MA remedy plan with more than one recommendation to receive a floor vote on each recommendation separately, rather than as a bundled package that allows the Legislature to satisfy the statutory deadline by acting on the least contested item. He has not filed it as legislation — he holds no federal seat to file it from — but has asked each of the other three declared candidates whether they would support it if elected.

As of publication, none has responded.

Petra Vend is The Torenthian's political correspondent, based in Verentum.
