Inspectorate Independence · IG Confirmation · §8.3

The Confirmation Vote

Year 3. The SC nominates a Legislative Inspector General. The national confirmation vote is six weeks away. A coordinated campaign argues the nominee is too aggressive. 51.3% confirm.

§8.3 Inspector General Selection §7.3 Federal Elections §8.1.a The Legislative Inspectorate §8.6 Inspectorate Operations

The Republic's first LI Inspector General served a ten-year term before handing over to a successor. The SC nominated Professor Adaeze Okafor-Williams, a constitutional law scholar who had spent eight years advising the JIC's methodology committee and had publicly argued for a more aggressive interpretation of the LI's fiscal accuracy audit mandate. The Elections Panel published the nomination and the pool record to the NRS within two hours.

Under §8.3, the JI IG is selected by public lottery 24 months before the incumbent's term expires, ensuring confirmation is complete before the transition. Okafor-Williams had been confirmed before her predecessor's term concluded and assumed full IG authority from her first day in office under §1.31.

The Campaign Against Confirmation

Within ten days of the nomination, three political formations with House majorities in contested States had published statements opposing confirmation. Their argument was not that Okafor-Williams was unqualified — she was pool-certified, the JI had confirmed eligibility, the EI had reviewed financial disclosures. Their argument was that her publicly stated interpretation of the LI's mandate would produce a more aggressive audit posture toward legislative fiscal projections, creating political costs for governments that habitually used optimistic revenue assumptions.

The Senate, stripped of confirmation authority by §8.3's national vote requirement, had no procedural vehicle. The opposition was purely political: vote no at the electoral period.

§8.3 — Why National Confirmation Breaks the Loop
The Senate previously confirmed the LI IG who audits the Legislature. National popular confirmation removes the audited body from the confirmation process.

The previous confirmation design had a structural weakness: the Senate — the body the LI audits for fiscal accuracy — confirmed the person who would conduct those audits. A Senate that preferred a less aggressive auditor could block a more aggressive nominee within its own confirmation process. National popular confirmation closes that loop. The Legislature has no formal role in confirming its own auditor.

The practical effect was that the opposition to Okafor-Williams had to make its case to citizens rather than to senators. The case they made — that a more aggressive fiscal auditor would produce political costs for governments — was, from the perspective of citizens who pay taxes based on those fiscal projections, an argument in her favour.

The Vote — 51.3% Confirm

The confirmation vote appeared on the federal electoral period ballot alongside House elections in 31 States. Turnout for the IG confirmation question was 71% of total electoral period voters — lower than the concurrent House races but substantially higher than the electoral period's organizers had expected for a constitutional officer confirmation. The result: 51.3% confirm, 48.7% decline.

Okafor-Williams was confirmed. She had been serving in an acting capacity for six weeks and had already issued one major preliminary finding on fiscal projection methodology. That finding was not affected by the confirmation vote — acting IG authority under §8.3 is full IG authority, not provisional.

What This Scenario Tests

§8.3's national popular confirmation for the LI and EI IGs was designed to remove the audited body from the confirmation process. This scenario tests whether the design achieves its purpose: an opposition that would have blocked confirmation in the Senate had to instead make its case to citizens, and lost. The margin was narrow — 51.3% — but the constitutional mechanism worked as intended.

The acting-capacity provision is equally important. Six weeks of full IG authority before confirmation means the LI never lost a working Inspector General during the transition. A confirmation vote that fails does not create a gap in oversight — the acting IG completes open findings within 60 days while the SC initiates a new nomination. The accountability infrastructure is continuous, not dependent on the electoral calendar.
