The Unremovable

Year 5. Davan Iskal's residency certification is revoked for documented fraud, and a final removal order issues. His state of origin has ceased to function; no third country will accept him; the one government that offers to take him is the reason he cannot be sent. He is also the subject of a security assessment. §6.3.a was written for exactly this man — the case with no good outcome, where the constitution's floor is actually tested.

§6.3.a Unresolvable and Security Cases §1.21 Non-Refoulement §6.2 Enforcement, Removal, and Appeals §1.11 Habeas Corpus §6.3 Asylum and Protection

Every immigration system produces a small number of cases it cannot finish. A person is ordered removed — lawfully, after process, for cause — and then the order meets the world: the destination state will not issue travel documents, or has ceased to exist as a functioning authority, or would persecute the person on arrival, which §1.21 prohibits absolutely and non-derogably. Systems without a rule for this remainder drift into one of two answers. They detain indefinitely, warehousing people whose only remaining offense is being unreturnable. Or they deport anyway and stop asking what happens on the far end. §6.3.a exists because the Republic's founders had seen both answers and prohibited both.

Davan Iskal entered the Republic in Year 2 through the two-stage system: sponsored by Evantine, federally certified, credentialed. In Year 4 a routine certification audit found the fraud — his identity documents were authentic, but the employment and residency history supporting his certification category had been fabricated by the brokerage that arranged his application, with his participation. Revocation proceedings ran their full course under §6.2: statutory grounds, judicial challenge, appeal. In Year 5, Month 1, the removal order became final. That was the last routine event in the case.

The Order Meets the World

Iskal's state of origin had collapsed into contested administration two years earlier; no authority there could issue the acceptance a removal requires. Two third countries with historical ties declined. The one government that formally offered to receive him controlled the region his family had fled — and the Asylum Court, on his §1.21 claim, found a genuine risk of persecution on arrival. The finding barred that route absolutely. §1.21 admits no exception and §1.19.a lists it among the six non-derogable rights: not for fraud, not for security, not for the administrative convenience of finishing a file.

What sharpened the case was the second file. The federal certification review had flagged, and an intelligence assessment documented, Iskal's pre-entry association with an armed network in the collapsed state — the same association, as it happened, that made his return there lethal. The Republic thus held a man it had lawfully ordered removed, could not remove, and assessed as a security concern. In most constitutional orders, that combination resolves quietly into a detention cell with no calendar. §6.3.a is written against the quiet resolution.

National Record System — Federal District Court, Evantine — Year 5, Month 4
ORDER — Initial Detention Review, In re Iskal
Conducted under §6.3.a — Unresolvable and Security Cases

The court finds: (1) the removal order is final and cannot presently be executed; execution to the offering state would violate §1.21; (2) the security assessment meets the documentation standard and supports detention at this time; (3) detention conditions comply with the statutory minimum standards of humane treatment.

Detention is authorized. Next mandatory review is calendared at 180 days under §6.3.a. Indefinite detention is not available under any authority, and no showing by the government can make it available.

— Federal District Court

The Clock

§6.3.a builds detention in this posture as a supervised, expiring authority. Initial judicial review within 90 days — Iskal's came at day 74. Mandatory review every 180 days thereafter, before an independent federal court, with the government carrying the burden each time. Statutory minimum conditions of humane treatment, audited. And an outer discipline: no detention beyond 5 years without annual judicial review by the court holding the case. The clock is the constitutional argument. A detention that must justify itself every 180 days is a detention that ends when the justification does.

Year 6, Month 8 — The Fourth Review
The assessment goes stale, and the court says so.

At the fourth review, the government's security submission was substantially the file from Year 5: the pre-entry association, now years old, with no evidence of continued contact, direction, or intent developed since. The court's finding was narrow and sufficient — the documented threat no longer supported detention as against the statutory supervision regime the Legislature had built for §6.3.a cases. Iskal was released to that regime: reporting conditions, a designated residence in Evantine, work authorization within the minimum-status framework, and the standing possibility of re-detention on new evidence, subject to the same review clock.

The government did not appeal. The file, in truth, was the argument the court had accepted.

The Status

What Iskal held afterward is the part legislatures find hardest to write and §6.3.a compels them to: a defined minimum status for a person the Republic can neither keep in a cell nor put on a plane. The statutory regime gave him lawful presence without a path to the certification he had forfeited, material conditions above the destitution line, Article I protections he had never lost — an Inhabitant holds them by presence, not by paperwork — and periodic review in both directions: of his conditions, and of whether removal had become executable. In Year 9 it had not. The case remains open, reviewed, and recorded, which is the only resolution the constitution promises for it.

What This Scenario Tests

§6.3.a is the constitution operating at its point of maximum temptation. Iskal is the least sympathetic figure the immigration system produces — a proven fraud with a security file — and the provision's protections do not depend on sympathy. Indefinite stateless detention is constitutionally prohibited, full stop; the sentence contains no exception for hard cases because it was written for the hard cases. A floor that holds only for the deserving is not a floor.

The provision's architecture is judicial supervision on a mandatory calendar. Nothing about Iskal's release required official mercy or political risk: the 180-day clock forced the government's evidence back before an independent court until the evidence could no longer carry the weight. The design converts a question no elected official wants to answer — when does this man get out? — into a question institutions answer routinely: does the current record justify the current detention?

And the pairing with §1.21 closes the system honestly. Non-refoulement without §6.3.a creates the warehouse; §6.3.a without non-refoulement invites the quiet flight to the persecuting state. Together they force the Republic to hold its hardest case inside the law — supervised, humanely, expensively, indefinitely if need be — because the alternatives are the two things the constitution decided the Republic would not be.
