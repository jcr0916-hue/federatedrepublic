Basic Needs Floor · §10.1.b · §10.1.b.i–iii · Constitutional Promise

The Guarantee

The MA's annual certification finds the BNF basket is not being delivered at the constitutional adequacy standard. The EI publishes a breach notice. An Inhabitant files a constitutional claim in federal court. The Legislature disputes the MA's finding before the SC rather than remediating. The SC upholds the certification. The 90-day clock restarts. The Endowment backstop activates. The basket is delivered.

§10.1.b BNF Adequacy Certification §10.1.b.i EI Breach + Justiciability §10.1.b.ii Legislative Response Obligation §10.1.b.iii National Endowment Backstop §10.1.c BNF Categories §10.6 National Endowment §2.1.d PM Fiscal Certification

The BNF has been underfunded for two budget cycles. Not dramatically — not zero delivery — but the MA's methodology for §10.1.c Category 3 (preventive healthcare access) shows a gap between the certified adequacy standard and what the annual budget actually funds. The gap is largest in three inland States with dispersed rural populations where healthcare delivery costs more per person. The PM's budget office has classified this as a delivery efficiency question, not an adequacy question. The MA disagrees.

The MA's annual adequacy certification — published to the NRS at the close of the fiscal year — finds the BNF basket is not meeting the constitutional standard in the healthcare access category across fourteen territorial zones. The finding is specific: the statutory delivery mechanism is funded at 87% of what the MA's adequacy model requires. The gap is not hypothetical. People in those fourteen zones are not receiving preventive healthcare access at the constitutionally required level.

Layer 1 — Breach Notice and Justiciability — §10.1.b.i

§10.1.b.i is self-executing. The MA's certification triggers two things immediately and simultaneously: the EI publishes a constitutional compliance breach notice to the NRS, and the non-compliance becomes directly justiciable by any Inhabitant who can demonstrate they are not receiving the basket at the certified level.

The EI's breach notice is published within six hours of the MA's certification. It names the specific category, the fourteen zones, and the funding gap percentage. It is not a finding of bad faith — the EI's mandate is factual, not punitive. The notice simply records, permanently and publicly, that the Republic is not meeting its constitutional promise to a defined class of Inhabitants.

§10.1.b.i — The Court Filing
The justiciability is self-executing. No enabling statute needed.

Within three weeks of the breach notice, a constitutional claim is filed in the Republic's federal court system by a resident of one of the fourteen zones — an agricultural worker who has been unable to access the preventive healthcare services the BNF entitles her to. The claim is direct: the Republic has certified it is not delivering what it constitutionally promises, and she is among those not receiving it. The courts may order the Republic to restore compliance.

The PM's legal counsel raises a preliminary argument: the claim is premature because the Legislature has 90 days to respond under §10.1.b.ii and that process should be allowed to run. The court accepts the submission for consideration but does not stay the proceedings — §10.1.b.i is explicit that justiciability is immediate and self-executing. The 90-day process runs in parallel, not as a precondition.

Layer 2 — The Legislature's Response — §10.1.b.ii

The Legislature has 90 days from the EI's breach notice to publish a funded remediation plan or formally dispute the MA's certification before the SC. The PM's budget office, which had classified the gap as a delivery efficiency issue, advises the Legislature to dispute — the MA's adequacy model is contested, and the dispute mechanism exists precisely to resolve such disagreements through the SC rather than through budgetary capitulation.

The Legislature adopts the advice. On Day 34, it files a formal dispute of the MA's certification before the SC. The submission argues that the MA's preventive healthcare adequacy model overestimates what the constitution requires for that category — the BNF's healthcare component mandates access, not outcome, and access in rural zones can be satisfied by mobile clinics and telehealth arrangements that the MA's methodology fails to account for.

§10.1.b.ii — SC Resolution
The SC has 30 days to resolve a formal dispute. If the certification is upheld, the 90-day clock restarts.

The SC's ruling comes on Day 59 — 25 days after the dispute was filed. The ruling is mixed. On the telehealth methodology question, the SC finds partly for the Legislature: the MA must update its adequacy model to account for telehealth access in future certifications. But on the current year's finding, the SC upholds the MA's certification: the existing model is the operative standard, the 87% funding gap is real, and the Republic is not meeting its obligation under the current methodology. The dispute is resolved against the Legislature on the operative question.

Under §10.1.b.ii, when the SC upholds the certification, the 90-day remediation clock restarts from the SC's ruling date. The Legislature now has 90 days from Day 59 to publish a funded remediation plan. Day 149 is the deadline.

Layer 3 — The Endowment Backstop — §10.1.b.iii

The Legislature debates a supplementary budget appropriation for the healthcare access gap through Days 60–140. The numbers are not large — the 13% funding gap across fourteen zones represents less than 0.3% of the annual BNF allocation. But the budget cycle is mid-year and the supplementary appropriation requires a majority vote in a House that is closely divided. Three weeks of negotiations produce a draft bill. It passes the House on Day 138. The Senate debates it for four days. On Day 148, the Senate vote is 48–52. The appropriation fails by three votes.

Day 149 passes without a published remediation plan and without a new pending SC dispute. The LI certifies a second compliance breach: the Legislature has failed to meet its §10.1.b.ii obligation within the restarted 90-day window. The Endowment backstop is now available.

§10.1.b.iii — Endowment Backstop Activation
Day 0: MA certification — 87% delivery in 14 zones
Day 0: EI breach notice + justiciability activated
Day 34: Legislature files SC dispute
Day 59: SC upholds certification; 90-day clock restarts
Day 149: Legislature fails to pass remediation bill (48–52)
Day 149: LI certifies second breach; Endowment backstop available
Day 152: Endowment board authorizes emergency BNF healthcare expenditure
Day 153: PM certifies expenditure under §2.1.d; published to NRS
Backstop covers the 13% gap for up to 6 months

The Endowment board authorizes expenditure on Day 152. Three board members dissent — not on the legality, but on the precedent: using Endowment funds to cover a gap that should be in the annual budget removes the fiscal pressure on the Legislature to act. The majority responds that the constitution is clear. The Endowment's purpose includes exactly this backstop. The Inhabitants in the fourteen zones cannot wait for the Legislature to find three more Senate votes.

The PM certifies the expenditure on Day 153 under §2.1.d. The notice is published to the NRS. Healthcare access funding reaches the fourteen zones at the certified adequacy level within 11 days of Endowment authorization, through supplementary contracts with mobile healthcare providers in the affected areas.

The Court Case — Running in Parallel
The Endowment backstop does not end the litigation.

The federal court case filed in Day 3 reaches a hearing on Day 161. The Republic's legal position has shifted: the gap is now being filled by the Endowment backstop, but the court's jurisdiction is not extinguished by that fact. The constitutional claim is that the Republic failed to deliver — the Endowment delivery is remediation, not a finding that no failure occurred.

The court's ruling on Day 197: the Republic was in breach of its constitutional obligation to the claimant from the date of the MA's certification. The Endowment backstop restored delivery but does not extinguish the breach. The court orders no damages — the constitution does not provide for monetary compensation in BNF adequacy cases — but issues a prospective order: the Republic must fund Category 3 BNF delivery in the fourteen zones at the certified adequacy level in the next annual budget. The order is published. It is the first court order ever issued against the Republic for BNF inadequacy.

The Legislature's next annual budget, passed six weeks after the court order, fully funds the healthcare access category at the MA's revised adequacy standard — which, following the SC's methodology ruling, now incorporates telehealth access credits for rural zones. The 13% gap closes. The Endowment backstop winds down on Day 333. The LI's annual report notes that the BNF adequacy mechanism worked as designed: the promise was kept throughout, even when the Legislature failed to fund it through the normal process.

The Architecture of §10.1.b.i–iii

The three layers are not alternatives — they run in sequence, each adding pressure without replacing what came before. The EI breach notice and justiciability (Layer 1) create immediate political and legal accountability. The legislative obligation and SC dispute process (Layer 2) give the elected branches a defined window and a dispute mechanism. The Endowment backstop (Layer 3) ensures the constitutional promise is kept regardless of legislative outcome.

The dissenting Endowment trustees were not wrong that the backstop reduces fiscal pressure on the Legislature. That is the price of guaranteeing the promise. The constitution made a commitment to Inhabitants. The Endowment exists to honour it when the annual budget fails to.
