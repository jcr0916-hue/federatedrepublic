The Acting Inspector

Year 6. LI Inspector General Marcus Osei dies unexpectedly mid-term. The §8.1.d acting mechanism activates — pool-sourced, both executives approve within 5 days. Four weeks later, the Acting IG publishes a major fiscal finding. The Legislature argues it carries no weight. The constitution disagrees.

§8.1.d IG Term Expiry and Incapacity §1.31 Continuity of Constitutionally Mandated Offices §8.6 Inspectorate Operations §8.3 Inspector General Selection §9.1 National Record System

LI Inspector General Marcus Osei was four years into his ten-year term when he died of a cardiac event on a Tuesday morning in Year 6. His authority terminated at that moment — not by proceedings, not by proclamation, simply as a constitutional fact under §8.1.d. The Legislative Inspectorate was without an Inspector General before noon.

§8.1.d activated immediately. The most senior eligible member of the LI Candidate Pool by continuous pool tenure who accepted designation would serve as Acting Inspector General until a successor was confirmed through the normal §8.3 process. The Elections Panel published the pool's seniority roster to the NRS. Professor Yuki Asante-Mensah — a former federal appellate judge, eight years in the pool — accepted designation within two hours. Both executives approved within the constitutional five-day window; in practice, both approved the same afternoon. The designation was published to the NRS by end of day.

Asante-Mensah held full constitutional authority of the LI from the moment of designation. She was also, under §8.1.d, explicitly ineligible for permanent appointment to the position in which she was serving. The acting role carried no pathway to the substantive appointment. Upon conclusion of her acting service, she would return to the pool — still eligible for the EI or JI IG positions, but not the LI IG role she was currently filling.

The Finding

Four weeks into Asante-Mensah's acting tenure, the LI published its annual compliance report. The timing was not her choice — the report had been substantially complete before Osei's death, and the LI's institutional calendar required publication within a defined window. Deferring it would itself have been a constitutional compliance issue.

The report contained the Republic's first Significant Concern finding on fiscal accuracy: the Legislature's revenue projections in the prior three budget cycles had systematically overstated collections by an average of 4.8%. The finding was not a Critical Failure — that threshold required a pattern across a majority of budget cycles — but it was material, detailed, and supported by four years of comparative data. It was published to the NRS permanently on a Wednesday morning.

By Wednesday afternoon, House members whose budget projections the finding had criticised were arguing that an acting IG lacked authority to issue major findings — that the pool-sourced acting mechanism produced a provisional official whose constitutional standing was inherently limited, and that a finding of this significance should be deferred until a confirmed IG was in office.

§8.1.d — Acting IG Holds Full Constitutional Authority
The Acting IG holds full constitutional authority of the office.

The LI's legal counsel published a brief interpretive note to the NRS within 24 hours. The note was five paragraphs. Its conclusion was one sentence: there is no provisional category of Inspectorate findings, and no constitutional distinction between a finding published by a confirmed IG and a finding published by an Acting IG designated under §8.1.d. The Acting IG holds full constitutional authority. The finding stood.

The House members arguing for deferral had no procedural vehicle. The LI's findings are not subject to legislative approval, delay, or challenge on grounds of the finding officer's appointment status. The NRS record was permanent from the moment of publication — §8.6 makes suppression, delay, or alteration by any official a constitutional violation. The argument was constitutionally unavailable from the moment it was made.

The Ineligibility Question

A secondary argument emerged from a different direction. If Asante-Mensah was ineligible for the permanent appointment, her critics argued, the finding she had issued was structurally suspect — an official whose tenure was designed to be temporary had issued a finding with permanent consequences. This framing misunderstood the ineligibility provision's purpose.

§8.1.d — Ineligibility Protects the Finding, Not the Role
The Acting IG is not eligible for permanent appointment to the position in which they serve.

The ineligibility provision exists precisely to protect the integrity of findings issued during acting service. An Acting IG who was eligible for permanent appointment would face a structural incentive to manage their findings to maximize their chances in the subsequent confirmation process. Asante-Mensah had no such incentive. She could not be confirmed to the LI IG position regardless of what any finding said or how the Legislature responded to it. The ineligibility provision removes the conflict of interest that would otherwise compromise acting authority. It strengthens the finding's legitimacy; it does not weaken it.

The Succession Timeline

Under §8.3, the SC was required to initiate a new nomination from the LI Candidate Pool within 60 days of the vacancy. The nomination must be completed no later than 24 months before the anticipated confirmation — in this case, positioned to align with the next federal electoral period. Asante-Mensah would serve as Acting IG for approximately 22 months, through two LI annual compliance reports and one joint Inspectorate quarterly assessment, before a confirmed successor could take office under §1.31.

During that period, the LI continued to function with full constitutional authority. Every finding published was permanent. Every compliance analysis was valid. The constitutional continuity that §1.31 was designed to protect in normal transitions applied equally to the acting mechanism: the Republic's Legislative Inspectorate did not lose a day of operational capacity from the moment Osei died to the moment his confirmed successor assumed office.

What This Scenario Tests

§8.1.d's acting mechanism is designed to ensure that an Inspectorate never loses operational capacity because of an unexpected mid-term vacancy. The scenario tests whether pool-sourced acting authority is genuinely equivalent to confirmed authority in its constitutional effect — and the answer is unambiguous: it is. The full authority provision and the permanent NRS record together make the argument for deferral constitutionally unavailable regardless of how the acting official was designated.

The ineligibility provision tests a subtler point: the design feature that appears to limit the Acting IG's standing actually strengthens it. An acting official who cannot benefit from their performance in the role has no institutional incentive to manage that performance for personal advantage. The findings of an ineligible Acting IG are structurally cleaner than the findings of an eligible one would be. This is the design working correctly — not despite the ineligibility rule, but because of it.

The scenario also demonstrates §1.31's continuity principle in its unexpected-vacancy application. Normal transitions produce seamless IG-to-IG succession with no acting period. Unexpected vacancies produce pool-sourced acting authority with no gap in institutional function. The constitutional order does not pause while the Republic processes the loss of a constitutional officer. It continues, on the same mandate, with the same authority, through a different person.
