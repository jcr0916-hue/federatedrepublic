LI Inspector General · Acting Authority · §8.3

The Acting Inspector

Year 8. The SC nominates a new LI IG. She serves immediately in an acting capacity. Six weeks before the confirmation vote, a major fiscal finding drops. Does it carry full constitutional weight?

§8.3 Inspector General Selection §8.6 Inspectorate Operations §8.11 Eight-Year Assessment §9.1 National Record System

When LI Inspector General Marcus Osei completed his ten-year term in Year 8, the SC nominated Professor Yuki Asante-Mensah to succeed him. The JI certified Registry eligibility. The EI cleared financial disclosures. The Elections Panel published the nomination to the NRS. Under §8.3, Asante-Mensah took office in an acting capacity immediately — full IG authority, pending the national confirmation vote at the next federal electoral period, six weeks away.

On Week 4 of her acting tenure, Asante-Mensah published the LI's annual compliance report. It contained the Republic's first Significant Concern finding on fiscal accuracy: the Legislature's revenue projections in the prior three budget cycles had systematically overstated collections by an average of 4.8%. The finding was not a Critical Failure — the threshold for that was a pattern across a majority of budget cycles — but it was material, detailed, and published to the NRS permanently.

The Confirmation Campaign and the Finding

The Significant Concern finding arrived six weeks before the national confirmation vote. Immediately, House members whose budget projections the finding had criticised argued that an acting IG should not issue major findings — that the acting authority was provisional and the finding should be deferred until confirmation.

§8.3 — Acting IG Has Full Authority
The nominee takes office in an acting capacity immediately on nomination and exercises full IG authority pending confirmation.

The LI's legal counsel published a brief interpretive note to the NRS: acting IG authority under §8.3 is identical to confirmed IG authority. There is no provisional category of Inspectorate findings. A finding issued by an acting IG is a finding of the Republic's Legislative Inspectorate. The Significant Concern finding stood. The NRS record was permanent from the moment of publication.

The House members arguing for deferral had no procedural vehicle. The LI's findings are not subject to legislative approval or delay. The finding was published. The only question was whether citizens would confirm the IG who had issued it.

The Confirmation Vote — 54.1%

The confirmation ballot question was the most contested of the three IG confirmation votes held in Year 8's federal electoral period. The other two — a JIC lottery for the Advisory Council and a Senate vote on an unrelated judicial appointment — attracted minimal public attention. The LI IG confirmation attracted 74% of electoral period voters, with organized campaigns on both sides.

The result: 54.1% confirm. Asante-Mensah was confirmed as full LI Inspector General. The Significant Concern finding that had preceded the vote remained on the NRS record. Her confirmed status did not alter the finding's standing — it had full constitutional weight from the moment she published it as acting IG.

§8.6 — Findings Cannot Be Suppressed
Every finding must be published to the NRS permanently and free of charge. No official may suppress, delay, classify, or alter an Inspectorate report before publication.

The House members who had argued for deferral acknowledged, after the confirmation vote, that the argument had been constitutionally unavailable. The acting IG had full authority; the finding had been published; the NRS record was permanent and unalterable. The confirmation vote had resolved the only remaining question — whether citizens wanted the person who had issued the finding to remain in the role. 54.1% had said yes.

What This Scenario Tests

§8.3's acting-capacity mechanism is designed to ensure that the LI never loses operational capacity during a transition. The scenario tests whether acting authority is genuinely equivalent to confirmed authority — and the answer is unambiguous: it is. An acting IG who defers a major finding to avoid affecting the confirmation vote would be subordinating the LI's constitutional mandate to their own political survival. The design makes that subordination constitutionally unavailable by making the finding's publication authority immediate and the NRS record permanent and unalterable.

The confirmation vote then becomes exactly what it should be: a citizen judgment on whether the person who has been exercising the IG's authority should continue to exercise it. Not a judgment on whether their findings were popular, but on whether they exercised the mandate with integrity. 54.1% found that they had.
