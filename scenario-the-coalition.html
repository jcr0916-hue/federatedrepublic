Executive Accountability · §2.6 · Constructive No Confidence

The Coalition

The Assembly has the votes to remove the Civic Consul. It does not have the votes to agree on who comes next. Under a constructive vote of no confidence, that is the same as having no votes at all.

§2.6 Constructive No Confidence §2.6.a Government Formation §2.5 The Civic Consul §3.2 The Speaker

The Assembly elected Sera Vonn as Civic Consul in Year 1 by the narrowest of margins — an absolute majority of 251, assembled from three factions that agreed on little except that the alternative was worse. By Year 2 the coalition has frayed. A tax measure split her own bloc; a procurement scandal touched a minister she was slow to dismiss; her approval among Assembly members, by any informal count, has fallen well below half. The Conservative Alliance, the largest single faction in the chamber, wants her gone and says so openly.

Wanting her gone is not the constitutional question. The constitutional question is what the Assembly must actually do to remove her — and the answer is more demanding than a show of displeasure.

The Mechanism

Under §2.6, the Assembly may remove the Civic Consul only through a constructive vote of no confidence: a single vote that simultaneously removes the sitting Civic Consul and installs a named replacement, carried by an absolute majority of full seated Assembly membership. The word that governs everything here is constructive. A vote that merely expresses that the Assembly no longer wants Sera Vonn — a bare censure, a confidence vote with no named successor — has, in the constitution's own words, no constitutional effect.

This is not a threshold problem. The Conservative Alliance does not need a supermajority. It needs a simple absolute majority — 251 of 500 — the very number that installed Vonn in the first place. On paper, the numbers to remove her exist: a clear majority of members would vote against her in a heartbeat. The difficulty is that the constitution does not offer them a vote against her. It offers them only a vote for someone else.

The Design in One Line

You cannot remove a Civic Consul because a majority is angry. You can remove one only if that same majority can agree on who replaces her — in the same breath, on the same ballot.

The Arithmetic of "Against"

The Conservative Alliance holds 190 seats — the largest faction, but not a majority. To reach 251 it needs partners, and the partners are the two factions that helped elect Vonn and have since drifted from her: the Territorial bloc (72 seats) and a cluster of independents and small-party members (roughly 60). Every one of these members would vote to end Vonn's tenure. Not one of them agrees on what should follow it.

The Conservative Alliance's price for its 190 votes is one of its own in the chair. The Territorial bloc will not hand the executive to the Conservative Alliance — the whole reason it broke from the governing coalition was to resist that faction's agenda; installing its leader as Civic Consul would be a defeat larger than leaving Vonn in place. The independents will move only for a genuine compromise candidate, someone none of the three factions controls — and no such person can assemble 251, because each faction would rather block a rival's choice than accept a neutral one.

So the chamber arrives at the constitutional paradox the drafters built on purpose. There are, without question, more than 251 members who want Sera Vonn out of office. There is no set of 251 members who want the same person in it. Under §2.6, the second fact is the only one that counts.

The Attempt

The Conservative Alliance forces the issue anyway. It moves a constructive vote of no confidence naming its own deputy leader as successor, publishing its grounds to the NRS as §2.6 requires — the procurement scandal, the tax failure, a loss of the chamber's confidence. The grounds are real and the filing is proper. The vote is a political act; it is not reviewable by any court. It simply has to command 251 votes, and it does not.

The motion draws 194 — the Alliance and a handful of independents who could stomach its candidate. The Territorial bloc votes no, not because it supports Vonn but because it will not install the Alliance's deputy. The motion fails. Sera Vonn, whom a clear majority of the Assembly would remove if removal were a simple matter of removal, remains Civic Consul. She has survived not because she is protected by a high bar, but because her opponents are divided by a low one.

Why the Design Holds This Shape

The instinct on first reading is that this is a flaw — that a Civic Consul the chamber plainly rejects should not be able to hide behind the opposition's disunity. The drafters saw it the other way. The alternative to a constructive vote is a destructive one: a chamber that can remove an executive on a wave of anger without having to answer the question and then what? That produces exactly the instability the Republic's dual executive was built to avoid — serial removals, caretaker vacuums, an executive chair that turns over every time a coalition sours, governance by perpetual crisis.

By fusing removal to installation, §2.6 forces the chamber to convert its opposition into an actual decision. A majority that can govern — that can agree on a direction and a person to carry it — can remove and replace a Civic Consul in a single afternoon by simple majority. A majority that can only object, that agrees on nothing but its grievance, cannot. The mechanism does not protect unpopular Consuls. It protects the office from being emptied by factions that have the numbers to destroy but not the agreement to build. Sera Vonn's survival is not a bug in that design. It is the design reporting, accurately, that the Assembly does not yet have a government to replace her with.

The Reading

Constructive no confidence turns "we reject her" into "we choose him" — and a chamber that cannot make the second statement has not, constitutionally, made the first. Stability here is not a high wall around the executive. It is the requirement that the power to unmake a government be identical to the power to make one.
