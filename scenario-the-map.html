Redistricting · LM Review · §3.3 · Elections Panel

The Map

Year 2. A State submits its Assembly district map. The LM finds two districts fail the compactness criterion. The finding carries no binding force — but it carries standing for every citizen to bring an SC challenge.

§3.3 Electoral Districts§9.1.a Legislative Monitor§4.5 Judicial Review and Constitutional Authority§11.1 NRS Panel and Elections Panel

The State of Rennmark submitted its federal Assembly district map to the LM six weeks before the electoral filing deadline. The map had thirty-one districts. It passed the population equality test. It passed geographic contiguity. It drew no district using racial composition as a primary criterion.

The LM published its findings eleven days later. Two districts failed the compactness criterion. One district — the Seventh — was drawn in a shape that divided a coherent urban community across three non-contiguous segments connected by narrow land corridors. The LM's finding was specific: the Seventh District did not meet the reasonable compactness standard of §3.3 and bore the hallmarks of intentional community dilution. The finding was published to the NRS. It was information. Nothing else.

§3.3 — What the LM Finding Does
The finding carries no binding force. It carries everything else.

The LM does not have the authority to reject the map, order a redraw, or block the election. Its mandate is information only. The finding is published to the NRS permanently, alongside the map itself, as part of the electoral record.

What the finding does: it gives standing. Any citizen, any State government, any parliamentary minority may challenge the map before the SC under §3.3. The LM's published analysis is the factual foundation for that challenge. The clock matters — the filing deadline is six weeks away.

§3.3 — The Elections Panel Backstop
If the map fails, the Elections Panel draws an interim map. This is not punitive — it is procedural.

A coalition of community organizations in Rennmark's Seventh District filed an SC challenge within forty-eight hours of the LM publication. The State government, facing the deadline, had two choices: redraw the two failing districts, or let the SC rule.

The State chose to redraw. A revised map was submitted four days before the filing deadline. The LM reviewed the revised districts within seventy-two hours and published a clean compliance finding. The Seventh District, redrawn, now followed the community's natural boundaries. The LM's finding on the original map remained on the NRS — permanently, alongside the revised map — as part of the electoral record of the State.

Had the State not redrawn, the SC would have ruled. Had the SC found the map non-compliant, the Elections Panel would have drawn an interim map — applying the same five criteria — that would have governed the election. The State's map would have been retired and the interim map used until the State submitted a compliant version.

Three things are true simultaneously: the LM's finding was information only; it triggered a challenge that gave the State a credible reason to act; and the constitutional backstop — interim Elections Panel map — was ready if the State had not. The mechanism doesn't require anyone to capitulate. It requires that someone is always ready to draw a compliant map.
