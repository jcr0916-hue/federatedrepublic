States & Territories · §15.9 · Voluntary State Independence

The Referendum

Year 9. Carenthal holds an independence referendum. 61.3% in favor, 62.1% participation. Both thresholds met. The Elections Panel certifies and publishes to the NRS. The election is irrevocable from that moment. What the constitution requires next — and what it prohibits.

§15.9 Voluntary State Independence §11.1 Elections Panel §10.1 NRS Publication §2.14 Council of Ministers §2.1 Legat Consul §2.5 Civic Consul

Carenthal's independence movement had been building for six years before the State legislature voted on whether to submit the question to citizens. The arguments were cultural, economic, and geographic — a coastal State that had always felt distinct from the interior, whose commercial relationships ran more naturally across the Western Sea than toward Eldenvar. None of that was constitutionally relevant. What mattered was whether the legislature could resolve by 2/3 majority to hold the referendum, and whether the referendum itself could clear both thresholds: 60% of votes cast, and 60% participation among eligible voters.

The legislature resolved 41-20. Two-thirds required 40.67 — the vote cleared it by one. The referendum was called. Under §15.9, the Republic's response was defined: it must provide factual information about the independence process upon request. It may not prefer, discourage, or offer inducements toward any outcome. The CC's office published a twelve-page factual summary to the NRS. Nothing else was constitutionally permitted.

Referendum day: 62.1% of Carenthal's eligible voters participated. 61.3% voted in favor of independence. Both thresholds were met. The Elections Panel certified the result and published the independence petition to the NRS.

§15.9 — Irrevocability
The election is irrevocable from the moment of NRS publication.

The NRS publication is not a formality. It is the constitutional moment at which Carenthal's independence election becomes permanent. From that moment, neither Carenthal's legislature nor its citizens could withdraw the petition. The Republic could not decline to process it. No court could suspend it. The constitution does not provide a path back — by design. The irrevocability threshold is NRS publication, not legislative ratification or transition agreement. Both of those come later and may fail. But the election itself cannot be undone.

This is the most consequential feature of §15.9's Stage One. It means the threshold for calling a referendum must be understood as the point of no return, not a request for consideration. Carenthal's legislature, in voting 41-20, committed the State to an irreversible constitutional process the moment the referendum passed.

§15.9 — Stage Two: Recusal and the Chamber Math
Both chambers vote within 90 days. The petitioning State's own members are recused.

Within 90 days of NRS publication, both chambers were required to hold a concurrent ratification vote. The required threshold — 60% — was calculated from the remaining seated members after recusal, not from the full chamber. Carenthal held 3 Assembly districts and 2 Senate seats. Those 5 members were recused from the vote entirely.

Assembly: 200 total seats, 3 recused, 197 eligible. 60% threshold: 118 votes needed. The Assembly voted 124 in favor, 73 opposed. The threshold cleared.

Senate: 24 total seats (12 States, 2 each), 2 recused, 22 eligible. 60% threshold: 14 votes needed. The Senate voted 15 in favor, 7 opposed. The threshold cleared.

The recusal mechanism is not a curiosity — it is load-bearing. It means a State seeking independence cannot help ratify its own departure. The vote must reflect the will of the Republic that remains, not the will of the State leaving it. A State with high political pull in both chambers might clear Stage One but find Stage Two harder. A State that is genuinely peripheral — as Carenthal was — may find Stage Two easier than a more integrated one would.

§15.9 — What Happens If Either Chamber Fails
Failure or non-vote within 90 days: the petition lapses. Stage One may restart without restriction.

If the Assembly had voted 117-80 — one short of the 118 needed — the petition would have lapsed. If either chamber had simply failed to schedule a vote within 90 days, the petition would have lapsed. In either case, Carenthal could immediately re-initiate Stage One: another legislative resolution, another referendum, another NRS publication. There is no five-year bar on restarting after a Stage Two failure — that bar applies only to a failed Stage One referendum on the same grounds. A Stage Two failure sends the process back to Stage One; Stage One succeeds again; Stage Two opens again.

This matters. It means that a determined State can persist through repeated Stage Two failures, building political pressure on the chambers until the math changes. The constitution does not cap the number of attempts. It only imposes the 90-day window and the recusal-adjusted threshold each time.

§15.9 — Transition Agreement
Both executives negotiate. The Legislature ratifies by 2/3. The agreement governs everything that follows.

Upon ratification by both chambers, the transition agreement process began. The Legat Consul held primary authority on border and defense terms under §2.1. The Civic Consul held primary authority on domestic implementation under §2.5. Both operated through the Council of Ministers under §2.14. The agreement specified asset and liability allocation, transition timeline, treatment of federal property, fiscal obligations, and continuing obligations of the new state.

The Legislature ratified the final agreement by 2/3 of full seated membership of both chambers — at this stage, Carenthal's members were not recused. They remained seated members of the Republic until the transition agreement took effect. Their votes counted on the ratification of their own independence terms.

Constitutional Finding

§15.9 is structured to make independence hard to start, impossible to reverse once started, and possible to complete only with genuine majority support in the chambers that remain. The 60%/60% referendum thresholds are deliberately high — designed to require both broad support and genuine participation, not just enthusiasm among those who show up. The irrevocability on NRS publication places the point of no return before Stage Two, not after — meaning a failed Stage Two ratification leaves the petition irrevocably published but unratified, and Stage One must be run again.

The recusal mechanic in Stage Two is the most unusual structural feature: the State seeking independence has no vote in whether the Republic lets it leave. That decision belongs to the Republic that remains. A State that is constitutionally successful in Stage One may still be held by a Stage Two that reflects how much the rest of the Republic needs it — or wants it gone.

The prohibition on inducements cuts both ways. The Republic cannot offer incentives to stay, but it also cannot impose penalties for trying to leave. The process is genuinely neutral. Whether that neutrality favors independence movements or disfavors them depends entirely on the thresholds, the recusal math, and the political geography of any given State.
