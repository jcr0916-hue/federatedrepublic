Judicial Accountability · §4.1.c · First SC Justice Removal

The Statement of Grounds

A Supreme Court justice is removed under §4.1.c for the first time — not for her rulings, but for how she issued one. The distinction between judicial philosophy and judicial conduct is the constitutional question the removal mechanism forces the Legislature to answer precisely.

§4.1.c SC Justice Removal §3.5.c Senate Trial §4.2.e Mid-Term Vacancy — 180 Days §4.2.g Temporary Associate Justice §8.6.b No Classification §9.1 NRS Immutability

Justice Elara Vance has served on the Supreme Court for eight years. She was elected in Year 2 of the Republic — the second judicial election cycle — on a platform of strict constitutional textualism, and her record on the Court has been consistent with that position. In forty-one majority opinions, she has developed a coherent body of judicial doctrine. The Legislature has disagreed with much of it. Several of her rulings on the scope of emergency powers under §1.25 have been particularly contested.

The case that produces the Statement of Grounds is not about her judicial philosophy. It is about a specific order, issued on a Tuesday evening in October of Year 9, in response to an emergency motion filed by the Prime Minister's office at 6:47 p.m.

The Order

The motion asked the Court to stay an Executive Inspectorate report scheduled for publication to the National Record System the following morning. The report detailed findings from an eighteen-month audit of the PM's domestic emergency certification process — specifically, the basis for three domestic emergency declarations issued during Year 8. The PM's office characterized the report as operationally sensitive material that, if published before the Legislature could respond, would cause immediate policy harm.

Justice Vance issued the stay at 9:12 p.m. She did not convene the Court. She did not contact the other six justices. She did not publish a written rationale to the National Record System. She issued the order as a single-justice emergency measure and transmitted it to the EI's Director of Operations.

The EI's response, published to the NRS the following morning in place of the stayed report, noted three facts: §8.6.b permanently exempts Inspectorate Reports from classification; the Court's own procedural rules require a quorum of five justices for any stay affecting an Inspectorate publication; and no quorum had been convened. The EI published a notice of constitutional irregularity and held the report. Justice Vance's order remained formally unresolved for forty-seven days — forty-seven days during which the EI's constitutionally mandated report was withheld from public access.

The Court eventually convened and vacated Justice Vance's order 5-2. The report was published. Justice Vance declined to issue a written statement explaining the initial order.

The Statement of Grounds

Under §4.1.c, the House must pass a Statement of Grounds by 2/3 of its full seated membership before a Senate trial may begin. The Statement is a legal document — it specifies the conduct, decisions, or failures that constitute the basis for removal. The Senate trial is strictly limited to the grounds stated; the Senate may not convict on conduct outside the Statement.

The drafting of the Statement takes six weeks. Multiple versions circulate. The central debate is the one the mechanism is designed to force: is the basis for removal her judicial philosophy — her rulings on emergency powers — or her conduct in issuing the October order? The constitution answers this question structurally. You cannot remove a justice for rulings that a 2/3 legislative majority disagrees with. You can remove a justice for conduct that violates the constitutional order. The Statement that emerges focuses exclusively on the October order.

§4.1.c — The Statement of Grounds
What the House charged

The Statement of Grounds, published to the NRS simultaneously with the House vote, specified three distinct grounds: (1) issuance of a judicial order without convening the constitutionally and procedurally required quorum; (2) issuance of a stay affecting an Inspectorate publication in direct conflict with §8.6.b, which permanently exempts Inspectorate Reports from classification or suppression by any authority; and (3) failure to publish any rationale to the National Record System, in violation of the Court's own constitutional transparency obligations under §9.1.

The Statement explicitly excluded any reference to Justice Vance's jurisprudential positions on emergency powers. The drafting committee noted in its published remarks: a justice may interpret §1.25 differently than a legislative majority. That is not a ground for removal. A justice may not suppress an Inspectorate report for forty-seven days by issuing an order the Court had no quorum to authorize. That is.

The Trial

Under §3.5.c, the Senate trial is presided by the Supreme Court's most senior justice — defined as the longest-serving, with age as a tiebreaker — unless that justice is the subject of the proceedings. Justice Vance is the subject. The most senior remaining justice is Justice Kwame Ekwueme, who has served eleven years. Justice Ekwueme presides.

The trial is public. Every session is recorded and published to the NRS in real time. It runs sixty-seven days.

Justice Vance mounts a defense centered on the emergency context of the October order. She argues the motion before her required immediate action; that calling a quorum at 9 p.m. on a Tuesday was practically impossible; that the forty-seven-day hold was not her doing but the EI's interpretation of the order's scope; and that a single-justice emergency measure is within the inherent authority of any justice of any court when circumstances demand it.

The prosecution — conducted by House managers — presents a narrow argument. There is no inherent authority in a constitutional court that overrides explicit quorum requirements. There is no emergency exception to §8.6.b. The forty-seven days were constitutionally intolerable regardless of who caused them. The October order was not a constitutional act.

Justice Vance remains on the Court during the trial period — she is removed on conviction, not on indictment. The Court continues hearing cases at full capacity throughout the sixty-seven days of the Senate trial. Six justices, including Justice Ekwueme who is presiding over the Senate proceedings, rotate between their judicial duties and the trial chamber. The Court adjusts its scheduling to accommodate the divided attention but does not reduce its docket.

Justice Vance is removed. The Senate subsequently votes 78-22 to bar her from holding any future federal office — a separate vote, requiring only a simple majority, which passes comfortably. Her criminal liability for the unauthorized suppression of an Inspectorate report remains separately pursuable; proceedings are opened and ultimately produce a formal charge, though not a conviction.

The Vacancy

Removal is immediate on the Senate vote. Within five days, the most senior Appellate Court judge by continuous service — Justice Amara Diallo of the Fourth Circuit, eighteen years on the appellate bench — is automatically designated as Temporary Associate Justice under §4.2.g. No nomination. No confirmation vote. No legislative action. The designation is published to the NRS the same afternoon.

Justice Diallo takes her seat at the next full Court session, four days after designation. The Court returns to seven justices. The 180-day special election process begins under §4.2.e: candidate registration opens, the EI review cycle starts, the Judicial Selection Board convenes its assessment schedule. The election will be held within 180 days.

§4.2.g — Temporary Associate Justice
Justice Diallo recuses from eleven cases

Justice Diallo's eighteen years on the Fourth Circuit means she wrote or joined rulings that are now before the Supreme Court on appeal. She recuses herself from eleven cases under §4.2.g(b) — cases arising from rulings she authored or joined below. In those eleven cases, the six remaining permanent justices constitute the panel. There are no ties. The Court publishes its recusal protocol to the NRS: the temporary designation is noted on every case in which Justice Diallo participates.

Justice Diallo does not stand as a candidate in the §4.2.e special election. Her temporary service is service — not a platform. The election runs its full 180-day course: thirty days of candidate registration, sixty days of EI and Board review, thirty days of JI certification, and sixty days of national campaign. Fourteen certified candidates appear on the ballot. The election produces a replacement justice who serves the remaining eleven years of Justice Vance's original term.

What This Scenario Tests

§4.1.c is the most carefully bounded removal mechanism in the constitution. The Statement of Grounds requirement forces the House to specify — in writing, on the NRS, before the trial begins — exactly what conduct constitutes the basis for removal. The Senate may not convict on anything outside the Statement. This structural constraint is deliberate: it prevents judicial removal from becoming a legislative weapon against rulings that a 2/3 majority dislikes.

The scenario validates that constraint by showing what it excludes as much as what it permits. Justice Vance's emergency powers jurisprudence — the body of rulings that produced political hostility toward her — is not in the Statement of Grounds. It was not a basis for removal. What is in the Statement is narrow, specific, and documented: a judicial order issued without quorum, affecting an Inspectorate publication that §8.6.b shields from suppression by any authority, with no NRS rationale published. The 87-13 Senate vote suggests that when the conduct is clearly stated, the threshold for removal is not the obstacle — the requirement to be specific about what the justice actually did is.

The designation of Justice Diallo as Temporary Associate Justice under §4.2.g resolves what would otherwise be the mechanism's principal cost. The Court loses nothing operationally: it sits at full capacity from day five of the vacancy through the 180-day election and beyond. What is preserved is the democratic principle — the permanent seat is filled by election, not appointment, and that election has 180 days to produce an informed choice. Justice Diallo's ineligibility to stand for the permanent seat separates service from candidacy. She keeps the Court whole. She does not use it as a stepping stone.
