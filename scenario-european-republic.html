The European Republic

Year 0 to Year 35. Following a decade of institutional crisis, fourteen European states adopt the constitutional framework at a convention in Bruges. What follows is thirty-five years of federation-building — a currency union, a rule-of-law confrontation, a devolution referendum, and the question of what Europe becomes when it stops being a treaty and starts being a republic.

YEAR 0 — THE BRUGES CONVENTIONThe Wager

The treaty-based union had not failed suddenly. It had failed the way old structures fail — gradually, then visibly, then in ways that couldn't be managed by another summit communiqué. The democratic deficit had been debated for forty years. The enforcement problem — a court whose rulings took half a decade to produce consequences that states could largely absorb — had been papered over by optimism about convergence. And the unanimity requirement, which gave every member government a veto over every significant reform, had turned the union into a museum of its founding compromises.

The Bruges Convention opened on a cold Tuesday in February of Year 0. Fourteen states sent delegations with authority to ratify. Seven others sent observers who would spend the next two years watching before their own referenda. The framework document had been circulating for eighteen months — translated into twenty-two languages, debated in every national parliament, praised by some and called dangerous by others. The danger it posed was specific: it would end the fiction that European integration could be achieved without European government.

The convention's hardest negotiation was not about rights — the Individual Sovereignty Floor was the provision that attracted the least resistance, because every delegation had come from somewhere that had already incorporated a rights framework into its national law. The hardest negotiation was about the Senate.

Rheinia and Frankia, the two largest states, had held outsized influence in the old union's weighted-voting Council. The Senate's structure — two senators per state, full stop — was unacceptable to both delegations on the first day and agreed by both delegations on the last. What changed was not the provision. What changed was the recognition that the alternative was not the old union's weighted Council restored; the alternative was no federation at all. Frankia's chief negotiator, Isabelle Moreau, summarized the shift in the terms that appear in every account of the convention: "We came here to win the argument about seats. We leave having understood that the argument was about whether the republic would exist."

The constitution was ratified by all fourteen founding states within the transition window. Rheinia ratified first, 71-29 in referendum. Frankia ratified last among the founders, 53-47 — the closest vote, the most consequential, and the one that produced, in its narrowness, the first political fault line of the new republic.

YEARS 1–5 — TRANSITIONThe Machinery Starts

The transition provisions in Article XIX were designed for a republic forming from scratch or from a prior state. They had not been designed for a republic forming from a functioning supranational bureaucracy with 40,000 employees, an existing court system, a currency union, and forty years of accumulated law. The convention had added supplementary transition protocols, but the underlying constitutional logic still applied: Phase 1 was provisional administration, Phase 2 was institutional construction, Phase 3 was first elections, Phase 4 was full constitutional operation.

The transition produced several problems that were foreseeable and one that was not.

The foreseeable problems: the old union's Court of Justice became the basis for the new Supreme Court's inferior court structure, but its composition — judges appointed by member state governments under intergovernmental negotiation — was constitutionally incompatible with the open-candidacy election model. The first Supreme Court elections, held in Year 3, produced nine justices who had no relationship to the old court's appointment pathways. The old court's sitting members were offered positions in the inferior court structure. Most accepted. Three retired.

The Monetary Authority's relationship to the existing currency was the second foreseeable problem. The old central bank had been governed by a council of national central bank governors — an intergovernmental body whose independence was statutory rather than constitutional. The new MA board was constitutionally independent in a different and stronger sense. The transition protocol provided a five-year conversion period during which the old institutional machinery wound down and the MA assumed full authority. The first MA Director, elected in Year 2 and confirmed by the Senate 38-20, was Dr. Lena Hartmann, a Rheinish economist who had spent fifteen years at the old central bank and eight years writing academic papers about why it was structurally insufficient for a genuine monetary union.

The unforeseeable problem was Therenia.

YEARS 6–8 — THE THERENIA CRISISWhat Enforcement Looks Like

Therenia was one of the smaller founding states — population 9 million, economy mid-sized, a parliamentary tradition that had been functioning for sixty years. It had ratified the constitution 58-42. What it had also done, in the three years before the Bruges Convention, was elect a government that ran on the proposition that national identity was under threat from continental integration, and that the new republic — if it came — should be joined on terms that preserved Therenia's ability to govern itself as Therenia's people wished.

The government that came to power in Therenia in Year 4 was not the government that had ratified the constitution. It had won its election honestly, with a 17-seat majority in a 180-seat parliament. In Year 5 it passed the Judicial Appointments Reform Act, which removed the independence requirements for Therenian state court appointments and gave the governing party majority control over the seven-member Therenian judicial appointments commission. In Year 6 it passed the Media Registration Act, requiring all news organizations operating in Therenia to register with a government board whose members were appointed by the Prime Minister's office. In Year 7 it passed the Civil Society Limitation Act, restricting foreign funding for non-governmental organizations and requiring NGOs receiving any foreign contribution to register as "foreign agents."

Under the old union, each of these acts would have triggered an infringement proceeding. The old court would have issued a ruling, eventually, perhaps three years later, and Therenia would have had months to comply, and would have paid a fine, and might have complied partially. The process was known. It was also known to be manageable.

Under the republic, the process was different.

The Executive Inspectorate opened a review of the Judicial Appointments Reform Act on the day it passed. Its mandate under §8.1 was the constitutional conduct of executive and legislative officers — and a legislative act that removed judicial independence from state courts within the republic's Article I enforcement structure was, the EI found, a constitutional conduct matter. The EI published its preliminary finding to the NRS forty-one days after the Act's passage: the Judicial Appointments Reform Act violated the republic's constitutional requirement that courts hearing Article I claims be independently constituted. The finding named the Therenian Prime Minister, the Justice Minister, and the members of the governing parliamentary majority who had voted for the bill.

NRS Entry — EI Preliminary Finding — Year 6, Day 41 — Therenia Judicial Appointments Act The Executive Inspectorate finds that the Judicial Appointments Reform Act (Therenia, Year 5) is constitutionally incompatible with the Republic's requirement that courts of competent jurisdiction within the federal structure be independently constituted for purposes of Article I enforcement. Named officers: Prime Minister Antal Vásár, Justice Minister Katalin Boros, and 94 members of the National Assembly voting in favour. This finding is preliminary. A formal determination will be published within 90 days following response from named officers. The finding is permanent and publicly accessible at this NRS reference.

This was not a proceeding that took three years. The finding was on the NRS on Day 41. Forty-one days. The named officers had 90 days to respond. Their names were published. The finding was permanent.

The Therenian government's response, published on Day 89, was a 200-page legal brief arguing that state court appointments were a matter of state domestic governance under Article XIII, that the EI had exceeded its mandate, and that the republic's constitution did not require states to adopt any particular model of judicial appointment. The brief was written by a team of constitutional lawyers who were, by all accounts, excellent, and who were, by the EI's published assessment, wrong on the central question.

The EI's formal determination on Day 131 was 340 pages. Its conclusion occupied one paragraph: the Judicial Appointments Reform Act, as applied to courts hearing claims under Article I, was constitutionally void. Its application to purely domestic Therenian law matters not touching federal constitutional rights remained within state competence. Therenia's courts hearing Article I claims were required to be constituted independently of political appointment. The NRS would record any judicial decision issued by an improperly constituted Therenian court as a constitutional nullity for federal enforcement purposes.

This was the enforcement mechanism that the old union had never had. There was no fine to calculate, no years-long proceeding to manage. There was a permanent published finding on the NRS. Every Article I claim brought before a Therenian court that had been constituted under the Reform Act was a constitutional nullity. Every plaintiff who lost such a case could take it to the federal inferior court structure directly. Therenia's judicial system was not shut down. It was rendered procedurally irrelevant for the cases that mattered most.

The Media Registration Act and the Civil Society Limitation Act received EI findings within sixty days of passage. The Media Registration Act finding was published jointly with an LI finding that it violated the enabling framework of the republic's press freedom protections. The Civil Society Limitation Act finding cited §1.12 directly.

By Year 8, the Therenian government had a choice. It could continue passing legislation that accumulated NRS findings and rendered its courts ineffective for federal constitutional purposes, or it could govern within the constitutional framework it had ratified. The political cost of the first option was not abstract — Therenian citizens who had Article I claims were bringing them directly to federal inferior courts at a rate that was publicly tracked on the NRS. The numbers were published monthly. By Year 8 Month 6, 4,200 active federal court cases originated from Therenian claimants bypassing Therenian courts.

The Vásár government fell in the Year 9 election. Its successor repealed the Judicial Appointments Reform Act in Year 10. The EI noted the repeal on the NRS. The prior findings remained — permanent, public, and indexed.

"The old system gave governments a proceeding to manage. This one gives them a record to live with." — Isabelle Moreau, in a lecture at the Bruges Institute, Year 11

YEARS 10–18 — THE MONETARY UNIONDr. Hartmann's Second Term

The republic's Monetary Authority inherited a currency that was already nineteen years old and a monetary union that had survived a debt crisis, a pandemic contraction, and a decade of asymmetric recovery by improvising institutional patches at successive summits. Dr. Hartmann's view, expressed in her confirmation testimony and in every MA annual report during her first term, was that the improvisation period was over. The republic had a constitutionally independent monetary authority with a mandate to maintain price stability and monetary integrity. That mandate was now constitutional, not statutory. It could not be amended by the Senate. It could not be overridden by the PM's Fiscal Objection. It existed in the same constitutional stratum as Article I.

This created, in Year 12, the republic's first major fiscal-monetary confrontation.

The recession of Year 11-12 was not the worst the continent had experienced, but it was severe in three founding states — Velania, Lusitana, and Iberia — whose economies were structurally more exposed to external trade shocks. All three had significant Basic Needs Floor obligations under §10.1 that had been activated by rising unemployment and wage compression. The BNF was self-executing: its activation was automatic, its funding was a constitutional obligation, and it sat outside the ordinary budget process. But the scale of the BNF activation in all three states simultaneously put pressure on the republic's fiscal position in a way that the PM's office, led by Prime Minister Karolina Stein of Rheinia, found alarming.

PM Stein's government proposed a Fiscal Stability Compact — a statutory framework that would require states with BNF activations above a certain threshold to submit to federal fiscal oversight as a condition of BNF funding. The proposal was, constitutionally, questionable. The BNF was not conditional. §10.1 said what it said. But PM Stein was a careful politician and the Compact was drafted carefully: it did not condition BNF payments on state compliance, but it did create a reporting structure and an LI audit trigger that, in practice, would give the federal government significant information and leverage over states in BNF activation.

The MA's position on the Fiscal Stability Compact was separate from the BNF question but entangled with it. Three state governments — Velania, Lusitana, and Iberia — were privately pressing the MA board for an accommodation on monetary conditions that would ease their fiscal position. Dr. Hartmann's response was published to the NRS as a formal board communication: the MA's monetary policy was not available as a fiscal instrument for individual states. The board had considered the macroeconomic position of all fourteen states and determined that current monetary conditions were consistent with its mandate. The communication was three paragraphs. It was published the day after the third informal approach.

The publication was the point. Under the old system, those approaches would have been private conversations between finance ministers and central bank officials. They would have been recorded in no permanent place. The MA's response would have been communicated through back channels and reflected in policy adjustments that were technically unexplained. Under the republic, the approach was on the NRS within twenty-four hours of the MA board's decision to publish. The three state governments' finance ministers were not named — the MA's communication described "informal approaches from three member state governments" — but the timing and circumstances made attribution a matter of elementary political arithmetic.

Dr. Hartmann was reconfirmed for a second term in Year 15 by a Senate vote of 42-16. She served until Year 20. The fiscal-monetary framework she established in those years — independent, published, and constitutionally grounded in a way that the old central bank had never been — became the settled architecture of the republic's economic governance.

YEARS 18–22 — ENLARGEMENTThe Eastern Question

The seven observer states from the Bruges Convention had been watching for eighteen years. By Year 18, four of them had completed their ratification referenda and joined the republic as full states. Three had not, and of those three, one — Kravonia — had spent a decade asking a more fundamental question: whether the republic's framework was compatible with a state that had spent thirty years building democratic institutions against sustained external pressure and had emerged from that experience with a particular and legitimate sensitivity about sovereignty.

The Kravonian debate was the republic's first serious encounter with what the constitution genuinely offered to a potential member state and what it genuinely cost. The offer: the Individual Sovereignty Floor, the enforcement mechanism, the inspectorate structure, the Basic Needs Floor, the MA's protection against the monetary pressure that had been weaponized against Kravonia's economy on two occasions in the preceding decade. The cost: two Senate seats in a body of 32, a PM who emerged from a parliamentary majority and might for many years be from Rheinia or Frankia, and a Supreme Court elected by the entire republic's citizens rather than appointed by Kravonian political authorities.

The Kravonian Constitutional Convention of Year 19 debated these terms for eight months. Its final report — 600 pages, translated into twelve languages and published in full on the republic's NRS at Kravonia's own request, before it had even ratified — is one of the republic's foundational documents of constitutional thinking. Its conclusion: the cost of membership was real. The protection it offered against external pressure was constitutionally guaranteed in a way that no treaty relationship could replicate. The report's final sentence: "A republic that cannot be made to look away is worth what it costs to join."

Kravonia ratified 61-39 in Year 20. The transition period completed in Year 22. Two Kravonian senators took their seats. The republic had eighteen states.

YEARS 25–28 — THE FRANKIA REFERENDUMThe Consent That Is Ongoing

The referendum was not a surprise to anyone who had been paying attention. Frankia had ratified 53-47 in Year 0, and the 47% had not disappeared. They had organized, run candidates in every election, won a significant minority in the Frankian regional assembly, and spent twenty-five years arguing that the republic's federal structure had moved too far, too fast, from what Frankian voters had understood themselves to be ratifying.

The devolution process under Article XIII required a trigger: a formal petition from the state legislature or a citizen initiative meeting the threshold. The Frankian People's Sovereignty Movement had run on a platform of initiating that process since Year 18. In Year 25, they won enough seats in the regional assembly to pass the petition. The petition was published to the NRS. The republic's devolution process, as specified in §13.3, began.

What the devolution framework required, before any vote on independence, was a two-year Early Warning period in which the Inspectorates published assessments of Frankia's governance, its economic integration with the republic, and the projected consequences of devolution. The Early Warning was not an obstacle — it was not designed to prevent devolution, only to ensure that citizens voting on it had access to authoritative information about what they were deciding. The EI's assessment of Frankian governance: exemplary, fully constitutionally compliant, zero outstanding findings. The LI's assessment: Frankia's economy was 34% export-dependent within the republic's internal market, held €2.3 trillion in cross-border obligations with other states, and had 1.1 million citizens employed in other states who held Frankian residential status.

The Remediation Plan — required under §13.3.b — was jointly published by the Frankian regional government and the federal government in Year 26. It was 1,400 pages. It covered the currency transition pathway, the status of Frankian citizens employed in other states, the border arrangements, the fate of the 340,000 citizens of other states living in Frankia, and the relationship between a sovereign Frankia and the republic under Article XX's Associated Community framework.

The devolution referendum was held in Year 27. The question was simple. The Remediation Plan was published in full. Every Frankian citizen had access to the EI and LI assessments. The republic made no argument for or against — PM Stein's successor, Prime Minister Adaeze Obi of Velania, published a single statement: "The republic respects the right of Frankian citizens to decide. Whatever they decide, the republic will honor the process the constitution provides."

The result: 58% voted to remain in the republic. 42% voted to begin the independence process.

The 58-42 result was, in one sense, a victory for continued membership. In another sense it was a data point about the republic's legitimacy in its largest founding state. The PM's office commissioned no celebration. The EI published a note to the NRS: the devolution petition had been filed, the process had been completed, the result was recorded. Both the petition and the result were permanent entries. Frankia remained in the republic, and the republic knew — with constitutional specificity — by how much.

YEAR 35 — THIRTY-FIVE YEARSWhat Europe Became

The republic at thirty-five years is not the Europe that its founders imagined in the 1950s, and it is not the Europe that its critics feared at Bruges. It is something that neither tradition of European thought had fully anticipated: a federal republic with genuine enforcement, a floor of individual rights that governments cannot negotiate away, and a permanent public record of everything done in the republic's name.

Forty-one states have considered ratification. Twenty-two have ratified and become full members. Seven hold Associated Community status under Article XX — the framework that the Bruges Convention had almost removed from the draft as too speculative. Three of those seven are in active accession negotiations. Four appear content with compact relationships that give them the republic's currency, the republic's Article I floor, and access to its internal market, without the obligations of full statehood. Whether that is a stable equilibrium is the republic's open constitutional question at thirty-five years.

The NRS contains 847 million indexed entries. Every law passed by every state assembly in the republic for thirty-five years. Every EI and LI finding. Every MA board communication. Every Senate vote. Every PM certification. Every Supreme Court ruling. The Therenian findings from Year 6 are still there — indexed, searchable, permanent. The Frankia referendum result is there. Dr. Hartmann's published response to the three state government approaches in Year 12 is there.

The question that haunts the republic's constitutional scholars in Year 35 is not whether the document works. The accumulated record demonstrates that it does — imperfectly, slowly, with the friction that any federal system generates, but demonstrably. The question is what the document cannot do. It cannot make a people want to be a republic together. It can make it constitutionally costly to leave. It can make backsliding constitutionally visible. It cannot generate the affection for shared institutions that makes a republic self-sustaining across generations. That work is not constitutional work. It is political and cultural work that the document creates conditions for but cannot perform.

The Bruges Convention's chair, retired at eighty-two and living in Velania, gave a lecture in Year 35 at the institute that bears the convention's name. He was asked whether the republic had been worth building. He said he could not answer that question because it was the wrong question. "We did not build a republic to answer a question," he said. "We built one because the alternative was to keep watching the old structure fail slowly, and we had run out of patience for slow failure. Whether it was worth it depends on what happens next, and that is not something I will live to see."

What This Scenario Tests

The European Republic scenario is primarily a test of what makes the constitutional framework different from treaty-based international arrangements, and why that difference matters. The Therenia crisis is the clearest illustration: under a treaty-based system, the infringement procedure took years and produced manageable consequences. Under the republic's inspectorate model, the finding was on the NRS in forty-one days. The named officers' political exposure was immediate and permanent. The enforcement mechanism was not a fine or a court ruling years in the future — it was the immediate procedural irrelevance of courts constituted in violation of federal requirements. States learned quickly that constitutional non-compliance was not a negotiation with delayed consequences. It was a permanent public record.

The monetary union test shows how the MA's constitutional independence differs from statutory independence. The old central bank was independent by treaty and by statute — which meant it was independent until the political pressure was sufficient to overcome the treaty and the statute. The MA's independence is in the same constitutional stratum as Article I. The PM's Fiscal Objection authority does not reach it. The Senate cannot amend it away. Dr. Hartmann's published response to informal state government approaches was not a gesture of defiance — it was the MA board performing its constitutional function, with the transparency that the NRS requires, in a manner that made future approaches of the same kind politically costly for the governments that made them.

The Frankia referendum tests what the devolution framework is actually for. It is not for preventing independence — the 58-42 result was not foreordained and could have gone differently. It is for ensuring that citizens deciding on independence have authoritative, independent, comprehensive information about what they are deciding, and that the republic's response to any outcome is determined by the constitution rather than by the government of the day. PM Obi's single public statement — that the republic would honor whatever the process produced — is the right answer. It is also the constitutionally required answer. The PM had no constitutional authority to campaign against the referendum. The republic had no constitutional mechanism for preventing the vote. What it had was a transparent process and a permanent record of the result.

The scenario's deepest question — what the document cannot do — is not a critique of the framework. It is an acknowledgment of the distinction between constitutional work and political work. The republic's constitution can establish a floor. It can make backsliding visible. It can protect individuals against their own governments. It cannot make those governments legitimate in the eyes of the people they govern. That legitimacy has to be earned by the people who hold office under the document, year after year, in ways the document enables but cannot compel. Therenia's Vásár government failed to earn it and fell. Frankia's 58% chose to sustain it, for now. The republic continues.
