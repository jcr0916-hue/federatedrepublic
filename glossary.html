A
Associated Community §20.1 →

A polity that has entered a compact relationship with the Republic through Article XX — below Territory status, above a treaty relationship. Associated Community residents hold full Article I protections regardless of the compact's internal governance structure. The pathway is available to independent entities that choose a closer relationship with the Republic without pursuing Statehood.

Distinguished from Territories, which are part of the Republic's State structure, and from indigenous nations, which hold prior sovereignty recognized by Article XIV.

Absolute Majority §3.1 →

A majority of the full seated membership of a chamber — not merely those present or voting. Used for PM selection, constructive no-confidence votes, and other threshold votes. Distinguished from simple majority (those present) and supermajority (2/3 or 3/4 of full membership).

The distinction between absolute majority and simple majority is load-bearing: it prevents slim-quorum majorities from making consequential constitutional decisions.

Where the MA Director position is vacant or the Director is incapacitated, the board of five members selects an Acting Director from among themselves by majority vote within 7 days. The Acting Director holds all Director powers except the veto right under §10.4.f, which is suspended until the vacancy is filled. Cannot be nominated as the permanent elected Director for the vacancy period they filled.

C
Constitutional Pool Framework §8.13 →

The standard mechanism governing all constitutionally established appointment pools. Every pool operates through four sequential stages: Entry (self-nominated, State-designated, or executive-nominated — never requiring Senate confirmation at this stage), Certification (JI qualification report within 30 days; EI financial disclosure for offices with significant financial authority), Selection (JIC public lottery or deliberate nomination from pool, depending on whether independence or accountability is the primary requirement), and Confirmation (Senate confirmation of the selected individual where required — never of pool entry). Pool records are public NRS records at all times.

Senate confirmation is never required for pool entry. If an office needs Senate confirmation at entry, it should be an elected office, not a pooled appointment. The six constitutional pools (SC Registry, Advisory Council, Endowment Trustees, DIC Panels, JI IG) each specify which variant of each stage applies.

Candidate Register §8.4 →

A list of pre-vetted, eligible candidates maintained for appointed positions — including MA board members and DIC Director. Candidates submit their own disclosure records; the relevant Inspectorate reviews for factual accuracy, applies constitutional eligibility criteria, and publishes the record with its recommendation. The PM nominates from the Register; the Senate confirms. Distinguished from the Supreme Court Candidate Registry (§8.4.e), which serves as the eligibility register for SC candidacy.

Caretaker PM §2.3.c →
RSA Technical Integrity Commission
A decennial expert commission that reviews whether the National Record System architecture continues to meet constitutional requirements for distributed custody, cryptographic verification, and public accessibility. Constituted by a public lottery drawing five members from the RSA Technical Pool — built over ten years from nominees confirmed by the Senate (one per year each from the President and PM). All three Inspectorates certify the draw, oversee working sessions, and co-publish findings to the NRS. The pool resets after each cycle; previously confirmed non-serving members may re-enter through fresh nomination. Findings cover cryptographic integrity, node distribution adequacy, and technical standard compliance. §8.12

The Senior Domestic Affairs Council Officer who serves as acting Prime Minister during any PM vacancy. Restricted to maintenance of existing obligations only — no new policy or spending may be initiated during the interim. Exception: the Caretaker PM retains full domestic emergency authority during any vacancy period.

Constitutional Logic Test CLT §5.4 →

The assessment required for earned citizenship through naturalization and the National Service path. Tests knowledge of the mechanics, powers, limits, and jurisdictional boundaries of the Republic's government — not cultural trivia, not historical knowledge, not political views. Constitutional logic specifically. Administered by the CLT Authority, an independent body whose Administrator is nominated by the PM and confirmed by the Senate. The test must be fair, publicly available for preparation, and not a proxy for cultural conformity. The Administrator must have no government position within the prior five years, no financial interest in testing entities, and demonstrated expertise in psychometrics, constitutional law, educational policy, or civic literacy research.

Constructive Vote of No Confidence §2.3.b →

The mechanism by which the House removes the Prime Minister — by simultaneously electing a named successor by absolute majority in the same motion. A vote of no confidence that does not name and elect a replacement is constitutionally void and has no effect. This requirement means the House must agree not only that it has lost confidence in the current PM but that it has found a specific successor with majority support.

The constructive requirement closes the "destructive opposition" problem — oppositions cannot manufacture governmental instability without being able to agree on an alternative.

State Plurality Requirement §7.2.b →

The tiebreaker metric used in presidential elections where no candidate achieves a national RCV majority: 65% weight applied to national first-preference vote share, plus 35% weight applied to the fraction of presidential States won. Territory citizens contribute to the 65% national component but not to the State plurality component — Territories are not assigned to States.

D
Domain Default §2.1.k →

Where the domain of a proposed executive action is disputed outside the context of active military operations, the PM's residual authority is presumptive pending resolution. Presidential authority is enumerated — a Presidential domain claim must identify the specific enumerated power engaged. The JEC (§2.11) is the first forum for resolution; if the JEC cannot resolve the dispute, either executive may petition the SC, which must rule within 30 days. The PM's residual authority governs while the SC ruling is pending.

Distinct from §2.1.h (military operation domain disputes, which require SC ruling within 24 hours). The domain default rule gives practical effect to the constitutional principle that Presidential authority is enumerated and PM authority is residual.

Digital Infrastructure Commission §16.1 →

The constitutionally independent body governing both the National Record System and the National Voting System. Governed by two independent panels of three administrators each — the NRS Panel and the Elections Panel. The Chair of each panel is its most senior administrator by continuous service within that panel. References in the constitution to "Elections Panel of the DIC" or "the EC" refer to the Elections Panel of this body. Panel administrators are appointed through the pool mechanism: PM nominates from the DIC Administrator Pool, the JI certifies qualifications and publishes reports to the NRS, and the Senate confirms by 2/3 supermajority. The Legislature defines technical standards, interface requirements, and budget by statute; the panels implement.

An independent expert body convened every ten years to review whether the NRS and NVS architectures meet their constitutional requirements. Members are drawn by public lottery from a pool of State-nominated, JI-certified technical experts. Advisory only — findings are recommendations to the Digital Infrastructure Commission, which must respond within 90 days with published reasons. The Senate holds a mandatory public session on findings within 90 days of publication, completing the accountability chain from State nominations to Senate scrutiny.

Declaration of National Emergency §1.25 →

A formal joint declaration by the President and Prime Minister, ratified by a 2/3 vote of both chambers within 7 days, authorizing temporary limitation of specified Article I rights. Must specify: the nature of the emergency; the specific rights being limited; a duration not exceeding 90 days; and the minimum necessary geographic scope. Renewable for successive 90-day periods at the same threshold. Cannot limit the seven non-derogable rights enumerated in §1.25.b. Cannot affect elections.

Deemed Confirmed §4.1.b →

The mechanism by which an inferior court judge nominee is automatically confirmed if the House fails to vote within the Legislature-defined confirmation period. Prevents House inaction from indefinitely blocking judicial appointments. The Legislature-defined period cannot be set at a duration that would push Deemed Confirmation past the 180-day constitutional ceiling from the date of vacancy.

Domestic Affairs Council DAC §2.2 →

The Prime Minister's governing body, coordinating domestic policy, the civil service, and the budget. The Legislature defines its specific structure and composition by statute. Ministers appointed by the PM serve within the DAC and exercise only PM-domain authority. Distinguished from the National Security Council, which coordinates the President's foreign affairs and defense domain.

Dual Executive Article II →

The constitutional architecture that divides executive authority between a directly elected President (military, intelligence, foreign affairs) and a PM chosen by and accountable to the House (domestic administration, budget, civil service). Neither executive may act in the other's domain. When a single event implicates both domains simultaneously, both executives act within their respective domains concurrently.

Cohabitation — where the President and PM represent different political coalitions — is the design's stress test. §2.1.h provides a specific conflict resolution mechanism during active military operations.

E
Emergency Governance Protocol §2.8 →

The framework that activates when the Legislature is physically unable to convene — authorizing the Senate to pass legislation with House equivalent authority for a period not exceeding 30 days. Requires the Senate Speaker to certify the House's physical incapacity on the NRS before the Senate acts. The House must ratify or reject all emergency measures within 30 days of reconvening.

Emergency Panel §4.2.e →

A standing panel of four Supreme Court justices designated by rotation at the start of each two-year election cycle. Has sole jurisdiction over constitutional scope challenges to Tier 1 presidential authority during active operations, and over dual-executive conflict disputes under §2.1.h. Must rule within 48 hours of any challenge. A scope ruling is binding on the full Court unless overruled by a majority within 72 hours. Rotation maintains continuity: at least two members must have served on the prior cycle's Panel.

Emergency Rights Writ §14.3.c →

A petition to the Supreme Court by any individual within an indigenous jurisdiction alleging a specific, ongoing violation of their Article I rights. The Court must rule within 14 days. A granted Writ is targeted to the specific violation and expires when the violation is remedied — it does not authorize general law enforcement operations within the indigenous jurisdiction.

F
Fiscal Objection §2.4 →

The PM's power to object to legislation on specific solvency grounds — that the bill, if enacted, would render the Republic insolvent or unable to meet its social state constitutional obligations. Narrower than the suspensive veto: only solvency grounds qualify. Overridden by simple majority of full House membership. The PM may use either the suspensive veto or the fiscal objection on any given bill, but not both.

For-Cause Removal §8.5.a →

Removal of an Inspector General on stated grounds — documented dereliction, incapacity, undisclosed conflict of interest, or criminal conviction. Requires 2/3 of both chambers simultaneously. Before the legislative vote, the State Designee Panel must publish a verification finding. The Panel's finding does not block the vote — it becomes permanent public record alongside it. A For-Cause removal vote at the constitutional threshold is not a constitutional violation for voting members solely by reason of political motivation.

Founding Congress §19.1 →

The transitional legislative body drawn from the elected assemblies of all ratifying polities by proportional delegation. Governs during Phases 1 and 2 of the transition period. Produces the founding statutes required before the Phase 3 national elections. Disbanded when the first Legislature is seated at Phase 4 activation.

Free, Prior, and Informed Consent FPIC §14.3.a →

The consent required from indigenous nations before the Republic takes any legislative or executive action materially affecting their lands, sovereignty, or treaty rights. Freely given without coercion or economic inducement; obtained before any action is taken; based on full information. A process that produces objection does not produce consent regardless of its duration or the government's good faith. Where an indigenous nation withholds consent, the government's only recourse is to propose modifications, seek alternatives, or accept the proposed action will not proceed.

G
Gate 1 / Gate 2 §6.1 →

The two-stage immigration system. Gate 1 is State or Territory sponsorship — a community-level decision about whom to welcome, governed by the sponsoring State's own criteria. Gate 2 is federal certification on four grounds only: health, criminal history, national security, and prior deportation. No nationality quotas permitted at either gate. The President must ministerially issue residency credentials once both gates are cleared — refusal is a constitutional violation. Statistical disparity presumption applies: a pattern of denials significantly correlated with protected characteristics triggers a presumption of discrimination.

Gross National Revenue GNR §10.1 →

The measure underlying constitutional fiscal calculations, including social state funding formulas. Certified annually by the Monetary Authority Director — not by any political body. The MA's certification methodology is subject to judicial challenge by the Director (§10.4.d) and legislative review (§10.1.f) but cannot be altered by executive direction. The Legislature may define GNR by statute within constitutional bounds; it cannot define GNR in a way that eliminates the social state floor's practical effect.

I
Individual Sovereignty Floor §1.1 →

The irreducible minimum of rights and protections held by every person within the Republic's jurisdiction, regardless of citizenship or residency status. Article I is a floor, not a ceiling — rights enumerated there may be expanded but never contracted by amendment. The floor applies to all Inhabitants: citizens, residents, asylum seekers, visitors, and undocumented persons alike.

Inhabitant Article I →

Any person within the Republic's jurisdiction — regardless of citizenship or residency status. All Inhabitants hold the full protections of Article I. The term is deliberately broader than "citizen" or "legal resident" to make clear that constitutional rights are not contingent on immigration status.

The preamble uses "inhabitants" to establish scope: "Every person within our borders." This is intentional — the constitution protects persons, not statuses.

Inspectorate Article VIII →

One of three independent constitutional oversight bodies: the Legislative Inspectorate (audits House and Senate), the Executive Inspectorate (audits both executive offices and the MA), and the Judicial Inspectorate (audits courts, maintains the Judicial IG Candidate Pool (§8.4.a) and the Supreme Court Candidate Registry (§8.4.e)). Each is headed by an Inspector General selected through a process that excludes the watched branch. Inspectorates publish facts and recommendations — they have no enforcement power. Their findings are constitutional facts that the Legislature, courts, or the People may act upon.

Indigenous Nation Status Elections §14.6 →

The constitutional menu available to any indigenous nation recognized under Article XIV, exercisable at ratification or at any subsequent time by the nation's own governance process. Four options: (a) Article XIV compact (default — no action required); (b) Associated Community status under Article XX; (c) territorial integration via the §14.5.d → §13.6 two-step pathway; (d) full sovereign independence outside all Republic constitutional frameworks. The right to initiate is continuous; each change takes effect only when the relevant constitutional process is complete.

Option (d) independence: geographic position inside Republic territory does not affect the right — it affects only the treaty arrangements. The Republic must negotiate and conclude a treaty within 24 months. A nation that completes independence may not re-elect Article XIV status; re-entry follows general sovereign pathways (§20 or §13.6).

L
Legal Resident §5.2 →
Jury Service §4.5 →

Citizens are required to serve on juries when called. Compensated at the national median daily income rate. Unexcused refusal is subject to civil consequences defined by statute. Does not constitute forced labour under §1.4.

A person who has cleared both immigration gates and holds residency credentials issued by the President. Legal Residents hold most Article I protections but do not hold voting rights or the right to hold elected office until naturalized. May apply for citizenship after the statutory residency period by completing the CLT.

Legislative Declaration of Defense §2.1.c →

A 2/3 concurrent majority of both chambers authorizing military operations beyond 30 days from initial deployment. Simultaneously authorizes extended operations and triggers the Emergency Defense Allocation. Renewable every 180 days. Subject to a 5-year maximum and the Successive Declaration Standard — a new Declaration within 12 months of a mandatory cessation must be based on a materially different threat.

M
Mandatory Devolution §13.3 →

A constitutional process by which a State that fails three consecutive annual Statehood Audits returns to Territory status. After each failing audit, the LI publishes an Early Warning and the State must publish a Remediation Plan within 60 days. After three consecutive failures, citizens vote in a State referendum on whether to accept devolution or a two-year federal remediation commission. Under Path A: sitting senators serve the remainder of their current elected terms — seats are not vacated — but no new Senate elections may be held until Statehood is restored. Restoration is automatic upon passing the Statehood Audit and completing the Provisional Membership Period.

Path A protects citizens who elected senators in good faith — they did not cause the State's audit failures. Senate representation reduces gradually through natural term expiry rather than immediate disenfranchisement.

N
National Record System NRS Article IX →

The Republic's permanent, tamper-evident, publicly accessible repository of all official government actions, laws, expenditures, court decisions, and Inspectorate reports. All entries are permanent — corrections are made by new entries that reference and supersede prior entries, with both permanently visible. Maintained by a distributed network of independent node operators; no single entity may control a majority of nodes. Accessible to all Inhabitants at no cost, including through non-digital pathways. An executive order, contract, or expenditure not recorded on the NRS within 24 hours is void.

National Security Council NSC §2.1 →

The President's governing body for military, intelligence, and foreign affairs. The Legislature defines its composition by statute. Distinguished from the Domestic Affairs Council, which coordinates the PM's domain.

National Voting System NVS Article XVI →

The Republic's cryptographically secured platform for all elections, referenda, and petition signature collection. Constitutionally separate from the National Record System. Administered exclusively by the Elections Panel of the DIC. No election result is constitutionally valid unless conducted through it. No State may take any action that reduces NVS access, functionality, or processing capacity in its jurisdiction.

Removal of an Inspector General without stated grounds — requiring 3/4 of both chambers simultaneously and a 90-day waiting period. The higher threshold and mandatory delay distinguish it from For-Cause Removal. The Judicial IG has no No-Cause track — For-Cause only, reflecting the courts' institutional independence.

Non-Refoulement §1.27 →

The absolute prohibition on returning, expelling, or extraditing any person to a territory where they face genuine risk of persecution, torture, or deprivation of life. Applies regardless of immigration status or mode of entry. Cannot be suspended by any emergency declaration. One of the non-derogable rights that cannot be suspended even in emergencies under §1.26.

O
Official Oath §10.5.h →

The oath taken by all constitutional officers upon assuming office, recorded on the National Record System within 24 hours of administration. No constitutional authority vests before the Oath is taken and recorded. A constitutional officer who takes the Oath but has not yet seen it published to the NRS holds their authority from the moment of administration, not the moment of publication.

Optional Referendum §11.1 →

A national vote on legislation or policy that may be triggered by a citizen petition meeting the JI-verified threshold, or automatically where the Legislature has failed to enact a constitutionally mandated statute. The Optional Referendum may direct the Legislature to enact a specific statutory framework or establish a default rule that operates until the Legislature acts. Distinguished from the Mandatory Referendum — votes constitutionally required for specific actions, including independence (§13.9.a) and constitutional amendment through the citizen-led path (§15.1.c).

P
Provisional Membership Period §13.3 →

A time-limited status for Territories that are on a defined Statehood pathway but have not yet completed the conditions for full Statehood. Territories in the Provisional Membership Period have defined representation rights, contribute to the national popular vote in presidential elections, and are subject to a remediation framework if they fail to meet Statehood conditions within the statutory period.

Presidential Veto §2.1.f →

The President's power to veto legislation that raises a genuine constitutional concern within the presidential domain — military operations, intelligence, treaty obligations, or border security. The connection must be direct, specific, and material: a general, inferential, or speculative security connection is not sufficient. The Supreme Court reviews any veto within 48 hours on petition from either chamber's Speaker. Three voided vetoes within 180 days triggers automatic suspension of veto authority for the remainder of that period.

The presidential veto is a domain protection mechanism, not a general legislative power. The PM's suspensive veto is the general legislative objection tool.

Phase Extension Mechanism §19.3 →

New in v143. Allows any of the four transition phases to run beyond its scheduled window when specific circumstances — security situations, infrastructure failures, natural disasters, institutional resistance — prevent timely completion. First extension: the joint Inspectorate panel certifies up to 90 additional days by unanimous agreement, with published statement of circumstances and NRS publication within 24 hours. Second extension: requires 2/3 majority of the caretaker legislature in joint session within 14 days. Hard cap: 180 days total extensions across all phases. All caretaker provisions remain fully operative during any extension. The mechanism is a bridge over specific obstacles toward Phase 4 activation — not a pathway to indefinite caretaker government.

Presidential Officer §2.1.i →

A senior officer in the President's executive domain — including the Director of Intelligence, Secretary for Foreign Affairs, Secretary for Defense, and equivalent positions established by statute. Appointed by presidential nomination and Senate simple majority confirmation; Deemed Confirmed if Senate fails to act. Removed by presidential Notice of Intended Removal to the Senate: Senate approves by simple majority within 30 days, or removal is deemed approved. Senate may deny removal by simple majority, leaving the officer in post with a one-year bar on resubmission. Officers under active §3.5 impeachment proceedings may not be removed by the President, preventing obstruction of legislative accountability.

R
State redistricting process §7.2.b →

The body responsible for drawing presidential States. Constituted by public lottery following each decennial census: the Judicial Inspectorate draws one member from each State’s standing State redistricting pool, certified jointly by all three Inspectorates in session. The Commission operates independently of the Legislature and the Elections Panel of the DIC — the Legislature sets the number of regions by statute, but plays no role in drawing them. Pool eligibility requires five continuous years of Republic citizenship, five continuous years of residency in the nominating State, and no current elected office. Each State may add up to five members per year to its Pool; positions are extinguished if vacated and are not replaced. The Commission submits maps to the LI for compliance review; SC challenge is available within 60 days of certification. Members are compensated by their State for days in active session; the federal government reimburses. See §7.2.b.i through §7.2.b.x for the full mechanism.

constitutional violation Article VIII (Inspectorates) →

A Supreme Court finding that an official has violated a specific constitutional provision. Consequences are automatic — no further vote or order required: immediate suspension from duties and pay freeze; revocation of access to classified information, government systems, and official vehicles; a Senate trial within the statutory period. Only courts make constitutional violation determinations — Inspectorate findings are facts the courts, Legislature, or People may act upon, not constitutional violation findings themselves. For former officials, a constitutional violation finding within five years of the conduct triggers a permanent bar on federal office and forfeiture of federal pension.

The automatic consequence structure — rather than a discretionary response — is the key design choice. It prevents political interference with accountability enforcement.

S
Social State §10.1 →

The Republic's constitutional commitment that every Inhabitant is entitled to conditions of life consistent with human dignity. Implemented through three mandatory systems: universal healthcare insurance funded by mandatory employer, employee, and federal contributions; social assistance for those outside the insurance system; and free compulsory education for all children. Replaces the earlier Basic Needs Floor basket model. The design follows the German Sozialstaatsprinzip — a constitutional principle with statutory implementation rather than a specific enumerated basket.

Severe Contraction Protocol §10.1 →

A constitutional mechanism allowing mechanism activating National Endowment backstop during a documented economic contraction. Requires MA certification of a Severe Contraction, subject to Legislative Inspectorate review within 30 days. The Legislature may override the certification by 2/3 of both chambers. Disbursements continue until the MA certifies the contraction has ended and social state systems are self-suftions cease.

A verification body constituted per proceeding whose sole constitutional function is to assess, prior to any qualifying For-Cause removal vote, whether the stated grounds for removal are substantiated within the enumerated constitutional categories. Activated for Inspector General removal (§8.5.a) and Elections Panel of the DIC removal (§16.2.a). Constituted fresh for each proceeding: the JI draws five members from the combined national State Designee Pool plus two alternates within five days of activation, in a session certified jointly by all three Inspectorates (session may be conducted remotely per statute). Drawn members accept or decline within 48 hours; the named officer or initiating body may challenge a drawn member within 48 hours on conflict-of-interest grounds (§8.9.d). The 21-day review clock starts from full Panel constitution. Each State maintains a standing pool; eligibility requires five continuous years of Republic citizenship, no current or recent federal office, no pending criminal proceedings. Annual addition cap of five per State; no carryover; positions extinguished on exit. A member who completes service may be re-added by their State within the annual cap. The Legislature sets meeting parameters by statute (remote participation, quorum, platform) subject to a constitutional floor against undue burden. The Panel publishes a binary finding within 21 days; the Legislature may proceed to a vote regardless of direction; both the finding and any vote are permanent NRS record. SC review available within 14 days of finding publication (§8.9.i). If the national pool falls below thirty members at activation, or a State has added no one for three consecutive years with fewer than two eligible members, the JI issues a Mandatory Designation Notice — each notified State must designate one eligible person (meeting all §8.9.a standards) within ten days (§8.9.a). See §8.9.a through §8.9.f for the full mechanism.

T
Term Limits §2.3.b →

The Republic applies non-renewable term limits to all major appointed offices and a dual limit to the PM. SC justices serve a single non-renewable 12-year term (§4.2.d). Inferior court judges serve a single non-renewable 15-year term (§4.1.c). The PM faces two simultaneous limits: no more than 8 consecutive years of service, and no more than 12 total years across all periods of service (§2.3.b). Partial years count in full for both PM limits; whichever limit is stricter governs at any given moment.

Non-renewable terms serve independence — an appointed officer who cannot be reappointed cannot be influenced by reappointment pressure. The PM's total cap closes the gap in the consecutive-only limit: a PM who serves 8 years, steps aside, and returns can serve at most 4 more years.

J
Joint Executive Council (JEC) §2.11 →

The standing cross-executive coordination body comprising the President's domain officers and the PM's Cabinet ministers meeting jointly. The JEC is the constitutional first forum for matters that cross executive domains — where a proposed executive action materially affects the other executive's domain, the proposing executive notifies the Council before acting. The JEC makes recommendations and coordinates positions; it does not exercise executive authority. Both the President and PM must each attend at least one session per calendar quarter.

The JEC is also the first step in the civilian domain dispute resolution process under §2.1.k — JEC coordination precedes any SC petition. Its session summaries are published to the NRS within 14 days; existence and subject of sessions may not be classified.

Joint Inspectorate Council (JIC) §8.12 →

The standing coordination body formed by the three Inspector Generals jointly. The JIC formally houses all jointly certified constitutional functions — including the Eight-Year Assessment (§8.11), all constitutional pool lottery draws under §8.13, and any matter where two or more Inspectorates hold concurrent jurisdiction. The JIC publishes a joint annual report to both chambers and the NRS. Joint findings carry the authority of all three Inspectorates simultaneously.

Where the Inspector Generals cannot reach consensus on a joint matter, each publishes their individual assessment and the disagreement is noted in the permanent NRS record. The JIC has no authority to direct or override the individual mandate of any Inspectorate.

Judicial IG Candidate Pool §8.4.a →

Renamed in v142 from "Qualified Jurist Pool." Maintained by the Judicial Inspectorate. The Judicial IG's function is systematic fact-finding and reporting — not legal determination. Specific professional qualifications are defined by statute and must reflect research and analytical competence rather than judicial credentials. Constitutional minimums: no government position in the prior 10 years, no financial conflicts, no criminal conviction in office. The Legislature may add to these minimums but not reduce them. Separate from the Supreme Court Candidate Registry (§8.4.e).

Five inferior court judges selected by lottery from those with 10+ continuous years on the bench. Serves one SC selection cycle. No political branch appoints members — the lottery is run by the JI. Publishes an advisory assessment of all registered SC candidates 60 days before the election: judicial record, demonstrated legal reasoning, and professional independence relative to the SC's constitutional mandate. Has no gatekeeping authority — cannot remove candidates, makes no endorsement. The Board is the expert advisory voice; the election is the democratic voice.

Coordination Failure Protocol §2.14.a →

A structured resolution mechanism for operational deadlocks between the two executives where both are acting within their correct domains but their actions are materially incompatible. Distinct from a domain dispute (which §2.14 and the SC resolve) — a coordination failure occurs when neither executive's authority is contested, but the actions conflict. Triggered by written NRS declaration; the JEC has 14 days to produce a joint resolution, after which the Legislature may resolve by concurrent resolution within 30 days. The PM's residual authority governs where no resolution is reached.

Institutional Compromise Protocol §8.12.a →

The mechanism for addressing an Inspectorate found to be operating in demonstrable bad faith — actively acting contrary to its constitutional mandate rather than merely underperforming. Two of the three Inspectorates petition the SC directly for confirmation of the finding; an Acting Inspector General is immediately designated from the longest-tenured member of the affected Inspectorate's candidate pool, subject to approval by both executives. The Acting IG is ineligible for permanent appointment to the position and returns to the pool upon conclusion of acting service.

The two-of-three petition requirement prevents any single Inspectorate from weaponizing the protocol against another; the SC confirmation requirement prevents it from being used as a political tool by the petitioning Inspectorates.

Registry Integrity Standards §8.14 →

Constitutional standards applying to all registries and candidate pools maintained under constitutional mandate. Eligibility criteria must be strictly objective — years of practice or service, office status, and financial interests as defined by the Constitution or statute. No registry administrator may apply criteria relating to legal philosophy, judicial temperament, ideological orientation, or political association. Exclusions require published grounds within 14 days; excluded applicants may appeal to the SC on whether the stated grounds correspond to an established criterion.

Classification Duration Framework §9.5.a →

The constitutional ceiling on how long government records may remain classified. No record may remain classified beyond 25 years from the date of original classification. A single 5-year extension may be granted where the responsible executive publishes a justification to the EI; the EI certifies and publishes its finding to the NRS. No material may remain classified beyond 30 years under any circumstances. All other classification procedures and periods are defined by statute within this constitutional ceiling.

NRS Parallel Authentication System §9.1 →

A continuously operating authentication system maintained by the JI that is physically and logistically independent of the National Record System. It operates in parallel at all times — not merely as a contingency — and records all authenticated constitutional acts with full constitutional effect from the moment of their issuance. Government acts take legal effect from the moment of issuance regardless of NRS status. Upon NRS restoration, parallel system records migrate to the NRS as permanent records with their original timestamps. If the NRS is not restored within 180 days, the parallel system becomes the constitutional record of the Republic.

Senate Bypass — Public Confirmation §4.4.a →

A mechanism available when the LI has certified a Senate breach in confirming an SC nominee. The PM, with 2/3 House consent, may place the serving Temporary Associate Justice on the next federal electoral period ballot for public confirmation. Confirmation requires 60% of votes cast. If confirmed, the justice serves the remainder of the original term and the Senate's confirmation authority for that seat is extinguished. If not confirmed, the justice steps down and the next most senior Appellate Court judge auto-designates.

The bypass requires PM initiative and 2/3 House consent — it cannot be triggered unilaterally by either executive or by the Temporary Associate Justice themselves.

Judicial Removal Track (Independent Officers) §3.9.a →

An alternative removal path for independent statutory officers where legislative gridlock prevents the standard 2/3 removal process from functioning. Two of the three Inspectorates jointly certify for-cause grounds and petition the SC; the SC determines within 30 days whether the grounds constitute sufficient cause for suspension. If suspension is ordered, the Senate has 60 days to hold a 3/5 removal vote. Where the Senate fails to act, the matter proceeds to a public election requiring 60% for removal. An affirmative Senate acquittal (a vote that fails to reach 3/5) closes the process and reinstates the officer.

IG Term Expiry and Incapacity §8.1.d →

Constitutional provisions governing what happens when an Inspector General's term ends or they become incapacitated. Authority terminates automatically at term end — no further proceeding required; any act taken after expiry is void. Where no successor has been confirmed, the most senior deputy holds full IG authority on an acting basis for up to 90 days before the LI must certify a compliance breach. Where an IG is certified incapacitated by the other two Inspectorates jointly, the most senior deputy assumes acting authority immediately; the incapacitated IG's authority is suspended from that moment.

Supreme Court Quorum §4.3.a →

The minimum number of SC justices required for valid court proceedings. A majority of seated justices (including Temporary Associate Justices) constitutes quorum for ordinary decisions. Constitutional questions require a minimum of five justices regardless of total seated membership. Where insufficient Appellate Court judges are available for Temporary Associate Justice designation, the most senior federal District Court judges by continuous service fill remaining positions on the same temporary basis.
