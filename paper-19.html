Paper No. 19

The Coordination Problem

Paper No. 2 makes the philosophical case for dividing executive authority between a President and a Prime Minister. This paper addresses the practical question that follows: once you have two executives, how do they actually govern together — and what does the constitution do when they cannot?

The dual executive's central theoretical weakness has always been obvious. Foreign and domestic policy do not occupy separate universes. A trade agreement restructures domestic labour markets. Immigration policy is simultaneously border security and social services administration. Climate treaties impose domestic regulatory obligations. Digital infrastructure is both a national utility and a foreign intelligence target. Divide executive authority along a foreign/domestic line and you guarantee that a significant portion of meaningful government action will land on both sides of the divide simultaneously.

The constitution does not pretend otherwise. It acknowledges the problem in §2.1.i's concurrence mechanism, in §2.1.k's default rule, and most directly in §2.11's creation of a standing joint coordination body. These provisions are not afterthoughts. They are the practical machinery that makes the dual executive workable — the answer to the coordination problem that the design inherits from its own architecture.

The Standing Table

The Joint Executive Council§2.11 is not a crisis mechanism. It does not convene when the two executives disagree about something important. It is a standing coordination body — both executives' officers meeting jointly as a matter of constitutional routine. The President's domain officers and the PM's Cabinet ministers sit at the same table regularly, as an institution, because the government functions they represent are too intertwined to be managed in parallel without constant communication.

The design reflects an observation about how governments actually work. The Secretary of Defense knows more about specific military procurement questions than the President does. The Finance Minister knows more about fiscal policy detail than the PM does. The people below the principal level — the ones who spend years in a domain — frequently understand it better than the executive who holds formal authority over it. The JEC gives those people a regular, constitutionally sanctioned forum for cross-domain coordination that does not require their bosses to be present. Both the President and PM must each attend at least one session per calendar quarter — the quarterly floor ensures genuine executive engagement — but the Council runs continuously at the officer level regardless of whether the principals show up.

This is not just administrative efficiency. It is a structural recognition that the foreign/domestic boundary is managed most reliably by the people who inhabit it every day, not by the executives who hold authority over it on either side. A trade negotiator who works with Finance Ministry officials every month in JEC sessions will identify domestic implementation problems before the treaty is signed, not after. An intelligence officer who coordinates with the Domestic Affairs Council on cross-border digital threats will flag potential NRS implications before the President issues a classified directive. The coordination that prevents domain conflicts from becoming constitutional crises mostly happens at this level, invisibly, as a product of institutional routine.

The JEC makes recommendations. It does not decide. Each executive retains full constitutional authority in their own domain; nothing the Council produces is binding on either executive. What the Council produces is a coordination record — a permanent NRS-published account of which cross-domain matters were presented, when, and with what outcome. The existence of that record is itself a coordination incentive. An executive who acts unilaterally on a cross-domain matter without presenting it to the JEC has created a permanent gap in the consultation record that their counterpart — and the public — can observe. The accountability runs through transparency rather than veto.

The Default Rule

When coordination fails — when the JEC cannot reach a consensus position and both executives believe their constitutional authority covers the disputed action — the constitution needs a default that does not itself create a constitutional crisis. §2.1.k provides one: where the domain of a proposed executive action is disputed, the PM's residual authority governs pending resolution.

This default follows directly from the architecture of the dual executive itself. The President's authority is enumerated — listed explicitly in §2.1. The PM's authority is residual — everything not enumerated as Presidential falls within the domestic domain by definition. If the President claims a particular action falls within their authority, they must identify the specific enumerated power they are exercising. If they cannot, the action is not Presidential by constitutional design.

The default rule makes this principle operational. A disputed action does not stall indefinitely while the two executives negotiate. The PM's residual authority governs while the JEC attempts coordination; if that fails, either executive petitions the Supreme Court for a domain determination and the SC rules within 30 days§2.1.k. The President is not helpless during this period — Presidential authority over the enumerated domains continues unaffected. But where the domain boundary is the dispute, the residual authority presumption holds until a constitutional determination is made.

The 30-day SC ruling requirement is the mechanism that prevents the default from becoming a permanent structural advantage for the PM. A disputed domain question is not an open-ended negotiation with no resolution mechanism — it is a 30-day constitutional process with a binding outcome. The SC rules on domain, not on policy merits. Its analysis is narrow: does this specific action fall within the President's enumerated authority under §2.1, or does it fall outside that enumeration and therefore within the PM's residual domain? The ruling is published to the NRS as permanent constitutional precedent. Future domain disputes in the same area are resolved by reference to the published ruling rather than by fresh litigation.

The Trade Concurrence

The narrowest and most practically significant coordination mechanism is §2.1.i's PM concurrence requirement for trade agreements. Earlier drafts of the constitution required PM concurrence wherever a trade agreement "materially affects the domestic economy" — a formulation that would have captured every significant trade agreement ever negotiated, since all meaningful trade affects the domestic economy. The PM would have held an effective veto over Presidential trade authority, which would have hollowed out one of the reasons for having a President in the first place.

The current design is more precise. PM written concurrence is required for three categories of trade agreement provision:§2.1.i provisions that impose national treatment obligations within Republic territory; provisions that require new domestic legislation to implement; and provisions that create direct obligations on domestic persons or entities. Everything outside these categories — tariff schedules, market access terms in foreign markets, international negotiating positions — is within the President's sole authority. The PM is not a veto over the President's foreign economic policy. The PM is a required participant in the specific provisions of that policy that reach inside the Republic's domestic architecture.

The severability design is the mechanism that makes this workable in practice. Where the PM withholds concurrence on a specific provision, that provision may be transmitted to the House Speaker as ordinary domestic legislation — subject to the normal legislative process — while the remaining agreement terms proceed to Senate ratification independently. A PM who objects to Annex IX of a fifteen-annex trade agreement cannot block Annexes I through VIII and X through XV. The domestic implementation of the contested provision takes the domestic legislative path it should have taken anyway. The international agreement is not hostage to one executive's domestic objection.

This design encodes a constitutional judgment about what the dual executive's domain division means for foreign economic policy. External terms — what the Republic offers and seeks in foreign markets — are Presidential. Domestic terms — what the Republic commits to doing within its own territory, to its own citizens and institutions — are subject to PM participation. The line is not between "economic" and "non-economic" foreign policy. It is between external commitments and internal obligations.

When Neither Executive Yields

The default rule handles domain disputes — situations where both executives believe they have constitutional authority over the same action. The SC resolves those disputes within 30 days, and the PM's residual authority governs in the meantime. But there is a structurally distinct problem the default rule was not designed for: both executives acting within their correct domains whose actions are nevertheless materially incompatible.

Consider a treaty the President has concluded that requires domestic implementation the PM declines to provide. The President is within their authority. The PM is within theirs. No domain is disputed. The SC has nothing to resolve — there is no boundary question, only a genuine disagreement between two constitutionally valid positions. The JEC can surface the conflict, but it cannot compel resolution. Under the original design, this deadlock had no constitutional exit. The executives would either negotiate their way out or the government would simply fail to function in that area.

The Coordination Failure Protocol§2.14.a provides the exit. Either executive may declare a coordination failure by written NRS notice — immediately creating a permanent public record that a deadlock exists and who declared it. The JEC then has 14 days to produce a joint resolution. If the JEC cannot, the matter moves to the House and Senate Speakers jointly: the Legislature may resolve it by concurrent resolution within 30 days. A concurrent resolution under this provision binds both executives to its terms for the duration of the impasse only — it does not redraw domain boundaries or create permanent legislative override of executive authority. Where the Legislature does not act within 30 days, the PM's residual authority governs until agreement is reached or the Legislature acts.

The protocol's design reflects a constitutional judgment about the relationship between executive deadlock and democratic accountability. An impasse between two constitutionally valid executive positions is fundamentally a political disagreement — the kind of disagreement that representative institutions exist to resolve when the parties cannot. The 14-day JEC window gives the executives every reasonable opportunity to resolve it themselves; the 30-day legislative window gives the representative branches a defined role without making them the first resort; the PM's residual authority default prevents indefinite paralysis where neither body acts. Every step is published to the NRS. The public can see, in real time, who declared the failure, whether the JEC resolved it, whether the Legislature was asked to act, and which default governed in the end.

The protocol is not a mechanism for routine disagreements. The NRS record of a coordination failure declaration is permanent — an executive who invokes it frivolously has created a documented account of their own bad faith that future political actors and historians will have. The mechanism is available for genuine impasses and carries a reputational cost for opportunistic use. That cost is itself a coordination incentive.

What Coordination Cannot Solve

The JEC, the default rule, and the trade concurrence mechanism together address the coordination problem in its normal form: two executives with overlapping jurisdictions, working through their respective institutions, managing the practical friction of shared governance. They do not address the harder version of the problem: two executives with different political mandates, different electoral bases, different ideological commitments, who genuinely disagree on the direction of government.

This is the cohabitation problem. The President is elected nationally by the whole Republic; the PM commands a House majority. If they are from the same political coalition, coordination is a management challenge. If they are from different political coalitions — different parties, different worldviews, mutual institutional hostility — coordination mechanisms are necessary but not sufficient. The JEC still functions; officers still coordinate at the working level. But when the executives themselves disagree on a cross-domain matter, no coordination body produces agreement from genuine political opposition.

The constitution does not pretend to solve this. What it provides instead is a set of clear default rules that operate without executive agreement. The PM's residual authority governs domestic matters regardless of what the President thinks about domestic policy. The President's enumerated authority governs foreign affairs and defense regardless of what the PM thinks about those domains. The SC resolves boundary disputes with a binding ruling. The NRS record makes both executives' positions permanently visible. The electorate can see, in real time, when the two executives agree and when they do not, what each claims authority over, and how each uses the constitutional mechanisms available to them.

France's Fifth Republic — the most extensively studied real-world dual executive — has had three periods of cohabitation since 1958. None produced constitutional collapse. The governments functioned, sometimes with surprising effectiveness. What cohabitation produces is a government that is visibly divided and a public that can see the division clearly. In a republic designed around transparency and democratic accountability, the ability to see clearly who is responsible for which decisions and where the disagreements lie is not a weakness. It is the system working as designed.

The Quarterly Session

There is something worth observing about the constitutional structure of the quarterly executive attendance requirement. The President and PM must each attend at least one JEC session per quarter.§2.11 This is not a high bar — four sessions a year, distributed so that both executives are genuinely present in each three-month period. But it is a constitutional floor, not a recommendation.

The floor matters because its absence would matter. An executive who never attends the coordination body is an executive who has structurally separated themselves from the practical management of cross-domain issues. Their officers coordinate; their counterpart coordinates; but they receive briefings rather than participate in the institutional process through which coordination actually happens. The quarterly requirement ensures that both executives remain genuinely engaged rather than insulated by their staffs from the practical realities of shared governance.

What happens at those quarterly sessions — what gets resolved, what gets escalated, what pattern of cooperation or friction they reveal — will tell future observers more about how a particular government actually governed than almost any other single institutional data point. The NRS will publish session summaries. Future constitutional scholars, historians, and citizens will be able to read, quarter by quarter, across administrations, the record of how the Republic's two executives chose to use the institution designed to hold them together. The constitution creates the institution. The institution's quality over time depends on the people who show up to it.
