Civic Consul Formation · Senate Designation · Constructive No-Confidence · §2.6

The Senate's Choice

Year 3. A fractured House cannot elect a Civic Consul. The Senate designates one. The Assembly votes against her without naming a successor — and discovers that vote has no constitutional effect.

§2.6 Civic Consul Formation and Accountability §2.5 Caretaker PM §10.1 NRS Publication

The Republic's third general election produces a House that cannot govern. The seat distribution after RCV counting leaves three distinct blocs — none with an absolute majority, none willing to form a coalition with another. The outgoing Civic Consul had designated Deputy Civic Consul Lara Voss as Caretaker before the election. Voss holds the office from the moment the outgoing government loses its mandate.

The Caretaker can maintain services and honour existing commitments. She cannot make new policy, introduce legislation, or commit the Republic to new obligations. The constitutional obligation on the Assembly is clear: elect a Civic Consul by absolute majority within the statutory period.

Three Failed Votes

Day 14
First vote fails

Bloc A nominates their leader. Bloc B votes against as a bloc. Bloc C splits. Result: 49 in favour, 51 against. No absolute majority. No Civic Consul.

Day 31
Second vote fails

Bloc C attempts a compromise candidate acceptable to neither Bloc A nor Bloc B. The vote fails 42–58. The candidate withdraws.

Day 78
Third vote fails

Bloc B nominates their leader. Blocs A and C combine in opposition. Result: 44–56. The statutory period has elapsed. The House has failed to elect a Civic Consul.

The Senate Designates

Under §2.6, where the Assembly fails to elect a Civic Consul within the statutory period, the Senate may designate a serving Assembly member as Civic Consul by 2/3 supermajority. The Senate has been watching the deadlock. After its own deliberation it identifies Commerce Minister Daria Svet — a member with working relationships across all three blocs and a reputation for pragmatic negotiation — as someone who might hold the office without being captured by any one bloc's agenda.

Day 80
Senate designates by 12–2

Svet is Civic Consul. The designation is published to the NRS. Full Civic Consul authority transfers from that moment — she can set policy, introduce legislation, and direct the executive branch. The Caretaker period is over. Caretaker Civic Consul Voss steps down. The governance problem is resolved.

The House Votes Against Her — To No Effect

Blocs A and C, unwilling to accept a Civic Consul they did not choose, call an immediate confidence vote. Fifty-five members vote against Svet. Forty-five vote to retain her. A clear majority against the Civic Consul.

The vote has no constitutional effect.

§2.6 is unambiguous on this point: the Assembly may remove the Civic Consul only through a constructive vote of no confidence — a single vote that simultaneously removes the sitting Civic Consul and installs a named replacement, by absolute majority. A confidence vote that does not name and elect a successor has no constitutional effect whatsoever.

Day 83 — LM Assessment
Vote declared constitutionally void

The Assembly Speaker requests an LM assessment. The LM publishes within 24 hours: the confidence vote named no successor; it is constitutionally void; Svet remains Civic Consul with full authority. The House may call another vote at any time — but it must simultaneously name and elect a replacement or the vote will again have no effect.

The blocs opposing Svet cannot agree on who that replacement should be. The same inability to agree that prevented them from electing a Civic Consul in the first place now prevents them from removing the one the Senate installed.

The Position She Holds

Svet governs without a Assembly majority. Her legislative agenda is contested. Procedural obstruction is constant. But the Republic is governed — services continue, policy is maintained, the executive function is intact. The House retains its full constructive no-confidence power and can remove her the moment it can agree on a successor. That moment has not yet arrived.

The Senate designation does not expire. It does not lapse after a fixed period. It ends when the Assembly elects a Civic Consul by absolute majority — which terminates the designation — or when Svet is removed through a valid constructive no-confidence vote naming her replacement. Until one of those happens, she is Civic Consul.

Constitutional finding

The constructive no-confidence requirement is what makes the Senate designation robust. The House cannot simply register disagreement with the Senate's choice. It must agree on an alternative. The same inability to agree that produced the formation deadlock protects the designated Civic Consul from removal — until the Assembly resolves its internal disagreement by simultaneously naming and electing her successor, she holds office.

The voters elected this House. Their mandate is respected. No external mechanism dissolved it. The House retains every constitutional power it was given, including the power to replace the Civic Consul at any time. The constitution simply requires that replacement to be constructive — naming a successor, not just registering opposition. A House that can agree on a successor has resolved the deadlock that produced the problem. A House that cannot has not.
