The 120 Days

Year 3. A fractured House cannot elect a Prime Minister. The Senate designates one on Day 80. The House votes against her without naming a successor — and learns that vote has no constitutional effect.

§2.6 PM Formation and Accountability §2.5 Caretaker PM §3.2 House Elections §7.1 Elections Panel

The Republic's third general election produces a House that cannot govern. The seat distribution after RCV counting leaves three distinct blocs — none with an absolute majority, none willing to form a coalition with another. The outgoing PM had designated Deputy PM Lara Voss as Caretaker before the election. Voss holds the office from the moment the outgoing government loses its mandate.

The Caretaker can sign routine instruments, maintain services, and represent the Republic in existing commitments. She cannot make new policy, introduce new legislation, or commit the Republic to new obligations. The Republic is administered but not led. The constitutional obligation is clear: the House must elect a PM by absolute majority.

Days 1–78: Three Failed Votes

Day 14
First vote fails

Bloc A nominates their leader. Bloc B votes against as a bloc. Bloc C splits. Result: 49 in favour, 51 against. No absolute majority. No PM.

Day 31
Second vote fails

Bloc C attempts a compromise candidate acceptable to neither Bloc A nor Bloc B. The vote fails 42–58. The candidate withdraws.

Day 78
Third vote fails

Bloc B nominates their leader. Blocs A and C combine in opposition. Result: 44–56. The House has now failed three times within the statutory period.

Day 80: The Senate Designates — Governance Resolved

Under §2.6, where the House fails to elect a PM within the statutory period, the Senate may designate a serving House member as PM by 2/3 supermajority. The Senate has been watching the deadlock and has identified a candidate who maintains working relationships across all three blocs: Commerce Minister Daria Svet, a Bloc B member with a reputation for fiscal discipline and cross-bloc negotiation.

Day 80
Senate designates by 12–2

Svet is PM. Full constitutional authority, from the moment of NRS publication. She can legislate, set policy, and direct the executive branch. The Caretaker period is over. The governance problem — the absence of an elected PM — is resolved. Caretaker Voss steps down.

Day 83: The House Votes Against Her — To No Effect

Bloc A and Bloc C, unwilling to accept a Senate-designated PM they did not choose, call an immediate confidence vote. Fifty-five members vote against Svet. Forty-five vote to retain her. A clear majority against.

The vote has no constitutional effect.

§2.6 is unambiguous: the House may remove the PM only through a constructive vote of no confidence — a single vote that simultaneously removes the sitting PM and installs a named replacement, by absolute majority. A confidence vote that does not name and elect a successor has no constitutional effect.

Day 83 — LI Assessment
Vote declared constitutionally void

The House Speaker asks the LI for an assessment. The LI publishes within 24 hours: the confidence vote did not name a successor; it is constitutionally void; Svet remains PM with full authority. The House is free to call another vote at any time — but it must name and elect a replacement simultaneously or the vote will again have no effect.

The blocs that voted against Svet cannot agree on who that replacement should be. The same deadlock that prevented them from electing a PM in the first place prevents them from removing the one the Senate installed.

Day 120: A Fresh House

Svet has been governing for 40 days. She is not an ideal PM — she has no House majority, faces constant procedural obstruction, and her legislative agenda is stalled. But the Republic is governed. Services continue. Policy is maintained. The executive function is intact.

Day 120 arrives. Under §2.6, where no PM has been elected by absolute majority within 120 days of a general election, the Elections Panel automatically administers a new House general election. This is a separate mechanism from the Senate designation — it fires regardless of whether a PM is in office. Its purpose is not to resolve a governance crisis, which is already resolved, but to give the public an opportunity for a fresh House mandate.

Day 120 — Constitutional operation
New election triggered

The Elections Panel publishes the election date — 45 days hence. Svet continues as Senate-designated PM throughout the campaign period. She governs. She does not step down. The Caretaker mechanism does not reactivate — there is no vacancy in the PM office.

Election Day — 45 days later
New House, direct mandate

The second election of the year produces a different result. Two blocs, having watched 120 days of their own deadlock hand governance to the Senate, campaign on a joint platform. On Day 8 of the new House, they elect Svet — the Senate's designee — as PM by 63–37. Her Senate designation terminates at the moment the House vote is certified. She now holds the same office, with the same authority, but under a direct House mandate.

Two Mechanisms, Two Problems

The scenario illustrates the constitution's separation of two distinct problems and two distinct solutions.

The first problem — no PM in office — was solved on Day 80 by the Senate designation. Svet had full PM authority from that moment. The House's attempt to remove her on Day 83 failed because it did not follow the constitutional form: a constructive no-confidence vote must name a successor. The blocs that opposed Svet could not agree on who that successor would be, which is precisely why they could not elect a PM in the first place.

Constitutional finding

The 120-day trigger is not a response to governance failure. Governance was resolved forty days earlier. The trigger is the constitution's answer to a different question: does a House that has demonstrated it cannot form a government retain its mandate indefinitely? The answer is no — after 120 days, the public gets the opportunity to send a different one.

The constructive no-confidence requirement is what makes the Senate designation robust. The House cannot simply register disagreement with the Senate's choice. It must agree on an alternative. The same inability to agree that produced the deadlock protects the designated PM from removal until the House can demonstrate it has resolved that deadlock — by simultaneously naming and electing her replacement.

In this case, the public sent back essentially the same answer. The blocs that deadlocked chose to cooperate in the new election. The PM they elected is the same person the Senate chose. The House needed a new election to find the consensus the Senate could already see.
