The 120 Days

Year 3. A fractured House cannot elect a Prime Minister. The Caretaker holds. The Senate designates. The House rejects. Day 120 arrives and the constitution resolves what politics could not.

§2.6 PM Formation §2.5 Caretaker PM §3.2 House Elections §7.1 Elections Panel

The Republic's third general election produces a House that cannot govern. The seat distribution after RCV counting leaves three distinct blocs — none with an absolute majority, none willing to form a coalition with another. The outgoing PM, Tobias Henn, had designated Deputy PM Lara Voss as Caretaker before the election, consistent with §2.6. Voss holds the office from the moment Henn's government loses its mandate.

The constitutional obligation is clear: the House must elect a PM by absolute majority. The clock begins. The Caretaker can sign routine instruments, maintain services, and represent the Republic in existing commitments. She cannot make new policy, cannot introduce new legislation, cannot commit the Republic to new obligations. The Republic is governed but not led.

Days 1–78: Three Failed Votes

Day 14
First vote — no majority

Bloc A nominates their leader. Bloc B votes against as a bloc. Bloc C splits. Result: 49 votes in favour, 51 against, with abstentions. No absolute majority. No PM.

Day 31
Second vote — no majority

Bloc C attempts to build a coalition by nominating a compromise candidate acceptable to neither Bloc A nor Bloc B. The vote fails 42–58. The nominated candidate, a respected infrastructure minister, withdraws from consideration.

Day 78
Third vote — no majority

Bloc B nominates their leader. Blocs A and C vote against in an unusual display of combined opposition to a candidate both dislike. Result: 44–56. The House is demonstrably unable to form a government from within its current composition.

Day 80: The Senate Designates

Under §2.6, where the House fails to elect a PM, the Senate may designate a House member as PM by 2/3 vote. The Senate — watching the House deadlock with increasing concern — has been discussing candidates privately. They have no constitutional obligation to reflect House preferences; they are designating a person who can command the Republic's domestic governance, not a person the current House blocs have approved.

The Senate designates by 12–2 vote: Commerce Minister Daria Svet, a Bloc B member who has maintained working relationships across all three blocs despite formal affiliation. The designation is published to the NRS.

Day 83
House rejects Senate designation

Under §2.6, the House may override a Senate designation by absolute majority. The constructive no-confidence mechanism applies — the House must simultaneously name a successor to Svet if it rejects her. After two days of attempting to agree on an alternative name, the House votes: 55 to reject Svet, 45 to confirm her, with 2 present but not voting. The override passes. Svet is rejected. The House named no successor — they could not agree on one.

The Caretaker continues. The clock continues. Day 83 of no elected PM.

Day 120: The Clock Runs Out

At midnight on Day 120 from the last general election, the constitutional trigger activates. The Elections Panel automatically administers a new House general election. No vote is required. No resolution is passed. The trigger is self-executing.

The Elections Panel publishes the election date — 45 days hence, the minimum period for candidate registration and preparation. Caretaker PM Voss continues to hold the office through the campaign period and until the new House elects a PM.

Election Day — 45 days later
New House, new government

The second election of the year produces a different result. Bloc A and Bloc C, having watched 120 days of paralysis cost them public support, campaign on a joint platform. They win a combined 58 seats. On Day 8 of the new House, they elect Commerce Minister Svet — the Senate's original designee — as PM by 58–42. She assumes office from the moment of the House vote certification. Caretaker PM Voss returns to Cabinet.

What the 120 Days Cost

The Republic operated for 165 days — 120 days of formation deadlock plus 45 days of campaign — under Caretaker authority. Social state services continued. Military readiness was maintained. Existing treaty obligations were honoured. No new policy was made. Several significant legislative decisions were deferred. The LI published three compliance notices during the period noting policy mandates that could not be advanced without an elected PM.

Constitutional finding

The 120-day trigger is the constitution's final answer to a House that cannot govern itself. It does not try to force agreement. It does not impose a PM. It removes the existing House — the one that produced the deadlock — and asks the electorate to send a different one. The democratic accountability runs not to the blocs, but to the voters who elected them.

The Caretaker mechanism is what makes the 120-day window constitutional rather than dangerous. The Republic never lacked a government — it lacked an elected PM. The Caretaker held the office with full constitutional legitimacy for every one of those 165 days. The country did not drift. It waited for politics to resolve what politics had created.

The irony is not lost on the Republic: the person the Senate designated on Day 80 — over the House's objection — is the person the new House elected on Day 8 of its first session. The House needed a new election to find what the Senate could already see.
