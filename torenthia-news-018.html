What Rehn Wants to Finish

Casimir Rehn has five months left in an office he cannot hold again. A conversation about the Toren River, the eastern lake, and what he thinks he owes the person who replaces him.

Petra Vend Political Correspondent · Verentum — Year 13, Month 2

Casimir Rehn's office in the Consular Wing still has eighteen months of calendar pinned to the wall behind his desk, in a republic that gave him six non-renewable years and is now down to five months of them. He has not taken the old pages down. "I keep meaning to," he said, when I asked about it. "Then I think — why. The work doesn't know what month it is."

This is the first extended conversation Rehn has given since his Senate address in Month 11 of last year, and the first time in this campaign cycle he has spoken about anything other than Lake Varda in operational terms. He agreed to it, his office said, because the Toren River treaty had reached a point where talking about it publicly would no longer complicate the negotiation. That point arrived three weeks ago. He did not tell me what changed. He told me the treaty is close, and that "close" has meant something different every month since Month 9 of last year, and he is aware of how that sounds.

Rehn is 58. He came to the Legat Consul's office after eleven years in the Senate Foreign Affairs Committee, the last four as its chair, which means he arrived already fluent in the parts of the job that trip up most first-term consuls — the classification boundaries, the §2.3 intelligence-sharing obligations to the Civic Consul, the slow grinding mechanics of treaty ratification. What he was not fluent in, he said, was the isolation. "You brief the CC. You brief the committees. You publish to the NRS. And you are still the only person who has read everything in the room at the same time. Nobody warns you about that part."

"I don't get to vote for who inherits this. I only get to decide what's still open when they do."

The Toren River treaty is the thing he wants to close before the date arrives, not after. He was careful, in our conversation, not to characterize the four Caldris visits his team has made since Month 10 — confirmed only because this paper's earlier reporting on the negotiation forced the question into the open. "I'm not going to walk you through the text," he said. "I'll tell you what's left: water allocation by season is settled. Maintenance responsibility for the joint stretch is settled. What's not settled is the investment program — how much, over what years, audited by whom. That's the part that takes time, because it's the part that costs money, and the money question always goes last."

I asked whether he thought he would close it before his term ends. "I think I have a better chance than whoever comes after me," he said. "Not because I'm better at this. Because I've been in the room for four years and they'll be in the room for zero. There's a cost to that handoff no matter how clean the file is."

On Lake Varda, he was more guarded, and visibly more tired discussing it than the river. He confirmed what NRS-Y12-3198 already established — that the Republic's diplomatic channel did not reach the Sunderland faction actually responsible for the incident — and said the correction to that channel "is done, but corrections like that don't undo what already happened. Seven people went into that water because two governments were talking past each other for longer than either wanted to admit." He would not say how long the Republic had known the channel was inadequate before Carrow's intelligence summary made it public. "That's not mine to characterize. The Director did her job. I'll let her document speak."

I asked, finally, what he thought about the field running to replace him. He laughed — briefly, the first time in forty minutes. "I read the coverage like everyone else. I have opinions. I'm not going to give them to you, because the last thing this office needs is an outgoing consul trying to pick his successor from the doorway." He paused. "I'll say this much. Whoever wins inherits a Caldris file, a Sunderland file, and an EM fiscal review with a Legislature that has ninety days and counting to respond to it. None of that is a six-month problem. I'd ask the next consul to remember that on day one, the inbox doesn't care that you just won an election."

The calendar behind him still says eighteen months. He noticed me looking at it again on the way out. "Ask me in Month 6," he said. "I'll have taken it down by then. One way or the other."

Petra Vend is The Torenthian's political correspondent, based in Verentum.
